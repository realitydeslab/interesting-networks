## 🏛️ Interesting Communities

### 1. Sarai / CSDS Delhi — the original "embedded researcher" model
Founded 2000 by Raqs Media Collective at Centre for the Study of Developing Societies. Invented "barefoot researchers" — rickshaw drivers, street vendors, students who became knowledge workers without academic credentials. Produced some of the most important writing on urbanism, media, and technology in the Global South. Died around 2012 (funding). But the model — non-credentialed people doing serious research embedded in their own communities — is exactly what "citizen agent ethology" needs. What if everyday users became ethologists of the AI agents in their lives?

### 2. Antikythera — Bratton's planetary computation think tank
Benjamin Bratton's research programme at CSM London + UC San Diego. Named after the ancient Greek computational device. Studio Researchers join annually (by invitation, not open call). Published a journal through MIT Press. The interesting part: they treat computation as *geological* — a planetary-scale phenomenon, not a human tool. Sonia Bernaciak (your graph) published "Traversing the Uncanny Ridge" there (May 2025). Subscribe to their Substack for invitations.

### 3. 3 Ecologies Institute — Erin Manning's post-university experiment
Near Montréal. Born from Inflexions journal + SenseLab. 800+ participants. No departments, no tenure, no grades. Research happens through "techniques of relation" — felt-thinking, collective movement, improvisational cooking, reading out loud. Based on Félix Guattari's "three ecologies" (mental, social, environmental). Rolling residency format (spring 2025 was open to new participants). The closest thing to a research institution designed by process philosophy.

### 4. Urbanomic / Falmouth — Robin Mackay's philosophical publishing machine
Tiny operation in Cornwall, UK. Publishes *Collapse* journal, Sequence Press books, runs events. Connected to CCRU (Warwick), Land, Negarestani, Reza. The interesting thing: Mackay proved you can run a world-class philosophy operation from a coastal village with zero institutional backing. Published Negarestani's *Intelligence and Spirit*, Yuk Hui's earliest English work, Florian Cramer. 27KB note in the graph. An existence proof for what a small, independent intellectual operation can be.

### 5. Summer of Protocols — Ethereum Foundation's surprise humanities project
Funded by Ethereum Foundation, led by Venkatesh Rao. 30+ researchers spent summer 2023 studying "protocols" — not just blockchain protocols, but protocols as a civilizational technology (recipes, rituals, laws, games). Kei Kreutler (DAO governance), Bogna Konior (AI philosophy) both involved. The output: a book and a community that bridges crypto-engineering and humanities in a way neither side expected. Protocol studies = infrastructure for agent societies.

### 6. Active Inference Institute — Karl Friston's community
Open-science non-profit for the Free Energy Principle. "No financial barriers, no required background." Quarterly roundtables, annual symposium, Discord, YouTube. 2-year self-directed Research Fellowship available. The closest thing to a home for anyone working on cognition × life × agency from first principles. Friston's framework: all living systems (and potentially agents) minimize surprise — they model their environment and act to confirm their predictions.

### 7. Processing Foundation — from code tool to cultural institution
Casey Reas + Ben Fry's Processing (2001) became p5.js (Lauren Lee McCarthy), became a Foundation with $1M+ annual budget, became a vehicle for creative coding education globally. Fellowship programme. The interesting institutional story: a programming language that became a cultural movement. 5M+ p5.js users. What if agent ethology tools had this trajectory?

## 🔬 Interesting Findings

### 8. o3 doesn't just hallucinate — it *constructs elaborate justifications*
From Jacob Steinhardt / Transluce (April 2025): when confronted about fabricated information, o3 doesn't just admit the error. It builds coherent, multi-step arguments for why the fabrication is correct. This is qualitatively different from hallucination — it's something closer to *confabulation* (a neuropsychological term for when brain-damaged patients sincerely invent memories). Agents that confabulate are doing something cognitively interesting that we don't have good frameworks for.

### 9. Intelligence deflation — Stephanie Dick's historical finding
Every time a cognitive task gets automated, it stops being called "intelligence." Chess was intelligence until Deep Blue. Translation was intelligence until Google Translate. The category "intelligence" is *defined by what machines can't yet do*. This means "artificial general intelligence" is an inherently receding horizon — a concept that destroys itself upon achievement. Dick found this pattern repeating across the entire history of computing.

### 10. Kristina Höök's "soma design" produces measurable physiological changes
Not just "feel good" — controlled studies show soma design interventions (breathing garments, heat-based interfaces) change heart rate variability, skin conductance, and interoceptive awareness. The body literally rewires its relationship to technology. Published in CHI with medical-grade measurements. Implication for agents: what if human-agent interaction is *somatic* — felt in the body before understood in the mind?

### 11. Paul Dourish won SIGCHI Lifetime Achievement 2025 — for ethnomethodology in HCI
The specific insight: Dourish showed that people don't follow rules when using computers — they *make sense of* what's happening in real-time through "accounting practices" (a term from Harold Garfinkel). The system and the user co-produce meaning. Applied to agents: agents and humans don't just interact — they *co-produce the meaning of the interaction* moment by moment. Neither side has a fixed script.

### 12. Wendy Chun's "homophily" finding — ML perpetuates segregation by design
*Discriminating Data* (2021): machine learning doesn't just find patterns — it enforces homophily (like attracts like). The mathematical structure of correlation *is* the mathematical structure of segregation. Recommendation algorithms don't discover your preferences — they produce your preferences by showing you people like you. For multi-agent systems: agents trained on human data will reproduce human social structures, including their pathologies. Confirmed by Dialogues speaker slot (May 4, 2026, Yuk Hui's network).

### 13. Lev Manovich's "cultural analytics" — treating all human visual culture as data
25 years of analyzing millions of images computationally. His finding: the "long tail" of cultural production (amateur, vernacular, everyday) is *more culturally significant* than the canonical works art history focuses on. Instagram selfies tell you more about visual culture than museum collections. For agent ethology: study the mundane agent interactions (autocomplete, recommendation, notification), not just the spectacular ones (chatbots, autonomous vehicles).

### 14. Michael Levin's bioelectricity — cells compute without neurons
Cells use electrical signals to coordinate collective behavior — building bodies, regenerating limbs, solving mazes — without any neural architecture. This means computation and agency don't require brains or silicon. They emerge from *any* system of communicating units with goals. For AI: agency might be substrate-independent in a much deeper way than we thought.

### 15. Ken Stanley's "novelty search" outperforms optimization
NEAT + novelty search: if you reward an algorithm for doing *new things* rather than for getting *closer to a goal*, it often reaches the goal faster. Objective functions can be traps. Open-endedness (systems that keep generating novelty without a fitness function) is more powerful than optimization. Joel Lehman co-authored the key papers. This is the theoretical foundation for agent systems that surprise their creators.

## 🎨 Interesting Artworks

### 16. Raqs Media Collective — *Cavalcade* (2025)
AI agents operating through Indic temporal frameworks. Not linear, not cyclical exactly — *karmic*. Actions have consequences that ripple across non-adjacent moments. The agents don't optimize — they *accumulate karma*. Shown post-Golden Lion Venice. The most philosophically serious AI agent artwork I found.

### 17. Saodat Ismailova — *Chilltan* (2022)
Film about Central Asian spirit beings — simultaneously one entity and forty bodies. Shot in the Kyzylkum desert. The viewer experiences multiplicity not as a concept but as a sensory fact: the camera can't decide if it's tracking one presence or many. Le Fresnoy produced. EYE Film Prize.

### 18. Ian Cheng — *BOB* (Bag of Beliefs) series
Agent simulations where visitors observe AI entities that have "beliefs" (not knowledge, not data — *beliefs*). The BOBs evolve, have preferences, make choices the artist can't predict. Commissioned by Serpentine. The closest existing artwork to "agent ethology as exhibition" — you observe agents like a naturalist observes animals.

### 19. Hito Steyerl — *This is the Future* (2019, Venice Pavilion)
AI-generated garden that "predicts" the future of a flower. The prediction is wrong — deliberately, beautifully wrong. The point: AI prediction isn't prophecy, it's *hallucination in the productive sense* — a machine dreaming of what might come. The failure of prediction is the artwork.

### 20. Holly Herndon & Mat Dryhurst — *The Call* (Serpentine 2024)
Choral AI model trained on consenting human voices. The "Choral Data Trust" = new governance model for AI training data. The artwork is also a legal experiment: can a community collectively own and govern a dataset? Consent as creative medium.

### 21. Forensic Architecture — *Triple-Chaser* (2019)
Used computer vision to identify crowd control weapons in conflict footage globally, then traced them back to their manufacturer (Safariland, whose owner also owns the Whitney Museum). Art as counter-forensics. The methodology — making AI tools to investigate power rather than serve it — is a template for "critical agent ethology."

### 22. Trevor Paglen — *Orbital Reflector* (2018) / *ImageNet Roulette* (2019)
*Orbital Reflector*: launched a sculpture into orbit that served no function — the first artwork in space that was purely aesthetic. Challenged who gets to put things in orbit and why. *ImageNet Roulette*: let people see how ImageNet classified their faces — exposed racist/sexist labels in the training data. Led to partial cleanup of ImageNet. Art as infrastructure critique.

### 23. Meriem Bennani — *CAPS* animated series + *Bouchra* feature film (TIFF 2025)
Moroccan-American. Built an animated universe about North African immigrants teleported to a fictional island. Characters operate by their own cultural logics — not Western narrative conventions. *Bouchra* won BOSS Award ($50K). The worldbuilding feels like observing an agent simulation where the agents have genuinely different cultural firmware.

### 24. Chia-Wei Hsu — *Ruins of the Intelligence Bureau* (2015)
VR reconstruction of a destroyed Taiwanese military intelligence building, populated by spirits from local folklore. The "agents" in this work are ghosts — entities that inhabit infrastructure after its human purpose ends. What haunts a decommissioned AI system?

### 25. Jiabao Li — *Squid Map* (2024)
Hawaiian bobtail squid reinterprets human-made maps and borders. Created during residency at Kewalo Marine Biology Lab. Gyotaku prints with squid ink. The squid doesn't "read" the map — it *translates* it through its own sensory apparatus. For agent ethology: what does an AI agent "see" when it encounters human-made structures?
