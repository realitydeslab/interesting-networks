## What

TEI is the premier ACM conference for research at the intersection of tangible, embedded, and embodied interaction. Now in its **20th edition**, TEI brings together researchers, artists, designers, and practitioners who work where the physical and digital meet — from wearables to robotics to mixed reality to craft.

**TEI 2026** takes place **March 8–11, 2026** in **Chicago, Illinois, USA**, hosted at:

## Submission Tracks (Typical TEI Structure)

### Full Papers
Archival, peer-reviewed research. 10+ pages ACM format. Topics: new interaction paradigms, systems, tools, materials, studies. Published in ACM DL. Acceptance rate historically ~25–30%.

### Pictorials
A TEI signature format — **design process documentation** in a visual, magazine-style layout. 8–12 pages with emphasis on images. Perfect for documenting design artifacts, prototypes, speculative objects. NOT a photo essay — must have scholarly contribution.

### Work-in-Progress (WIP) / Demos
2–4 page extended abstract + hands-on demonstration at the conference. Lower bar for acceptance. Ideal for early-stage work or novel prototypes. Poster + demo session.

### Art Installations / Studios
Practice-based research. Interactive art, performative computing, speculative artifacts. Typically shown at the conference venue. Strong overlap with speculative design and material computing.

### Doctoral Symposium
1-day workshop for PhD students to present and receive structured feedback. Requires supervisor nomination.

### Student Design Competition
Teams compete with tangible computing design proposals. Faculty-mentored.

## The TEI Community

### What TEI Is

TEI sits at a productive intersection:
- **HCI** (ACM SIGCHI family) — rigorous user studies, system design
- **Tangible Computing** — physical affordances, craft, materials
- **Design Research** — RTD (Research Through Design), practice-based methods
- **Embodied Cognition** — soma, body, movement as computation
- **Art & Technology** — installations, speculative artifacts, critical making

TEI is **smaller and more intimate than CHI** (typically ~200–400 attendees vs. CHI's 4,000+). This is a feature: community cohesion, genuine dialogue, less noise.

### Relation to Other Venues
- **vs. CHI:** TEI is smaller, more artifact-focused, more design-research tolerant. Less systems engineering. CHI is where the field reports; TEI is where the field thinks through making.
- **vs. DIS:** Both value design research and tangible artifacts, but DIS skews more critical/social. TEI skews more materials/body/craft.
- **vs. UIST:** UIST is systems/engineering; TEI is design/embodied. Very different cultures.
- **vs. RTD:** RTD (Research Through Design) is a symposium, not a conference; closer to TEI Pictorials in spirit.

### Historical Context

- **Founded:** 2007 at MIT Media Lab. Founding chairs included **Hiroshi Ishii** (MIT), **Bill Buxton** (Microsoft Research), **Brygg Ullmer** (LSU), among others.
- **Origin story:** TEI grew out of frustration with CHI's bias toward screen-based GUI research. The community wanted a home for physical/tangible/embodied work.
- **Name evolution:** Originally "Tangible and Embedded Interaction" → "Tangible, Embedded and **Embodied** Interaction" (added "Embodied" around 2010s to reflect turn toward soma/body/movement research).
- **Scope evolution:** tangibles/TUIs → wearables, e-textiles → somaesthetics → materials computing → speculative design artifacts → AI+body.
- **2026:** 20th edition. Return to North America after 7 years (last NA edition was 2019 in Tempe, Arizona).

## For Amber

### Relevance Analysis

TEI is **one of the most natural venues for Amber's work**. The embodied/tangible community is explicitly where:
- Mixed reality as embodied computing lives
- Speculative design artifacts are published
- Practice-based research (making as knowing) is valued
- The body-as-material, not-just-screen approach is native

**HoloKit** is a tangible MR artifact — it's literally a physical object you hold to your face that transforms embodied perception. This is deeply TEI territory. A HoloKit pictorial or design paper would fit naturally.

**Agent ethology / embodied AI** — if framed through physical/embodied interaction (what happens in the body when agents are present? what tangible affordances mediate human-AI relations?), this could find a TEI home. More challenging pitch — TEI expects physical artifact contribution.

**Speculative design** — TEI has a strong speculative/critical making track. Speculative artifacts that ask questions about AI, more-than-human, machine behavior can thrive here.

### Strongest Submission Types for Amber

1. **Pictorial** — Document the HoloKit design process. TEI Pictorials love: open-source hardware design, iterative prototyping, the making process as inquiry. HoloKit's evolution from foam prototype to mass-produced headset is a compelling design story. 10K users, open-source, MIT-adjacent — this is exactly what TEI celebrates.

2. **Demo / WIP** — Show HoloKit live. The tangible community *loves* hands-on demos. A demo booth at TEI would get huge engagement from the exact community that cares about physical MR artifacts.

3. **Full Paper** — "Embodied AI Agents: Tangible Interaction with Non-Human Machine Actors" — frame agent ethology through the lens of embodied interaction. What changes about human-AI behavior when the AI is embodied in a physical space? Harder to land at TEI (needs physical prototype/study) but strong conceptual fit.

4. **Art Installation** — A speculative installation exploring human-machine embodiment, using HoloKit or related artifact. TEI loves this, especially for 20th anniversary.

## Why Interesting (for Amber's Research Taste)

TEI sits at the **edge of chaos** for Amber's research. The field is:
- Actively absorbing AI/embodied agents into its scope
- Grappling with more-than-human design (multispecies tangibles, living materials)
- Bridging art practice and HCI rigor in ways that aren't typical at CHI
- Deeply interdisciplinary: CS + design + craft + art + biology
- Small enough to actually form relationships and be known

TEI is NOT an anti-pattern venue — it's not pure engineering, not "AI for X", not industry showcase. It asks: *what does it mean to make physical things that think, feel, or act?* That's exactly Amber's territory.

The 20th anniversary theme "Tide + Tied" — interdisciplinary convergence — resonates with Amber's interest in how communities cross-pollinate.
