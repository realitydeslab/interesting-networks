**The sixth decennial Aarhus Conference on critical computing.** Held August 18–22, 2025, Aarhus, Denmark. Theme: **Computing (X) Crisis**. The (X) is a variable — contributions speculate on replacements: *with, after, in, against*. The conference marks 50 years since the first Aarhus conference (1975).

🎉 **Amber presented here** — critique "Autonomous Realities" on protocol fiction and mixed reality futures.

---

## What

The Aarhus decennial is the **premier venue for critical, political, and design-oriented computing research**. Born from Scandinavian participatory design traditions (democracy, workers' rights, co-design), it has evolved into a broad critical computing conference bringing together HCI, CSCW, STS, design research, and political theory.

Unlike CHI (huge, diffuse), Aarhus is **intimate, politically charged, and intellectually serious**. No corporate showcase. Real critique. Real design experiments. Real stakes.

**Theme: Computing (X) Crisis**
The call invited contributions on how computing shapes, causes, responds to, and is entangled with crisis — climate, economic inequality, democracy erosion, the "polycrisis" of interconnected collapses. The (X) variable was itself generative: participants were asked to propose their own preposition or relation.

From the CFP: *"Today, 'crisis' characterises seemingly perilous moments linked to the climate, economic and social inequality, democracy, relations among societies and, more broadly, a flourishing life for all critters, human and otherwise."*

## Amber's Paper Here

**"Autonomous Realities: A Journey into Protocolizing Digital Object Permanence in a Future of Many Mixed Realities"**
*Botao Amber Hu, Reality Design Lab*
Published as: **Accepted Critique** (archival, ACM DL)
[Proceedings DOI](https://dl.acm.org/doi/proceedings/10.1145/3744169)

> *"We are moving toward a future of multiple overlapping and co-existing mixed realities. This paper employs protocol fiction as a speculative design method to explore this near-future scenario. We follow the journey of a fictional digital pet rock as it travels through successive protocol eras of mixed reality..."*

Four protocol chapters: Centralized Realities → Distributed Realities → Persistent Realities → **Autonomous Realities**. A comic-style narrative examining ontological questions about digital object permanence. This is Amber doing what she does best: speculative design + protocol studies + more-than-human digital ontology.

**Format note:** The "Critique" track at Aarhus blends analysis and artistic/speculative practice — exactly the right fit for Amber's work.

## Selected Papers of Note (Amber-Adjacent)

### Directly Relevant

- **"Sticking with Affect in HCI and Design: from Interaction to Relation"** — Jonas Fritsch (IT University of Copenhagen). Explicitly engages **more-than-human design** and affect theory. Cites Boehner et al. 2005 Aarhus paper. Four themes: affect + more-than-human, affective encounters, affect and care across ecologies, affect in crisis.

- **"Contemporary Participatory Design: Research Agendas for Societal Crisis"** — Rachel Charlotte Smith, Liesbeth Huybrechts et al. (Aarhus, UHasselt, Roskilde, Mozilla). Maps PD's evolution into: *politicising, diversifying, relationality, transforming*.

- **"Feeling like a State: Affect and Control in the Age of AI"** — Silvia Lindtner (U Michigan). Feminist ethnography of AI governance in rural China. Control through affect rather than surveillance.

### Interesting Critical Work

- **"Robots are Increasingly: Imagination Crisis in HCI Research"** — Mafalda Gamboa (Chalmers). A critique/poem assembled from robotics paper openings. Refuses inevitability; calls for "union for computing alternatives."

- **"Utopian Epistles: computing [codesign] crises"** — Pelle Ehn (legendary PD figure). Letters to younger generations on 50 years of codesign and democracy. Special event: letters + responses from multiple generations.

- **"The Colonial Legacy of the Human in HCI"** — Jensine Raihan (UC Irvine). Sylvia Wynter's sociogenic principle applied to design. Decolonial computing.

- **"Steps towards an Ecology for the Internet"** — Madhavapeddy et al. (Cambridge, Imperial). Internet as biological system, re-decentralization, mutualism vs surveillance capitalism. Cambridge connection.

### Demos Worth Noting

- **"Invitation to an Under-ground Party"** — Margrete Lodahl Rolighed & Lone Koefoed Hansen (Aarhus). Posthuman plant sensing: contact microphone in soil → disco ball speed. Literally more-than-human design.

- **"ReefSense"** — Andersen & Savage (Copenhagen). Passive computational system helping shade corals. Computation *with* nature. Climate + HCI.
