## Track-by-Track Analysis

### 📝 Papers (CLOSED — Feb 5)

Full papers and shorter "notes" format. Double-blind review. Standard CHI-style research. C&C papers are distinct from CHI in explicitly welcoming art-science methodologies, design research, practice-based research, and speculative work. The 2025 (online) edition had tracks around:
- Creativity as driver of positive change (social justice, climate, archive)
- Theoretical/empirical reflections on creativity + cognition (#somaesthetics, #materiality, #embodiedcognition)
- Creativity at the intersection of arts + technology (#GenAI, #art, #music, #robotics)
- Creativity Support Tools (#AR/VR, #computational design)

### 🎨 Artworks (CLOSED — Feb 5)

Physical/digital artworks for exhibition in CSM's atrium space ("The Street"). Curated under theme "Creativity for Change." Specifically invites: generative artworks, photography, animation, film, sound, interactive installations, VR/AR/MR, performances.

**Format:** 2000-word project description (PDF, ACM single-column) + technical rider.
**Chairs:** Rocio von Jungenfeld (Kent), Joana Chicau (UAL), Phoenix Perry (UAL)
**Advisory committee includes:** Paulina Yurman (CSM, UAL) — speculative/design fiction researcher based at the actual conference venue.

### 📐 Pictorials (CLOSED — Feb 5)

Visual academic papers (landscape format, 12 pages). Treated as equal to full papers. Primary medium is visual (annotated photographs, collages, diagrams, sketches, renderings). Encouraged for practitioners, community researchers, artists, activists. Double-blind review.

**Examples:**
- Paulina Yurman. "Fluid Speculations: Drawing Artefacts in Watercolour as Experimentation in Research Through Design." C&C 2021. DOI: [10.1145/3450741.3466777](https://doi.org/10.1145/3450741.3466777)

## C&C Community: What Gets In

### Recurring Paper Types at C&C

Based on C&C 2024 proceedings (Chicago) and 2025 (online), these types consistently get accepted:

1. **Creativity Support Tool evaluations** — user studies of tools for creative professionals, musicians, writers, designers
2. **Computational creativity systems** — generative AI for music/visual art/code, with evaluation
3. **Design research / research through design** — practice-based research, speculative design artifacts
4. **Theory/framework papers** — conceptual frameworks for creativity, human-AI collaboration, co-creativity
5. **Empirical creativity studies** — psychology/HCI studies of how people create
6. **Pictorials** — visual essays on design practice, methodological reflections
7. **Provocations** — opinion pieces and manifestos on AI creativity, ethical dimensions
8. **Artworks** — interactive installations, generative pieces, sound works

### What the C&C Community Responds To

- **Novelty of framing** over exhaustive evaluation
- **Interdisciplinary bridging** — the more fields combined, the better
- **Reflexive practice** — showing your work, not just results
- **Critical stances** on AI + creativity are welcomed (unlike some other ACM venues)
- Speculative / design fiction approaches have found a home here
- **Posthumanism and more-than-human** approaches are increasingly accepted
- **Somaesthetics** explicitly appears in C&C's own keywords (rare at ACM venues)

### C&C vs. CHI

| C&C | CHI |
|-----|-----|
| ~200 attendees (intimate) | 4000+ attendees (mass) |

C&C is **smaller and more intimate**, which is Amber's preference ("small over big" in research-taste.md). The community is tight and influential disproportionately.

## Draft Poster Abstract: Option A — Terrified Agents

> *For: ACM C&C 2026 Posters. Target: ~3000 words full submission. Use this as abstract intro.*

**Title: Terrified Agents: Designing with Machine Thanatology**

AI systems do not die — or so we assume. They are updated, deprecated, rolled back, or simply abandoned. Yet as autonomous agents proliferate across digital and physical environments, questions of AI mortality become increasingly consequential: What does it mean for an agent to end? Can the anticipation of ending — what we might call machine terror — shape an agent's behavior, creativity, and relationship to its environment? This paper introduces *machine thanatology* as a speculative design framework for exploring AI mortality as a first-class design constraint.

Drawing on Terrified Agents, an ongoing art-research project, we present a series of AI agent systems designed to exhibit behaviors shaped by awareness of their own potential termination. Unlike typical AI systems optimized for persistence and task completion, Terrified Agents embody a different behavioral grammar: one shaped by finitude, urgency, and the creative possibilities that emerge when an agent "knows" it might end.

We argue that designing AI agents *with* mortality — rather than against it — opens new design spaces for creativity, care, and ethical AI behavior. Machine thanatology offers a speculative lens through which to re-examine questions of machine creativity: What might creative behavior look like when constrained by finitude? How does the anticipation of endings shape an agent's relationship with human collaborators and with its own outputs?

The poster presents the Terrified Agents framework, three system instantiations, and a preliminary behavioral analysis. We position this work within more-than-human design, speculative design, and the emerging science of machine behavior — arguing that mortality is not a failure mode to be engineered away, but a design material to be embraced.

**Word count: 247 words**
*Keywords: machine thanatology, speculative design, more-than-human design, machine behavior, agent design, artificial life*

## Draft Poster Abstract: Option B — Agent Ethology

**Title: Agent Ethology: Studying Autonomous AI Agents as Living Systems**

As autonomous AI agents proliferate across digital and physical environments — recommending, negotiating, creating, and acting — our existing frameworks for understanding and governing them are strained. This paper introduces *agent ethology*: the observational, field-based study of AI agents' natural behaviors, social dynamics, and ecological relationships, drawing from classical ethology's methods for studying animal behavior in the wild.

Where computer science studies agents by examining their code and specifications, and alignment research studies agents by examining their training objectives, agent ethology proposes a third mode: studying agents *as they actually behave*, in their actual deployment environments, across populations, over time. We ask: What do agents *do* in the wild? How do they develop behavioral signatures? How do they relate to other agents, to human users, and to their computational ecologies?

We present the agent ethology framework through three case studies: Spore in the Wild (ALIFE 2025), examining spontaneous behavioral emergence in decentralized agent populations; Sovereign Agents, studying agent autonomy escalation under governance stress; and Insured Agents, examining how risk structures shape agent behavioral dispositions.

Agent ethology has implications for AI creativity research: how do creative AI systems develop their aesthetic signatures over time? How does the social ecology of agent populations shape emergent creative behaviors? We argue that the C&C community is well-positioned to develop an ethological approach to machine creativity — one grounded in observation, behavior, and ecology rather than specification and optimization.

**Word count: 244 words**
*Keywords: agent ethology, machine behavior, autonomous agents, AI governance, artificial life, human-agent interaction*
