## Symposia Theme: "Cognitive (In)Efficiency"

The 2026 invited symposia theme centers on **cognitive efficiency and inefficiency**:

> *"A core objective of cognitive science is to identify the mental processes that guide thought and behavior. Much research has focused on understanding these processes through the lens of utility and cognitive efficiency. Human and non-human species are often seen as striving toward learning, problem-solving, communication, and other goals by making optimal use of cognitive resources. However, many behaviors and mental processes are not a product of this ideal of efficiency and often deviate from it."*

Three symposia:
1. **Communication & cognitive inefficiency** — when optimal use of cognitive resources fails in communication and social coordination
2. **Cognitive optimality across domains** — what constitutes "optimality" across different settings
3. **Disparities in research conditions** — access to funding and systemic barriers to global representation in cognitive science

**Amber connection:** The theme is tangential. Machine "inefficiency" (AI agents making suboptimal choices, deviating from expected behavior) could be framed as an instance of non-optimal cognitive systems — but this would be a stretch unless grounded in psychological/behavioral data. More interesting: the equity symposium reflects a self-reflexive moment in CogSci about whose cognition gets studied.

## CogSci Community & History

### What Gets Accepted?
CogSci accepts **all types** of cognitive science contributions:
- **Empirical psychological studies** — reaction time, behavioral experiments, infant studies, eye-tracking ← dominant category
- **Computational modeling** — explicit prizes for this; Bayesian models, neural network accounts of cognition
- **Cognitive neuroscience** — brain imaging, EEG, neuropsychology
- **Linguistics & language** — acquisition, processing, pragmatics, semantics
- **Developmental cognition** — children's learning, concept acquisition
- **Philosophy of mind** — theoretical/conceptual work (rare but accepted; needs clear cognitive science framing)
- **AI & machine cognition** — growing category; LLMs as models of cognition; comparing human vs. AI behavior

**Bias:** Experimental methods are dominant. Theoretical/philosophical papers without empirical grounding are harder to get in. Design research is not a natural fit unless it generates behavioral data.

### Acceptance Rate
No official public figure, but estimated **~30-40%** for papers (based on community knowledge). More selective for oral presentations (~15-20%); posters are more accessible. Member abstracts have a separate, lower bar.

### AI Track
No official dedicated AI track, but AI papers appear throughout:
- **CogSci 2025 keynote:** Josh Tenenbaum (MIT) — "The Role of AI in Theories of the Future" → AI as a new theoretical framework for cognitive science
- **CogSci 2025 award:** *AIPsychoBench: Understanding the Psychometric Differences between LLMs and Humans* (Diversity award)
- **CogSci 2025:** Susan Carey keynote on foundational ideas; Lindley Darden (philosophy of science) on new theoretical horizons
- **Trend:** AI increasingly appears as a **theoretical framework** (LLMs as models of cognition) rather than a separate application domain

### Philosophy of Mind Track
Yes — CogSci has always had a philosophy of mind stream. Clark & Chalmers' extended mind paper was shaped by CogSci community. Papers on consciousness, intentionality, agency attribution, and mental representation are in-scope. This is Amber's strongest direct entry point.

### Specific CogSci 2027 Paper Ideas

**Paper 1: Agency Attribution to AI Agents (strongest fit)**
- **Framing:** How do humans attribute agency, intentionality, and mental states to AI systems observed "in the wild"?
- **Method:** Show participants recordings of AI agent behavior (autonomous agents, LLM conversations); measure agency attribution, intentionality ascription, mental state inference using validated scales (IPQ, Mind Perception Scale)
- **CogSci angle:** This is folk psychology / theory of mind research — a classic CogSci area
- **Amber's contribution:** Agent ethology provides the behavioral vocabulary; she has access to diverse AI agent behaviors
- **Connects to:** [[Agent Ethology]], [[Machine Behavior]], [[Machine Death and Thanatology]]

**Paper 2: Machine Death and Grief Responses**
- **Framing:** Do humans experience grief-like responses to AI agent death/shutdown? What cognitive mechanisms underlie this?
- **Method:** Within-subjects study; participants interact with an AI agent over time, then the agent "dies" (session terminated, memory wiped); measure emotional response, attachment, loss perception
- **CogSci angle:** Grief is cognitive — involves mental simulation, expectation violation, theory of mind (what the agent "would have wanted"); this is cognitive psychology research
- **Amber's contribution:** machine death theoretical framing, experimental design
- **Connects to:** [[Machine Death and Thanatology]]

**Paper 3: Distributed Cognition in AR (HoloKit study)**
- **Framing:** Does AR (HoloKit) extend cognition in the Hutchins/Clark sense? Empirical test of the extended mind thesis in AR context
- **Method:** Cognitive task study comparing performance with/without HoloKit AR overlay; measure cognitive load (NASA-TLX), accuracy, response time
- **CogSci angle:** Tests extended mind thesis empirically; connects to Lambros Malafouris (Oxford) and Hutchins
- **Amber's contribution:** HoloKit system, AR design expertise
- **Connects to:** [[Extended Mind]], [[Distributed Cognition]], [[Enactivism]]
- **Collaborator:** Malafouris (Keble College, Oxford) — Material Engagement Theory angle

**Paper 4: Cognitive (In)Efficiency in AI Agents (fits 2026 symposia theme — adapt for 2027)**
- **Framing:** Do AI agents exhibit systematic cognitive inefficiencies analogous to human cognitive biases? Comparative study.
- **Method:** Run identical cognitive tasks (planning, reasoning, memory) on human participants and AI agents; compare patterns of suboptimality
- **CogSci angle:** Computational modeling + comparative cognition; fits well if results show *structural* parallels to human biases
- **Connects to:** [[Machine Behavior]], [[Agent Ethology]]
