## Themes & Intellectual Scope

The conference asks:
- How will AI impact human communication, friendship, education, ethics, cooperation, and conflict?
- How do we ensure AI safety when human cognitive limitations are an attack vector?
- What is the impact of AI on human innovation and culture?
- Are there lessons from biology or political science for controlling a more powerful intelligence?

**Methods embraced:**
- Behavioral science: experimental paradigms for AI behavior in real-world contexts
- Complexity science: GenAI + human interaction as complex system
- Cultural evolution: co-evolution of humans and GenAI
- Economics: economic behavior of machines and social impact

**This is not:** an AI safety conference. Not a tech conference. Not a HCI conference. It's a *behavioral science* conference that takes AI systems seriously as behavioral entities — closer to ethology or social science than engineering.

## Agent Ethology ↔ Machine Behavior: Overlap Analysis
| Concept | Machine Behavior framing | Agent Ethology framing (Amber's) |
|---------|-------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Unit of study | AI system behavior in social context | AI agent as organism with behavioral repertoire |
| Methods | Behavioral science experiments, social simulation | Ethogram, naturalistic observation, ecological study |
| Goal | Understanding to control | Understanding as form of recognition / care |
| Frame | Governance + safety | More-than-human + cosmotechnics |
| Machine death | Not addressed | Central question |
| Cultural specificity | Some attention (Moral Machine had cultural data) | Foregrounded via cosmotechnics |

**Key insight:** Machine behavior as currently practiced is primarily *experimental social science* on AI. Agent ethology would add the *naturalistic observation* layer — watching agents in situ rather than designing behavioral paradigms. Amber's framing is the missing methodology, not a competing field.
