## Theme: "Trust in the Loop"

> "Trust is a cornerstone of libraries, archives, and museums. Trust in the Loop extends the idea of 'human in the loop' to focus on the work LAMs must do to ensure that AI-enabled services are built on trust, authenticity, and accountability."

The Program Committee welcomes proposals exploring how trust is **challenged, maintained, or strengthened** as LAMs experiment with, implement, and are impacted by AI technologies.

## Topics & Tracks

### Topic 1: Implementations
How AI tools and systems are being implemented in LAMs operating under real constraints (limited resources, legacy systems, complex data, community accountability).

Key questions:
- What governance structures guide AI investment?
- How are risks identified and mitigated?
- How is success/impact measured?
- How are pilots scaled to operational systems?
- What implementations are being reconsidered or deliberately avoided?

### Topic 2: Data
AI systems are data-driven; responsible AI depends on data quality, structure, and context. LAMs steward vast culturally significant data — often messy, multilingual, historically situated.

Key questions:
- Risks and benefits of using LAM data in AI
- Data prep, documentation, standards for AI use
- Quality validation of AI outputs
- Controlled vocabularies in AI workflows
- Shared benchmark datasets for LAM content
- MCP or other approaches to manage external data use
- Policies for data scraping and commercial AI relationships
- Emerging governance practices

### Topic 3: People
AI adoption affects users, visitors, patrons, staff, and communities.

Key questions:
- How are AI tools engaging users in new ways?
- Staff training and capacity-building programs
- Integrating staff expertise into AI workflows
- Community consent when LAM content becomes AI training data
- Managing expectations with vendors, boards, oversight bodies

### Topic 4: Models & Infrastructure
LAMs have a long tradition of collaboration around shared infrastructure. AI presents new collective challenges.

Key questions:
- Shared infrastructure needs for AI in LAMs
- Long-term data storage, security, transfer
- Sharing models, data, documentation across institutions
- Evaluating AI tools/models for LAM use
- Incident reporting for AI operations
- Managing long-term costs

## For Amber

### The Honest Assessment

**Amber is not a library/archives person.** This is a practitioner conference for GLAM professionals, not a venue for HCI researchers, speculative designers, or ALife theorists. The dominant language here is: governance, risk management, provenance tracking, data standards, vendor relationships. Not agent ethology, machine death, or cosmotechnics.

That said, there IS a non-trivial intellectual overlap zone:

**Where Amber could plausibly fit:**
1. **Trustworthy agentic systems in cultural heritage** — Amber's work on accountability structures for AI agents (sovereign agents, insured agents) maps onto the "Trust" theme. Archives are among society's most trust-sensitive institutions. How do autonomous AI agents behave when processing archival knowledge? This is a legitimate intersection.

2. **Protocol studies + provenance** — Archival provenance chains (chain of custody, authority records) as protocols governing AI behavior. AI as disruptor of epistemic authority. This is genuinely interesting territory where Amber's protocol angle could land with this community.

3. **Content authenticity** — The community is actively grappling with AI-generated content vs. authentic records. "Machine Behavior in Archival Contexts" could be framed for this audience.

4. **MCP angle** — The CFP explicitly mentions "MCP or other approaches to manage external use of data." Amber's interest in protocol infrastructure for AI systems is directly relevant.

**Why it's still peripheral:**
- The audience expects concrete case studies, not philosophical frameworks
- Washington DC location adds travel friction (Amber is Oxford/Cambridge based)
- 53% acceptance = easy to get in, but is it worth the time investment?
- The community will be politely interested but unlikely to become active collaborators on agent ethology / ALife
- No connection to Amber's core communities (ALIFE, CHI, FAccT, ALife art scene)

**One warm connection:** [[Daniel Chávez Heras]] presented at FF2025 and is connected to this community. If Amber submits, Chávez Heras would be a useful bridge.

### If Amber Does Submit

**Best bet: Lightning talk or Short paper** (lowest effort, tests the water)

Possible pitch:
> **"Agents in the Archive: Machine Behavior and Trust in AI Systems for Cultural Heritage"**
> Drawing on agent ethology — an observational framework for studying how AI agents behave in complex environments — this talk examines what it means for AI systems operating in archival contexts to be trustworthy. When AI agents process culturally significant materials, their behavior must satisfy epistemic, ethical, and institutional accountability requirements simultaneously. What can the behavioral study of AI systems teach us about designing trust?

This pitch bridges Amber's work with the community's concerns without requiring him to pretend he's a librarian.

**Alternative: Skip it.** For the same effort, a lightning talk at CHI, FAccT, or a submission to ALIFE would have more strategic return. This is a nice-to-have, not a priority.
