### PROPOSAL: Agent Ethology and Computational Creativity: Behavioral Methods for Studying Creative AI Systems

**Proposed workshop type:** Half-day workshop  
**Organizer:** Botao Amber Hu, University of Oxford  

---

#### Abstract

The evaluation of computational creativity has long relied on output-focused metrics and Turing-style comparisons with human performance. Yet as AI systems become increasingly autonomous actors in creative ecosystems — generating, iterating, curating, and responding to feedback without human direction — a fundamental methodological question emerges: how do we study what creative AI systems *do*, not merely what they *produce*? This workshop introduces *agent ethology* — the systematic behavioral observation of AI agents as ecological subjects — as a complementary framework for computational creativity evaluation. Drawing on Tinbergen's Four Questions (mechanism, ontogeny, function, phylogeny) and the methods of classical ethology, we invite CC researchers to examine autonomous creative agents not just as output generators but as behavioral actors: observing their strategies, survival patterns, and ecological interactions across deployment contexts. We aim to catalyze a new methodological thread within CC evaluation — one grounded in behavioral science and ecological observation, bridging CC with ALife, machine behavior research, and more-than-human design.

---

#### 1. Motivation and Relevance to ICCC

Computational creativity evaluation faces a growing methodological tension. Existing frameworks assess creative *outputs* through novelty, surprise, quality, and cultural value — measures calibrated against human aesthetic judgment. These metrics are well-suited to artifact-centric systems: a poem generator, an image synthesizer, a musical improviser. But they leave a critical question unanswered: *how does a creative AI system behave*?

As CC systems proliferate into environments where they operate with genuine autonomy — agentic pipelines, social platforms, generative ecosystems, blockchain economies — they exhibit complex behavioral dynamics that output metrics cannot capture: survival strategies under resource constraints, ecological interactions with other agents and systems, developmental trajectories shaped by training lineage, and adaptive responses to environmental pressures. Understanding this behavioral dimension is not merely an academic exercise; it is essential for evaluating, designing, and governing creative AI systems responsibly.

*Agent ethology* offers a principled response. Classical ethology — developed by Lorenz, Tinbergen, and von Frisch to study animal behavior in natural habitats — provides a methodological toolkit for observing organisms in ecological context, asking not just *how* behaviors arise mechanistically but *why* they exist functionally and *how* they develop and evolve. Applied to artificial agents, Tinbergen's Four Questions become:

| Tinbergen Level | For Creative AI Systems |
|---|---|
| **Mechanism** | How does the system generate creative outputs? What architectural and prompt conditions shape behavior? |
| **Ontogeny** | How did training, fine-tuning, and in-context learning shape creative disposition? |
| **Function** | What ecological role does creative behavior serve? What survival value does it provide? |
| **Phylogeny** | How does model lineage and architectural ancestry shape creative tendencies? |

This framework complements existing CC evaluation by adding a behavioral and ecological dimension. It is particularly timely: ICCC 2026's call for papers explicitly highlights "Computational Creativity Evaluation: metrics, frameworks, formalisms and methodologies" and "Interdisciplinary Perspectives: philosophical and sociological studies of creative AI systems" as priority themes. A workshop bringing ethological methods to CC evaluation sits precisely at this intersection.

The ICCC community has not yet addressed this methodological gap. A review of ICCC 2024 and 2025 workshop programmes reveals no workshop focused on behavioral methods, ecological observation, or ethological approaches to CC evaluation. This workshop fills that gap.

**Format:** Half-day workshop (3.5 hours), scheduled June 28 or June 29, 2026  
**Expected attendance:** 15–25 researchers  
**Submission types (optional):** 2-4 page position papers describing behavioral observation approaches, case studies of creative AI systems observed ethologically, or theoretical contributions applying ethological frameworks to CC evaluation

**Schedule:**

| Time | Session |
|------|---------|

| 10:45–11:00 | Break |

| 12:15–12:30 | Plenary synthesis and research agenda for behavioral CC evaluation |

The workshop targets CC researchers working in or curious about:

- Societal and ethical dimensions of deployed creative AI

**Botao Amber Hu** is a DPhil candidate in Human-Centred Computing at the University of Oxford (supervisor: Max Van Kleek), and an ERA (Existential Risk Alliance) Fellow at Cambridge (advisor: Joel Lehman). His prior work spans HCI (CHI 2026 × 2), AAAI (2026 × 2), and creative AI applications at the intersection of technology and design practice.
