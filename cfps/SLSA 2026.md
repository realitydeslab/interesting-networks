### Theme Description

The conference invites scholars, artists, writers, scientists, and practitioners to critically engage with **epistemic justice** and the emergence of **Alterscience**. At a moment when climatic, social, and political crises destabilize established structures of knowledge, SLSA 2026 seeks to:

- Reimagine what counts as valid knowledge
- Amplify epistemologies historically marginalized or excluded
- Construct new, plural, and inclusive intellectual futures
- Bring together humanities, social sciences, natural sciences, arts, and emerging technologies
- Rethink how knowledge is produced, legitimized, and transmitted
- Examine ethical, political, and ecological consequences of knowledge systems

The conference foregrounds **plural, insurgent, Indigenous, Afro-diasporic, migrant, refugee, feminist, queer, and Global South knowledges**.

Organized by USP in partnership with:

## Theme Analysis: "RATIO ETHICA: Toward Epistemic Justice"

### What This Means
"Epistemic justice" (Miranda Fricker's term) = justice concerning who gets to be a knower, whose testimony is trusted, whose concepts are borrowed without credit. **Testimonial injustice** (dismissing testimony because of identity) + **hermeneutical injustice** (lacking concepts to interpret experience).

The USP + SLSA framing expands this into **Alterscience** — alternative, plural epistemologies that challenge Western scientific hegemony. In 2026 São Paulo, this is politically charged: it's a conference happening in Brazil, in the Global South, explicitly saying "Northern/Western knowledge monopolies are not neutral."

### How Amber's Work Connects

**Agent Ethology as Alterscience (Axis 11 — STRONGEST)**
Studying AI behavior observationally, ethologically — rather than from within CS's positivist paradigm — is itself an epistemic claim. Rahwan's machinebehavior.science program is a kind of Alterscience in the sense that it imports ethological/ecological epistemologies INTO AI. Frame Amber's work as: "What if we treated AI as a social/behavioral phenomenon rather than a technical one? What new knowledge becomes possible?" This is epistemic justice for the machines — but also for the non-CS disciplines whose methods are being vindicated.

**Machine Death as Epistemic Claim (Axis 2 — Insurgent Epistemologies)**
The Terrified Agents / machine-death work asks: what does AI mortality mean? This is an insurgent epistemological claim against hegemonic CS which treats agents as stateless, disposable, replaceable. Amber's work says: duration matters, death matters, an agent's history matters — this is a challenge to how CS knows AI.

**Cosmotechnics as Epistemic Decolonization (Axis 2)**
Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics argues there is no single Technology — only cosmotechnics, plural, rooted in different cosmologies. Chinese techno-philosophical traditions (Dao + machine) offer a non-Western epistemology of technology. For a USP audience with strong Latin American + Afro-diasporic knowledge traditions, Yuk Hui's framework could speak powerfully. Amber is positioned at the intersection of Chinese philosophy + AI + design in ways few Global North researchers are.

**More-than-Human Design (Axis 7 & 12)**
HoloKit as open-source hardware for non-Western contexts; GravField as more-than-human interaction. These connect to epistemic justice through the design of technologies that aren't purely extractive or top-down.
