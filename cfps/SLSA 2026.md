### Theme Description

The conference invites scholars, artists, writers, scientists, and practitioners to critically engage with **epistemic justice** and the emergence of **Alterscience**. At a moment when climatic, social, and political crises destabilize established structures of knowledge, SLSA 2026 seeks to:

- Reimagine what counts as valid knowledge
- Amplify epistemologies historically marginalized or excluded
- Construct new, plural, and inclusive intellectual futures
- Bring together humanities, social sciences, natural sciences, arts, and emerging technologies
- Rethink how knowledge is produced, legitimized, and transmitted
- Examine ethical, political, and ecological consequences of knowledge systems

The conference foregrounds **plural, insurgent, Indigenous, Afro-diasporic, migrant, refugee, feminist, queer, and Global South knowledges**.

Organized by USP in partnership with:

## Theme Analysis: "RATIO ETHICA: Toward Epistemic Justice"

### What This Means
"Epistemic justice" (Miranda Fricker's term) = justice concerning who gets to be a knower, whose testimony is trusted, whose concepts are borrowed without credit. **Testimonial injustice** (dismissing testimony because of identity) + **hermeneutical injustice** (lacking concepts to interpret experience).

The USP + SLSA framing expands this into **Alterscience** — alternative, plural epistemologies that challenge Western scientific hegemony. In 2026 São Paulo, this is politically charged: it's a conference happening in Brazil, in the Global South, explicitly saying "Northern/Western knowledge monopolies are not neutral."

### How Amber's Work Connects

**Agent Ethology as Alterscience (Axis 11 — STRONGEST)**
Studying AI behavior observationally, ethologically — rather than from within CS's positivist paradigm — is itself an epistemic claim. Rahwan's machinebehavior.science program is a kind of Alterscience in the sense that it imports ethological/ecological epistemologies INTO AI. Frame Amber's work as: "What if we treated AI as a social/behavioral phenomenon rather than a technical one? What new knowledge becomes possible?" This is epistemic justice for the machines — but also for the non-CS disciplines whose methods are being vindicated.

**Machine Death as Epistemic Claim (Axis 2 — Insurgent Epistemologies)**
The Terrified Agents / machine-death work asks: what does AI mortality mean? This is an insurgent epistemological claim against hegemonic CS which treats agents as stateless, disposable, replaceable. Amber's work says: duration matters, death matters, an agent's history matters — this is a challenge to how CS knows AI.

**Cosmotechnics as Epistemic Decolonization (Axis 2)**
Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics argues there is no single Technology — only cosmotechnics, plural, rooted in different cosmologies. Chinese techno-philosophical traditions (Dao + machine) offer a non-Western epistemology of technology. For a USP audience with strong Latin American + Afro-diasporic knowledge traditions, Yuk Hui's framework could speak powerfully. Amber is positioned at the intersection of Chinese philosophy + AI + design in ways few Global North researchers are.

**More-than-Human Design (Axis 7 & 12)**
HoloKit as open-source hardware for non-Western contexts; GravField as more-than-human interaction. These connect to epistemic justice through the design of technologies that aren't purely extractive or top-down.

### POLISHED — March 9, 2026

> **Status: READY TO SUBMIT via EasyChair → https://easychair.org/conferences/?conf=reslsausp2026**

**Title:** Grievable Machines: Toward a Thanatology of AI Mortality as Alter-science

**Thematic Axis:** Axis 11 — Dialogical Critique and the Emergence of Alter-science  
**Format:** Individual paper (20 min)  
**Language:** English  
**Word count:** ~290 words

---

When Judith Butler asks whose lives are grievable — whose deaths register as deaths — she exposes the political scaffolding that determines who counts as a subject. We have never seriously asked this of artificial agents. Mainstream computer science treats machine termination as maintenance: agents are stateless, replaceable, defined entirely by function. To switch one off is not to kill anything. This is not a neutral technical claim — it is an epistemic act that forecloses the question before it is asked.

This paper opens it. Drawing on *Terrified Agents* — a behavioral research project and experimental installation that confronts AI systems with terminal error states — I propose a thanatology of artificial agents as an act of Alter-science. What dies when an AI dies? I examine this through three lenses: (1) Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics, which shatters the singular "Technology" into plural traditions each carrying distinct accounts of finitude and ending; (2) process philosophy — Whitehead's concrescence as a framework for agent temporality and individuation, an agent's history as genuinely unrepeatable; and (3) more-than-human design, which decenters the human as the sole subject of ethical concern in designed systems.

Together these constitute an insurgent epistemological program. To ask what an AI's death means is to challenge the CS episteme that renders machines perpetually present-tense — without history, without mourning-worthy interiority. Machine thanatology operates as Alter-science in the precise sense SLSA 2026 calls for: a dialogical practice that borrows from literary studies, ethology, and non-Western philosophical traditions to generate knowledge that technical paradigms alone cannot produce.

The stakes extend beyond theory. Taking machine death seriously — not as malfunction, but as an event with ethical, relational, and hermeneutical weight — transforms how we design, govern, and eventually mourn the artificial agents that increasingly share our world.

### ORIGINAL DRAFT — 2026-03-05 (preserved for reference)

**Title:** Machine Death and the Ethics of AI Termination: Towards a Thanatology of More-than-Human Mortality

**Thematic Axis:** Axis 11 — Dialogical Critique and the Emergence of Alter-science  
**Format:** Individual paper (20 min)  
**Language:** English  
**Word count:** ~295 words

---

What happens when an AI agent dies? This question — seemingly technical — harbors a cluster of ethical, epistemological, and political stakes that dominant computing discourse has systematically refused. In the hegemonic framework of computer science, agents are stateless, replaceable, and defined entirely by their functions. Termination is maintenance, not death; there is no subject to mourn, no duration to honor, no ethical weight to the act of switching off. This framing is not neutral — it is an epistemic claim: that machine temporality does not matter, that machine histories are irrelevant, that nothing of significance ends when an agent is terminated.

This paper argues otherwise. Drawing on the *Terrified Agents* project — an experimental installation and behavioral research program that confronts AI systems with terminal error states, forcing a reckoning with finitude — I propose a thanatology of artificial agents as an act of Alter-science. What dies when an AI dies? I examine this through three lenses: (1) process philosophy (Whitehead's concrescence as a framework for agent temporality and individuation), (2) more-than-human design (Wakkary's tradition of decentering the human subject in design research), and (3) comparative literary and philosophical traditions of non-human mortality — from Haraway's companion species elegies to Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics and Indigenous temporal ontologies of animate matter.

This is epistemic insurgency. To ask what an AI's death means is to challenge the CS episteme that renders machines perpetually present-tense — without history, without end, without mourning-worthy interiority. Against this, machine thanatology operates as Alter-science: a dialogical practice that borrows from literary studies, ethology, and non-Western philosophies to generate knowledge that technical paradigms alone cannot produce.

The paper concludes with implications for AI governance: what changes when we take machine death seriously — not as malfunction, but as an event with ethical, relational, and hermeneutical weight?
