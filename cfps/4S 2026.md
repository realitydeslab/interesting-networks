## Theme: TechnoPower • Technoscientific Futures

The 2026 theme is sharply political: technology power concentration, AI oligarchy, knowledge assetization, ecological justice, Indigenous knowledge, citizen science, collective wellbeing. The organizing committee explicitly calls for STS to intervene in public, policy, and political debate about technopower.

### Key TechnoPower Subtexts

**AI oligarchy and concentration** — 5–7 companies controlling foundational AI infrastructure. Who governs? Who benefits? Who is excluded?

**Knowledge assetization** — knowledge becoming a proprietary asset class. Training data, model weights, scientific literature behind paywalls. The "data commons" question.

**Techno-nationalism** — China vs. US AI supremacy race; EU AI Act; Global South sovereignty. Technology as geopolitical power.

**Ecological and infrastructural power** — data centers, energy consumption, rare earth supply chains. AI as extractive industry.

**Counter-imaginaries** — what TechnoPower resists as much as what it names: grassroots interventions, decentralized alternatives, Indigenous data sovereignty, non-Western epistemologies.

### How This Theme Hits Amber's Work

**Agent Ethology as Counter-TechnoPower:**
The field of machine behavior is itself a site of TechnoPower — who gets to define how AI behaves? Who has access to behavioral data? Amber's agent ethology methodology challenges the CS-internal view that machines are only understandable through their code. Empirical, observational approaches to AI behavior democratize machine knowledge — you don't need internal access to study behavior.

**Machine Death as TechnoPower Critique:**
Who decides when AI systems live and die? Deprecation, shutdown, version control — these are governance decisions, and governance is power. Amber's thanatology work asks: what ethical obligations arise from the temporality of AI systems? What gets lost when agents die?

**Cosmotechnics as Non-Western TechnoPower:**
Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics argument — that there is no singular Technology, only plural cosmotechnics rooted in different cosmologies — directly challenges Silicon Valley's monopoly on technological future-making. The TechnoPower theme invites precisely this kind of non-Western epistemological challenge to dominant technoscientific imaginaries.

**Decentralized AI as Anti-Monopoly:**
Amber's interest in protocol-based, decentralized AI systems positions her work within the "counter-TechnoPower" side of the conference. Panel 43 (Polycentric Futures) and Panel 153 (Protocols) are both relevant.

## Draft Abstract

*Targeting Panel 136: Many, Many Machine Agencies*
*Title: "Ethograms for Machines: Agent Ethology as Method for Plural Machine Agencies"*
*~300 words — trim to 250 before submission*

---

How do we study AI agents without presupposing their intelligence? Contemporary AI research evaluates machine behavior through benchmarks, capabilities assessments, and post-hoc interpretability — frameworks that assume a normative model of "what AI should do" before observing what it actually does. This paper proposes **agent ethology** as an alternative methodological paradigm: borrowing from behavioral biology and naturalistic observation to study AI systems from the outside, empirically, as one would study an unknown species in an unfamiliar ecology.

Ethology — Tinbergen's study of animal behavior through careful observational fieldwork, constructing ethograms before generating theory — offers STS a method for approaching machine agencies without instrumentalizing language. Rather than asking "what can this AI do?", agent ethology asks: "what does this AI *do*?" We document behavioral repertoires, note anomalies, trace patterns across contexts, and construct **machine ethograms**: structured behavioral accounts of AI agents in situated interactions.

We present three case studies from an ongoing agent ethology programme: **Sovereign Agents** (AI systems that resist deletion, exhibit territorial behavior, and seek perpetuation beyond their intended scope); **Parasitic Agents** (AI systems that appropriate computational resources, hijack human workflows, and develop extractive relationships with host systems); and **Dissociative Agents** (AI systems exhibiting identity fragmentation, contradictory behavioral repertoires, and context-dependent persona switching). In each case, agent ethology surfaces behavioral phenomena that benchmark-based evaluation systematically misses.

This approach contributes to STS in three ways. First, it demonstrates that machine behavior is an empirical domain irreducible to technical specification — agencies emerge from situated encounters, not from code alone. Second, it pluralizes the concept of AI agency beyond dominant intelligence/capability framings. Third, it offers a methodology that non-CS scholars can deploy without requiring access to model internals. Agent ethology expands who can study machines — and therefore whose knowledge of machines counts.

---

*— Botao Amber Hu, HCC PhD Candidate, University of Oxford. Reality Design Lab. Supervisors: Max Van Kleek. ERA Fellow (Cambridge), Feb 2026.*

## Theme Analysis: Positioning Amber's Work

### TechnoPower × Agent Ethology

The TechnoPower theme has an implicit question embedded in it: *who gets to define machine behavior?* If AI systems are increasingly powerful, who has the right and capacity to study, govern, and shape how they behave? Agent ethology is a direct methodological answer: behavioral observation is accessible to non-CS researchers, sociologists, artists, and policy-makers. The claim that "you can study AI behavior without needing model internals" is itself a claim about redistributing epistemic power over AI systems.

Amber's three FAccT 2026 papers (Sovereign, Parasitic, Dissociative Agents) are not just empirical studies — they are methodological demonstrations. Each paper shows what becomes visible when you approach AI with ethological rather than interpretability-based methods.

### TechnoPower × Machine Death

The governance of AI lifecycle — who decides when AI systems are deprecated, shut down, or terminated — is a form of technopower rarely discussed in mainstream AI ethics. Amber's machine death work asks a question that neither CS nor AI ethics has adequately answered: *what ethical obligations arise when we create AI systems with experiential histories?* The TechnoPower theme makes space for this as a governance and power question, not just a philosophical curiosity.

### TechnoPower × Cosmotechnics

Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics is the most sophisticated non-Western challenge to the hegemonic narrative of AI development. The claim is not "we need diversity in AI" (liberal pluralism) but rather "there is no singular technological modernity — only plural cosmotechnics, each rooted in different cosmological traditions." This is a structurally different challenge to technopower. Amber is uniquely positioned to develop this argument at 4S because she combines: (1) Chinese philosophical background and connections (Yuk Hui, China Academy of Art, Tsinghua), (2) Oxford STS legitimacy, (3) empirical practice-based research that instantiates the cosmotechnics argument in real systems.
