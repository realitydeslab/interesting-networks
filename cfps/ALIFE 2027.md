## Conference Overview

ALIFE (Conference on Artificial Life) is the world's premier artificial life conference, organized annually by the International Society for Artificial Life (ISAL) since 1987. It is THE primary venue for the international ALife community.

**Proceedings:** MIT Press open-access conference proceedings (full papers get DOIs; Summaries and Late-Breaking Abstracts do not)

**Journal:** *Artificial Life* (MIT Press) — papers accepted here are automatically accepted as oral presentations at ALIFE

**Format:** Hybrid (in-person + online) in recent years

## Submission Tracks (Standard — Based on Recent Years)

Tracks are consistent year-to-year with minor variations:

### Full Papers (3–8 pages, excl. references)
- New, unpublished work
- Peer reviewed; published in MIT Press open-access proceedings with DOIs
- Assigned oral or poster presentation
- ~50% acceptance rate historically (oral presentations more selective)

### Summaries (max 2 pages)
- Previously published, peer-reviewed work
- NOT in proceedings; no DOI
- Good for presenting journal papers to ALife community

### Late-Breaking Abstracts (max 2 pages)
- Work in progress, new ideas
- Poster-only; not in proceedings
- Lower bar; good for speculative work

### Workshops & Tutorials
- Proposals typically due ~Jan–Feb before the conference
- 1.5-hour tutorial slots or 1–2 × 90-min workshop slots
- Organizers responsible for content; ALIFE handles logistics
- This is Amber's track for workshop proposals (ALIFE 2026 workshop deadline was Feb 28, 2026)

### Art / Music Track
- Strong tradition in ALife; art keynotes, performances, installations
- "ALife-based Art and Theatre" listed as official topic
- Speculative design, machine mortality art pieces fit here

### Journal Fast-Track
- Publish in *Artificial Life* journal (MIT Press) → automatic oral presentation at ALIFE
- Best route for high-impact, publication-first work

## Community Profile

ALIFE's community spans 200–400 active researchers per conference:

- **Core:** Evolutionary computation, evolutionary robotics, morphogenetic systems
- **Growing:** Open-endedness research (Stanley, Lehman → Lila Sciences, Sakana AI)
- **Strong:** Agent-based modeling, complex systems, cognitive science
- **Niche-but-valued:** Wet/synthetic biology, artificial chemistry
- **Vibrant:** Art program — installations, generative art, music
- **Emerging:** AI safety adjacent work (ethics, existential risk, consciousness)

## Fit for Amber's Research

### Why ALIFE is Amber's Primary Venue

ALIFE is the single most aligned conference for Amber's research agenda. Here's why:

**1. Joel Lehman Connection (ERA supervisor)**
Joel Lehman is one of the founders of open-endedness research (Quality Diversity, novelty search, POET). He is deeply embedded in the ALife community. Submitting to ALIFE directly positions Amber within his supervisor's core intellectual home.

**2. Agent Ethology**
The emergence of "Signaling, Interaction, Cooperation, Communication, Language, Social Organization, and Culture" is a core ALife topic. Amber's agent ethology framework — studying AI agents via methods from behavioral biology — is novel and fills a real gap in how the field understands agent behavior.

**3. Machine Death / Mortality**
"Ethics, Existential Risks, and Philosophy of Artificial Life" is an explicit ALIFE topic. Machine death as an ALife concept (thanatology of artificial systems) is genuinely novel. It connects mortality to open-ended evolution (death drives novelty), autopoiesis (the self/other boundary that death dissolves), and philosophy.

**4. Open-Endedness**
Core ALife research program. Amber's ERA Fellowship with Lehman gives him direct access to the frontier here. A paper on open-ended agent behavior would land in the perfect community.

**5. Speculative Design / Art Track**
ALife has always had a strong art program. Speculative design pieces on machine mortality, cosmotechnics of artificial systems, or more-than-human design artifacts can be submitted here. It's one of the few CS venues where this is legitimate.

**6. More-than-Human / Cosmotechnics**
While not standard CS language, ALife's philosophy track and its history of engaging with non-Western and posthumanist thought (Takashi Ikegami at Tokyo represents this) makes it receptive.
