# LOST Research Group

**Law, Organization, Science and Technology** — an international research network founded in 2002 by [[Richard Rottenburg]] at Martin Luther University Halle-Wittenberg. LOST operated from a small Halle-based group to a decentralized network of 40+ scholars across Europe, Africa, and North America. Its core program: *Making Evidence, Designing Futures* — studying how evidence is produced under conditions of uncertainty, how juridico-political protocols certify matters of fact, and how techno-science governs emerging futures. **Now an archive** — activities wound down between 2022–2024; website archived at [lost-research-group.org](http://lost-research-group.org).

## Mission & Objectives

LOST worked at the intersection of three transdisciplinary fields:
- **L** — Anthropology of Law
- **O** — Organization Studies  
- **ST** — Science and Technology Studies

Core intellectual program: **"Making Evidence, Designing Futures"** — inquiring into the production of evidence under conditions of deep uncertainty and how this relates to the enactment of futures. Starting premise: modern public life requires institutionally certified "matters of fact," certified through juridico-political protocols of accountability. This certification process determines what futures become possible.

Following pragmatist, genealogical, and post-foundational traditions (Schütz, Garfinkel, Nietzsche), LOST examined evidence-making as ontological politics — asking who gets to certify reality, and what futures are enabled or foreclosed by these certifications.

Empirical focus areas:
- **Global health** — biomedicine in Africa, malaria, Ebola, HIV, vaccine trials
- **Sudan studies** — conflict, land, displacement, post-colonial state-making
- **Quantification & indicators** — how metrics govern public life
- **Biometrics & digital identity** — Aadhaar, Ghana census, African fintech
- **Translating models** — how juridical, organizational, scientific models travel and mutate

The network explicitly addressed: *how do potential futures, not emerging from established juridico-political protocols of modernity/capitalism, become possible without undermining the political achievements of those protocols?*

## Key Contributions

**1. Traveling Models Framework**
LOST's most influential conceptual contribution — the idea that juridical, organizational, scientific, and technological models "travel" across contexts (from Global North to Global South, or between cultures), undergo translation, and transform in transit. Rottenburg's *Far-Fetched Facts* demonstrated this through development aid ethnography. The framework is directly applicable to how ML systems, AI governance standards, and quantification regimes travel globally today.

**2. Evidence-Making as Ontological Politics**
LOST's programmatic claim: producing evidence isn't neutral recording of fact but political act — who certifies what counts as real determines what futures become possible. This framework bridges STS, legal anthropology, and postcolonial theory.

**3. Quantification & Indicator Studies**
The *World of Indicators* volume (2015) laid groundwork for what is now a major STS subfield: how metrics, rankings, and quantification systems co-produce governance and social life. Directly relevant to AI/algorithmic governance studies.

**4. Africa-Centered STS**
LOST made Africa a serious empirical site for STS — not just "case study" for Western theory but generative ground for new theoretical frameworks. Established the [STS-Africa Network](http://sts-africa.org/) (2011). Challenged methodological eurocentrism in STS.

**5. Infrastructure Studies (African variant)**
Calkins, Rottenburg, Beisel, and others contributed to infrastructure studies from Global South perspective — lab toxicity, plant/human entanglements in science, drone logistics, biometric infrastructure.

## Why Interesting

LOST matters to Amber for one central reason: it developed the most sophisticated anthropological vocabulary for understanding how **models, protocols, and technical systems travel** across cultural contexts — and what gets lost, transformed, or imposed in that translation. This is exactly what's missing from mainstream AI governance discourse.

The network's work on:
- Evidence-making as ontological politics → how AI training data, benchmarks, and evaluation frameworks construct reality
- Quantification as governance → how metrics govern behavior at scale (Amber: how do evaluation metrics govern AI agent behavior?)
- Africa-centered STS → critique of how Western AI standards impose as "universal"
- Infrastructure studies → how technical substrates shape what kinds of futures are possible

LOST produced the conceptual toolkit that Amber's work on decentralized AI governance and machine behavior implicitly needs. The fact that LOST is now archived makes it a stable intellectual resource rather than active community.
