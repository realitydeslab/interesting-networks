# Filmaktion

A loose-knit collective of experimental British filmmakers active in the early 1970s, all core members of the [[London Film-Makers Co-operative (LFMC)]]. The defining feature of Filmaktion was their insistence on film as a **live, participatory event** — projection reimagined as performance, the projector and audience space as a single integrated environment. They rejected conventional cinemas entirely, screening in art venues, warehouses, and galleries. Improvisation was central: each showing was materially different from the last.

The term "Filmaktion" itself names the **performance/expanded-cinema tendency** within the broader British Structural Film movement — a tendency that treated the act of showing film as a form of action, not archival playback.

## Mission & Objectives

Filmaktion was not a formal organisation but a **shared practice tendency** within the LFMC. Its core premise: film projection is an act, not a transmission. By refusing the cinema auditorium and working in spaces where the physical setup was part of the piece, Filmaktion artists insisted that:

1. **The projector is a performer** — its whirr, its beam, its heat, its flickering are material
2. **The space is the work** — the room, the light conditions, the audience positioning are compositional
3. **The body is present** — artist and audience share the same time, the same air
4. **Improvisation is structural** — works looked different each time they were performed

This aligned with a broader international wave of **expanded cinema** (Stan Van Der Beek coined the term in the mid-1960s), but Filmaktion gave it a distinctly British materialist-structural inflection: less psychedelia, more formal rigor.

## Key People

### [[Annabel Nicolson]] (b. 1946)
Studied Hornsey College of Art, Edinburgh School of Art, then postgraduate film at St Martins (1970-1). Ran the New Arts Lab gallery 1969-70; cinema programmer at LFMC 1974, 1976-77, 1992/3. Founder member of [Circles — Women's Film in Distribution](https://cinenova.org.uk/) (1979). Her *Reel Time* (1973) is the emblematic Filmaktion work: she sewed a 16mm loop with a sewing machine during projection, creating tears in the film that redirected the projected image across the room. Work held in: Belgian National Film Archive, BFI, Canterbury University, Women Artists' Slide Library, V&A. [LUX page](https://www.luxonline.org.uk/artists/annabel_nicolson/)

### [[Malcolm Le Grice]] (1940–2024)
Studied painting at Arts University Plymouth and Slade. **Founded the LFMC workshop** in the late 1960s. Introduced film to fine art students at Saint Martin's (where he established the Film Department) and Goldsmiths. Professor Emeritus, University of the Arts London; former Dean of Media Art, University of Westminster. Described by the BFI as "probably the most influential modernist filmmaker in British cinema." Key Filmaktion works: *Horror Film 1* (1971) and *Love Story 1* (1971) — both shadow-play performance films. Wrote the field-defining book *[Abstract Film and Beyond](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262620772/)* (1977, MIT/Studio Vista). Died 3 December 2024. [LUX page](https://www.luxonline.org.uk/artists/malcolm_le_grice/), [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malcolm_Le_Grice)

### [[Gill Eatherley]] (b. 1950)
Studied Winchester School of Art (1969-70), Saint Martins (1970-73), Royal College of Art (1974). Began making films in 1971 at the LFMC. Participated in Filmaktion 1973 performance at the Walker Art Gallery, Liverpool. Part of major touring survey *Shoot Shoot Shoot* (British Avant-Garde Film 1966-76). After 1975, relocated to France, shifting to painting, drawing, and documentary film. Co-edited *Like a Fox* (1988) with Malcolm Le Grice. [LUX page](https://www.luxonline.org.uk/artists/gill_eatherley/)

### [[William Raban]] (b. 1948)
Studied painting at Saint Martins (BA, 1971), MA Fine Art at Reading University (1974). **Manager of LFMC Workshop 1972-76** — a key institutional role. Published bi-monthly journal *Filmmakers' Europe* 1977-81. Part-time senior lecturer in Film at Saint Martins 1976-89. Reader in Film at University of the Arts, London. Editorial board of *Vertigo* film magazine. "The films mirror the processes of production constructing the reflexive space in which the audience can be directly engaged." [LUX page](https://www.luxonline.org.uk/artists/william_raban/)

## Historical Context: The LFMC

The [London Film-Makers' Co-operative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Film-Makers%27_Co-op) (1966-1999) was the institutional matrix that made Filmaktion possible. Founded in 1966 by Malcolm Le Grice, Stephen Dwoskin, and Bob Cobbing (inspired by Jonas Mekas's [Film-Makers' Cooperative](https://filmmakerscoop.com/) in New York), the LFMC was organized as an **egalitarian worksharing cooperative** — it assisted production as well as distribution, a key difference from the New York model.

The LFMC grew from 1960s London counterculture: Better Books bookstore screenings → Arts Lab on Drury Lane → New Arts Lab → Institute for Research in Art and Technology → squat in former dairy, 13a Prince of Wales Crescent, Kentish Town → Gloucester Avenue, Camden → Lux Centre, Hoxton Square (1997) → liquidation (2001).

Other key LFMC members alongside Filmaktion's four: [Peter Gidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Gidal), [Lis Rhodes](https://www.tate.org.uk/art/artists/lis-rhodes-19680), [Carolee Schneemann](https://www.tate.org.uk/art/artists/carolee-schneemann-5446), [Sally Potter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sally_Potter), [Stephen Dwoskin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Dwoskin), David Larcher, Mike Dunford.

The LFMC's successor is **[LUX](https://lux.org.uk/)** — founded 2002, based at Waterlow Park, Highgate; current distributor for Filmaktion-associated works and much of the LFMC archive.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Filmaktion operated on a **counter-institutional circuit** — explicitly rejecting mainstream cinema venues:
- **Production base:** LFMC workshop (13a Prince of Wales Crescent, Kentish Town)
- **Primary performance venues:** ICA London; Arts Lab; warehouses; gallery spaces
- **UK institutional venues:** Walker Art Gallery Liverpool (1973); Tate Modern (2012 retrospective)
- **International:** Amsterdam; Cologne; Biennale de Paris; Documenta 6 (Le Grice)
- **Academic circuits:** Saint Martins; Goldsmiths; Reading University — education was a key propagation vector

The Filmaktion circuit is the **direct ancestor** of the expanded cinema / artists' moving image circuit that runs through LUX, Tate Modern, and the contemporary UK experimental film scene.

## Key Contributions

1. **Defined performance film as a practice** — Filmaktion named and demonstrated that film could be a live art form on equal footing with performance art and theatre
2. **Influenced British experimental film pedagogy** — Le Grice at Saint Martins, Raban at Saint Martins and UAL, Eatherley at RCA — all became educators who carried Filmaktion aesthetics into institutional training
3. **Institutional infrastructure** — Le Grice's role at LFMC, Raban as LFMC Workshop Manager 1972-76, Nicolson as cinema programmer — they built the infrastructure that would become LUX
4. **The LFMC as model** — the egalitarian cooperative model influenced later UK arts infrastructure (LUX, [Film and Video Umbrella](https://www.fvu.co.uk/), [Cinenova](https://cinenova.org.uk/))
5. **Feminist strand** — Annabel Nicolson co-founded Circles — Women's Film in Distribution (1979), which became [Cinenova](https://cinenova.org.uk/) — the feminist expanded cinema infrastructure

## Why Interesting

Filmaktion is the **historical prototype** for immersive, embodied, participatory performance with media — which makes it a direct ancestor of today's work in immersive XR, interactive installation, and live computational performance. The specific claim — that the projection event itself is the artwork, not the film strip — resonates with current thinking in HCI about **live computation**, **performative systems**, and **soma-centered design**.

From research-taste.md perspective:
- **Embodied Interaction** — Filmaktion literally made the body present in the cinema event; Nicolson's *Reel Time* is as somaesthetic as it gets
- **Participatory & Democratic Design** — the LFMC's cooperative structure is a historical model of design-with, not design-for
- **Practice-based** — values making, building, performing as forms of knowing; Filmaktion never theorized in advance of practice
- **Small over big** — ICA, warehouses, the LFMC dairy — not Odeons. The counterinstitutional model.
- **Critically engaged** — this is not neutral art; rejecting cinema conventions is a political act about spectatorship and power
