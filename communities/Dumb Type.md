## Mission & Objectives

Dumb Type emerged in 1984 from a group of frustrated arts students who wanted to make something new that addressed the technological transformation of Japanese society. The name "Dumb Type" evokes both "mute" and "stupid" — a critique of a society growing superficial and overwhelmed by media information. As founder Teiji Furuhashi said: *"We should always have a political view. We should represent that this is Japan."*

Their working method is radically collaborative: no king, no fixed director, no permanent membership. Ideas are presented collectively and pursued only if the group is interested. This democratic model has allowed the collective to evolve continuously across 40 years and multiple generational shifts, while maintaining a distinctive critical voice.

Their work ranges across performance, installation, video, sound, and publication — always exploring how technology mediates (and deforms) human experience, communication, memory, desire, and political life.

## Major Works (Chronological)

### Early period — Kyoto (1984–1988)
- **PLAN FOR SLEEP #1–#8** (1984–1986) — First works, at Kyoto City University of Arts and Artspace Mumonkan
- **LISTENING HOUR #1–#2** (1985) — Performance/installation/video
- **PLEASURE LIFE** (1988) — First major international touring show: NY, Münster, London (ICA), Copenhagen, Kyoto

### International breakthrough (1990–1996)
- **[pH](https://dumbtype.com/works/)** (1990–1995) — Landmark high-tech performance. Toured globally: Vienna, Madrid, Sydney, New York, Tokyo. Won: Sole d'Oro Award (TTVV-Riccione), Best Stage Recording/Studio Adaptation (IMZ Dance Screen 92). Video broadcast by WOWOW Japan and Television Española. Incorporated treadmills, video projections, and text — bodies moving in technologized space.
- **S/N** (1992–1996) — Explicitly political: HIV/AIDS activism, queer identity, signal-to-noise ratio as metaphor for information society and marginalized voices. BuBu de la Madeleine as HIV-positive performer. Toured: Adelaide, Montréal, Seattle, Yokohama, Tokyo, Sevilla, Hong Kong, Wellington NZ, Brussels, Zurich. Also *S/N #1* installation shown at Guggenheim SOHO (1994), SFMOMA, Yokohama Museum.
- **MONKEY BUSINESS CLASS** (1996) — Collaboration with [Hotel Pro Forma](https://www.hotelproforma.dk/) (Danish theatre group) and [Diller + Scofidio](https://dsrny.com/) (architects-as-artists). Showed in Malmø, Copenhagen.

### Post-Furuhashi era (1997–2009)
- **OR** (1997–2005 performance; 1997, 2000, 2002, 2005 installation) — First work after Furuhashi's death, Ryoji Ikeda's sound. Premiered Ars Electronica 1997 and NTT ICC 1997. Toured globally. OR installation in permanent collection of [[NTT ICC]].
- **cascade** (2000–2001) — Installation & performance. Shown in "Translated Acts" at Haus der Kulturen der Welt, Berlin; "Stanze e Segreti" Milan.
- **memorandum** (1999–2003) — Tribute to Teiji Furuhashi. Memory, loss, digital witnessing. Toured extensively: Israel, Chicago, Seattle, Minneapolis, Singapore, Kuala Lumpur, Seoul, Venice (la Biennale 2003), Melbourne, LA (REDCAT), Berlin, Madrid, Paris.
- **Voyages** (installation, 2002) — [[NTT ICC]], Tokyo.
- **Voyage** (performance, 2002–2009) — Toured 15+ countries. YCAM Yamaguchi (2004), Bogotá (2008), Athens Concert Hall (2009).
- **MEMORANDUM OR VOYAGE** (installation, 2014–present) — Toured Seoul, Rome, Leipzig.

### New era (2020–present)
- **2020** (2020) — New performance staged without audience during COVID at ROHM Theatre Kyoto. Film screening Oct 2020.
- **[2022](https://venezia-biennale-japan.jpf.go.jp/e/art/2022)** (2022) — Japan Pavilion, 59th Venice Biennale. Mirrors on rotating stands, lasers projecting text from an 1850s geography textbook, parametric speakers with rotating beams of sound. Explores post-truth, liminal space, internet-mediated reality. Members: Shiro Takatani, Ryuichi Sakamoto (new), Ken Furudate, Satoshi Hama, Ryo Shiraki, Takuya Minami, Marihiko Hara, Hiromasa Tomari, Norika Sora, Yoko Takatani.
- **2022: remap** (2023) — Return exhibition, Artizon Museum Tokyo, Feb–May 2023.
- **AUDIO VISUAL LIVE 2022: remix** (2023) — Concert at [[NTT ICC]], March 2023, for ICC's 25th anniversary. Also performed at Teatro Goldoni, Venice (Nov 2022).
- **WINDOWS** (2025–2026) — Ongoing installation, Museum Tower Kyobashi, Tokyo.

## Key Contributions

1. **Pioneered Japanese media performance** — Dumb Type established a model of rigorously political, technologically sophisticated performance art in Japan in the 1980s-90s when this barely existed. They influenced the entire subsequent generation: Ryoji Ikeda, Daito Manabe, and the broader media art scene.

2. **Queer AIDS activism in art** — *S/N* (1992–1996) was radical in Japan, which had intense cultural taboos around HIV/AIDS and sexuality. The work made HIV-positive bodies visible and beautiful on stage, organizing symposia and activist events — rare in Japanese performance of the era.

3. **Democratic collective model** — No director, no king, changing membership over 40 years. This structural innovation is itself an artistic statement and has proven extraordinarily durable: the collective is still producing in 2026.

4. **Technology as political critique, not celebration** — Unlike much Japanese tech art of the 1980s-90s that celebrated the hi-tech, Dumb Type consistently positioned technology as a site of alienation, control, information overload, and loss. Their work prefigured contemporary anxieties about social media, post-truth, and digital surveillance.

5. **Permanent collections** — MoMA (New York), Centre Pompidou (Paris), Musée d'Art Contemporain de Lyon, Tokyo Metropolitan Museum of Photography, National Museum of Art Osaka.

## Why Interesting

Dumb Type is the **origin point of Japanese critical media art**. They invented a form — politically charged, technologically sophisticated, collectively made, globally touring — before almost anyone else in Japan. [[Ryoji Ikeda]] passed through them. The model of "collective with no director" is an institutional form worth studying.

For Amber specifically: their treatment of the body in technologized space (embodied computing), their use of computation to process memory and loss (machine memory, mortality), their explicit political critique of information society (critical computing), and their durability as a collaborative structure (40 years) — all directly relevant to Amber's research threads.

The 2022 Venice work is particularly interesting: rotating mirrors deflecting lasers to project geography-textbook questions about the world onto walls while parametric speaker beams surprise visitors. This is computation as spatial, embodied, political experience — not a screen.
