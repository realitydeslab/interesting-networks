# Feral Atlas

*"Feral" ecologies that human infrastructure built but cannot control — the Anthropocene as unintended consequence.*

A landmark digital humanities platform mapping what happens when non-human entities entangle with human infrastructure and escape beyond control. 79 field reports by scientists, humanists, and artists document "feral" ecologies — the unintended, out-of-control ecological effects of empire, capital, invasion, and acceleration. The platform itself is non-linear, curiosity-driven, and intentionally disorienting, structured like the ecological processes it documents.

Published by Stanford University Press in October 2020 as an open-access, interactive scholarly work. Funded by the Andrew W. Mellon Foundation. Won the 2025 ACLS Open Access Book Prize (Multimodal category). Averages ~80,000 unique visitors and 575,000 page views per year — one of the most-visited academic digital projects in existence.

## Vibe

Playful yet politically urgent. Dense but navigable through curiosity. Think: ecological horror story told as a treasure map with 79 separate expeditions. The landing page is populated by feral entities — rats, fungi, algae, fire, chemicals — drifting across the screen, crossing each other's paths. You click one, an explosion fills the screen, a landscape emerges. You are inside the Anthropocene.

The aesthetic is simultaneously scientific, artistic, and poetic. Field reports sit next to poems by Jericho Brown. Video essays by Studio Armin Linke sit next to mycological surveys. The intentional disorientation is designed — mirrors the subject matter. You cannot read Feral Atlas from start to finish. There is no start and finish. This is a feature, not a bug.

## Mission & Objectives

Feral Atlas argues that **the Anthropocene is infrastructure**. Not just pollution or climate — but the unintended consequences of building railroads, dams, shipping lanes, cities, plantations. When human infrastructure creates new habitats, enables new dispersal routes, or applies new chemical gradients, non-human entities respond — and often exceed, escape, or outlast human intent.

The project:
1. **Documents feral ecologies** at specific sites worldwide — from cane toads to ragweed, from uranium tailings to shipping ballast water
2. **Theorizes the mechanisms** through which infrastructure generates feral effects: 4 Detonators, 7 Tippers
3. **Models transdisciplinary collaboration** between biology, anthropology, art, history, poetry
4. **Innovates digital humanities** form: non-linear navigation, multimedia integration, peer-reviewed but publicly accessible
5. **Intervenes in Anthropocene discourse** by centering infrastructure over individual behavior or abstract "humanity"

## Structural Framework

### 4 Anthropocene Detonators (organizing axes)
- **Empire** — colonial and state projects that rearranged landscapes for extraction
- **Capital** — industrial and financial systems driving ecological conversion
- **Invasion** — movements of species across ecological borders via human infrastructure
- **Acceleration** — speed-up of industrial/chemical processes since the mid-20th century

### 7 Tippers (Modes of Infrastructure-Mediated State Change)
- Infrastructure moves entities across ecological thresholds
- Each Tipper has associated video essays by the Tipper Video Artists team
- TAKE is one Tipper (shown in the documentation example); others include similar infrastructure-disruption concepts

### 79 Field Reports
- Each report covers one "feral entity" (organism, substance, or process)
- Each entity associated with one Detonator Landscape and one or more Tippers
- Each entity has "Feral Qualities" — conceptual attributes (e.g., "Creatures of Conquest")

### Luminary Essays
- Broader context essays by prominent external scholars
- Not tied to specific entities but to systemic analysis

### Super Index
- Complete listing of all feral entities with author, Detonator, and Tippers visible
- Alternative navigation entry point

## Key Contributions

### To Environmental Humanities
1. **Reframing the Anthropocene** — Moves discourse from abstract "human impact" to specific **infrastructure projects** with traceable, feral effects. The Anthropocene is not a diffuse condition; it has addressable mechanisms.

2. **"Feral" as analytical concept** — Names a category of entity/process that existing frameworks miss: not wild (pre-human), not domestic (fully controlled), but **escaped from human management**. Invasive species, antibiotic-resistant bacteria, urban foxes, firestorms — all feral in this sense.

3. **Infrastructure critique** — Connects environmental harm to the built environment in a non-moralizing way. Not "bad humans" but "infrastructure with unintended affordances."

### To Digital Humanities
1. **Peer-reviewed interactive scholarship** — Demonstrated that non-linear, multimedia, born-digital works can be rigorously peer-reviewed and academically credible. Stanford University Press established the process.

2. **Non-linear academic narrative** — Proved that academic argument can survive — and benefit from — fragmented, curiosity-driven navigation.

3. **Technical model** — Contentful CMS → static JSON + custom JS frontend → LAMP hosting → preserved in Stanford Digital Repository and web archive. A reproducible model.

4. **Institutional innovation** — SUP's Digital Publications program (funded by Mellon Foundation 2015-2023) processed Feral Atlas as #7 in the series. The program now has 16 publications.

### To Art-Science Collaboration
1. **Transdisciplinary depth** — Not tokenistic. Scientists, humanists, poets, artists each contribute from depth expertise, not as decorative elements.

2. **Exhibition + publication** — Physical biennial exhibitions preceded digital release, generating art-world attention before academic launch.

3. **Global south voices** — Contributors from Australia (Indigenous knowledge), Indonesia, Southeast Asia, Africa, Latin America — not just Global North Anthropocene discourse.

## Why Interesting (for Amber)

**The "feral" concept is a direct bridge to Amber's ALife and machine behavior interests.** "Feral" entities are those that:
1. Were enabled by human infrastructure (designed into existence)
2. Developed emergent behaviors beyond their designed parameters
3. Cannot be recalled or fully controlled once released
4. Are neither "natural" (pre-human) nor "artificial" (fully designed)

This is *exactly* the ALife concept of emergent behavior. It's exactly what happens with AI systems deployed in human social environments — they develop "feral" behaviors unintended by their designers. The fungal network that spreads along railroad rights-of-way is formally analogous to the recommendation algorithm that shapes beliefs in ways its designers never intended.

**Feral Atlas provides the conceptual vocabulary and methodological model for a "Feral AI" research project.** The 4-Detonator, 7-Tipper, Field-Report structure could map:
- **Detonators** → AI infrastructure contexts (platforms, recommendation engines, content moderation, robotics)
- **Tippers** → modes of AI-mediated state change (attention capture, preference distortion, labor displacement)
- **Field Reports** → specific documented cases of AI systems developing feral behaviors

**Why now:** Amber is at UCSC/Stanford adjacent, which gives direct institutional access to the Feral Atlas network. The project won a major award in 2025, signaling continued vitality. The editors are active and potentially open to extending the framework.

**The collaborative model**: Feral Atlas demonstrates how to lead a 79-contributor transdisciplinary project that earns both academic credibility (peer-reviewed, DOI, Stanford UP) and art-world presence (Istanbul Biennial, Sharjah). For Amber, who sits exactly at the art-science-tech intersection, this is the career model most relevant to her actual position.

**The digital form is inseparable from the argument.** The non-linear, entity-driven navigation is not a presentation layer on top of a conventional argument — the form IS the argument. For Amber's work on AI agents and more-than-human ecologies, this suggests that the output format of research should itself enact the distributed, non-hierarchical dynamics being studied.
