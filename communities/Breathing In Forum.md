# Breathing In: Air and Atmospheres Forum

Online research forum convened by [[Sarah Nuttall]] and [[Isabel Hofmeyr]] at [[WISER]] (Wits Institute for Social and Economic Research), Johannesburg, over 6 months in 2024. The second in a trilogy of elemental online forums generating new work from the Global South — after **Heated Conversations** (2023) and before **Plant Lives** (2025). Air follows heat and precedes plant life in an emerging elemental humanities curriculum building from Africa outward. Now reaching publication as a book/special issue.

## Mission & Objectives

"Exploring the growing non-transparency of air and the importance of making air explicative." — Sarah Nuttall's description

The forum proceeds from a double provocation: that air has become *less transparent* (through pollution, toxicity, smog, COVID aerosols, atmospheric weaponization, electromagnetic spectrum crowding) and that making it *explicative* — not just a medium but a theoretical object, an agent — is an urgent task for the humanities in a time of climate and political crisis.

Key political anchors for the forum:
- **COVID-19 as pneumatic crisis** — a pandemic spread through breath; disproportionate respiratory death among racialized communities in the Global South
- **George Floyd** — "I can't breathe" as the defining political demand of the 2020s, inseparable from breath as biopolitical category
- **Achille Mbembe's "Universal Right to Breathe"** — published in *Critical Inquiry* at the height of the pandemic, asserting breath as a fundamental right denied by racial capitalism and its long history of suffocation (from colonial gas chambers to polluted townships to asphyxiation at the hands of police). Direct WISER origin; Mbembe co-anchors the intellectual framework even if not a named convener.
- **Mine dust in Johannesburg** — South African mines have a century-long history of silicosis (lung disease) among Black miners; Joburg itself is built on air-borne gold dust. Air as racialized harm.
- **Urban air pollution** in African cities — a postcolonial condition of concentrated particulate matter, vehicle emissions, industrial exhaust; breathing differently depending on your position in the racial-economic hierarchy.

## Intellectual Framework

### Air as Elemental Object

The forum extends WISER's "elemental humanities" method — treating elements (water, heat, air, plant life) not as backdrop or metaphor but as **analytical objects** with political histories, material lives, and epistemological weight. Nuttall + Hofmeyr's prior move: *Reading for Water* (with Charne Lavery, Routledge) and *Dockside Reading: Hydrocolonialism and the Custom House* (Hofmeyr, Duke UP, 2022) made water an epistemological category; Breathing In does the same for air.

Air as:
- **Biopolitical medium** — who breathes clean, who breathes poison
- **Sonic medium** — radio waves, wireless spectrum, electromagnetic air as infrastructure
- **Epistemic object** — atmosphere as what carries thought (plumes, spores, particles)
- **Postcolonial ecology** — mine dust, crop-burning, urban smog as legacies of colonial extraction
- **Therapeutic/spiritual register** — breathing practices, climate grief, respiratory sovereignty

### Mbembe's "Universal Right to Breathe" as Theoretical Anchor

Mbembe's essay (Critical Inquiry 47, 2021: S58–S62) — originally published in Mediapart (French) at the pandemic's onset — is the forum's intellectual starting point. Key moves:
- Breath as the most intimate act of being-in-the-world, now weaponized
- The long colonial history of **asphyxiation**: slavery (ship holds, chains), mining (silicosis, cave-ins), the township (dust, fumes, no trees)
- Floyd's "I can't breathe" as crystallizing a centuries-long deprivation of pneumatic rights
- A utopian demand: breathing as a **right**, not merely a biological function — implying universal obligations around air quality, pollution control, respiratory sovereignty
- The virus as **revealing** what was always already unequal: whose air was clean, whose lungs were expendable

This essay connects Mbembe's *necropolitics* (who is made to die) to atmospheric politics (by what medium death circulates).

## Topics Explored

Based on the forum's stated mission and the broader WISER intellectual project:

- **Air as opacity** — pollution, smog, particulate matter; the moment air becomes *visible* is the moment it's already dangerous
- **Atmospheric violence** — mine dust, industrial fumes, gas in warfare, COVID aerosols; the weaponization of air
- **Breath as biopolitics** — who breathes freely, who asphyxiates; respiratory healthcare deserts; silicosis histories
- **The Universal Right to Breathe** — Mbembe's framework as political demand and literary/political provocation
- **Electromagnetic atmosphere** — radio frequencies, wireless spectrum, signal propagation; air as digital infrastructure
- **Sonic atmospheres** — noise pollution, acoustic environments, soundscapes as environmental justice issue
- **Phytogenic air** — plants and oxygen production; the link forward to the Plant Lives forum
- **Climate atmospherics** — CO2, methane, ozone depletion; air as climate crisis medium
- **Postcolonial ecologies of breath** — African urban breathing, plantation atmospheres, haze from burning cane fields, South African mine dust
- **Respiratory aesthetics** — literary and visual art treatments of breath, suffocation, atmosphere; Southern African writing about air

**Online research forum, 6 months (2024).** Widely subscribed internationally. Speakers from across Africa, Europe, US, and Global South. Likely weekly or bi-weekly Zoom sessions with pre-circulated papers and recorded discussions — following the model of WISER's own Print Seminar, where papers are read before the session and discussion is primary.

The Breathing In framework could productively be extended to computing's own atmospheric politics: who gets spectrum? Whose electromagnetic environment is clean/polluted? ## Why Interesting

Air is the most intimate and most political medium there is — we breathe it constantly, we die without it, we pollute it together, we exploit it differently by race and class. WISER's Breathing In forum is the first sustained academic project to treat air as an elemental humanities object from a specifically postcolonial, African-Southern perspective. The COVID pandemic unlocked this inquiry: when breath became survival and surveillance simultaneously, and when "I can't breathe" became a global political demand, air became inescapably political.

The forum doesn't just describe atmospheric crisis — it produces new methodological tools for *reading* air: as text, as archive, as political infrastructure. That's rare. And for Amber's work at the intersection of more-than-human design, computing infrastructure, and climate politics — it's directly actionable.
