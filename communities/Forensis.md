# Forensis

Berlin-based research agency and sister institution to [[Forensic Architecture]], founded ~2021 by former FA researchers. Applies forensic architecture methodologies to European and historical colonial cases — specifically the German colonial genocide in Namibia (1904–1908) and far-right violence in Germany. Where FA operates globally from London, Forensis is rooted in the German cultural and political context, particularly HKW Berlin's decolonial research landscape.

The name draws on the Latin *forensis* — "belonging to the forum, public" — which is also the etymological root of the word "forensic." This carries a specific political charge: forensic evidence is not merely technical but is always *addressed to a public*, always a political act. Making evidence public, presenting it in the forum, is itself a form of power. Counter-forensics is the practice of seizing that power from the state and returning it to communities.

> "The forensic principle is not a neutral, technical procedure. It is always political: who gets to speak, in what forum, with what kind of evidence, matters." — from the Forensis conceptual tradition

## Background: The "Forensis" Concept (2014)

The conceptual foundation was laid by the 2014 book ***[Forensis: The Architecture of Public Truth](https://www.sternberg-press.com/product/forensis-the-architecture-of-public-truth/)*** (ed. [[Eyal Weizman]] and Matthias Wittmann, Sternberg Press, 575pp). This was the first major theoretical compilation of Forensic Architecture's practice — gathering investigations, essays, and legal analyses into a unified framework.

A major exhibition called **Forensis** ran concurrently at **[[Haus der Kulturen der Welt]]** (HKW), Berlin in 2014, then travelled to **Fundación Proa**, Buenos Aires in 2015. The HKW exhibition established Berlin as the intellectual home for this approach to forensic architecture — which later provided the institutional context for a Berlin-based agency to emerge.

The book/exhibition argued that:
1. Architecture, space, and matter can serve as *witnesses* — not just passive containers of events but active carriers of evidence
2. The "forum" is not just a courtroom: museums, parliaments, citizens' tribunals, and the media are all forums where forensic evidence can be deployed
3. Counter-forensics is the practice of using forensic tools against states and corporations rather than on their behalf

## Key Project: German Colonial Genocide in Namibia

The major active investigation is the **German colonial genocide in Namibia** — the systematic extermination of the Ovaherero and Nama peoples by the German Empire between 1904 and 1908. An estimated 65,000–80,000 Ovaherero (out of 80,000) and 10,000 Nama (out of 20,000) were killed, making it arguably the first genocide of the 20th century.

Key sites of investigation include:
- **Shark Island** (Lüderitz, Namibia) — the most notorious German concentration camp, where prisoners were worked and starved to death in extreme conditions. The island's geography was deliberately hostile: cold Atlantic winds, no fresh water. Bodies were dumped in the sea.
- **Waterberg** — site of the Battle of Waterberg (August 1904), after which German General Lothar von Trotha issued the *Vernichtungsbefehl* (extermination order), the first order explicitly calling for the annihilation of a people
- **Multiple massacre and burial sites** across Namibia, many unmarked

**Methodology:** Forensis applies FA's "situated testimony" approach, adapted for transgenerational oral memory. Since the genocide occurred 120 years ago, survivors' direct testimony is unavailable. Instead:
- Oral history transmitted through Ovaherero and Nama communities across generations is treated as legitimate evidence
- Traditional memory practices (songs, recitations, place names) are analyzed as spatial information
- Archival documents — German colonial records, military dispatches, missionary accounts — are cross-referenced with oral testimonies
- Site visits, drone photography, and ground-penetrating radar map undocumented burial sites
- 3D reconstruction of known camps (especially Shark Island) creates immersive environments for community testimony

This work directly challenges the German government's narrative around the genocide and the 2021 agreement in which Germany officially recognized the genocide and pledged €1.1 billion in development aid — but without reparations or formal legal recognition (which the Nama and Ovaherero communities rejected as inadequate).

**Partners:** Ovaherero Traditional Authority, Nama Traditional Leaders Association, various Namibian civil society groups; [[Forensic Architecture]]; HKW Berlin; German academic institutions.

**Political context:** Forensis's work enters a live political negotiation. Germany's "recognition" without reparations has been rejected by affected communities. The forensic evidence produced by Forensis provides *legal ammunition* for communities seeking full reparations and accountability — potentially including the return of human remains (many Namibian skulls were taken to German museums for racist scientific experiments and only partially returned).

## Relationship to Forensic Architecture

Forensis is not a separate methodology but a separate *institutional context*:

- **FA** operates from Goldsmiths, London. It is a university-based research agency with ERC funding, art institution relationships, and a global scope
- **Forensis** is Berlin-based, working more directly within the German political and institutional ecosystem — HKW, German funding agencies, German parliamentary and legal contexts

The relationship is roughly analogous to how **[[Earshot]]** (Lawrence Abu Hamdan's audio forensics agency) spun off from FA while maintaining methodological continuity. Forensis extends FA's approach into German colonial history — a domain where the Berlin context (HKW's decolonial programmes, German institutional memory debates, proximity to German archives and courts) is essential.

## Methodological Contribution: Intergenerational Testimony

FA pioneered "situated testimony" — using 3D spatial reconstructions to help survivors recover fragmented traumatic memories. Forensis extends this into *historical time*: what happens when no direct survivors remain?

Their innovation: treating **oral tradition as spatial memory**. When Ovaherero elders narrate place-names, routes of flight, or camp layouts, they are transmitting spatial information encoded in narrative form. Forensis's researchers work to decode this spatial data, cross-reference it with archival sources, and build reconstructions that communities can validate.

This is a significant methodological contribution to:
- **Transitional justice** — how to adjudicate historical crimes when living witnesses are unavailable
- **Indigenous epistemologies** — oral tradition as legitimate evidence, not a lesser form of testimony
- **Counter-forensics** — turning the tools of colonial archival power against the colonial archive

> The question becomes: what counts as evidence across 120 years? Who gets to validate it? Forensis argues that communities whose oral traditions carry the memory are the authoritative validators.

## Why Interesting

Several threads relevant to Amber's research:

**1. Computation against colonial archives.** The same 3D reconstruction and photogrammetry tools used to analyze drone strikes can be used to reconstruct German concentration camps from 1904. The tools are politically neutral; their deployment is not. Forensis makes this political choice explicit.

**2. Intergenerational oral memory as evidence.** This is a genuinely novel epistemological claim: that oral tradition, transmitted across 4+ generations, can function as admissible evidence in legal and political forums. This directly challenges the positivist evidentiary tradition that privileges documents over testimony, and written records over oral ones.

**3. The German context as test case.** Germany has been a global leader in *Erinnerungskultur* (memory culture) — particularly around the Holocaust. But the selective application of that memory culture (acknowledging the Holocaust while minimizing colonial genocide) reveals how memory culture can itself be an instrument of state power. Forensis applies counter-forensics not just to events but to the *discourse* about those events.

**4. More-than-human epistemology in historical time.** Soil, geography, bones, and atmospheric records don't forget. Forensis's use of ground-penetrating radar and satellite data to find unmarked burial sites is an application of Susan Schuppli's "matter as witness" concept extended to colonial history. The land holds memory that colonial archives erased.

**5. Proximity to Amber's research infrastructure interests.** The Forensis model — a small, nimble research agency that works at the intersection of legal, artistic, and community-based practice — is relevant to thinking about what research infrastructure looks like at the borders of academia. Not a university department, not an NGO, not an art collective: something else.
