# Repeater Books

Independent publishing imprint based in London, founded 2014 by former [[Zero Books]] editors and authors. Part of [Watkins Media](https://watkinsmedia.com/). Tagline: **"We are alive and we don't agree."**

The de facto home of British cultural theory for people under 40. Carries the intellectual DNA of the CCRU diaspora through Mark Fisher's legacy — refusing the split between "serious" academic theory and accessible writing, taking popular culture (music, film, horror, games) as philosophically serious objects, insisting on the political dimensions of everyday affect. Now ~30-40 titles per year across philosophy, cultural studies, music, politics, gender studies, horror theory, psychogeography, and experimental fiction.

## History & Origin

### The Zero Books Break (2007–2014)

**Zer0 Books** was founded in **2007** by **Tariq Goddard** and **[[Mark Fisher]]** as an imprint of John Hunt Publishing — later known as Collective Ink. The impulse: to fight anti-intellectualism in contemporary culture with serious ideas in accessible prose. It worked spectacularly. Fisher's *Capitalist Realism* (2009) became the defining cultural theory text of its era, reframing how a generation talked about capitalism, depression, and political possibility. In its first seven years, Zero was the most interesting radical publisher in the UK.

But Zero was owned by John Hunt Publishing — and by 2014, fundamental disagreements had opened up between the editorial team and the parent company. In December 2014, Tariq Goddard published "**Why We Quit: Tariq Goddard on Leaving Zero Books**" in 3:AM Magazine, explaining the break. The immediate cause was editorial control; the deeper issue was that the structure didn't allow the imprint to operate as a genuinely autonomous cultural project.

**The departure:** Goddard, Fisher, Matteo Mandarini (editor and philosopher), Alex Niven (cultural critic), and publicist Tamar Shlaim resigned together. Etan Ilfeld — who had bought Watkins Books (London's oldest esoteric bookshop, est. 1897, in Cecil Court, Covent Garden) out of bankruptcy in 2010 — had already built Watkins Media as a publishing group. He agreed to house the new imprint.

**Repeater launched January 2015.** First two titles: *The Isle of Minimus* (M.K.L. Murphy, experimental fiction) and *Lean Out* (Dawn Foster, feminist polemic). Distribution: [GBS](https://www.gbs.tbs-ltd.co.uk/) in UK, [Random House](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/) in US.

### The Fisher Years (2015–2017)

Mark Fisher's death in January 2017 was the defining event in Repeater's short history. He had published his final complete book — *The Weird and the Eerie* — at Repeater just before his death. The imprint became his literary executor. *k-punk: The Collected and Unpublished Writings* (2018) and *Postcapitalist Desire: The Final Lectures* (2021) have become Repeater's most important books — not just commercially but culturally, sustaining and extending Fisher's influence.

### Reunification with Zero Books (2021)

In October 2021, Etan Ilfeld purchased Collective Ink (formerly John Hunt Publishing) — the parent company of Zero Books. This reunited both imprints under Watkins Media. Repeater announced: *"The team that started and ran Zer0 for its first seven years have bought Zer0 Books."* Some Zero Book assets were transferred back. Zero Books continues as a separate imprint under Collective Ink, but both now share the same ownership ecosystem.

### Post-Goddard (2024–present)

In **July 2024**, Tariq Goddard — the editorial engine for both Zero and Repeater from 2007–2024 — departed. This is a significant generational shift. The imprint is now run by Vicky Hartley (Interim Publisher) and Carl Neville (Commissioning Editor), with Etan Ilfeld as owner.

## The CCRU Genealogy

Repeater sits at the end of a traceable intellectual lineage that runs from the University of Warwick in the late 1990s:

**CCRU (1995–2003)** → Warwick's rogue theory collective (Fisher, Nick Land, Sadie Plant, Kodwo Eshun, Robin Mackay, Kode9/Steve Goodman, Ray Brassier). Explored accelerationism, theory-fiction, jungle music, cybernetics, and occultism. Fisher's PhD thesis there: *"Flatline Constructs: Gothic Materialism and Cybernetic Theory-Fiction"* (1999).

**k-punk blog (2003–2017)** → Fisher's blog shed the CCRU's aggressively pro-capitalist accelerationism for a left-leaning, more humanist take. But retained the CCRU's seriousness about popular culture as theory-material. Became "a one-man magazine superior to most magazines in Britain" (Simon Reynolds).

**Zero Books (2007–2014)** → Institutionalizes k-punk's aesthetic as a publishing line. *Capitalist Realism* (2009) is k-punk as book.

**Repeater Books (2014–present)** → The successor. Fisher's posthumous books published here. CCRU legacy preserved without endorsing Nick Land's reactionary turn. The imprint carries hauntology, acid communism, theory-fiction, Gothic Marxism forward.

**What Repeater inherits from CCRU:**
- Taking music (jungle, post-punk, electronic, grime) as primary theoretical material
- Theory-fiction as genre — blurring essay and narrative
- Refusal of academic respectability while maintaining intellectual rigor
- Interest in the occult as cultural/political metaphor (see the Repeater Classics series)
- Hauntology — culture haunted by lost futures — as the dominant aesthetic mode

**What Repeater doesn't inherit:** Land's right-accelerationism. Repeater is explicitly left. Fisher himself explicitly broke with the CCRU's "pro-markets, anti-capitalism" line, seeing it as incompatible with the actually-existing neoliberal bureaucracy he encountered teaching FE college.

## Tariq Goddard — The Founder

**Tariq Goddard** (b. 1975) is one of British publishing's most unusual figures: simultaneously novelist and publisher, editor and cultural critic. His trajectory:

- **Education:** Philosophy at King's College London; Continental Philosophy at University of Warwick and University of Surrey — where he encountered the milieu that produced the CCRU.
- **Novels:** *Homage to a Firing Squad* (2003), *Natural Disasters* (2006), *The Picture of Contented New Wealth* (2011), *Dynamo* (2013), *The Great Eastern* (2018), *High John the Conqueror* (2022). His fiction tends toward psychedelic, baroque, politically charged historical and contemporary British settings.
- **As publisher:** Founded Zer0 Books 2007 with Fisher. Ran both Zer0 and Repeater for a combined 17 years. His editorial instinct shaped the house style: smart, working-class accessible, not-academic, politically left, culturally adventurous.
- **Co-edited:** *The Repeater Book of Heroism* with Alex Niven.
- **Departure (July 2024):** Goddard posted a leaving statement. The precise reasons aren't public, but the imprint now faces the question of what it is without its founding editorial personality.

## Mark Fisher's Legacy at Repeater

Fisher is the north star. Every decision about taste, tone, and cultural alignment traces back to his influence.

**Repeater titles directly by/about Fisher:**

- **[The Weird and the Eerie](https://repeaterbooks.com/product/the-weird-and-the-eerie/)** (2017) — Fisher's final completed book. Analyses the "weird" and "eerie" as aesthetic modes in literature and film (Lovecraft, Daphne du Maurier, Kubrick, Lynch). Core concept: these modes concern the "outside" — what lies beyond human comprehension. 136 pages; lean, rigorous, beautiful.
- **[k-punk: The Collected and Unpublished Writings of Mark Fisher (2004–2016)](https://repeaterbooks.com/product/k-punk-the-collected-and-unpublished-writings-of-mark-fisher-2004-2016/)** (2018) — Edited by Darren Ambrose, foreword by Simon Reynolds. The definitive collection of his blog writing. Shows how k-punk functioned as a laboratory for ideas that became books.
- **[Postcapitalist Desire: The Final Lectures](https://repeaterbooks.com/product/postcapitalist-desire-the-final-lectures/)** (2021) — Edited by Matt Colquhoun. Transcripts of Fisher's final CCRU-inflected seminar at Goldsmiths on desire, capitalism, and left-accelerationism. His most explicitly hopeful work. Key question: *"Do we really want what we say we want?"*
- **[Egress: On Mourning, Melancholy and Mark Fisher](https://repeaterbooks.com/product/egress-on-mourning-melancholy-and-mark-fisher/)** (2020) by Matt Colquhoun — A theoretical/personal memoir about Fisher's death and its afterlife. Colquhoun was Fisher's student at Goldsmiths.

**Key Fisherian concepts running through the Repeater catalogue:**
- **Capitalist realism** — the pervasive sense that it's easier to imagine the end of the world than the end of capitalism
- **Hauntology** — contemporary culture haunted by the "lost futures" of modernity, cancelled by neoliberalism; "pining for a future that never arrived"
- **The weird and the eerie** — aesthetic modes that mark the limits of human comprehension and the breakdown of social reality
- **Acid communism** — reclaiming 1960s countercultural psychedelia for a new left politics (unfinished at his death)
- **Gothic Marxism** — reading capitalism through horror, the occult, and the uncanny

## Editorial Taste — What Repeater Publishes

Repeater is the UK's primary home for **left cultural theory written for people who read**. The house voice is: serious but not academic, political but not preachy, grounded in popular culture (music, film, horror, games) but theoretically ambitious.

### Core Intellectual Threads

**1. Hauntology & the Gothic**
Fisher's hauntology — culture haunted by lost futures, the past refusing to die — runs through everything.
- *The Weird and the Eerie* (Fisher) — the definitive text
- *Capitalism: A Horror Story* (Jon Greenaway) — Gothic Marxism explicitly
- *Sick Houses: Haunted Homes and the Architecture of Dread* (Leila Taylor)
- *Purging the Devil: Exorcism and Possession After the Death of God* (Grafton Tanner)
- *The English Heretic Collection: Ritual Histories, Magickal Geography* (Andy Sharp)

**2. Post-Internet & Nostalgia Politics**
How the internet reshapes subjectivity, attention, and political affect.
- *Narcissus in Bloom: An Alternative History of the Selfie* (Matt Colquhoun)
- *The Hours Have Lost Their Clock: The Politics of Nostalgia* (Grafton Tanner)
- *Seeking Attention: 30 Ways of Being Present* (Dominic Pettman)
- *After the Fact?: The Truth About Fake News* (Marcus Gilroy-Ware)
- *Post-Self: Journeys Beyond the Human Body* (Roy Christopher, 2025) — posthumanism/transhumanism

**3. Technology Criticism (Anti-Capitalist)**
Not libertarian tech; left critique of Silicon Valley and techno-utopianism.
- *Abolish Silicon Valley: How to Liberate Technology from Capitalism* (Wendy Liu)
- *Stealing the Future: Sam Bankman-Fried, Elite Fraud, and the Cult of Techno-Utopia* (David Z. Morris)
- *Blockchain Radicals: How Capitalism Ruined Crypto and How to Fix It* (Joshua Dávila)
- *The Limits of Control: Experiments in Mediation and Virulence* (Ryan Diduck)

**4. Music Criticism with Political Theory**
Music as primary material for cultural analysis — not music journalism.
- *Rebel Rebel: The Songs of David Bowie, 1963–1976* (Chris O'Leary)
- *The Great Psychic Outdoors: Lo-Fi Music and Escaping Capitalism* (Enrico Monacelli)
- *You're History: The Twelve Strangest Women in Music* (Lesley Chow)
- *Justify My Love: Sex, Subversion, and Music Video* (Ryann Donnelly)
- *Tough Breaks: The Story of Baltimore Club Music* (Al Shipley)

**5. Left Politics & Economics**
- *Against Ambition* (Bill Peel)
- *Stolen: How to Save the World from Financialisation* (Grace Blakeley)
- *How the Railways Will Fix the Future* (Gareth Dennis) — infrastructure politics
- *Walking the Streets/Walking the Projects* (Owen Hatherley)

**6. Gender & Queer Theory**
- *Collapse Feminism: The Online Battle for Feminism's Future* (Alice Cappelle)
- *Corpses, Fools and Monsters: The History and Future of Transness in Cinema* (Caden Mark Gardner & Willow Catelyn Maclay)
- *Deep Sniff: A History of Poppers and Queer Futures* (Adam Zmith)
- *Which As You Know Means Violence: On Self-Injury as Art and Entertainment* (Philippa Snow)

**7. Continental Philosophy (Accessible)**
- *Embracing Alienation: Why We Shouldn't Try to Find Ourselves* (Todd McGowan) — Lacanian cultural theory
- *Fluid Futures: Science Fiction and Potentiality* (Steven Shaviro) — posthumanism + SF
- *Quit Everything: Interpreting Depression* (Franco Berardi "Bifo")
- *Anti-Oculus: A Philosophy of Escape* (Acid Horizon collective)
- *How to Read Like a Parasite* (Daniel Tutt)
- *Mimesis, Expression, Construction: Fredric Jameson's Seminar on Aesthetic Theory* (Fredric Jameson)
- *Code: Damp: An Esoteric Guide to British Sitcoms* (Sophie Sleigh-Johnson)

**8. Repeater Classics** — Canonical texts with new critical introductions:
- Herbert Marcuse *Psychoanalysis, Politics, and Utopia* — intro by Ray Brassier
- Karl Marx & Friedrich Engels *The German Ideology: A New Abridgement* — intro by Tom Whyman
- Arthur Schopenhauer *On the Suffering of the World* — intro by Eugene Thacker
- Francis Bacon *New Atlantis* — intro by Robert Barry
- Carl von Clausewitz *On War* — intro by Graham Harman

## Submission Guidelines

**How to submit:** Via [Submittable](https://repeater.submittable.com/submit). The specific form opens/closes seasonally — check the contact page.

**Contact:** https://repeaterbooks.com/contact/ (form on page)

**Key editorial contact:** Carl Neville (commissioning editor) — reach via website contact form or through known authors.

**What Repeater wants:**
- Accessible but intellectually rigorous
- Not academic jargon — write for a reader who reads widely but isn't in the academy
- Strong voice, opinionated
- Grounded in specific cultural objects (films, albums, books, technologies)
- Political perspective (left of center, not neutral)
- Willing to take unusual or "difficult" positions
- Length: typically 30,000–80,000 words for nonfiction

**What they DON'T publish:**
- Pure academic monographs
- "Balanced" journalism or neutral analysis
- Straight political memoir without theoretical weight

## Why Interesting

Repeater is *the* independent publisher in the UK doing critical theory that actually matters to people under 40. They've built a real culture — not just an imprint. The Fisher legacy alone makes them historically significant: this is where the most important British theorist of his generation published his last books, and where his literary estate now lives.

What's interesting now: with Goddard's departure (2024), a new editorial vision is being formed. The imprint is at a generational inflection point. This is the moment to pay attention — and potentially to get involved.

For Amber specifically: Repeater's technology section is genuinely underdeveloped compared to the rest of the catalogue. Their strongest critics (Fisher, Berardi, Wendy Liu) look at capitalism through technology but rarely examine the specific behaviors of AI systems, or Eastern techno-philosophy. Amber's work at that intersection — machine behavior, posthumanism, non-Western computing theory — could find a genuine home here.

The tagline "We are alive and we don't agree" is a real editorial philosophy. They publish dissent.

**The intellectual genealogy:** CCRU (1995–2003) → k-punk blog (2003–2017) → Zero Books (2007–2014) → Repeater Books (2014–present). This is one of the clearest unbroken intellectual lineages in contemporary British theory. Amber is working in adjacent intellectual territory — the question is whether she wants to be part of this lineage.
