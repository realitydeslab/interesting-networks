# SEMEFO

Mexican artists' collective founded in Mexico City in 1990 by [[Teresa Margolles]], Arturo Angulo, and Carlos Lopez (with a loose rotating membership). The name is an acronym of the Mexican coroner's office — *Servicio Médico Forense* — signaling the collective's central preoccupation: death, forensic materials, and the bodies of violence's victims.

## Mission & Objectives
SEMEFO used "the detritus of murder investigations to confront violence in the country." Through performance and installation-based work, the collective commented on social violence and death in Mexico. Margolles left the collective in the late 1990s to pursue a solo practice that refined and expanded the SEMEFO methodology.

## Key People
- **[[Teresa Margolles]]** — co-founder and primary figure. Left late 1990s for solo practice.
- **Arturo Angulo** — co-founder
- **Carlos Lopez** — core member

## Key Works / Contributions
SEMEFO's work pioneered the use of actual forensic materials — animal and human remains, morgue detritus, decomposing organic matter — in conceptual art installation in Mexico. Their practice prefigured what would become Margolles' internationally recognized solo practice.

## Why Interesting
The origin point of one of the most radical materialist practices in contemporary art. Understanding SEMEFO as a collective context helps explain how Margolles developed her methodology in a collaborative environment before refining it in solo work. Also: a rare example of a Latin American artists' collective developing a forensic/forensic-adjacent practice.
