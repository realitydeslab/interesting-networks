# FAccT — ACM Conference on Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency

The premier academic conference for critical AI research — where computer science, law, social science, and the humanities collide. Founded 2018 as FAT* (Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency in Sociotechnical Systems), renamed FAccT in 2020 when adopted formally by ACM. Publishes in the ACM Digital Library. ~300-500 submissions, ~20-25% acceptance rate. Multidisciplinary and deliberately uncomfortable — this is the venue where "AI ethics as structural critique" (not just checklists or principles) became academically legitimate.

**Amber connection:** Two FAccT 2026 submissions in progress — **Sovereign Agents** and **Dissociative Agents**. Notification of accept/revise/reject: **March 2, 2026**. Conference: June 25–28 in Montréal.

## Mission & Objectives

FAccT brings together scholars from computer science, statistics, law, social science, and humanities to investigate ethical, legal, and social challenges in algorithmic systems. The conference interrogates whether decisions should be outsourced to data-driven systems at all — not just how to make those systems "fairer" within existing frameworks. Key themes: algorithmic bias, data governance, accountability mechanisms, power and structural critique, philosophical foundations of fairness.

From the CFP: *"We particularly seek to evaluate technical solutions with respect to existing problems, reflecting upon their benefits and risks; to address pivotal questions about economic incentive structures, perverse implications, distribution of power, and redistribution of welfare."*

Six focus areas for 2026:
1. **Evaluations and evaluation practices** — audits, metrics, algorithmic assessments
2. **Experiences and interactions** — HCI, co-design, **speculative/critical design** ← Amber's angle
3. **Law and policy** — governance, regulation, legal frameworks
4. **Normative foundations and implications** — philosophy, STS, epistemology ← Amber's angle
5. **Power and practice** — anthropology, cultural studies, structural critique ← Amber's angle
6. **System development and deployment** — fairness-aware ML/NLP/CV

**Origin story:** The conference grew from a research workshop series on "Fairness, Accountability, and Transparency in Machine Learning" (FAT/ML) begun around 2014-2016. Founders/early organizers include Moritz Hardt, Solon Barocas, Arvind Narayanan, and others who were frustrated that ML conferences weren't taking ethical implications seriously. The first standalone conference launched in 2018 as FAT*. The asterisk (*) was meant to denote "and more." ACM adoption in 2020 gave it institutional stability and proceedings in the ACM Digital Library.

**Sponsorship:** Funded by industry (Google, Meta, Microsoft, etc.) and foundations (Rockefeller, Ford, MacArthur, Luminate). Sponsors contribute to a general fund with no earmarking and no say in program. This has been criticized as enabling "ethics washing."

## Landmark Papers

### Algorithmic Bias & Fairness
- [**Equality of Opportunity in Supervised Learning**](https://arxiv.org/abs/1610.02413) (Hardt, Price, Srebro, 2016) — foundational formal fairness criterion; one of the most cited papers in the field
- [**Fairness Through Awareness**](https://arxiv.org/abs/1104.3913) (Dwork et al., 2012) — individual fairness via metric constraints
- [**Inherent Trade-Offs in the Fair Determination of Risk Scores**](https://arxiv.org/abs/1609.05807) (Chouldechova, 2017) — impossibility theorem for competing fairness definitions
- [**On the (Im)possibility of Fairness**](https://arxiv.org/abs/1609.07236) (Friedler et al., 2016) — structural critique of mathematical fairness

### Data Documentation
- [**Datasheets for Datasets**](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.09010) (Gebru et al., 2018/2021) — the seminal paper on dataset documentation; published at FAccT 2021 after long preprint circulation. ~3000+ citations.
- [**Model Cards for Model Reporting**](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.03993) (Mitchell et al., 2019, FAT* 2019) — standardized ML model documentation. ~2000+ citations.
- [**Data Statements for Natural Language Processing**](https://aclanthology.org/Q18-1041/) (Bender & Friedman, 2018) — documentation for NLP datasets

### Language Models & AI Critique  
- [**On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big?**](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445922) (Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major, Mitchell, FAccT 2021) — the most famous FAccT paper. ~4000+ citations. Prompted Gebru's firing from Google.
- [**A Critical Race Theory of Algorithmic Accountability**](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3531146.3534647) (FAccT 2022) — CRT framework applied to algorithms

### Accountability & Transparency
- [**Algorithmic Impact Assessments: A Practical Framework**](https://ainowinstitute.org/aiareport2018.pdf) (Diakopoulos, 2016) — accountability mechanisms
- [**Counterfactual Explanations Without Opening the Black Box**](https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.00399) (Wachter, Mittelstadt, Russell, 2017) — Sandra Wachter's "right to explanation" paper; heavily cited at FAccT
- [**Why Should I Trust You? Explaining the Predictions of Any Classifier**](https://arxiv.org/abs/1602.04938) (Ribeiro et al., 2016) — LIME paper; widely cited in FAccT context
- [**Algorithmic Accountability**](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2564955) (Diakopoulos, 2015) — journalism + algorithmic systems

### Power, Labor & Structural Critique
- [**The Exploited Labor Behind Artificial Intelligence**](https://www.noemamag.com/the-exploited-labor-behind-artificial-intelligence/) (Gray & Suri, "Ghost Work" — related)
- [**Dissecting Racial Bias in an Algorithm Used to Manage Health**](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.aax2342) (Obermeyer et al., Science 2019) — landmark healthcare bias study; FAccT-adjacent
- [**Dirty Data, Bad Predictions**](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3333423) (Richardson et al., 2019) — predictive policing datasets
- [**Algorithmic Reparation**](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445902) (Hanna, Denton, Smart, Smith-Loud, FAccT 2021) — structural critique of "debiasing"

### AI Governance & Policy
- [**Closing the AI Accountability Gap**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2001.00973) (Raji et al., FAccT 2020) — audit frameworks
- [**Participation is Not a Design Fix for Machine Learning**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2007.02423) (Sloane et al., 2020) — critique of participatory design in ML
- [**AI and the Production of Ignorance**](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3531146.3534651) (FAccT 2022) — epistemological critique

### Agent Behavior / Newer Directions (Amber-relevant)
- [**Value Alignment Verification**](https://arxiv.org/abs/2104.06178) — accountability for agentic systems
- Papers on autonomous systems accountability are emerging as a new FAccT subfield

## Why Interesting

FAccT is where Amber's two active papers are going. But beyond that: FAccT represents the institutionalization of something genuinely interesting — the point at which "AI ethics" stopped being a corporate ethics-washing exercise and became a site of genuine structural critique, power analysis, and interdisciplinary friction. The community actively debates whether computing should exist in certain forms at all — not just how to make it nicer.

For Amber's work on machine behavior, agent ethology, sovereign agents: FAccT is the venue that would take agent *rights* seriously if framed through power/accountability, not just efficiency. The "Normative foundations" and "Power and practice" tracks are explicitly designed for philosophical and critical-theory approaches. Amber's framing of agents as entities with interests, sovereignty, and the possibility of death maps directly onto FAccT's concerns about who/what computational systems serve.

The community has strong UK representation (OII Oxford, UCL, Edinburgh) and Amber's supervisor Max Van Kleek is adjacent through the Oxford connection. Sandra Wachter at OII is the most direct UK-based FAccT figure worth knowing.
