# Tissue Culture & Art Project (TC&A)

The founding collaboration of biological art. Established 1996 by [[Oron Catts]] and [[Ionat Zurr]] in Perth, Western Australia — TC&A is the practice that invented the field. Not art *about* biology but art *made from* biology: living cells grown over polymer scaffolds into sculptures, garments, and performances that ask what it means to be alive, who is responsible for a living thing, and what futures biotech is creating before ethics has caught up. Coined the "Semi-Living" as a philosophical category. Executed the world's first cultured meat performance (2003), the first tissue-engineered clothing (2004), and built the institution ([[SymbioticA]]) that has since trained over 100 biological artists globally.

## Mission & Objectives

**"To explore the philosophical, cultural, and ethical implications of the semi-living and the contestable futures scenarios they offer us."**

TC&A was established with three interlocking objectives:

1. **Art as ethical anticipation** — use artistic practice to materialize futures that science is creating but has not ethically examined. Cultured meat (2000), victimless leather (2004), human/animal chimeras — TC&A brought these into the gallery decades before they became industry debates.

2. **The Semi-Living as philosophical category** — cells and tissues isolated from organisms and coerced into predetermined shapes. They possess characteristics of life but require laboratory technologies and human intervention to survive. Not alive in a conventional sense, not dead, not robotic. Forcing new questions: what are our obligations to these entities? Who is responsible for their lives?

3. **Critical engagement from inside the lab** — unlike artists who represent or comment on biology from outside, Catts and Zurr enter the laboratory, master the techniques, and use them to expose the assumptions embedded in those techniques. This is the structural move that gives TC&A its philosophical authority.

**Name etymology:** TC&A literalizes its medium — "tissue culture" (the technique) "and" "art" (the purpose). The ampersand is the method.

## Complete Works (Chronological)

### Foundational Period (1996–2001)
- **[Stage 1](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/stage-1/)** (1996) — Catts & Zurr. First tissue experiments: growing and monitoring tissue over scaffolds as artistic material. The conceptual origin of everything.
- **[Stage 2](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/stage-2/)** (1996) — Catts & Zurr. Further explorations of tissue culture as art medium.
- **[Stone Age of Biology](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/stone-age-of-biology/)** (2000) — Catts, Zurr & [[Guy Ben-Ary]]. Tissue-based objects critiquing the primitive state of our understanding of biological complexity.
- **[Semi-Living Steak](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/semi-living-steak/)** (2000) — Catts & Zurr. Lab-grown frog steak. Explores cultured meat implications more than a decade before the industry emerged.
- **[Pigs Wings](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/pigs-wings/)** (2000–2001) — Catts, Zurr & [[Guy Ben-Ary]]. Three sets of wings (bird/angelic, bat/demonic, pterosaur/culturally neutral) grown from pig mesenchymal bone marrow stem cells over biodegradable polymer scaffolds. Developed at Harvard Medical School residency. **MoMA permanent collection.**
- **[Semi-Living Worry Dolls](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/worry-dolls/)** (2001) — Catts, Zurr & [[Guy Ben-Ary]]. First tissue-engineered sculptures presented alive in a gallery context. Shown at [[Ars Electronica]] 2000.

"A prototype for a garment that does not involve killing an animal." **Exhibited at MoMA (2008)**; MoMA killed the living cells by ending the exhibition — irony entirely intentional.
- **[Semi-Living Zit](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/semi-living-zit/)** (2004) — Catts & Zurr. Commissioned by [*Wired Magazine*](https://www.wired.com/).

- **[DIY DVK (De-victimisation Kit)](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/diy-dvk/)** (2006) — Catts & Zurr. Ironically "freeing" tissue from victimhood.
- **[Teratological Prototypes](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/teratological-prototypes/)** (2006) — Catts, Zurr & Bioteknica (Jennifer Willet & Jason Knight).

## Key Contributions

### World Firsts
1. **First systematic use of tissue engineering as artistic medium** (1996) — not representing biology but growing actual living tissue as art material.
2. **First cultured meat performance** — *Disembodied Cuisine* (2003, Nantes): cultured frog muscle cooked and served in a gallery, the live frog present throughout. A decade before cellular agriculture became an industry.
3. **First tissue-engineered clothing** — *Victimless Leather* (2004): mouse stem cells grown into stitchless jacket. Exhibited at MoMA (2008).
4. **Field naming** — Won the inaugural Prix Ars Electronica Golden Nica for Hybrid Arts (2007), naming and legitimizing "biological hybrid art" as a category.
5. **First university-based biological arts research laboratory** — co-founded [[SymbioticA]] (2000), the model that has since inspired programmes worldwide.
6. **First peer-reviewed science journal publication by visual artists on tissue engineering** — "The Use of Tissue Engineering as a Medium for Artistic Expression," *Tissue Engineering* 6(6), 2000.

### Intellectual Contributions
- **The "Semi-Living" concept** — philosophical category for entities simultaneously biological and constructed; not alive conventionally, not dead, not robotic. Forces new ethics of care and stewardship.
- **"Neolifism"** — critique of the way "life" is instrumentalized as raw material and rhetorical device in biotechnology marketing.
- **"Contestable futures"** — methodological concept: using art to materialize technological futures that science is creating faster than ethics can examine.
- **"The irony trap"** — structural methodology: designing works so their existence forces the host institution into an uncomfortable position (MoMA kills Victimless Leather by ending its exhibition; the gallery serves the cultured frog to visitors; the frog watches).
- **Art-in-labs model** — demonstrated that artists embedded in genuine scientific laboratories, mastering real techniques, can produce philosophical novelty that external critique cannot.

## Theoretical Framework: The Semi-Living

TC&A's central invention is not a technology but a **category**: the *semi-living*.

> "The semi-living are cells and tissues that are isolated from organisms and coerced to grow in predetermined shapes. While the semi-living possess some of the characteristics of life, they require numerous laboratory technologies and human intervention in order to survive."

This concept does philosophical work across three registers simultaneously:

**Ontological**: The semi-living exist in a grey zone between the alive and the artifact. They are neither fully organisms nor objects. They demand that we ask what "life" actually is.

**Ethical**: If something is alive, we have obligations to it. If it requires our intervention to survive, we are responsible for it. TC&A forces this responsibility into the gallery and onto the institution: MoMA killed Victimless Leather. The visitors ate the frog's muscle. These are not metaphors.

**Critical**: Biotechnology's rhetoric constantly invokes "life" — to valorize its products, justify its investments, and displace ethical scrutiny. *Neolifism* is TC&A's critique: when life becomes raw material for commercial application, the concept of life is instrumentalized and depleted. TC&A's artworks use the same tools to make this instrumentalization visible and uncomfortable.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

TC&A has exhibited globally across **five distinct circuits**:

### 1. Scientific Infrastructure
Harvard Medical School (2000–01), CERN Arts at Geneva (2019), SynthSys University of Edinburgh, Penn State Huck Institutes. *This is what makes TC&A different from all other bio-art: the work cannot exist without genuine wet lab access.* The art is physically impossible without the science infrastructure. This constraint determines collaborators, schedule, and location.

### 2. European Media Art Festivals
[[Ars Electronica]] (Linz, multiple editions 1997–present, Golden Nica 2007), [[ISEA]] (multiple editions), [[transmediale]] (Berlin), L'Art Biotech / Le Lieu Unique (Nantes, 2003 — inaugural, and the site of the first cultured meat performance globally), Laznia Centre for Contemporary Arts (Gdansk, 2012 retrospective), Touch Me Festival (Zagreb). *The circuit that validated biological art as serious critical practice.*

### 3. Major Collecting Institutions
[MoMA](https://www.moma.org/) (New York), [Mori Art Museum](https://www.mori.art.museum/) (Tokyo), [NGV](https://www.ngv.vic.gov.au/) (Melbourne), [GoMA](https://www.qagoma.qld.gov.au/) (Brisbane), [National Art Museum of China](http://www.namoc.org/) (Beijing), [Yerba Buena Center for the Arts](https://ybca.org/) (San Francisco), [Centre Pompidou](https://www.centrepompidou.fr/) (Paris). *The circuit that canonizes the work in art history.*

### 4. Australian Institutional Circuit
Art Gallery of Western Australia (Perth, Biomess 2018–19; now AGWA hosts SymbioticA), Perth Institute of Contemporary Arts (PICA), Lawrence Wilson Art Gallery (UWA), John Curtin Gallery (Perth), Old Customs House (Fremantle), Powerhouse Museum (Sydney), Adelaide Biennial. *Deep local institutional relationships; Perth is the operational base.*

### 5. Festival & Site-Specific Circuit
[Kenpoku Arts Festival](https://kenpoku-art.jp/en/) (Japan, Compostcubator commission), Shuffle Festival (Tower Hamlets Cemetery Park, London, The Golem/Compostcubator 2016), [Edinburgh International Science Festival](https://www.sciencefestival.co.uk/) (Crossing Kingdoms 2018), [GYRE Gallery](https://gyre-harajuku.jp/) (Harajuku Tokyo, Ex Utero 2025). *TC&A willingness to work in unusual contexts: a cemetery, a Japanese arts festival, a fashion-lifestyle gallery.*

**The small venue that changed everything:** Le Lieu Unique, Nantes (2003) — relatively small French new-media centre, but it hosted the world's first public cultured meat performance. The choice of venue reveals TC&A's method: find an adventurous curator willing to do something genuinely transgressive.

## For Amber

### Why Interesting
TC&A is the foundational institution of biological art — literally the practice that invented the category Amber orbits. If Amber's work touches more-than-human design, posthuman bodies, critical computing through biology, or speculative design with materials that have agency, TC&A is the canonical genealogy she should know cold.

### How Can Amber Engage
1. **[[SymbioticA]] Residency** — open to designers, artists, researchers. Level 2 bio lab access, scientific mentors. This is the most immersive engagement point. Perth-based (remote), but potentially transformative. [symbiotica.uwa.edu.au](https://www.symbiotica.uwa.edu.au/).

## Why Interesting

**TC&A is the founding practice of biological arts.** Everything in contemporary bio-art either descends from TC&A or is in dialogue with it. Catts and Zurr didn't just make art — they invented the field, coined the key concepts, built the institution, executed the world firsts, and trained the next generation.

**Relevance to Amber's core threads:**

1. **More-than-human (literally)**: The semi-living entities are the most philosophically rigorous materialization of more-than-human design anywhere. TC&A didn't discourse about non-human life — it grew it, displayed it, ate it, watched it die, and forced institutions to deal with the ethical implications. Amber's more-than-human design interests find their most extreme instantiation here.

2. **Critical computing, biological version**: What Catts & Zurr do with biotech is structurally identical to what Amber does with computing — enter the system, master the language, use it to expose the assumptions embedded in the technology. "Countering the Engineering Mindset" is explicit critique from inside the laboratory. Amber can learn the strategy and apply it in her own domain.

3. **Speculative design ante litteram**: Victimless Leather (2004), cultured frog steak (2003), lab-grown pig wings (2001) — these are speculative design scenarios materialized as wet biology, executed a decade before Dunne & Raby formalized speculative design in London. TC&A is the genealogical root of the practice [[Anthony Dunne]] and [[Fiona Raby]] developed — even though it comes from biology rather than product design.

4. **The irony trap as design method**: TC&A's structural innovation is designing works so the artwork's existence forces the institution into an uncomfortable ethical position. MoMA kills Victimless Leather. The audience eats cultured frog. The live frog watches. This is a generalizable design strategy: create artifacts whose existence demands a position.

5. **ALife adjacency**: Guy Ben-Ary's cellF (neurons as musical instrument), MEART (neural culture controlling robot), and TC&A's own interest in agency in semi-living material touch the ALife research Amber tracks. SymbioticA is one of the few art institutions where actual synthetic life questions are being asked with wet laboratory rigor.

6. **[[Lu Yang]] connection**: Lu Yang — already in Amber's network — did a SymbioticA residency (confirmed in 2022 defunding press coverage). Direct bridge into this community exists.

**Edge of chaos assessment:** The biological arts field is not fast-moving in the digital sense — tissue culture is physically slow. But *institutionally*, it is at an inflection point: the SymbioticA model is being exported (Biofilia, Science Gallery bio-art labs), cellular agriculture has become a billion-dollar industry (vindicating TC&A's 2000 aesthetic provocation), and the ethics of "semi-living" entities is entering mainstream political debate (cultured meat, synthetic biology regulation). The field TC&A built is becoming mainstream. Now is the moment to know it.
