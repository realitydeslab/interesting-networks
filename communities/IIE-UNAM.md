# Instituto de Investigaciones Estéticas (IIE-UNAM)

Mexico's oldest and most prestigious art research institute, housed at UNAM (Universidad Nacional Autónoma de México). Founded 1935 as the "Laboratorio de Arte" by art historian Manuel Toussaint. Today it spans pre-Columbian to contemporary art, with a politically engaged contemporary art department that co-produces with [[MUAC-UNAM]] (Museo Universitario Arte Contemporáneo). Over 600 publications. The institute where [[Cuauhtémoc Medina]] has built Mexico's most rigorous contemporary art theoretical infrastructure.

## Mission & Objectives

Founded 20 February 1935, when Manuel Toussaint declared: *"La historia de nuestras artes plásticas está por hacerse"* ("The history of our visual arts is yet to be written"). Within a year the Laboratorio de Arte became the Instituto de Investigaciones Estéticas. Over 85+ years it has grown into five research areas and seven support units, recently adding the Oaxaca campus (2007).

**Research areas:**
1. **Arte indígena en América** — pre-Columbian and indigenous visual traditions
2. **Arte de los virreinatos** — colonial-era art
3. **Arte moderno** — 19th–20th century Mexican art
4. **Arte contemporáneo** — living culture, politically engaged
5. **Estudios sobre técnicas y materiales** — conservation science

The institute functions as both academic research hub AND content-producer for [[MUAC-UNAM]], blurring the line between scholarship and curatorial practice. This dual role—producing both exhibitions and the theory that legitimizes them—is a structural peculiarity worth understanding.

## Key Publications & Output

- **[*Anales del Instituto de Investigaciones Estéticas*](https://www.esteticas.unam.mx/)** — journal since 1937. Mexico's flagship art history journal. Available as open-access PDF in full archive. ISSN: 0185-1276. Covers Mexican art, Latin American art, theory, and aesthetics.
- **Monograph series** — over 600 titles published since founding. Specialties: Mexican modern, colonial, pre-Columbian, and contemporary.
- **MUAC Cuadernos series** — shorter publications co-produced with MUAC. Key one: [*Museo digital: Futuros y posibilidades*](https://muac.unam.mx/assets/docs/cuaderno_muac-museo_digital.pdf) (Amieva & Vargas Santiago eds., 2019) — free PDF, directly relevant to digital art futures.
- **Ebooks del IIE** — digital publications, open access via UNAM digital library.

## Key Contributions

- **Institutionalized Latin American contemporary art history** — before IIE's contemporary area, no major academic institution in Mexico systematically historicized Mexican contemporary art. Medina + Debroise's *La era de la discrepancia* (2014, 2nd ed.) is still the field's essential reference.
- **Post-Mexican art concept** — Medina coined and developed "arte postmexicano" as a critical framework for Mexican art after 1990s identity politics.
- **Curatorial-academic hybrid model** — IIE researchers simultaneously produce academic theory AND curate exhibitions at MUAC. This blurring is intentional: theory produces exhibitions, exhibitions produce theory.
- **Centro de Documentación Arkheia** — IIE-linked documentation center at MUAC with archives of Mexican avant-garde movements ("Los Grupos," 1970s-80s collectives).

## Why Interesting

IIE is interesting as a **model of theory-practice integration** in the Global South. Unlike European or US art institutions where theory and curatorial practice are often separate careers, IIE researchers are simultaneously historians, theorists, and curators. Medina chairs both an academic research institute and a contemporary art museum.

For Amber's interests: the institute's trajectory—from purely historical to politically engaged, now beginning to touch ecology and SciArt—mirrors the broader shift from art-as-object to art-as-practice-in-the-world. The "Hibridaciones" seminar represents an early crack in IIE's traditional art-historical shell. If that trajectory continues, IIE could become a site where cosmotechnics, more-than-human design, and Latin American political aesthetics finally converge.

The Rancière-inflected work of Helena Chávez Mac Gregor (aesthetics as politics, necropolitics, ecology) is philosophically close to Amber's posthuman/more-than-human design orientation — and almost entirely unknown in the UK/US contexts where Amber works. There's a real bridging opportunity here.
