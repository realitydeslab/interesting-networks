The oldest and most radically interdisciplinary humanities PhD program in the US. Founded **1965** at the University of California, Santa Cruz. Named with deliberate enigma — the Sphinx was chosen as mascot by Hayden White in 1978 because the name "History of Consciousness" means "everything or nothing." Canonical home to Donna Haraway, Angela Davis, James Clifford, Victor Burgin, and the foundational texts of feminist science studies, postcolonial theory, queer theory, and critical race studies. Still one of the most intellectually ambitious doctoral programs in existence.

## Mission & Objectives

History of Consciousness (HistCon) operates at "the intersection of established and emergent disciplines and fields," with PhD research that is explicitly **problem-based** rather than discipline-based. The department defines itself through five major categories:

1. **Philosophy and Theory** — postcolonial studies, Marxism, psychoanalysis, political history/theory, STS, humanisms and posthumanisms, human rights, theology
2. **Science and Technology Studies** — physics, philosophy, science studies, poststructuralist theory, environmentalism, technology and society
3. **Race and Ethnicity; Gender & Sexuality** — African and African American Studies, ethnic studies, Jewish studies, queer theory, feminism, disability studies, animality studies
4. **Political Economy & Social Movements** — globalization, world systems, financialization, history of movements of left and right, environmentalism
5. **Poetics, Media, Aesthetics** — visual culture, music, literature, digital arts, popular culture, cultural studies

The Sphinx was chosen because the name "History of Consciousness" is an enigma — simultaneously everything and nothing. It is also "indeterminately male and female, animal and human, simultaneously Eastern and Western." (Said the Sphinx was briefly condemned as Orientalist post-Said, but survived.) The name and mascot capture the department's epistemological ambition: refuse easy categorization, inhabit the boundary.

## Key Contributions

HistCon has generated a disproportionate share of 20th-21st century critical theory:
- **Cyborg feminism** — Haraway's *Cyborg Manifesto* (1985) is HistCon's most-cited output. Changed science studies, feminist theory, and posthumanism globally.
- **Situated knowledges** — Haraway's epistemological framework, now standard in STS and feminist theory
- **Agential realism / New Materialism** — Karen Barad's quantum-feminist ontology
- **Prison abolition theory** — Angela Davis and Gina Dent's intellectual lineage
- **Postcolonial cultural studies** — James Clifford's *predicament of culture*
- **Multispecies studies** — Haraway's *When Species Meet*, Papadopoulos's ecological reparation
- **Feminist STS** — from Haraway to Puig de la Bellacasa's care theory: arguably THE department for feminist philosophy of science

## Events & Programmes

### PhD Program (Only)
- PhD only — no standalone MA. MA may be conferred at specific milestones with Graduate Director approval.
- **Normative time to degree: 7 years** (national average for humanities)
- Admits **2-4 students per year** from 100+ applications — extremely competitive
- Fall admission only (no January entry)
- GRE **optional** (not required)
- Most important application elements: **Statement of Purpose** (must detail interdisciplinary research project and faculty alignment), writing sample, letters of recommendation
- No transfer credits from other institutions accepted
- **Application contact:** Ruby Barnett (Department Manager) — rlbarnet@ucsc.edu

### What They Look For
Three explicit criteria:
1. Well-articulated, focused interdisciplinary project
2. Research that **cannot** be done in a single discipline (interdisciplinarity required)
3. Clear **match with faculty expertise** (must identify which faculty can supervise)

Prior activism, practice-based work, and work experience are considered positives.

### Funding & Fellowships
- All admitted students receive a **funding package** — details at graddiv.ucsc.edu/financial-aid/
- **Teaching Assistantships** available — both within HistCon and across Feminist Studies, CRES, HAVC, Film, Philosophy, Politics
- **Application fee waiver** available from department for students who don't qualify for Graduate Division waiver
- **Graduate Division funding and fellowships:** https://www.gradadmissions.ucsc.edu/financial-support
- **International students:** ISSP resources at issp.ucsc.edu

## For Amber

### Why Interesting
HistCon is the *intellectual home* of most of the feminist and post-humanist theory that Amber draws on — Haraway, Barad, Clifford, Davis. Understanding HistCon is understanding where the ideas came from. But it's not just historical: the current faculty (Barad, Puig de la Bellacasa, Papadopoulos) are exactly where Amber's interests in quantum ontology, feminist STS, and posthumanism live *right now*.

### How Can Amber Engage?

**Path 1: PhD Application (Direct Route)**
- Possible, but extremely competitive (2-4 from 100+ apps)
- Amber's profile — AI art, embodied computing, feminist tech, posthumanism — aligns with Barad, Puig de la Bellacasa, and Papadopoulos
- Key strategy: **write the Statement of Purpose to match a specific faculty member's current research** (Papadopoulos for STS + ecological reparation + posthumanism, or Barad for quantum-feminist ontology + AI)

- London/UK location doesn't hinder (fully residential at UCSC, but for a 7-year commitment)

## Why Interesting
HistCon is the canonical answer to "where does the most important interdisciplinary feminist, posthumanist, and STS theory come from?" It's the department that gave the world the *Cyborg Manifesto*, agential realism, multispecies thinking, and prison abolition as critical theory. Its current faculty are active producers of some of the most rigorous posthumanist and STS work today (Barad, Papadopoulos, Puig de la Bellacasa).

For Amber: HistCon is both **origin story** (where her theoretical vocabulary comes from) and **potential destination** (a PhD or visiting position here would be the most legitimate institutional endorsement of her intellectual trajectory). The Papadopoulos–Barad–Puig de la Bellacasa cluster is the current faculty most aligned with Amber's specific interests in STS, posthumanism, and AI critique.

The department's self-description as refusing easy categorization — the Sphinx who is everything and nothing — is exactly the epistemological flexibility Amber's work requires.
