# Macy Conferences (1941–1960)

The most consequential experiment in organized interdisciplinary synthesis in 20th-century science. Funded by the [Josiah Macy Jr. Foundation](https://www.josiahmacyfoundation.org/) and directed by **Frank Fremont-Smith**, held in New York (1941–1960). Brought together mathematicians, neurophysiologists, anthropologists, engineers, social scientists, and psychiatrists to construct a unified science of circular causation and feedback. Produced cybernetics, information theory, cognitive science, second-order cybernetics, and the foundations of artificial life — and a model of community-as-research-infrastructure that remains unmatched.

## Mission & Objectives

The explicit aim: **restore unity to science** by promoting meaningful communication across disciplines. The Macy Foundation was concerned about disciplinary isolation within medicine. Their solution: a sustained, multi-year conference series where participants present *work in progress* (not finished results), verbatim transcripts are widely circulated, and conversation always takes priority over papers.

**Two innovations:**
1. **Macy conferences** (oral) — conversations with circulated pre-papers, more formal than seminars, less formal than conferences. Participants deliberately chosen for interdisciplinary training or openness to crossing fields.
2. **Macy transactions** (written) — verbatim transcriptions circulated beyond attendees. Invited wider scholars into the exchange. Radically different from standard "polished proceedings" — you heard experts think out loud.

The cybernetics conferences were the most famous, but the total programme ran 160 conferences across 19 years, covering: aging, consciousness, group processes, neuropharmacology, nerve impulse, liver injury, blood clotting, etc. The phrase "Macy conference" is often used only for the cybernetics series, but that's an incomplete picture.

## Key Conference Series

### 1. Cerebral Inhibition Meeting (May 1942)
The proto-event. Organized by Frank Fremont-Smith and **Lawrence K. Frank**. Attendees included: [[Gregory Bateson]], Margaret Mead, Warren McCulloch, Arturo Rosenblueth. Topics: hypnotism (Milton Erickson) and conditioned reflexes (Howard Liddell). This was the warm-up that showed the interdisciplinary format could work.

### 2. Cybernetics Conferences (1946–1953)
Ten conferences held in New York (final one in Princeton). Chair: **Warren McCulloch**. The most intellectually diverse and most difficult to organize.

**Full session list:**
- 1st: March 1946 — "Feedback Mechanisms and Circular Causal Systems in Biological and Social Systems"
- 2nd: October 1946 — "Teleological Mechanisms and Circular Causal Systems"
- 3rd: March 1947
- 4th: October 1947 — "Circular Causal and Feedback Mechanisms in Biological and Social Systems"
- 5th: March 1948
- 6th: March 1949
- 7th: March 1950 — first use of "Cybernetics" in the title
- 8th: March 1951
- 9th: March 1952
- 10th: April 1953 (Princeton)

**Topics by year (sample):**
- 1946: self-regulating mechanisms, neural nets + propositional logic, computers learning to learn, ethics from science
- 1947: child psychology, analog vs. digital models of the psyche
- 1948: formation of "I" in language, formal models of social hierarchy
- 1949: are neurons sufficient to account for human capacities? Memory.
- 1950: analog vs. digital mind, Shannon's information theory + language
- 1951: decision theory, feedforward, group dynamics, game theory + psychic motivations
- 1952: cybernetics at cellular level, complexity as information, humor + paradox, do chess automata need randomness to beat humans?
- 1953: consensus review — what did we actually agree on?

### 3. Group Processes Conferences (1954–1960)
Five conferences. Participants studied *their own* group dynamics — a reflexive turn. Guests included Erving Goffman, Konrad Lorenz, Erik Erikson, Niko Tinbergen. This is where Mead and Bateson brought their communication + anthropology insights most forcefully. The term "interdisciplinitis" was coined here (joking about the difficulty of their enterprise).

### 4. Neuropharmacological Conferences (1954–1959)
Five conferences with a skipped year. Primarily about brain chemistry; LSD appeared (infamously), though not as the primary focus. Some participants later contributed to CIA's MKULTRA program.

## Key People

### Core Group (Cybernetics Conferences)
- **[[Norbert Wiener]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — mathematician, MIT; coined "cybernetics"; *Cybernetics* (1948). Foundational claim: all intelligent behavior is the result of feedback mechanisms that can be simulated by machines.
- **Warren McCulloch** ⭐⭐⭐ — neurophysiologist; chaired the cybernetics conferences; 1943 paper with Walter Pitts showing neural nets can compute boolean logic — one of the most important papers in the history of AI
- **[[Gregory Bateson]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — anthropologist; represented social/behavioral sciences; present for nearly all 19 years; the most culturally synthetic thinker there; later developed double-bind theory, ecology of mind
- **[[Claude Shannon]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — Bell Labs; information theory; famous rat-maze demo at the conferences
- **John von Neumann** ⭐⭐⭐ — computation; first-order logic; cellular automata (post-Macy); foundational to the analog/digital distinction debates
- **Heinz von Foerster** ⭐⭐⭐ — physicist; secretary for the cybernetics conferences (edited the Transactions volumes); later developed **second-order cybernetics** (the observer IS part of the observed system); founded the Biological Computer Laboratory at UIUC
- **Margaret Mead** ⭐⭐⭐ — anthropologist; one of the most culturally influential participants; attended both Cybernetics and Group Processes conferences; with Bateson, brought communication theory into anthropology
- **Arturo Rosenblueth** ⭐⭐ — Mexican physiologist, close collaborator of Wiener; co-authored the pivotal 1943 paper "Behavior, Purpose and Teleology" with Wiener and Bigelow — the proto-cybernetics manifesto
- **Walter Pitts** ⭐⭐ — autodidact mathematical prodigy; with McCulloch: "A Logical Calculus of Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity" (1943), the foundational paper of neural networks
- **J.C.R. Licklider** ⭐⭐ — psychoacoustics + computing; later wrote "Man-Computer Symbiosis" (1960) — the direct intellectual descendant of Macy thinking; ARPA director, funded ARPANET
- **William Ross Ashby** ⭐⭐ — psychiatrist + cybernetician; *Design for a Brain* (1952), *Introduction to Cybernetics* (1956); homeostasis + requisite variety
- **Kurt Lewin** ⭐⭐ — psychologist; field theory; group dynamics; connected to Group Processes strand
- **Donald MacKay** ⭐⭐ — physicist; key theorist of meaning, information, and the observer; argued against Shannon's purely probabilistic view

### Group Processes Strand
- **Erving Goffman** — sociologist; guest appearance at 3rd Group Processes conference; his dramaturgical sociology resonates with Bateson's communication theory
- **Erik Erikson** — psychoanalyst; development stages; group processes
- **Konrad Lorenz** — ethologist; animal behavior; key bridge between animal and human behavior studies
- **Niko Tinbergen** — ethologist; Nobel laureate; comparative animal behavior

## Key Contributions

The Macy Conferences produced fields, not just papers:

1. **Cybernetics** — the science of circular causation, feedback, and control in animals and machines. Wiener's *Cybernetics* (1948) named it.

2. **Information Theory** — Shannon's framework emerged partly in this context; information as a probabilistic, structure-independent quantity.

3. **Cognitive Science** — the Macy framework of mind-as-information-processing machine was the direct precursor. McCulloch's neural logic + Wiener's feedback + von Neumann's computation = the cognitive science program.

4. **Artificial Life** — von Neumann's cellular automata, the discussions of self-reproducing machines, and the idea of "life as pattern" (not substrate) were seeded here. Chris Langton's ALife emerged from this tradition.

5. **Second-Order Cybernetics** — Heinz von Foerster's contribution: the observer is always part of the observed system. This became the epistemological foundation for constructivism, autopoiesis (Maturana/Varela), and eventually much of complexity science.

6. **Systems Theory** (Bateson's branch) — feedback, double-bind, ecology of mind, the pattern that connects. Bateson's *Steps to an Ecology of Mind* (1972) and *Mind and Nature* (1979) are the direct output.

7. **Neural Networks / AI** — McCulloch-Pitts neurons → perceptrons (Rosenblatt) → deep learning (via back-propagation). The conceptual lineage is direct.

8. **Group Dynamics as Science** — the reflexive study of group processes pioneered a methodology; Bateson, Mead, and Goffman's inputs here are understudied.

## For Amber

### Why It Matters to Amber's Research
This is the **origin story** of every field Amber works in. Understanding the Macy Conferences is not antiquarian history — it's a map of the conceptual infrastructure that produced agent ethology, ALife, machine behavior, and open-endedness research.

**1. 🤝 Direct Research Connections**
- **Agent Ethology** ↔ Macy Conferences: The idea of studying agents behaviorally (what do they *do*?) is rooted in the cybernetics tradition. McCulloch's neural loops, Shannon's rat, Bateson's communication theory — all are behavioral, not cognitive-internalist.
- **Machine Behavior** (Rahwan et al.) is explicitly a neo-Macy project: studying AI as you would study animals (observationally, ecologically). Iyad Rahwan is *doing cybernetics* without always naming it.
- **ALife / Open-Endedness**: Joel Lehman (Amber's ERA mentor) works in the direct lineage: open-endedness, novelty search, quality diversity algorithms. These are computational operationalizations of von Neumann's cellular automata and Langton's ALife programs — seeded at Macy.
- **More-than-Human Design** ↔ Bateson's ecology of mind: the idea that mind is distributed, relational, and not located inside a skull is a Bateson thesis. Amber's posthumanism connects here.

**2. 💡 The Macy Model as Research Infrastructure**
The *process* of the Macy Conferences is as important as the content:
- Small core group (~20 people) sustained over years
- Radically interdisciplinary (mathematicians + anthropologists + psychiatrists)
- Work *in progress* presented, not finished results
- Verbatim transcripts circulated widely
- The goal: shared language, not consensus
This is a model for how to organize productive intellectual communities. It maps directly onto how Amber might think about building communities around her research interests. The Macy model = Amber's research taste made explicit.

**3. 🔮 Future Involvement / Engagement Paths**
- **ALIFE 2026** — Amber has a workshop proposal there. The ALife conference IS the living heir of Macy. Amber is literally participating in this tradition.
- **Santa Fe Institute** — the premier complexity science institution; SFI summer schools, external faculty programs, and the *Complexity* journal are entry points
- Read the **Macy Transactions** (6th–10th, edited by von Foerster/Mead/Teuber) as primary source — extraordinary to witness the arguments being made
- **Heims's *The Cybernetics Group*** (1991) — essential reading; reconstructs the social politics of the group (the Wiener/Bateson tensions, the military funding questions)

**4. 📚 Key Concepts to Know**
- **Circular causation / feedback loops** — the core innovation
- **Teleological mechanisms** — goal-directed behavior without a telos; machines with purposes
- **First-order vs. second-order cybernetics** — controlling systems vs. observing systems that include the observer
- **Autopoiesis** (Maturana/Varela) — self-producing systems; direct post-Macy development
- **The observer problem** — von Foerster's contribution; observer is always inside the observed system; this is why machine behavior is a science of situated observation

**5. 🌍 Areas to Know**
- The Macy cybernetics tradition is distinct from the Soviet/Eastern European cybernetics tradition (which Wiener tried to suppress, ironically). Worth knowing the Soviet strand — it's less studied in the West.
- The UK cybernetics tradition (Ashby, Pask, MacKay) is more performative, ontological, art-linked than the US strain. Andrew Pickering's *The Cybernetic Brain* is the key text. Gordon Pask ([[Gordon Pask]]) has a note already.
