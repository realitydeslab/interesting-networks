# Knowing Machines Project

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-01:** Deep research completed. Full profile: history, all team members, complete publications list, key ideas, awards, connections. Note expanded from stub to full gold-standard profile.
- **2026-02-26:** Initial note created during Kate Crawford deep research. Transatlantic research collaboration on AI training.

Research project tracing the histories, practices, and politics of how machine learning systems are trained to interpret the world. Jointly based at **USC Annenberg** and the **NYU Engelberg Center on Innovation Law & Policy**, funded by the Alfred P. Sloan Foundation. The project unites a transatlantic team of data scientists, artists, legal scholars, journalists, and domain researchers to excavate training datasets as sociotechnical artifacts. Methodologically positioned as "critical data studies" — treating training data not as neutral raw material but as political interventions that structure knowledge cultures and reproduce power.

## Mission & Objectives
**Mission:** Understand how ML systems come to know the world. The core claim: training datasets are not neutral collections of facts but deeply political assemblages that embed assumptions, reproduce inequalities, and shape what AI systems can and cannot perceive.

**Core philosophy:** The focus on "ground truth" construction in ML is a political act. Every dataset reflects choices about who gets to decide what counts as real, what categories matter, what images are included and excluded. Critical analysis of these choices is a prerequisite for responsible AI.

**Methodological approach:** "Archaeology of datasets" — excavating the material layers of training sets, cataloguing principles and values by which they were constructed, analyzing what normative patterns of life are assumed, supported, and reproduced. Combines investigative journalism, data science, legal analysis, art practice, and ethnography.

**Current focus areas:**
1. **Critical Dataset Studies** — reading list, investigative tools, methodology development
2. **Knowing Legal Machines** — legal framework for generative AI, copyright, consent
3. **Generative AI and media** — how the news industry frames and uses GenAI
4. **Dataset creator practices** — ethnographic study of how datasets are actually made
5. **Nature × ML** — citizen science, birding, and alternative models of training data

## Key Ideas

**Training datasets as political interventions:**
Every dataset is shaped by who has the power to decide what "counts," what categories matter, whose images get harvested. The ImageNet case study showed that this isn't marginal — the "person" category contained racial slurs, misogynistic labels, and physiognomy-adjacent classifications. These classifications are now embedded in AI systems that have moved into hiring, security, healthcare.

**"Ground truth" as constructed:**
The project's title — "Knowing Machines" — is a double entendre. It asks: how do machines come to "know"? The answer is always: through training data produced by humans, with all the politics that implies. "Ground truth" in ML is not discovered; it is manufactured.

**Dataset archaeology:**
Methodological approach: excavate datasets as you would an archaeological site. Catalogue layers, identify assumptions, trace provenance, analyze what worldviews are embedded. This is distinct from technical "bias audits" — it's humanistic scholarship applied to technical artifacts.

**The disappearing datasets:**
"Excavating AI" documents the wave of training datasets being quietly pulled offline (ImageNet "person" category, Duke MTMC, Microsoft MS-Celeb, Stanford Brainwash Cafe webcam) after ethical violations were exposed. This raises a forensic problem: if datasets disappear, we can't trace the provenance of bias in deployed systems. The project argues for preservation alongside critique.

**Alternative models: citizen science:**
The "Bird in Hand" work argues that birding and citizen science projects offer a model of data collection that is participatory, community-governed, ecologically embedded, and non-extractive. eBird (Cornell Lab) as a counter-model to Big Tech data harvesting.

**Legal accountability:**
The Knowing Legal Machines work addresses the copyright and consent gap: most generative AI was trained on data scraped without consent. The legal explainer and case collection make these questions accessible to the public.

## Why Interesting
Knowing Machines is the research project that has most rigorously developed the methodology for treating AI training data as a sociotechnical artifact. It fills a gap between technical ML research (which takes datasets as given) and abstract critical theory (which talks about "bias" without examining specific mechanisms).

**What makes it distinctive:**
1. **Interdisciplinary for real** — not just putting a social scientist in the same room as a computer scientist. The team includes a Pulitzer Prize-winning journalist (Buschek), a MacArthur Fellow theater director (Dorsen), a National Geographic Explorer data artist (Thorp), a legal clinic (Schultz/NYU), and an AI environmental researcher (Luccioni). Each brings real expertise, not decoration.
2. **Methods, not just critique** — they built SeeSet; they wrote a field guide; they produced a reading list that is now canonical. They are creating infrastructure for a field.
3. **Art as research** — Calculating Empires won the Venice Architecture Biennale Silver Lion as a research output. This is the full integration of art and research that most art-science projects only claim.
4. **Legal grounding** — The Knowing Legal Machines arm addresses concrete legal questions (copyright, consent, data provenance) that have enormous practical stakes for anyone working with AI.
5. **Public scholarship** — the podcast (Avishai), the legal explainer, the field guide — Knowing Machines actively works to make its research accessible beyond academia.

**For Amber's context:** This is the intellectual community that has most rigorously defined what "critical AI" means in practice — not as ethics consulting, but as genuine scholarly and artistic intervention. Their approach to "ground truth" as political construction directly parallels Amber's interest in how machine agents interpret and reproduce social worlds.
