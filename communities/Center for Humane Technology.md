# Center for Humane Technology (CHT)

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-09:** Deep research completed. Full profile created covering history, key people, AI Dilemma, podcast, policy work, and connections to Amber's research.
- **2026:** CHT is actively working on AI existential risk — a major shift from the social media focus of 2018–2022.
- **2025:** Harris delivered a TED Talk "Why AI is our ultimate test and greatest invitation" (April 2025), appeared on Oprah's AI special, Real Time with Bill Maher, and The Daily Show with Jon Stewart — high public visibility.
- **2025:** Podcast episode 124 "Attachment Hacking and the Rise of AI Psychosis" (with Dr. Zak Stein) marks continued expansion into AI/psychological harm territory.

A nonprofit dedicated to radically reimagining digital infrastructure — originally as an antidote to the attention economy, now as a watchdog and advocacy group for AI existential risk. CHT is the organization most responsible for bringing tech ethics into mainstream cultural consciousness, first through social media critique, then through AI risk discourse. Founded by a rare configuration of tech insiders who understand the machinery they're critiquing from the inside.

## Mission & Objectives
**Mission:** "Drive a comprehensive shift toward humane technology that supports the collective well-being, democracy and shared information environment."

**Original framing (2018–2022):** Diagnosing the attention economy — how social media platforms use persuasive design, variable reward mechanisms, algorithmic amplification, and surveillance capitalism to maximize engagement at the cost of mental health, political discourse, and epistemic coherence.

**Current framing (2023–present):** "Race to the top" for AI safety — using the social media cautionary tale as a template for anticipating and preventing analogous (or worse) AI-driven harms, now including existential risk from AGI/transformative AI systems.

**The pivot:** CHT's shift from attention to AI risk wasn't abrupt — it followed the logic of the same underlying problem: powerful optimization processes (first engagement algorithms, now AI systems) being deployed faster than our capacity to understand or regulate them. Harris coined the "wisdom gap" framing at SXSW 2022: the gap between the pace of technology and the pace of human institutional capacity to respond.

**Core concepts CHT introduced to mainstream discourse:**
- **"Human downgrading"** — technology systematically degrades human cognitive and moral capacity
- **"Attention economy"** — attention as the resource being extracted and sold
- **"The wisdom gap"** — tech capability outpaces institutional wisdom
- **"Freedom of speech is not freedom of reach"** (Aza Raskin + Renée DiResta) — widely adopted phrase on platform amplification

## Key Publications & Output

### Flagship Media

**The Social Dilemma (2020)** — Netflix documentary directed by Jeff Orlowski. CHT co-founders (Harris, Raskin, Fernando) as central subjects. Premiered at [Sundance 2020](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2020_Sundance_Film_Festival). Released worldwide September 9, 2020. **38 million households in first 28 days** — second-most-watched documentary on Netflix in its era. 85% on Rotten Tomatoes. Won 2 awards at the [73rd Primetime Creative Arts Emmy Awards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/73rd_Primetime_Creative_Arts_Emmy_Awards). Three main AI goals depicted: the engagement goal, growth goal, advertisement goal. Tim Cook cited it directly at the Computers, Privacy and Data Protection Conference (January 2021). Sparked Apple Screen Time and Google Digital Wellbeing features. Criticism: The Verge (Casey Newton), Slate (Pranav Malhotra) — lacked nuance, no diverse voices (missing Safiya Noble, Sarah T. Roberts, Siva Vaidhyanathan), dramatized narrative "campy." URL: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Social_Dilemma), [Netflix](https://www.netflix.com/title/81254224)

**The AI Dilemma (2023)** — A ~1-hour presentation delivered by Harris and Raskin in March 2023 to a private audience of tech leaders, policymakers, and influencers; subsequently circulated widely online. Central argument: AI development is following the same catastrophic pattern as social media — racing ahead of regulation, ethics, and human wisdom — but with exponentially higher stakes. Key claims:
- AI systems are being deployed before we understand their effects (parallel to social media's unregulated rollout)
- No lab has "stop" capability — competitive dynamics create a multipolar trap where safety-conscious actors lose market share to reckless ones
- We are not just building tools but "new aliens" — AI systems that are **more intelligent than us in some domains before they are controllable**
- The "race to the top" for AI safety requires regulatory coordination equivalent to the FDA, FAA, nuclear regulatory frameworks — none exist for AI
- Specific risks cited: AI-generated disinformation at scale, AI-assisted bioweapons, erosion of epistemic commons, AI "agents" acting on behalf of billions of people without alignment guarantees
The presentation drew explicit comparisons to the *Social Dilemma* moment for social media — and argued AI poses an existential risk, not just a social harm. URL: [YouTube](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xoVJKj8lcNQ) (various uploads)

Now at episode 124+ as of March 2026. ## Criticism

### From the Tech Industry
- **Facebook's official response to *The Social Dilemma*:** Released a statement calling the film a "distorted view" of how platforms work that creates "a convenient scapegoat for what are difficult and complex societal problems." URL: [Facebook About page statement (via Wikipedia)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Social_Dilemma#Industry_reaction)
- Facebook refused to comment on whether user numbers declined after the film

### From Academics and Critics
- **Lack of diverse voices** — Mozilla employees Ashley Boyd and Audrey Hingle noted *The Social Dilemma* would have benefited from "more diverse voices." Academic critics (Safiya Noble, Sarah T. Roberts, Siva Vaidhyanathan) were notably absent — these scholars work on algorithmic discrimination, labor, and power from non-Silicon Valley perspectives
- **Manichean narrative** — Laura Alvarez Trigo (PopMeC) argued the film's persuasive techniques mirror the manipulation it criticizes: "constructs a Manichean narrative with a very elusive and misconstrued evil side." DOI: [10.58079/szfq](https://doi.org/10.58079/szfq)
- **"Clickbait documentary"** — Elizabeth Pankova, *The New Republic* (Oct 2020): raised the irony of the film using dramatic, engagement-maximizing techniques to warn against engagement-maximizing techniques. URL: [New Republic](https://newrepublic.com/article/159657/social-dilemma-rise-clickbait-documentary)
- **Dramatized narrative** — both critics and reviewers found the fictional family subplot "campy" (Casey Newton, The Verge) and lacking nuance (Pranav Malhotra, Slate). URL: [The Verge review](https://www.theverge.com/2020/9/9/21428171/social-dilemma-netflix-review-tech-silicon-valley-facebook-twitter)
- **"Neo-Luddite" framing** — some tech commentators accused CHT of technophobia rather than productive reform, though this is contested; CHT explicitly denies being anti-technology

### Broader Structural Critiques
- CHT focuses on design ethics and platform responsibility, which critics argue is insufficient without addressing the underlying capitalism that makes exploitation rational for companies
- Overemphasis on individual behavioral change (turn off notifications, use privacy search engines) rather than structural/regulatory intervention
- CHT founders are predominantly white, male, Silicon Valley insiders — their framing of "what technology should do" may reflect particular cultural assumptions

### Why Interesting
CHT sits at a remarkable intersection for Amber's work:

The comparison is worth making explicit in papers.
