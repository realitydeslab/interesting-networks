# Algorithmic Justice League (AJL)

A civil rights movement disguised as an AI research organization. Founded by [[Joy Buolamwini]] in 2016 out of MIT, AJL combines art, research, policy advocacy, and media to expose and combat AI bias and algorithmic harms. They coined the language and set the template for what "algorithmic accountability" looks like in practice — from peer-reviewed audits to spoken word poetry to Netflix documentaries. AJL sits at the nexus of Black feminist technology studies, critical AI, and civil rights activism.

## Mission & Objectives

> "We mitigate the harms and biases of AI by promoting 4 core principles."

AJL's mission: raise public awareness about the impacts of AI, equip advocates with empirical research, build the voice and choice of the most impacted communities, galvanize researchers, policymakers, and industry practitioners to prevent AI harms.

**Four Core Principles:**
1. **Affirmative Consent** — people must have a real choice in how and whether they interact with AI systems
2. **Meaningful Transparency** — people must be able to understand AI processes in plain terms
3. **Continuous Oversight and Accountability** — robust mechanisms beyond self-regulation; independent third-party auditing
4. **Actionable Critique** — research that translates to practice, not just principles

**Positioning against mainstream:**
- Against "Ethical AI" (voluntary principles, no enforcement, self-regulation by big tech)
- Against "Inclusive AI" (inclusion without accountability can enable better surveillance)
- For "Equitable + Accountable AI" — civil rights framing, mandatory requirements, banning harmful uses

## Key Publications & Output

### Landmark Research Papers

## Key Contributions

- **Invented the algorithmic audit** as a recognized practice — Gender Shades defined the methodology
- **Shifted framing from "AI ethics" to "algorithmic justice"** — moved discourse from voluntary principles to civil rights enforcement
- **Art-research synthesis model** — proven template for making technical bias findings culturally legible (poetry → documentary → book → policy)
- **Forced corporate accountability** — IBM, Microsoft publicly announced improvements after Gender Shades; Amazon suspended police facial recognition sales
- **Policy wins** — IRS halted ID.me facial recognition after AJL campaign; contributed to federal guidance on AI in law enforcement
- **Gender Shades Justice Award** — first award for people harmed by facial recognition ("excoded"), won by Robert Williams (wrongfully arrested by AI)
- **Safe Face Pledge** (2018, now sunsetted) — early industry voluntary commitment on facial recognition weaponization

## Why Interesting

AJL is a template for how technical research can become a civil rights movement. They didn't just publish papers — they built a cultural apparatus: documentary, spoken word, congressional testimony, book, campaigns. The Gender Shades paper (2018) arguably did more to change AI industry behavior than a decade of IEEE ethics guidelines. 

For Amber, the key lesson is their **art-research-policy pipeline**: make the technical finding legible → make it emotionally resonant (poetry, film) → make it actionable (campaigns, testimony) → create systemic change (policy, corporate behavior). This is a model Amber could adapt for machine behavior and trustworthy agentic web work.

The AJL's expansion from facial recognition to generative AI and agentic systems creates direct overlap with Amber's research agenda. The governance principles they've developed (affirmative consent, meaningful transparency, continuous oversight) are exactly what an accountable agentic web would need.
