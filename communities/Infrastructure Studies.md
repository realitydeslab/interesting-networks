# Infrastructure Studies

A subfield within Science and Technology Studies (STS) that investigates technological infrastructure — not as neutral background, but as cultural, political, and material systems with profound social consequences. Infrastructure Studies asks: what makes something "infrastructure"? How are power, values, and social relations embedded in systems we take for granted? Rooted in feminist STS and symbolic interactionism, the field reveals the invisible work, excluded users, and political choices hidden in the mundane scaffolding of modern life.

## Mission & Objectives

Infrastructure Studies aims to make infrastructure visible as an object of inquiry. Susan Leigh Star's key insight (1999): infrastructure is only "infrastructure" relative to organized practices — what is infrastructure for one community is obstacle or irrelevance for another. The field examines:

- **Embeddedness** — infrastructure sinks into the background of other activities; it is "ready-to-hand" (Heidegger) until it breaks
- **Transparency** — infrastructure becomes invisible in use; you only see it when it fails
- **Reach and scope** — infrastructure spans multiple sites and practices
- **Standards and classification** — who gets to define the categories? What's left out?
- **Repair and maintenance** — keeping systems going requires continuous invisible labor (see Steven Jackson's "rethinking repair")
- **Ethics of infrastructure** — residual categories, those who fall outside classification schemes, as a site of injustice

The field is deeply feminist: Star's work highlights how those who don't "fit" existing categories (people with dietary restrictions in her classic "onions" essay, marginalized groups in classification systems) reveal the politics of apparently neutral infrastructure.

## Key People

### Founders & Core Theorists

- **[Susan Leigh Star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Susan_Leigh_Star)** (1954–2010) ⭐⭐⭐ — The field's most important figure. Sociologist at UC Santa Cruz, UCSD, Santa Clara, University of Pittsburgh. Student of Anselm Strauss (grounded theory). Collaborated with Bruno Latour and Michel Callon (1987–88, Paris). Key concepts: **boundary objects** (with Griesemer, 1989), **ethnography of infrastructure** (1999), invisible work, residual categories. Died unexpectedly 2010 while working on *This is Not a Boundary Object* with Bowker. President of 4S (2005–2007).

- **[Geoffrey C. Bowker](https://www.ics.uci.edu/~gbowker/)** — Professor of Informatics, UC Irvine. Star's partner and closest collaborator. Together produced *Sorting Things Out* (MIT Press, 2000), the canonical text. Also: *Memory Practices in the Sciences* (MIT Press, 2005). Co-founded the NSF Values in Design workshop series with Star and Helen Nissenbaum. 2024 Bernal Prize (4S's highest honor).

- **[Steven J. Jackson](https://sjackson.infosci.cornell.edu/)** ⭐⭐ — Professor of Information Science and STS, Cornell. Vice Provost for Academic Innovation. Key figure in contemporary infrastructure studies. Famous essay: "Rethinking Repair" (2014) — reframes infrastructure from a maintenance-first rather than innovation-first perspective. Runs the **Computing On Earth Lab**, which brings together social scientists, humanists, artists, and engineers to rethink the material and planetary foundations of computing. Active at CSCW, CHI, and 4S.

- **[Paul N. Edwards](https://si.umich.edu/people/paul-edwards)** — Professor, School of Information, University of Michigan. Historical approach to infrastructure. Author of *A Vast Machine: Computer Models, Climate Data, and the Politics of Global Warming* (MIT Press, 2010) and co-author of *Understanding Infrastructure: Dynamics, Tensions, and Design* (NSF, 2007, with Bowker, Jackson, and Knobel). Studies how scientific infrastructure produces "facts" — particularly in climate modeling.

- **[Safiya Umoja Noble](https://safiyaunoble.com/)** ⭐⭐ — Founder of [UCLA Center for Critical Internet Inquiry](https://c2i2.ucla.edu/). Author of *Algorithms of Oppression* (NYU Press, 2018). Extends infrastructure studies to algorithmic systems and search engines as discriminatory infrastructure. Bridge between infrastructure studies and critical race studies.

- **[Ruha Benjamin](https://www.ruhabenjamin.com/)** ⭐⭐ — Professor of African American Studies, Princeton. Author of *Race After Technology* (Polity, 2019). Coined "New Jim Code" — the embedding of racial hierarchy in technological infrastructure. Another key bridge to critical race/infrastructure studies.

- **[Yuk Hui](https://yukhui.org/)** — Philosopher of technology. Not infrastructure studies proper but adjacent: his concept of **cosmotechnics** (technology as embedded in particular cosmological/cultural frameworks) parallels infrastructure studies' attention to how values are baked into technical systems. Author of *Recursivity and Contingency* (2019). Visiting professor at China Academy of Art (Amber's territory).

- **[Tarleton Gillespie](https://tarletongillespie.org/)** — Principal Researcher, Microsoft Research, New England. Author of *Custodians of the Internet* (Yale, 2018). Studies platforms as infrastructure — content moderation, terms of service, algorithmic curation as infrastructure politics.

- **[Nick Couldry](https://www.nickcouldry.com/)** & **[Ulises Mejias](https://uaguilar.com/)** — Authors of *The Costs of Connection* (Stanford, 2019). Coined "data colonialism" — extension of infrastructure studies to global power dynamics of data extraction.

### Adjacent Figures

- **Bruno Latour** (1947–2022) — Actor-Network Theory. Infrastructure studies draws heavily on ANT for understanding sociotechnical assemblages.
- **Donna Haraway** — Feminist STS. Star was in her intellectual milieu at UCSC. Haraway's situated knowledge connects to Star's work on invisible labor.
- **Anselm Strauss** — Star's doctoral advisor. Grounded theory, sociology of work. Foundation for infrastructure studies methodology.

## Key Publications & Output

### Foundational Books
- [*Sorting Things Out: Classification and Its Consequences*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262522953/) — Geoffrey Bowker & Susan Leigh Star (MIT Press, 2000). **THE** canonical text. Traces how classification systems (medical, scientific, bureaucratic) embed moral and political order. DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/6327.001.0001
- [*The Ethnography of Infrastructure*](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/00027649921955326) — Susan Leigh Star (American Behavioral Scientist, 1999). Key methodological essay. DOI: 10.1177/00027649921955326
- [*Steps Toward an Ecology of Infrastructure*](https://doi.org/10.1287/isre.7.1.111) — Star & Ruhleder (Information Systems Research, 1996). First major infrastructure theory paper. DOI: 10.1287/isre.7.1.111
- [*Institutional Ecology, Translations, and Boundary Objects*](https://doi.org/10.1177/030631289019003001) — Star & Griesemer (Social Studies of Science, 1989). Origin of "boundary object" concept. DOI: 10.1177/030631289019003001
- [*A Vast Machine*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262517027/) — Paul N. Edwards (MIT Press, 2010). Infrastructure studies applied to climate science. DOI: 10.7551/mitpress/8155.001.0001
- [*Understanding Infrastructure*](https://deepblue.lib.umich.edu/handle/2027.42/49353) — Bowker, Edwards, Jackson, Knobel (NSF Report, 2007). Community self-assessment document, widely cited.

### Key Essays
- "Rethinking Repair" — Steven Jackson (2014) in *Media Technologies: Essays on Communication, Materiality, and Society* (MIT Press). Influential manifesto for maintenance-first infrastructure thinking. URL: https://sjackson.infosci.cornell.edu/files/2014/04/JacksonRethinkingRepairINPRESS.pdf
- "Power, Technologies and the Phenomenology of Conventions: On Being Allergic to Onions" — Star (1991). Famous essay making infrastructure politics concrete through dietary analogy.
- "Standards and their Stories" — Lampland & Star (eds., Cornell UP, 2009). Collection on standardization as infrastructure.

### Journals
- **[Science, Technology, & Human Values](https://journals.sagepub.com/home/sth)** (SAGE) — Primary journal for STS broadly, publishes infrastructure studies. Star was co-Editor-in-Chief.
- **[Social Studies of Science](https://journals.sagepub.com/home/sss)** — Flagship STS journal. Published Star's foundational boundary objects paper.
- **[Information Systems Research](https://pubsonline.informs.org/journal/isre)** — Published Star & Ruhleder (1996).
- **[Engaging Science, Technology, and Society](https://estsjournal.org/)** (ESTS) — Open access, newer, more explicitly political/engaged STS.
- **[New Media & Society](https://journals.sagepub.com/home/nms)** — Publishes infrastructure studies of digital systems.
- **[Big Data & Society](https://journals.sagepub.com/home/bds)** — Open access, infrastructure + data politics.

## Key Contributions

Infrastructure Studies has given the world:

1. **Boundary object** — one of STS's most cited concepts. Objects/ideas that function differently in different communities but maintain enough shared structure for coordination. Used across organizational theory, CSCW, design, information science, and beyond.

2. **Infrastructure inversion** — methodological move of deliberately foregrounding background systems to reveal their politics. "Turning things upside down to see what usually goes unnoticed."

3. **Invisible work** — recognizing the continuous labor of maintenance, care, and coordination that keeps systems going but receives no credit. Deeply feminist insight.

4. **Classification as power** — showing that categories are never neutral; who gets counted, who is excluded, what gets named matters politically and ethically.

5. **Rethinking repair** — Jackson's intervention: if breakdown and repair are normal, not exceptional, what does that mean for how we design? For the global south where infrastructure is always in a state of repair?

6. **Ethnography of infrastructure** — methodological approach: follow the wires, talk to the workers, find the breakdown points, look for the residual categories.

## For Amber

**Why interesting:**
Infrastructure Studies directly addresses how material technical systems embed power, values, and social relations — this is foundational for any critical account of AI, machine behavior, or agent systems. For Amber's work on machine behavior, agent ethology, and cyborg sociology, Infrastructure Studies provides:
- The vocabulary for understanding AI agents as **infrastructure** (for whom? against whom?)
- The concept of **boundary objects** (useful for thinking about how agents mediate between human communities)
- The **repair/maintenance** lens (what does it mean to "maintain" an AI agent ecosystem?)
- **Classification and residual categories** (who falls outside the agent's model of the world?)

- *Machine behavior / agent ethology:* Infrastructure Studies gives the sociotechnical vocabulary for studying agents as embedded systems, not just autonomous actors. What infrastructure do agents depend on? What labor is invisible in keeping them running?

## Why Interesting

Infrastructure Studies sits at a crucial intersection for Amber's work: it's the theoretical tradition that takes seriously the **material conditions of technical systems** without being either purely engineering or purely philosophical. Its feminist roots (Star, Haraway's milieu) mean it's already attuned to power, exclusion, and invisible labor — the same concerns that animate more-than-human design and critical AI.

The "rethinking repair" move is especially powerful for Amber: what if we think of AI agents not from the perspective of their launch/deployment, but from the perspective of their continuous maintenance, breakdown, and repair? This reframes "machine behavior" from a behavioral science question into a sociotechnical infrastructure question — much richer.

Infrastructure Studies is also where **protocol studies** and **platform theory** (Bratton, Antikythera) ultimately root themselves. Understanding infrastructure studies gives Amber the genealogy to position work at the intersection of agent systems, sociality, and material conditions.
