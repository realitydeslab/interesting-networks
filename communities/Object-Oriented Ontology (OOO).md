# Object-Oriented Ontology (OOO)

Philosophical movement that insists on treating objects — including non-human entities, natural phenomena, and abstract things — as having genuine reality independent of human perception. A branch of the broader **Speculative Realism** movement that emerged from a landmark 2007 workshop at Goldsmiths, University of London.

Key tenet: no entity (including the human mind) ever fully accesses another entity; all relations involve a degree of "withdrawal." This is simultaneously an epistemological claim (we can't fully know things) and an ontological one (things exist independently of being known).

## Mission & Objectives
To take non-human objects seriously as philosophical entities. To overcome "correlationism" (Meillassoux's term) — the assumption that reality only exists in correlation with human thought. To develop philosophy adequate to the ecological crisis, AI, and other posthuman challenges.

## Origins
- **2007 Goldsmiths Workshop** — "Speculative Realism" workshop organized by Ray Brassier and Alberto Toscano at Goldsmiths, University of London. Participants: Brassier, Meillassoux, Grant, Harman. Morton joined the OOO strand subsequently.
- The movement grew partly through blogs (Harman's "Object-Oriented Philosophy" blog, Morton's "Ecology Without Nature" blog) in the late 2000s.

## Why Interesting
OOO is the philosophical framework that most directly takes non-human agency and ecological entanglement seriously without falling into naive animism. Its key concepts (withdrawal, tool-being, hyperobjects, mesh) provide a rigorous vocabulary for posthuman and more-than-human research.
