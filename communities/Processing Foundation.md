The steward of the creative coding ecosystem — the nonprofit backstop that keeps Processing, p5.js, Processing Android, and related tools alive, free, and accessible for artists, designers, educators, and students worldwide.

## Mission & Objectives

**Mission:** "To promote software learning within the arts, artistic learning within technology-related fields, and to celebrate the diverse communities that make these fields vibrant, liberatory, and innovative."

**Core philosophy:** FLOSS (Free, Libre, Open Source Software) as political commitment. Software as medium and means of thinking. Access to tools = access to power. The most radical futures are built by marginalized communities — center them.

**Structural safeguard:** Unlike a company, the Foundation answers to no investors. Its non-profit status is a built-in guarantee that the software remains free, development is transparent, and community benefits come before profit.

**Explicit equity focus:** Race, gender, class, sexuality, immigration/documentation status, age, geographic location, disability — the Foundation explicitly tracks who has been excluded from tech and art and works to counter it.

## History

### Origins: The ACG and Design By Numbers (1996–2000)
Processing did not emerge from nowhere. It grew from John Maeda's [Aesthetics and Computation Group (ACG)](http://acg.media.mit.edu/) at the MIT Media Lab, itself a successor to the [Visual Language Workshop (VLW)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MIT_Media_Lab), founded 1975 by Muriel Cooper (graphic designer, founding art director of MIT Press). The VLW pioneered design research in screen-based information spaces. The ACG, started 1996, synthesized visual design with software.

Within the ACG, Ben Fry developed a C++ graphics toolkit called ACU (used on Silicon Graphics workstations costing tens of thousands of dollars). Casey Reas arrived as a grad student in 1999. Together they co-taught workshops using Maeda's [Design By Numbers (DBN)](http://dbn.media.mit.edu/) platform — minimal, 100×100px, grayscale only — which proved DBN's core insight: simple tools could teach non-programmers to think computationally through visual output.

### Creating Processing (2001)
**2001:** Casey Reas and Ben Fry start Processing at MIT Media Lab. Goal: take the ideas from DBN and ACG out of the lab and into the world. Processing = three things simultaneously: **language** (simplified Java with visual primitives), **environment** (an IDE called the "sketchbook"), and **community** (shared online forum, examples, culture). The name "sketch" was philosophically loaded — code as exploratory process, not pre-planned specification. Originally at the domain proce55ing.net (because processing.org was taken).

**2003:** Casey Reas moves to Los Angeles; joins UCLA Design Media Arts as professor.

**2004:** Domain acquired; project moves to processing.org.

**2005:** Processing wins the [Golden Nica at Prix Ars Electronica](https://ars.electronica.art/prix/en/) in the Net Vision category — the first major international recognition.

**2007:** *Processing: A Programming Handbook for Visual Designers and Artists* published by MIT Press. Still the definitive Processing reference.

**2008:** Ben Fry wins Nierenberg Chair of Design at Carnegie Mellon School of Design (2006–2007). Founds [Fathom Information Design](https://fathom.info) in Boston.

**2011:** Ben Fry wins [National Design Award](https://cooperhewitt.org/national-design-awards/) in Interaction Design from Cooper Hewitt Smithsonian Design Museum.

**2012:** **The Processing Foundation formally established as a 501(c)(3) nonprofit.** Casey Reas, Ben Fry, and Daniel Shiffman as founding leaders. Shiffman joins as third project lead.

**2013:** Lauren Lee McCarthy creates p5.js — a native JavaScript reimagining of Processing for the web. The Foundation officially supports it. p5.js becomes the most-used creative coding tool for web-based work.

**2021:** 20th anniversary of Processing. Major fundraiser raises enough to hire full-time staff for the first time. Processing 4 development begins.

**2022:** Processing 4 released (theme selector, GIF support, modern OS compatibility). p5.js reaches 4 million users.

**2023:** p5.js turns 10. 2023 Impact Report published. Qianqian Ye steps up as p5.js Lead (since April 2022); Kit Kuksenok becomes new p5.js Lead from 2024.

## Software Projects

### [Processing](https://processing.org/) (Java) — born 2001
The original. Java-based IDE ("sketchbook") + simplified language for visual design and interactive media. Every Processing program is called a "sketch." Low floor, high ceiling: simple sketches as simple as Design By Numbers, but the full power of Java available underneath. Current version: 4.3.1 (Nov 2024). Open source (GPL/LGPL). Used in art schools, design programs, and introductory programming courses worldwide.

**Key lineage:** Direct descendant of Design By Numbers (Maeda), the ACG toolkit ACU (Fry), and Muriel Cooper's Visual Language Workshop.

**Spin-offs:** The Processing design environment inspired [Arduino](https://arduino.cc/) and Wiring (hardware programming). The Radiohead "House of Cards" music video was made with Processing + LiDAR.

### [p5.js](https://p5js.org/) (JavaScript) — born 2013
Lauren Lee McCarthy created p5.js in 2013 as a native JavaScript library that reinterprets Processing's philosophy for the web. Not a port — a reimagining. p5.js runs in any browser, makes it trivially easy to start sketching. The "p5" name is a direct reference to the old "proce55ing" domain. 

**Scale:** 4+ million users as of 2023. The dominant creative coding tool for web-based work globally.

**Current leads:** Kit Kuksenok (2024–present, p5.js Lead). Qianqian Ye (2021–2025, on leave). Rachel Lim (p5.js Editor Lead, 2022–present).

**ml5.js:** A p5.js-adjacent library for machine learning developed by NYU ITP/IMA with Google Education grant support. Daniel Shiffman has made prominent tutorial videos for ml5.

### [Processing for Android](https://android.processing.org/)
Port enabling Processing sketches to run as Android apps. Community-maintained.

### Processing.py (Python)
Python interface to the underlying Java toolkit. Chiefly developed by Jonathan Feinberg from 2010. Contributions from James Gilles and Ben Alkov.

### [py5](https://py5coding.org/) (Python 3.8+)
More recent Python version. Uses JPype to make Processing Java jars available to CPython. Supports full Processing feature set.

### Discontinued: Processing.js
JavaScript port that allowed Processing Java code to run in the browser. Initially released 2008 by [John Resig](https://johnresig.com/) (creator of jQuery). Run through Mozilla Foundation + Seneca College partnership. Discontinued December 2018 — superseded by p5.js.

## Key Contributions
- **Democratized creative coding** — Made visual programming accessible to artists and designers who would never use Java or C++. Processing → p5.js progression tracks the web itself.
- **Created the "sketch" paradigm** — The idea of code as exploratory process, not pre-planned specification. Influenced pedagogical approaches far beyond creative coding.
- **Spawned an ecosystem** — Processing directly inspired Arduino (hardware programming), Wiring, and Fritzing. p5.js inspired ml5.js, p5.sound, and dozens of contributed libraries.
- **Equity-first open source** — One of the earliest major open-source projects to explicitly center racial justice, accessibility, and decolonization in software development practice.
- **Critical Coding Cookbook, Code Decolonized** — advocacy programs that interrogate power in code pedagogy.
- **Community infrastructure** — Processing Community Day (PCD) — a worldwide, distributed annual celebration of the community. CC Fest — free annual creative coding event.

## Events & Programmes

### Annual Fellowships (~June–October)
**Processing Foundation Fellowships** — $10,000 stipend each. For artists/collectives using Processing, p5.js, or p5.sound for creative/technical projects. Theme rotates annually. 2025 theme: Data Storytelling. 2 remote + 1 in-person (Los Angeles, Extended Realities Fellowship). Mentored by partner organizations. Timeline: Applications typically open April, close May, fellowship June–October.

**Mentor Partners (2025):** [MIT Open Doc Lab](https://opendoclab.mit.edu/), [Gray Area](https://grayarea.org/), [Interactive Fiction Technology Foundation](https://iftechfoundation.org/), [Arab Image Foundation](https://arabimagefoundation.org/), [Data Garden Collective](https://www.data-garden.org/)

### pr05 Developer Grant ($10,000)
Annual developer grant for early-to-mid-career software developers. 200 hours over 4 months contributing to Processing/p5.js ecosystem. Mentored by core contributors. 2025 theme: Building Bridges (interoperability across the ecosystem). Cohort meets with fellowship cohort.

### Google Summer of Code (2011–2025)
Annual program placing college students in open-source development with Processing/p5.js. The Foundation's longest-running external partnership.

### CC Fest (Creative Coding Festival)
Free annual event for anyone curious about creative code. Started 2016 (first event at NYU-ITP). Virtual and in-person editions. Next: March 21, 2026 (virtual, Zoom). Organized by Saber Khan. No prerequisites — radically open entry point.

### Processing Community Day (PCD)
Annual distributed worldwide celebration. Local organizers run events, share zines, cakes, artwork. 2021 edition celebrated Processing's 20th anniversary.

### Learning to Teach, Teaching to Learn (2016–2026)
Conference series for educators teaching programming in creative/artistic contexts. Organized in collaboration with [[School for Poetic Computation]].

### p5.js Contributor Conferences
2015 (first, at Frank-Ratchye STUDIO for Creative Inquiry, CMU) and 2019 editions. ~35 participants, one week, advancing code/documentation/community outreach.

### Code, Decolonized (2021–2022)
Project and living archive by shawné michaelain holloway and Xin Xin. Researches new ways of learning/teaching programming for the web through perspectives traditionally overlooked in the technical classroom.

### p5.js Access Day (2022)
Community event focused on accessibility in creative coding tools and pedagogy.

### Open Source Arts Contributors Conference (2023+)
Partnership with [Clinic for Open Source Arts](https://clinicopensourcearts.org/) (University of Denver). Brings open-source arts contributors together.

## Relationship to SFPC and Eyeo

The Processing Foundation, [[School for Poetic Computation]], and [[Eyeo Festival]] form an interlocking trio at the center of the creative coding world.

**SFPC:** The Foundation and SFPC run *Learning to Teach, Teaching to Learn* together (since 2016). SFPC co-founders Zachary Lieberman and Taeyoon Choi are both Processing/openFrameworks community veterans. Lauren Lee McCarthy taught at SFPC as affiliated faculty. The communities share personnel, values (code as poetic/political medium), and students.

**Eyeo Festival:** The Processing Foundation is listed as an official Eyeo partner (2022 edition). Processing co-creators Casey Reas and Ben Fry, p5.js creator Lauren McCarthy, and The Coding Train's Daniel Shiffman have all spoken at Eyeo. The festival was conceived partly to celebrate and promote the open-source creative coding philosophy that Processing embodies.

**The triangle:** Processing Foundation provides the *tools*; SFPC provides the *pedagogy* (small, intensive, critical); Eyeo provides the *annual gathering* (600 people, 4 days, Minneapolis). Three different scales and modes, mutually reinforcing. Understanding one means understanding all three.

1. **Critical coding as political practice** — The Foundation's work (Code, Decolonized; Critical Coding Cookbook; Code+Care) directly parallels Amber's interests in critical computing and equity. The tools are never neutral; the Foundation makes that explicit.
