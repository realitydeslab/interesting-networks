# Power Station of Art (PSA)
## 上海当代艺术博物馆

Shanghai's flagship state-run contemporary art museum and the permanent home of the **Shanghai Biennale** — China's oldest and most internationally visible contemporary art event. A converted coal-fired power station on the Huangpu River that became, in 2012, the first public (non-private) contemporary art museum in mainland China. The institution where the global Chinese art-tech circuit converges biannually, where Western curators come to China, and where Chinese contemporary art's most ambitious institutional ambitions take material form. The building's 165-metre chimney — now an elevator tower — is a statement: the infrastructures of industrialisation, hollowed out and re-energised as cultural infrastructure.

## Mission & Objectives

PSA's official mission is to serve as a centre for **contemporary art collection, exhibition, research, exchange, and education** — explicitly framed as a public cultural resource for the citizens of Shanghai. In practice, the institution balances three sometimes-competing mandates:

1. **National prestige:** As a state-funded institution under the Shanghai City Culture and Tourism Bureau, PSA is part of China's soft-power cultural infrastructure. It must demonstrate international credibility (hence inviting marquee Western curators for the Biennale) while remaining politically navigable.

2. **Biennale anchor:** The Shanghai Biennale is institutionally and reputationally PSA's core product. The museum's year-round programming supports, contextualises, and builds toward each biennial edition.

3. **Contemporary art infrastructure:** PSA provides Shanghai with infrastructure — for shows, collection, research, education — that a private institution couldn't. It has helped build the city's ecosystem alongside Rockbund Art Museum, UCCA Edge, Chronus Art Center, and the West Bund museums.

The tension between state patronage and avant-garde ambition has been navigated through the Biennale's strategy of importing credentialed international curators, giving them genuine creative freedom within certain constraints. This model — internationalise the curatorial voice, maintain institutional governance — has largely succeeded in making PSA legible to both Chinese cultural authorities and the global contemporary art world.

## Key People

### Institutional Leadership
- **Li Xu** (李旭) — Director, PSA. Appointed at founding. An insider figure who has managed the balance between state mandate and contemporary art ambitions. Former deputy director of the Shanghai Art Museum. Oversees PSA's year-round programming, institutional partnerships, and strategic direction. Low public profile internationally, high institutional authority.
- **Gong Yan** (龚彦) — Deputy Director, PSA. Previously editor-in-chief of *Art World* (《艺术世界》) magazine — one of China's most important contemporary art publications. Her editorial background shapes PSA's publications and critical framing. The key programmatic and editorial mind behind the institution's intellectual identity. Worth a stub note: ⭐⭐
- **Fang Zengxian** (方增先) — Founding director of the Shanghai Biennale (1996). Painter and art administrator who initiated China's first art biennale. Not at PSA, but the institutional ancestor.

### Shanghai Biennale Curatorial Alumni (since PSA era)

#### 9th Edition (2012–2013): "Reactivation"
- **Qiu Zhijie** (邱志杰) ⭐⭐ — Chief curator. CAA faculty artist-curator-theorist. Known for "total art" (总体艺术) integrating video, maps, performance, and critical writing. Curated the inaugural PSA-era biennale with remarkable conceptual coherence. He returned the biennale to its site — the power station's history as a site of energy/reactivation. Worth a full note as one of China's most important curatorial thinkers.
- **Johnson Chang Tsong-zung** (漢雅軒 / 張頌仁) — Co-curator. Hong Kong gallerist and curator, founder of Hanart TZ Gallery. Key bridge between Chinese contemporary art and international art world since the 1980s. Brought a Hong Kong/diasporic perspective.
- **Boris Groys** — Co-curator. German philosopher and media theorist, professor at NYU. Author of *Art Power*, *Going Public*, *In the Flow*. His curatorial co-presence at the 9th edition marked the seriousness of PSA's international intellectual ambitions.
- **Jens Hoffmann** — Co-curator. Costa Rican-German curator. Former Deputy Director at the Hammer Museum, LA. International pedigree amplifying PSA's global visibility.

#### 10th Edition (2014–2015): "Social Factory"
- **Anselm Franke** ⭐⭐ — Chief curator. Head of Visual Art and Film at HKW (Haus der Kulturen der Welt) Berlin. Critic, curator, theorist working on animism, cosmology, the "other" in modernity. Brought HKW's rigorous critical-theory framework to Shanghai. Likely still worth a stub note.

#### 11th Edition (2016–2017): "Why Not Ask Again?"
- **Raqs Media Collective** — Chief curators: **Jeebesh Bagchi**, **Monica Narula**, **Shuddhabrata Sengupta**. New Delhi-based collective founded 1992. Artists, theorists, curators. Founders of the Sarai media lab. Their biennale was structured into five sections (Terminals, Infra-Curatorial Platform, Theory Opera, 51 Personae, City Projects) — one of the most formally experimental editions. Authors of key texts on archives, time, labour, and the contemporary. Each member ⭐⭐, collectively ⭐⭐⭐ as a curatorial voice.

#### 12th Edition (2018–2019): "Proregress — Art in an Age of Historical Ambivalence"
- **Cuauhtémoc Medina** ⭐⭐⭐ — Chief curator. Mexican curator, critic, and historian. Senior researcher at UNAM. Chief curator of MUAC (Museum of Contemporary Art, Mexico City). One of the most intellectually powerful figures in global curating, known for politically incisive work on colonialism, capitalism, and modernity. Co-curated the Mexican Pavilion at Venice 2001. His biennale invoked e.e. cummings' neologism "proregress" to name the dialectical logic of history — neither pure progress nor regression. A significant critical moment for PSA's international standing.

#### 13th Edition (2020–2021): "Wetware"
- **Andrés Jaque** ⭐⭐ — Co-curator. Spanish architect and founder of Office for Political Innovation. Professor at Columbia GSAPP. Works on the politics of infrastructures, queer urbanism, and domesticity. Brought an architectural/infrastructural lens. Notable — he's an architect curating a contemporary art biennale.
- **Marina Otero Verzier** ⭐⭐ — Co-curator. Spanish architect and curator. Director of Research at Het Nieuwe Instituut, Rotterdam. Works on labour, infrastructure, and care.
- **Lucia Pietroiusti** ⭐⭐⭐ — Co-curator. Italian curator at the Serpentine. Founder of General Ecology project (Serpentine). Works on ecological thinking, more-than-human relations, and post-extractivism. Serpentine/ecology connection makes her a natural collaborator for PSA's relationship with the global ecology-art discourse. Very significant figure — should have a full note in this graph.

## Key Publications & Output

### Biennale Catalogues
Each Shanghai Biennale produces a comprehensive exhibition catalogue — published bilingually (Chinese/English) and serving as the primary critical documentation of each edition. From the 9th edition onward, PSA publishes these under its own imprint. The 11th and 12th edition catalogues are widely cited internationally.

### *PSA Journal / 当代艺术* (occasional)
PSA has produced exhibition-related publications, essays, and occasional journal-format publications. These serve both the institutional record and as contributions to contemporary art critical discourse in China.

### Digital Archive
PSA maintains a digital archive of exhibitions and events at [powerstationofart.com](https://www.powerstationofart.com/en/). The site is a React SPA requiring JavaScript, which makes scraping difficult — but it documents all exhibitions with images and texts.

### Shanghai Biennale Texts Online
Many biennale texts are republished on [e-flux.com](https://www.e-flux.com) and referenced in international art journals. The biennale's curatorial essays are among the most-read texts in Chinese contemporary art discourse.

## Key Contributions

### 1. China's First Public Contemporary Art Museum
PSA established that China could have a state-funded institution with genuine international contemporary art credibility. Before PSA, major contemporary art in Shanghai happened in private spaces (ShContemporary fair, private galleries). PSA created public infrastructure.

### 2. Professionalising the Shanghai Biennale
The move to PSA in 2012 transformed the Shanghai Biennale from an event associated with the Shanghai Art Museum (a more traditional institution) to a purpose-built contemporary art venue. The building's industrial scale — floor plates of 4,000+ square metres — enabled ambitious large-scale installations that weren't possible in the old venue.

### 3. Creating a Curatorial Rotation Model
The pattern of inviting a new international curator (or curatorial team) for each biennale while maintaining institutional continuity has become a model for how state-adjacent institutions in Asia can maintain critical credibility. Contrast with the Venice Biennale's curator model or Documenta's more intensive curatorial rotation — PSA's approach is faster and more globally diverse.

### 4. The Western Curator in China Pipeline
By consistently inviting Western curators (Groys, Franke, Raqs Media Collective, Medina, Jaque/Pietroiusti), PSA has made Shanghai a destination for international curatorial practice. This has created relationships, built networks, and brought critical discourse into China in ways that government-to-government cultural exchange alone cannot.

### 5. Critical Climate Discourse in China
The 13th Biennale's "Wetware" (2021) was one of the first major Chinese institutional shows to seriously engage with climate change, ecological systems, and the intersection of biology and technology. Lucia Pietroiusti's involvement (via the Serpentine's General Ecology project) brought this international ecological discourse into PSA's programming.

### 6. The Expo 2010 Infrastructure Repurposed
The physical building — converted from a coal plant to an Expo 2010 pavilion to a museum — is itself a statement about cultural transition. The 165m chimney, now an observation tower and elevator, is one of Shanghai's most recognisable cultural landmarks. The building's transformation narrative parallels China's own industrial-to-cultural-economy transition.

### Year-Round Exhibition Programme
Beyond the Biennale, PSA runs a continuous programme of temporary exhibitions across its 11 exhibition floors (~41,000 sq m total). This includes:

- **International loans and touring shows:** Major exhibitions from Centre Pompidou (opening show: "Electric Fields, Surrealism and Beyond," 2012), and ongoing partnerships with European and American institutions.
- **Chinese contemporary art solo shows:** Major solo presentations of leading Chinese artists — Zhang Peili, Xu Bing, Cao Fei, and others from the contemporary Chinese art canon.
- **Media art and technology-focused shows:** PSA has been a significant venue for Chinese and international media art. The building's industrial scale accommodates large-scale immersive installations that smaller venues cannot.
- **Architecture and design exhibitions:** Occasional architecture exhibitions, sometimes in conjunction with the Shanghai Biennale programme.
- **Group thematic shows:** Examples include "Death Ray on Coral Island" (featuring Gary Zhexi Zhang) — a reference to the 1980 Chinese science fiction film 《珊瑚岛上的死光》, exploring the relationship between Chinese sci-fi history and contemporary art; "Fragments in the Measure" / 小节线内的抽屉 (2025, group show).

### Education & Public Programmes (PSA Learning)
- **PSA Learning** — public education programme including guided tours, workshops, lectures, and school programmes
- **Young Curators** / **新策展人** research programme — supports emerging curators in China through mentorship, research residencies, and exhibition opportunities
- **International symposia** — organised around each biennale, bringing the curatorial team and invited thinkers to Shanghai for public talks

### Residency / Exchange
PSA has informal exchange relationships with international institutions, particularly through the Biennale curatorial process, but does not run a formal artist-in-residence programme in the conventional sense. The curators of each Biennale typically spend extended periods in Shanghai during the preparation period, creating informal knowledge exchange.

### Shanghai Biennale Submission / Engagement Angle
The Shanghai Biennale typically invites artists rather than accepting open submissions — it's curated, not juried. The path in is:
1. **Build institutional relationships** — Gong Yan, PSA curators
2. **Show work at related venues** (Chronus Art Center, Rockbund, UCCA Edge) to build visibility in the Shanghai circuit
3. **Publish critically** in Chinese bilingual publications (LEAP, *艺术世界*) to establish critical presence before an exhibition approach
4. **Leverage the CAA network** — PSA has deep relationships with CAA faculty including Gao Shiming, Qiu Zhijie, and Zhang Peili; Amber's CAA affiliation is a warm path

### Sinofuturism Connection
The Shanghai Biennale has been a site for some of the most significant art-technology-futures discourse in China:
- The 13th edition's "Wetware" engaged directly with biology/technology/ecology — terrain adjacent to Amber's embodied computing and somaesthetics work
- The biennale has consistently engaged with questions of infrastructure, labour, and technology's social impacts
- Amber's position at the intersection of XR/somaesthetics/HCI research is genuinely distinctive in the PSA/Biennale context — this scene has rich video art and digital art traditions but less HCI research-practice

**The sinofuturism angle:** PSA is the most visible institutional site where Chinese technological modernisation is aesthetically examined. If Amber were to develop work engaging with how Chinese bodies, Chinese labour, and Chinese technological futures intersect — that work would land powerfully in PSA's context. The question of what XR feels like for bodies shaped by Chinese somatic traditions (qi, martial arts, cultivation practices) is genuinely unexplored in this space.

### Visit Strategy
**On a visit to Shanghai:**
1. Visit PSA as a visitor first — understand the building's scale, see current exhibitions
2. Attend any public events (symposia, talks, openings) — these are where informal introductions happen

## Why Interesting

**For understanding China's position in contemporary art:** PSA is the institution where this question is most durably and consistently examined. The Shanghai Biennale's rotating-curator model means PSA is a site of genuine critical encounter between Chinese cultural politics and international contemporary art discourse — neither a pure propaganda vehicle nor a decontextualised international art fair.

**For Amber specifically:** Three threads:

1. **Institutional gateway:** The path into Shanghai's serious contemporary art scene runs through PSA. Understanding it, knowing its people, and finding the right point of entry is essential infrastructure knowledge.

2. **Ecological/bodily futures:** The recent editions ("Wetware," and presumably the 14th) suggest PSA is genuinely engaging with ecology, biology, and body-technology questions — terrain where Amber's somaesthetics/XR work is distinctive and potentially highly legible.

3. **Scale for ambitious work:** PSA's building — 11 floors, 41,000 sq m, industrial scale — is one of the few spaces in the world where genuinely ambitious large-scale immersive work can be realised. If Amber's work reaches a scale where venue size matters, PSA is aspirationally the right venue.

**For understanding the sinofuturism circuit:** PSA is where sinofuturism and cosmotechnics discourse meets institutional art practice. Every iteration of the question "what does it mean to make art about China's relationship to technology and futures" passes through PSA at some point. Gary Zhexi Zhang shows there. Cao Fei has shown there. The artists who are asking these questions collectively constitute a PSA network.

**Historical depth:** Uniquely in China, PSA carries both the site history (industrial, expo, museum) and the curatorial history (14 biennale editions) to offer real institutional depth. It is not a new institution pretending at history; it is a genuinely historic building (the power plant) with a compelling 30-year biennale history. That combination is rare anywhere in the world.
