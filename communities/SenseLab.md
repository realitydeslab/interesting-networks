# SenseLab

**Research-creation collective** founded in 2004 at Concordia University, Montréal. Founded and directed by [[Erin Manning]]; co-created with [[Brian Massumi]]. Affiliated with the **[Hexagram Network](https://hexagram.ca/en/)** (inter-university research-creation network in Montréal) and the **[Milieux Institute](https://milieux.concordia.ca/)** (Concordia's media arts research institute).

SenseLab ran from 2004 to ~2019, when it transitioned into the **[[3 Ecologies Institute]]** (3e) — which continues its work in a land-based, extra-university, post-capitalist form in Sainte-Anne-du-Lac, Québec.

**The core premise:** *Bodies think*. Thought is not located in the brain but distributed through movement, sensation, and encounter. Knowledge can be produced *through* aesthetic and choreographic experience, not merely *about* it. The task of SenseLab was to create events and publications that enact this understanding rather than merely theorize it.

Over 15 years, SenseLab became one of the most generative research infrastructures in the humanities — building the field of **research-creation** in Canada, originating the concept of the **anarchive**, producing the **Immediations** book series and **Inflexions** journal, running multi-day collective events at the intersection of process philosophy, movement arts, neurodiversity, and political thought.

## What SenseLab Did

### Research-Creation Events (Radical Pedagogy)

SenseLab's primary output was **events** — multi-day gatherings that were simultaneously philosophical, choreographic, and political. Not conferences or workshops in the conventional sense: no paper presentations, no expert-to-audience transmission. Instead, participants arrived with a shared reading, then collectively invented an event through **pop-up propositions** — anyone could propose an activity, the day's programme assembled emergently. Movement, making, discussion, and silence were equally legitimate research acts.

These events were explicitly designed to be inaccessible to conventional academic documentation. They were **experiences of research**, not research about experience.

Key event series:
- **Movements of Thought** — ongoing workshop series integrating movement research and philosophical inquiry
- **Knots of Thought / Nœuds de la Pensée** — sustained writing/reading/movement practice
- **Into the Midst** (October 2012) — flagship five-day event at the **SATosphere** (Society for Art and Technology's immersive dome, Montréal). Participants worked inside and around an interactive immersive projection environment. Documented in a special out-of-series *Inflexions* issue.
- **Immediations workshops** (series, c. 2012–2019) — tied to the book series; events at multiple international partner institutions
- Workshops at **Zürcher Hochschule der Künste (ZHdK)**, Zurich
- Events at **Australian partner institutions** (linked to UNSW Sydney / [[Andrew Murphie]])
- Brazilian partner events (Rio / São Paulo)

### Radical Pedagogy

Manning held a **Research Chair in Speculative Pragmatism, Art and Pedagogy** at Concordia. SenseLab functioned as the vehicle for her vision of "alter-pedagogy" — learning environments that do not separate knowledge from movement, theory from making, expertise from participation. These experiments inform her concept of the **parapedagogical**: teaching environments that subvert the institutional logic even while operating within it.

Her book *For a Pragmatics of the Useless* (Duke, 2020) theorizes the university as a site of uselessness — in the sense that the most generative knowledge has no immediate utility, and that defending uselessness is a political act against neoliberal instrumentalization of research.

### Neurodiversity as Epistemic Commitment

From early in SenseLab's history, Manning formed alliances with **autistic practitioners** — not as research subjects but as co-theorists. The key figures:
- **[[Tito Rajarshi Mukhopadhyay]]** — autistic poet and philosopher. Author of *Plankton Dreams: What I Learned in Special-Ed* (Immediations/OHP, 2015). Tito's experience of non-normative perception became a philosophical resource for SenseLab's theory of the "minor gesture."
- **[[Adam Wolfond]]** — autistic poet. His work on typing poetry and non-normative communication was woven into SenseLab events and publications.

Manning's concept of the **minor gesture** — drawn from her book *The Minor Gesture* (Duke, 2016) — theorizes the small, marginalized, not-quite-legible movement as the site of the most intense ontological creativity. Disability (particularly autism) becomes a way of *knowing differently*, not a deficit to be accommodated.

This commitment was structural, not rhetorical: neurodiversity participants were invited as intellectual contributors, not to perform neurodiversity for a neurotypical audience.

### Anarchiving

One of SenseLab's major theoretical contributions was the concept of the **anarchive** — a form of collective memory that does not reduce events to documentation. Traditional archives capture events as fixed representations; the anarchive keeps material traces alive in a living system. Process over product; potential over record.

The anarchive concept is developed in the collective book *The Go-To How-To Book of Anarchiving* (2016, ed. Andrew Murphie with SenseLab). It continues as a living practice at **[fuit.es](https://fuit.es/)** (enter token "murmurations," select 3E) — the 3e / SenseLab living anarchive.

The anarchive is not just a theory of documentation; it's a theory of memory as active — events don't end, they continue to generate potential. Relevant to Amber's interest in how research lives after events, how knowledge stays generative.

## Key Concepts

**Research-creation** — Manning and Massumi's most influential methodological contribution. Research-creation is not "art + research" or "practice-based research"; it is a specific mode of knowledge production where making IS theorizing. A concept formed in language is "transduced" into a non-language-based mode of experience (movement, installation, choreography), and reciprocally, the understanding embedded in non-language-based practice is brought to explicit conceptual expression. Neither direction is primary; the two-way exchange generates new modes of knowledge that exceed either. Canonized in the founding *Inflexions* issue "How is Research-Creation?" (2008).

**Immediation** — the concept developed across the Immediations research project (2012–2019): the quality of experience that is more than mediation but less than immediacy. Events that create new conditions of experience rather than representing pre-existing conditions. The two-volume *Immediation I & II* (ed. Manning, Munster, Stavning Thomsen) is the definitive statement.

**The Minor Gesture** — movements, perceptions, and forms of intelligence that are too small, too non-normative, or too embodied to register in mainstream epistemologies. These become the site of the most intense creativity and ontological novelty. Disability, neurodiversity, and the body's "useless" capacities are where the new comes from.

**Speculative Pragmatism** — Manning's term for SenseLab's philosophical approach: speculative (committed to possibility, emergence, the not-yet) but pragmatic (engaged with the real, the actual, the present practice). Against both pure speculation (untethered from experience) and pure pragmatism (limited to existing utility).

**Affect** — used in Massumi's Spinozist sense: not emotion, but the capacity to affect and be affected. A pre-personal, pre-linguistic dimension of experience. Not "feelings" but the field of potential that underlies them. Massumi's essay "The Autonomy of Affect" (1995) is the founding text of affect studies as a field.

**Transindividual** — Massumi's term for experience that is not limited to an individual subject. Affect is transindividual: it crosses bodies, infects environments, is shared before it is individuated. SenseLab events were designed to create transindividual fields of experience.

## Intellectual Heritage

SenseLab drew on a coherent and specific philosophical tradition:

**Process philosophy:**
- **Alfred North Whitehead** — *Process and Reality* (1929). The universe as composed of "occasions of experience," not substances. Becoming is primary; being is derivative. The source of SenseLab's conviction that events (not objects) are what's real.
- **Henri Bergson** — Duration, the virtual, intuition. Time as quality, not quantity.
- **Gilbert Simondon** — Individuation: how things come to be individual through a continuous process of relation. The pre-individual field. SenseLab's whole theory of collective experience draws here.

**Poststructuralism / Schizoanalysis:**
- **Gilles Deleuze & Félix Guattari** — *A Thousand Plateaus* (1980, tr. Massumi 1987). Rhizomes, assemblages, becoming, minoritarian politics, schizoanalysis. Guattari's *Three Ecologies* gives 3e its name.
- **Baruch Spinoza** — Affect as capacity to affect/be affected. The non-personal body. Ethics as ontology.

**Pragmatism:**
- **William James** — Radical empiricism: relations are real. Experience is primary.
- **Charles Sanders Peirce** — Abduction; firstness/secondness/thirdness. Massumi uses Peirce to theorize perception.

**Political philosophy:**
- **Michel Foucault** — Biopower, disciplinary power; Massumi develops "ontopower" in relation to these.
- **Félix Guattari** — micro-politics, ecosophy, transversality (both the concept and the relation-making practice)

The SenseLab circuit was: **small, international, committed**. Participants came from all continents. The shared framework (affect theory, process philosophy, research-creation) substituted for institutional affiliation as the membership criterion.

## Why Interesting

### For Amber Specifically

SenseLab is the most interesting research collective for Amber's work for several converging reasons:

1. **Research-creation as methodology directly applies to Amber's practice**: Amber designs experiences, workshops, and artifacts as forms of knowing — not illustrations of theories developed elsewhere. This is exactly what SenseLab was building a framework for. Manning's insistence that making IS theorizing legitimates the entire design-as-research approach.

2. **More-than-human orientation**: SenseLab's process philosophy premise — that affect is transindividual, that thought is distributed through bodies and environments, that the non-human is already participating in experience — maps directly onto Amber's more-than-human design interests. The SenseLab didn't use that language but the ontology is aligned.

3. **Neurodiversity as epistemic diversity**: SenseLab's commitment to neurodiversity wasn't just political — it was epistemological. The claim is that non-normative perception reveals things about experience that normative perception cannot access. This is a design resource, not just an ethics checklist. For work on ALife, machine behavior, and non-human agents, this thinking about what counts as a valid mode of intelligence is directly relevant.

4. **Process philosophy for artificial life**: Whitehead's ontology of events and occasions — which SenseLab developed extensively — is arguably the best philosophical framework for thinking about Artificial Life. ALife is about life-as-process, emergence, becoming, the event. Manning and Massumi's work is a 20-year development of exactly this philosophical tradition.

5. **Collective form and infrastructure**: Manning's concern with what kinds of collective structures can sustain research without institutionalizing it — the SenseLab-to-3E trajectory — is relevant to anyone thinking about how research communities should be organized. The pop-up proposition format is an exportable tool.

6. **Affect theory for machine/AI research**: Massumi's affect theory — affect as transindividual, pre-personal, below the threshold of conscious representation — is directly applicable to questions about machine behavior and AI affect. What would it mean for a machine to "have affect" in Massumi's sense? This is live research territory.

### The Unique Angle
SenseLab's rare quality: it **practiced what it preached**. Most affect theory is written by people sitting at desks. SenseLab's events were designed so the theory had to be lived — the gatherings themselves were attempts to create transindividual fields of affect, to produce collective intelligence through movement rather than argument. The anarchive concept emerged from needing to remember events that resisted documentation. This self-consistency is extremely rare.
