# CUDAN Open Lab

Tallinn University's research lab for **Cultural Data Analytics** — a genuinely interdisciplinary mixing of art history, network science, statistical physics, computational linguistics, media studies, and artistic practice. Built on a €2.5M EU Horizon 2020 ERA Chair (2019–2024) led by [[Maximilian Schich]], now sustained 2024–2027 through Tallinn University's Research Fund. The lab's defining thesis: that culture can be understood *scientifically* through a careful combination of qualitative inquiry, quantitative measurement, and computation — without reducing it to mere data.

## Mission & Objectives

CUDAN (CUltural Data ANalytics) was built around a radical academic-mixing thesis: that the most interesting cultural research happens at the intersections of disciplines that rarely speak to each other. The lab deliberately assembled scholars from art history, physics, linguistics, media studies, computer science, and artistic practice — and set them to work on shared cultural questions.

The [CUDAN Research Cooperation Strategy](https://cudan.tlu.ee/publications/) functions as the lab's manifesto: a published framework for how multidisciplinary academic mixing should work in practice. The result is a lab where a statistical physicist collaborates on newsreel analysis with a cultural historian, where an artist builds visualization tools used by museum researchers.

Key commitments:
- **Open science:** nearly all publications are open access; tools like the [Collection Space Navigator](https://collection-space-navigator.github.io/) are fully open source
- **Diversity of methods:** quantitative + qualitative + computational + creative practice, always in combination
- **Small-language scholarship:** significant work on Estonian and other small-language digital cultures often invisible in global anglophone research
- **Public seminar as intellectual output:** the Open Lab Seminar series (2020–2027) defines the field's scope by *who it invites*, treating the concert of speakers as an emergent definition of Cultural Data Analytics

## Key Publications & Output

### Flagship / Most-Cited

- **Schich et al. (2014).** ["A Network Framework of Cultural History"](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1240064) in *Science*. Landmark paper mapping 150,000 births and deaths of notable historical figures to trace cultural flows. Viral via Nature video *Charting Culture*. Defined an entire approach to computational history.

- **Karjus et al. (2023).** ["Compression ensembles quantify aesthetic complexity and the evolution of visual art"](https://doi.org/10.1140/epjds/s13688-023-00397-3) *EPJ Data Science*. Measuring visual complexity of artworks using image compression algorithms; tracking evolution across art history. [PDF](https://cudan.tlu.ee/pub/2023_EPJ-Data-Science_Karjus-et-al_Compression-Ensembles.pdf)

- **Ohm, Karjus, Schich et al. (2023/2025).** [Collection Space Navigator](https://collection-space-navigator.github.io/). Open-source browser tool for visual collection navigation with ML embeddings. Used with classical art datasets, museum collections. Presented at ACM VINCI 2023; extended preprint on arXiv.

- **Ohm, Karjus, Tamm, Schich (2025).** ["fruit-SALAD: A Style Aligned Artwork Dataset"](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41597-025-04529-4) *Scientific Data*. 1,000 artworks across 10 styles × 10 artists for probing visual similarity in embeddings. Open data/code. [GitHub](https://github.com/Style-Aligned-Artwork-Datasets/fruit-SALAD)

- **Oiva et al. (2024).** ["Soviet View of the World: Exploring Long-Term Visual Patterns in 'Novosti dnia' Newsreel Journal (1945–1992)"](https://doi.org/10.22148/001c.118495) *Journal of Cultural Analytics*. Computational analysis of how Soviet propaganda constructed global geography over 47 years.

- **Zemaityte et al. (2024).** ["Quantifying the global film festival circuit: Networks, diversity, and public value creation"](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0297404) *PLOS ONE*. Network analysis of the global festival circuit; socioeconomic factors in national representation. Open data/code.

- **Oiva et al. (2024).** ["A framework for the analysis of historical newsreels"](https://doi.org/10.1057/s41599-024-02886-w) *Humanities and Social Sciences Communications*. Full methodological framework for computational audiovisual history; 10 co-authors.

- **Mets et al. (2024).** ["Automated stance detection in complex topics and small languages"](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0302380) *PLOS ONE*. Immigration coverage in polarizing Estonian media; NLP + ML for under-resourced languages.

- **Karjus & Cuskley (2024).** ["Evolving linguistic divergence on polarizing social media"](https://doi.org/10.1057/s41599-024-02922-9) *Humanities and Social Sciences Communications*. Language divergence as social polarization signal.

- **Asadchy et al. (2024).** ["Perceived gendered self-representation on Tinder using machine learning"](https://doi.org/10.1057/s41599-024-03801-z) *Humanities and Social Sciences Communications*. ML classification of gender cues in dating app profiles.

## Key Contributions

1. **Collection Space Navigator** — most-used practical tool; open source; adopted by museums and researchers worldwide for navigating visual collections with vector embeddings
2. **Computational newsreel analysis** — framework for analyzing audiovisual historical archives at scale; first systematic analysis of Soviet propaganda through visual patterns
3. **Compression ensembles for aesthetic complexity** — novel method borrowing from information theory to measure art history
4. **Cultural history as network science** — the 2014 *Science* paper inaugurated a methodological tradition
5. **Film festival circuit mapping** — first quantitative global analysis of cultural value creation in the festival network
6. **CUDAN Research Cooperation Strategy** — published manifesto for how multidisciplinary academic mixing works, adopted as model for EU cultural research infrastructure

## Why Interesting

CUDAN proves something important: that *rigorous quantitative methods can yield genuinely humanistic insight* — not in spite of their rigor but because of it. The Soviet newsreel work reveals what propagandists could not hide (geographic blind spots, visual obsessions) through visual pattern analysis. The compression ensemble work reveals that artistic movements genuinely have measurable aesthetic signatures. The film festival work reveals global inequality in cultural recognition that critics suspected but couldn't prove at scale.

For Amber, who bridges computational practice and critical cultural work, CUDAN is both a model and a potential collaborator network. The lab occupies a different register — more science-facing than design-facing — but the questions are often the same: how does culture actually work at scale? What can AI see about culture that humans can't? What gets lost and what gets found when you compute the archive?

The tools (especially CSN and fruit-SALAD) are immediately practical. The seminar series is a free, ongoing education in the field. And the people — particularly Karjus, Ohm, and Canet Solà — are making work that Amber would find genuinely interesting, not just professionally adjacent.
