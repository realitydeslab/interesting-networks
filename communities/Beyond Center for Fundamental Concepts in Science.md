# Beyond Center for Fundamental Concepts in Science

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Deep research completed. Expanded from stub to full profile with history, research programs, events, funding, and key people connections.

The world's most adventurous institutional space for "big questions" science — founded in 2006 at Arizona State University by theoretical physicist [[Paul Davies]]. A pioneering center devoted to confronting the *really* big questions of science and philosophy: where did the universe come from, are we alone, what is life, what is time, why does anything exist at all? 

Unlike most research centers, Beyond deliberately works at the borders between physics, biology, philosophy, and cosmology — asking not just "how" but "why." Interdisciplinary to the bone, it transcends traditional academic departments to create space for risky, speculative, foundational research.

## Mission & Objectives

**Vision:**
- To create new and exciting ideas that push the boundaries of research a bit "beyond"
- To conduct research that transcends traditional subject categories
- To answer foundational questions in science, and explore their philosophical ramifications — what might be called "the big questions"
- To present science to the public as a key component of our culture and of significance to all humanity

**Core Philosophy:** Science is a way of knowing about existence, not merely solving engineering problems. The Center treats physics and philosophy as naturally connected — the deepest scientific questions inevitably have philosophical implications. This approach mirrors the work of historical natural philosophers who didn't separate science from metaphysics.

## History & Founding

**2006:** Paul Davies joins Arizona State University as Professor of Physics and founds the Beyond Center for Fundamental Concepts in Science. Davies had previously been Professor of Natural Philosophy at Macquarie University, Sydney, and before that at the University of Adelaide. The move to ASU was facilitated by the university's ambition to become one of the world's great research universities through strategic faculty hiring.

**2012:** Davies elevated to Regents' Professor at ASU — the university's highest academic rank.

The Center grew organically around Davies' network, with Sara Imari Walker eventually joining as Deputy Director and Maulik Parikh as a key faculty member. The cancer biology program — an unexpected expansion — began when Davies realized that cancer's evolutionary origins demanded exactly the kind of foundational, cross-disciplinary thinking the Beyond Center was built to do.

## Research Programs

### 1. Astrobiology & Life in the Universe
Whether life exists beyond Earth is one of the oldest and deepest questions. The Center investigates the conditions for life, the search for biosignatures, and the possibility that life may have started many times on Earth. Connected to the search for extraterrestrial intelligence and SETI research. Davies has been on the Advisory Committee for Breakthrough Listen — Yuri Milner's major SETI initiative.

### 2. Cosmology & The Multiverse
Research on the first split second after the Big Bang. Does the observed universe constitute only a tiny component in a multiverse of vastly different universes? Why is our universe so remarkably suited to support life — the "fine-tuning" or "Goldilocks" problem? Davies' book *[The Goldilocks Enigma](https://www.amazon.com/Goldilocks-Enigma-Universe-Just-Right/dp/0547053584)* (2006) is the essential introduction.

### 3. Quantum Mechanics & Foundations
Quantum mechanics is the most successful scientific theory in history, yet its interpretation remains profoundly contested. At Beyond, two new aspects of the observer/observation problem are investigated. Also exploring connections between quantum physics and biology — quantum effects in living systems, quantum aspects of consciousness.

### 4. Cancer Biology — NCI Center
**The most unexpected research direction:** Beyond runs the National Cancer Institute's Center for Convergence of Physical Science and Cancer Biology at ASU — one of 12 such centers nationally. This applies physics thinking to understanding cancer's deep evolutionary roots, treating cancer not as a malfunction but as a reversion to ancient cell behavior. The hypothesis: cancer is an evolutionary throwback to pre-multicellular life. This reconceptualization has implications for therapy. Davies has said this is the most important work he's ever done. Detailed projects at [cancerinsights website].

### 5. Origin of Life
Scientists have little idea how, where, or when life began. The Center supports experimental and theoretical research into biogenesis. Key investigation: whether life might have started many times on Earth (and we live alongside ancient lineages). Connection to [[Sara Imari Walker]]'s Assembly Theory — developing a rigorous mathematical framework for "lifeness."

### 6. Time & Its Arrow
Time is the most fundamental aspect of experience yet remains deeply mysterious. The "arrow of time" problem: why do physical processes appear to run one way (clocks run down, organisms age, stars burn out) when the laws of physics are symmetric in time? The Center investigates how temporal asymmetry emerges from symmetric laws — ultimately leading to cosmological explanations.

### 7. Laws of Physics & Their Origin
Science works because the universe has ordered, intelligible laws. But where do these laws come from? Why do they have the form they do? This is one of the deepest metaphysical questions in physics, touching on the relationship between mathematics and physical reality. Is mathematics discovered or invented? Why does mathematics describe reality so well?

- "The Hidden Simplicity of Biology" (2016, with Davies) — Physics approach to biological complexity
- Assembly Theory papers (with Lee Cronin) — Mathematical framework for measuring assembly and identifying life

## Events & Programs

### Beyond Annual Lecture
The premier public event of the year. A scientist or philosopher of international standing is invited to give a reflective account of their work and speculate "beyond" their normal comfort zone. Archive on YouTube: <https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLiDY7y_gGarpN1CD7sy-33mSElG7Eef1H>

Past lecturers include:
- **Sian Proctor** — First Black female spacecraft pilot (SpaceX Inspiration4, 2021); spoke about the "overview effect" from orbit as artist-astronaut

### Thinking Beyond Webinar Series (Monthly)
Updated evolution of the "Ask a Physicist" series. Features all three core Beyond Center physicists — Davies, Walker, and Parikh — addressing big questions in physics with viewer participation. More open format allowing broader topics. YouTube playlist: <https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLiDY7y_gGarpISj1bFeT2ySS_kqsmixFs>

### Ask a Physicist Webinar Series (archived)
The original monthly webinar format with Davies, Walker, and Parikh. Now archived but remains accessible. YouTube playlist: <https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLiDY7y_gGarouyO03s6RRsckSV9MtjyAN>

### Eugene Shoemaker Memorial Lecture (Annual)
An annual special award to a leading scientist honoring the life and work of Eugene Shoemaker — who, with his wife Carolyn Shoemaker, pioneered research in asteroid and comet impacts. Presenters include leading planetary scientists. Past example: Pete Worden (Breakthrough Listen initiative) discussing interstellar exploration and SETI. YouTube playlist: <https://youtube.com/playlist?list=PLiDY7y_gGarp3qiAA029K19nZWqxAOMPS>

## Why Interesting for Amber

### The Intellectual Overlap Is Deep

Beyond Center sits at the exact intersection of Amber's theoretical interests:

1. **Information as fundamental to life** — Davies and Walker's information-theoretic biology asks what it means for something to be "alive." This directly parallels more-than-human design questions: what agencies exist? Where does "agenthood" begin? Their work on the physics of information and life provides theoretical grounding for speculative design about non-human intelligence.

2. **Assembly Theory = physics of novelty** — Walker and Cronin's Assembly Theory is a framework for measuring *combinatorial creativity* in nature — how new things get made from old things. This is fundamentally a theory of emergence. For Amber's work on AI creativity, computational creativity, and artificial life, this is a crucial theoretical resource.

3. **Cancer as evolutionary throwback** — Treating cancer not as a malfunction but as a reversion to ancient life patterns is exactly the kind of counter-intuitive, deep-time, evolutionary reframing that characterizes strong speculative thinking. This kind of "wrong model that's actually more right" thinking is intellectually generative for design.

4. **Time's arrow** — Research on temporal asymmetry and why time flows one way has implications for how we think about computation, evolution, and history. For work on machine behavior and AI evolution, this matters.

5. **Multiverse and fine-tuning** — The Goldilocks problem (why is the universe suited for life?) is connected to deep questions about purpose, design, and anthropocentrism — exactly the questions that motivate more-than-human thinking.

6. **Sara Walker's dual role** — Walker is both at SFI (complexity science) and at Beyond (foundational physics). She bridges the two communities, making her a crucial connection for Amber navigating both spaces.

### The Public Face of Science

The Beyond Center is also notable as a model for *how* science can engage the public. The webinar series (Ask a Physicist, Thinking Beyond), the Annual Lectures, and Paul Davies' many popular books all demonstrate a philosophy that science must engage with culture, not retreat into technical specialization. This matches Amber's own position at the art-science interface.
