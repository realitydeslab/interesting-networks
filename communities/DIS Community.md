The intellectual home of design research that takes seriously the politics, aesthetics, and ethics of interactive systems. DIS is where HCI's most critical voices argue about what design is *for*, who it serves, and whether "interaction" is even the right concept anymore. Annual conference, part of [[ACM SIGCHI]], rotating international locations. ~25% acceptance rate, Tier A venue.

The community is defined by its refusal to separate making from knowing, critique from practice. "More-than-human," "somaesthetic," "decolonial," "posthuman" — these words appear in DIS papers and mean something. Not marketing language. This is where the field's edge lives.

## Mission & Objectives

DIS exists at the intersection of design research, HCI, and critical theory. The full name — Designing Interactive Systems — is increasingly a provocation: the community has been systematically questioning each word.

**What makes DIS distinct from CHI:**
- CHI is a mega-conference (~4,500 attendees, thousands of papers). DIS is intimate (~400–600 attendees, ~100-150 papers accepted).
- CHI favors evaluation studies and incremental contribution. DIS rewards conceptual boldness and design risk-taking.
- CHI accepts critical theory as a minority track. DIS has "Critical Computing and Design Theory" as a first-class subcommittee.
- CHI tolerates practice-based work. DIS invented the Pictorials format (visual-first publication) and Research through Design as a first-class contribution type.
- DIS has explicit workshop on decentering the human, speculative design, feminist HCI. These aren't fringe events — they're central to the program.

**Five contribution areas (also the paper subcommittees):**
1. **Critical Computing and Design Theory** — political, ethical, societal dimensions of computing; epistemologically pluralistic; welcomes decolonial, feminist, queer, postcolonial approaches
2. **Research Through Design (RtD)** — design as inquiry; speculative and critical design; design fiction; more-than-human approaches; soma design; slow design; piratical perspectives
3. **Design Methods and Processes** — advancing practice; participatory design; co-design; generative methods
4. **Artifacts and Systems** — building, evaluating, reflecting on interactive things; tangible/physical/digital; fabrication; AR/VR
5. **AI and Design** — design for AI, with AI, of AI; responsible AI design; implications for practice

**Unique format — Pictorials** (introduced DIS 2014, Vancouver): Visual-first 12-page publication. Images lead the narrative; text annotates. First-class archival publication, same prestige as papers. Perfect for speculative design, process documentation, design-led research. Unique to DIS in the HCI world.

## Key Contributions

DIS has given the field:

1. **Research Through Design as a first-class methodology** — Design as a way of knowing, not just doing. Annotated portfolios, design experiments that generate theory. Jon Zimmerman & Jodi Forlizzi's foundational "Research Through Design as a Method for Interaction Design" first appeared at CHI but became DIS practice.

2. **Pictorials format** — Introduced DIS 2014 (Vancouver). Visual-first peer-reviewed publication. Now copied by other venues. Signal that visual/material research deserves equal standing.

3. **More-than-human design** — Through Wakkary, Andersen, Nicenboim, and others, DIS has become the leading venue for posthumanist, multispecies, nonhuman-actor design research.

4. **Feminist HCI** — Shaowen Bardzell's 2010 paper (technically CHI but the DIS community adopted it fully) established feminist theory as a design methodology, not just critique.

5. **Critical Computing with teeth** — Unlike CHI's ethics track, DIS Critical Computing publishes papers that argue against technological solutionism, for decolonial design, for agonistic and adversarial approaches.

6. **The "why design?" tradition** — DIS regularly asks: what is design for? Who benefits? Who designs? This reflexive questioning is structural, not occasional.

## Sub-Communities within DIS

### 1. Research Through Design (RtD) Circle
The most methodologically ambitious sub-community. Uses design practice as primary research method. Annotated portfolios, design experiments, speculative prototypes as knowledge-carriers. Has its own companion conference ([[RTD Conference]] — biennial). Leaders: Wakkary, Odom, Zimmerman, Forlizzi, Gaver.

### 2. More-Than-Human Design Network
Explicitly posthumanist/multispecies. Design for and with non-humans: animals, materials, environments, data systems. Intersects with [[Design+Posthumanism Network]]. Leaders: Wakkary, Nicenboim, Andersen, Elisa Giaccardi.

### 3. Soma Design Community
Body-centered interaction design. Somaesthetics as design method. Design from first-person bodily experience. Aligns with [[TEI Community]]. Leaders: [[Kia Höök]], Pedro Sanches, Claudia Núñez-Pacheco, Joseph La Delfa.

### 4. Feminist HCI
Making feminist theory actionable in design: pluralism, participation, advocacy, ecology, embodiment, self-disclosure, activism. Leaders: Shaowen Bardzell, Jeffrey Bardzell, Daniela Rosner, Lilly Irani.

### 5. Critical Computing (DIS branch)
Adversarial design, agonistic design, design-as-politics. Examining power structures in technology design. Leaders: Carl DiSalvo, Christine Wolf, Mariam Asad.

### 6. Speculative / Design Fiction
Future-oriented design practice. Not futures as prediction but as provocation. Adjacent to the [[RTD Conference]]. Waks with [[DRS Conference]] speculative track. Leaders: James Auger (now at Royal College of Art), Julian Bleecker, Noam Toran.

## Network Analysis: How DIS Relates to Adjacent Communities

### Overlap with CHI (ACM)
CHI is DIS's parent/sibling conference. Many DIS researchers also publish at CHI. But CHI's sheer size (~4,500 papers/year) means DIS-style critical work gets diluted. DIS people often describe CHI as where you go for tenure points and DIS as where you go for intellectual community. **Significant crossover population; different cultures.**

### Overlap with TEI (Tangible, Embedded, Embodied Interaction)
Strong overlap in soma design, tangible computing, material interaction. [[Kia Höök]] is central to both. Wakkary was on TEI Steering Committee. Both communities value the physicality of computing. TEI is more hardware/making oriented; DIS is more theory/critique oriented. **High overlap in soma design, material computing strands.**

### Overlap with RTD Conference (Research Through Design)
RTD is basically the DIS RtD sub-community's standalone conference. Biennial. Run by the same people. DIS papers that take RtD seriously often also appear in RTD proceedings. RTD is smaller and more intimate. **DIS and RTD are sister communities.**

### Overlap with Aarhus Conference (Critical Computing / PDC)
[[Aarhus Conference]] is DIS's Scandinavian cousin: also critical, also political, also design-oriented. More explicitly rooted in participatory design (Scandinavian PD tradition since 1975). DIS and Aarhus share many researchers (Kim Halskov bridges both). DIS is larger, more internationally distributed; Aarhus is smaller, more politically explicit. **Meaningful overlap especially in Critical Computing track.**

### Overlap with C&C (Creativity & Cognition)
C&C overlaps with DIS in design-as-creativity, computational creativity, generative design. Less overlap in critical theory; more in tools-for-creativity. **Moderate overlap.**

### Overlap with NordiCHI
Nordic HCI conference. Many DIS researchers attend both (especially from Scandinavia). NordiCHI is more broadly HCI; DIS is more specifically design research. **Moderate overlap, especially soma/embodied and critical strands.**

## Why Interesting

DIS is one of the few places in academic computing where "more-than-human" is not a fringe position but the leading edge of the field. Where keynotes are about posthumanism and deep-sea creatures. Where papers argue against capitalism. Where a visual-first pictorial carries the same prestige as a 10,000-word paper.

**The 2026 theme "Beyond Interaction" is a paradigm-shift moment.** The community is explicitly questioning its own foundational concept — "interaction" itself — as potentially too anthropocentric, too dyadic, too limited. This is edge-of-chaos territory for the field. New vocabularies, new methodologies, new politics. Exactly where interesting work happens.

Three things make DIS specifically valuable for Amber:

1. **Research Through Design treats making as knowing** — not as illustration of research, but as research itself. Amber's practice-based work gets first-class citizenship here.

2. **Critical Computing has genuine teeth** — Papers argue against AI solutionism, for decolonial design, for agonistic and adversarial approaches. Not performative. A paper challenging anthropocentric AI would get serious engagement.

3. **Pictorials as format** — A 12-page visual-first publication format, archival quality, same prestige as papers. For speculative design, installation documentation, or design process research, nothing else like it exists in the HCI world.

DIS is where Amber belongs in the academic computing community.
