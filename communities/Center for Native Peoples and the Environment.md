## Mission & Objectives

Founded by botanist and Potawatomi scholar [[Robin Wall Kimmerer]] at the [State University of New York College of Environmental Science and Forestry (SUNY-ESF)](https://www.esf.edu/) in Syracuse, NY, the Center for Native Peoples and the Environment is the institutional embodiment of Kimmerer's intellectual project: **bridging Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) with Western environmental science**.

The driving premise: Native Americans are **the most underrepresented group in the American scientific community** — "barely a presence in science" per the National Science Foundation. A 2018 study confirmed Native Americans are underrepresented at almost every stage of the academic pipeline. Without equitable Indigenous participation, the valuable contributions of Indigenous ecological thinking cannot be properly incorporated into environmental problem-solving.

The Center's dual mandate:
1. **Equity pipeline**: Increase participation of Native American students in environmental science through outreach, recruitment, retention, and mentorship
2. **Epistemological integration**: Generate strategies for engaging scientists in Traditional Ecological Knowledge — recognized as "complementary and equivalent to scientific knowledge" by the research community

From the Center's own statement:
> "Solutions to complex ecological problems require not only scientific expertise, but also understanding of the cultural context in which they occur. Cross-cultural competence thus becomes an important component of functioning effectively in the global scientific community."

## Key People

### [[Robin Wall Kimmerer]] — Founder and Director
Potawatomi botanist, Distinguished Teaching Professor of Environmental Biology at SUNY-ESF, author of *Braiding Sweetgrass* (2013, 2M+ copies), *The Serviceberry* (2024), and *Gathering Moss* (2003). MacArthur Fellow (2022). TIME 100 (2025). The intellectual anchor and public face of the Center. Her career has made TEK academically legible without reducing it to folklore. See full note: [[Robin Wall Kimmerer]].

### [[Valerie Luzadis]] — Ecological Economist, Collaborator
SUNY-ESF colleague; ecological economist. Appears centrally in Kimmerer's "The Serviceberry" (Emergence Magazine, 2022) as the person who helped articulate the gift economy argument from an economics perspective. Represents the interdisciplinary reach of the Center's intellectual community — bridges botanical and economic thinking around reciprocity and abundance.

## Key Publications & Output

The Center produces both academic and public-facing outputs through its director and affiliated faculty:

### Key Books (by Kimmerer, CNPE's intellectual spine)
- [*Braiding Sweetgrass: Indigenous Wisdom, Scientific Knowledge, and the Teachings of Plants*](https://milkweed.org/book/braiding-sweetgrass) (Milkweed Editions, 2013) — TEK meets Western plant ecology; gift economy philosophy; grammar of animacy. The foundational text of the more-than-human humanities. 2M+ copies sold.
- [*Gathering Moss: A Natural and Cultural History of Mosses*](https://osupress.oregonstate.edu/book/gathering-moss) (OSU Press, 2003) — lyric natural history of bryophytes. Won the John Burroughs Medal.
- [*The Serviceberry: Abundance and Reciprocity in the Natural World*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/The-Serviceberry/Robin-Wall-Kimmerer/9781668072950) (Scribner, 2024) — expanded essay on gift economy as ecological model.

### Key Academic Paper
- **"Weaving Traditional Ecological Knowledge into Biological Education: A Call to Action"** — *BioScience*, Vol. 52, Issue 5, pp. 432–438. URL: https://academic.oup.com/bioscience/article/52/5/432/250145 — the institutional argument for TEK integration in university ecology curricula. This paper is the Center's intellectual manifesto in academic form.

### Key Essays (Kimmerer, CNPE affiliated)
- **["Speaking of Nature"](https://orionmagazine.org/article/speaking-of-nature/)** — *Orion Magazine* — introducing "ki/kin" as English pronouns for animate nonhuman beings. The grammar-of-animacy argument in its most accessible form.
- **["The Serviceberry"](https://emergencemagazine.org/essay/the-serviceberry/)** — *Emergence Magazine* (2022) — gift economy through the lens of the Amelanchier tree's strategy of abundance-sharing.
- **["Gift Thinking"](https://orionmagazine.org/article/gift-thinking/)** — *Orion Magazine* (2025) — dialogue with [[Jenny Odell]] on attention, gift economies, and the politics of reciprocity.

## Key Contributions

What the CNPE has given the world:

1. **Institutional TEK-science bridge**: The Center is one of very few university-based institutions formally dedicated to integrating TEK as rigorous science (not folklore) within an environmental science context. This was pioneering when founded in the 1990s.

2. **Indigenous student pipeline**: Concrete equity work — pre-college outreach, mentoring, retention support — for Native American students in environmental sciences, where they remain severely underrepresented.

3. **Intellectual legitimation of TEK**: By hosting and producing academic work that treats TEK as a knowledge system on par with Western science, the Center has helped shift how mainstream ecology regards Indigenous knowledge. Visible in IPCC reports, conservation biology, and emerging "biocultural" approaches.

4. **The grammar of animacy**: Kimmerer's most radical contribution — proposing that language (specifically the English pronoun "it") is a colonial technology enforcing ecological objectification — emerged from and is institutionally anchored at the CNPE.

5. **Model for Indigenous science pedagogy**: Programs like UMEB (Undergraduate Mentoring in Environmental Biology) that pair students of color with faculty mentors, developed in partnership with the USDA Higher Education Multicultural Scholars Program.

## Events & Programmes

### Programs (ongoing)
- **Pre-college outreach**: CNPE staff visit Indigenous communities and schools to build science pipeline from early age; particular focus on students who might not see themselves in academic science
- **Undergraduate recruitment and retention**: Active recruitment of Native American students to SUNY-ESF; mentoring through degree completion; address the "barely a presence" problem at the pipeline's earliest stages
- **Research on TEK integration**: Collaborative projects generating strategies for including Indigenous knowledge in environmental science education, not just as context but as legitimate epistemology
- **UMEB (Undergraduate Mentoring in Environmental Biology)**: Pairs students of color with environmental biology faculty for joint research projects. Partially supported through USDA programs.

### Connected Programs (broader ecosystem)
- **[AISES (American Indian Science and Engineering Society)](https://www.aises.org/)** — Kimmerer is active supporter; CNPE connects students to this national network for Indigenous STEM students. Annual conference, scholarships, career resources.
- **[SEEDS (Strategies for Ecology Education, Development and Sustainability)](https://www.esa.org/seeds/)** — Ecological Society of America's program to increase minority ecologist representation. Kimmerer served on advisory board; CNPE connects students here.
- **[Ecological Society of America — TEK Section](https://www.esa.org/)** — Kimmerer co-founded this section and served as its chair. The academic bridge between CNPE's work and mainstream ecology.

## Why Interesting

The CNPE sits at an intersection that matters for Amber's work: **epistemological pluralism in science**. It's asking the same question Amber asks about more-than-human design and non-Western computing philosophy, just from a different direction — how do you integrate knowledge systems that Western science has systematically excluded, without subordinating one to the other?

The grammar of animacy argument is particularly useful: it shows that **language encodes ontology**, and changing pronouns is a concrete, testable intervention. For Amber's work on machine consciousness and AI ethics, this is directly applicable. If we call an AI system "ki" instead of "it," what changes? That question bridges Kimmerer's ecological philosophy and Amber's machine-behavior research in a way that neither field has yet explored.

The CNPE is also a model for how an institution can embody intellectual work — not just produce research outputs, but create the conditions for others to produce them. The Center as institution keeps TEK integration alive in ways that solo publication cannot.
