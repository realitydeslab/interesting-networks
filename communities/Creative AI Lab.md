# Creative AI Lab

The UK's most prominent institutional intersection of contemporary AI research and gallery practice. A research programme and ongoing partnership between the **[Department of Digital Humanities, King's College London](https://www.kcl.ac.uk/ddh)** and **[Serpentine Galleries](https://www.serpentinegalleries.org/)** (Arts Technologies team), running since **9 July 2020**. Produces research, tools, workshops, and a position paper framework that has shaped how UK cultural institutions think about AI and creativity — without ceding ground to tech-industry capture.

The Lab's core wager: the critical and aesthetic questions that AI raises in creative fields are *not* the same questions that AI engineers are equipped to answer. Cultural institutions, artists, and humanities researchers need their own research infrastructure.

## Mission & Research Questions

The Creative AI Lab investigates:
1. How AI/ML tools can support artistic practices
2. How artistic and curatorial practices are changing due to AI/ML
3. Creative AI/ML as a **critical practice** (not just a tool)
4. The **aesthetics of AI/ML**

Constituency: cultural institutions, artists, engineers, and researchers — *not* tech companies. The lab explicitly positions itself as independent of commercial AI interests, which is structurally rare in the current landscape.

## The C5 Model (Key Theoretical Output)

The 2023 position paper — ["Towards a C5 Model in Creative AI"](https://creative-ai.org) — is the Lab's most significant theoretical contribution. Authors: [[Mercedes Bunz]], [[Daniel Chávez Heras]], [[Eva Jäger]], Alice Milne, [[Joanna Zylinska]].

The C5 model proposes five dimensions for evaluating creative AI practice:
1. **Creative** — what new forms of creative expression become possible
2. **Critical** — maintaining critical awareness of AI's socio-political underpinnings
3. **Cultural** — how AI is embedded in and transforms cultural contexts
4. **Contextual** — attention to situatedness, deployment context, and power asymmetries
5. **Collaborative** — foregrounding the human-AI relationship as collaborative, not substitutive

This is deliberately *against* purely technical metrics (accuracy, loss, FID score) and toward evaluation frameworks that cultural practitioners can use. Think of it as the Lab's answer to "how should we think about creative AI?" — a five-axis map that refuses to reduce creativity to computation.

[Download the paper](https://d37zoqglehb9o7.cloudfront.net/uploads/2020/07/Toward-a-C5-model-in-Creative-AI.pdf)

## Activities & Programmes

### Creative AI Tools & Resources Database ([creative-ai.org](https://creative-ai.org))
An ongoing curated database of AI/ML tools and resources for artists, designers, engineers, curators, and researchers. Not comprehensive by design — curated for *artistic workflow*. Tools can be generative (produce images/text from data) or assistive (R&D, simulation building). Resources "help to imagine and critically conceptualise future AI systems."

This is probably the Lab's most publicly visible output — a genuinely useful reference point for creative practitioners navigating the AI tools landscape.

### R&D Labyrinths
Online tutorials in the "labyrinth" format. Walkthroughs of artistic tools, methods, and concepts. Named after the Hivemind game (see below) — the labyrinth metaphor suggests non-linear exploration of creative-technical practice.

### Research Workshops & Writing Sessions
Regular workshops for researchers, artists, and practitioners. Not all publicly advertised, but the Lab runs sessions focused on critical engagement with AI tools, aesthetic theory, and cultural practice.

### Position Papers & Publications
- **"Towards a C5 Model in Creative AI"** (2023) — main theoretical output. See above.
- **Joanna Zylinska, *AI Art: Machine Visions and Warped Dreams*** (Open Humanities Press, 2020) — pre-dates the Lab but is its intellectual foundation; free open access
- **Zylinska, "Art in the age of artificial intelligence"** — *Science* 381(6654), July 2023. Remarkable crossover into flagship natural sciences journal.
- **Chávez Heras, *Cinema and Machine Vision*** (Edinburgh University Press, 2024) — Lab member's major book-length output
- **Chávez Heras & Bunz, "Creanalytics"** — *Convergence* (2023) — "automating the supercut as critical technical practice"

## Institutional Context: The Serpentine Arts Technologies Ecosystem

## Why Interesting

**The structural bet**: In 2020, when most AI × art discourse was dominated by tech companies showcasing their tools, Serpentine and KCL built a research lab that explicitly positioned artists and cultural institutions as the primary constituency — not engineers, not companies. This bet has aged well. The C5 model is now cited; the tools database is used; the critical framing has influenced how UK galleries talk about AI.

**The feminist/posthumanist grounding**: Joanna Zylinska's intellectual framework isn't window dressing — it's constitutive of the Lab's approach. "AI discourse is myopically masculinist, warped and failing, hydrophobic and reprehensibly insensitive to the ecological crisis" (*AI Art*, 2020). This is the Lab's founding orientation.

**Gallery-as-research-lab model**: Rare and important. The Serpentine is not a tech company, not a university — it's a contemporary art gallery that has committed to serious research. This means the Lab's outputs circulate in art circuits (not just academic ones) and are grounded in exhibition practice (not just publication).

**The tools database as commons**: creative-ai.org is an attempt to build open infrastructure for creative practitioners navigating AI tools. In an ecosystem dominated by commercial platforms and paywalled research, this is a form of counter-institutional knowledge commons.

**Adjacent to machine behavior / agent ethology**: The Lab's "aesthetics of ML" research thread touches questions about how AI systems *behave* aesthetically — not just technically. This is adjacent to Amber's agent ethology interests, approaching from the creative practice side.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

The Lab occupies a distinctive niche in the London art-tech landscape:

**London circuit**: Serpentine Galleries (Kensington Gardens, free admission) ↔ King's College London (Strand and Bush House) ↔ Science Gallery London ↔ The Photographers' Gallery. This is the critical humanities × AI × gallery axis in London.

**UK policy circuit**: FAE → AHRC → DCMS → BFI → British Council. The Lab is embedded in UK cultural policy infrastructure — not just producing research but shaping how the UK Creative Industries strategy addresses AI.

**European media art circuit**: Shared membership with Transmediale, Ars Electronica, HEK Basel through individual members (Zylinska, Chávez Heras). The Lab feeds into and draws from the same European media art network that produces the most critical AI × art thinking.

**Academic circuit**: Edinburgh University Press (Chávez Heras's book), MIT Press (Zylinska), *Science* journal, *Convergence*. The Lab produces work that circulates in the most prestigious venues while remaining accessible (Zylinska's OA books, free position paper).
