# Centre for Attention Studies (King's College London)

**ASK** — Centre for Attention Studies at King's. A radically interdisciplinary research centre working on attention as simultaneously a cognitive phenomenon, a political economy, and a cultural crisis. Tagline from William James: *"My experience is what I agree to attend to."* Directed by [[Joanna Zylinska]] (media philosophy) and co-led by Edmund Sonuga-Barke (developmental psychology/psychiatry/neuroscience). Sits inside KCL's [Digital Futures Institute](https://www.kcl.ac.uk/digital-futures). Part of a global network also anchored at Edinburgh Futures Institute, University of Edinburgh.

**Note (Feb 2026):** Recent events are organized under the name "Centre for the Ecologies of Attention and Perception" — possibly a rebranding or an evolution of the centre's identity. Watch for official announcement.

## Mission & Objectives

ASK was created to bring together what would otherwise be scattered disciplinary silos — cognitive science, media studies, philosophy, psychiatry, economics, and cultural studies — into a unified "Attention Studies" as a new field.

Key framing: **attention as both cognitive resource and political commodity**. The attention economy is not just a metaphor — it's a crisis. The centre aims to move beyond dystopian narratives ("attention warzone") to ask what flourishing attention might look like, and how to design for it.

Three operational ambitions:
1. **Research** — interdisciplinary empirical and philosophical work on attention
2. **Education** — curricula and reading groups
3. **Public impact** — work with policy, industry, creative and charity sectors

The XR strand specifically investigates how extended reality technologies reshape perception and attention — and what this means for design, culture, and ethics.

## Why Interesting

Three reasons this centre matters for Amber:

1. **Attention as critical computing**: ASK treats attention not as a neutral cognitive resource but as a politically contested terrain. This is the same move Amber's work makes — technology is never neutral, it shapes who we become. The "attention economy" framing gives Amber access to a policy discourse that pure HCI research doesn't reach.

2. **XR + perception + phenomenology**: The XR and Attention research group is doing exactly the kind of humanistic XR research that contextualizes Amber's hardware work. They lack hardware expertise; Amber has it.

3. **Intimate scale, London-based**: This is not a mega-conference. It's a small research centre running fortnightly reading groups and intimate symposia. The conversations are real. It's the kind of place where Amber could show up twice and know everyone. London proximity (KCL Strand) makes this very accessible from Oxford.

The centre is also actively expanding (global network, Edinburgh partnership, rebranding toward "ecologies of attention and perception") — it's in its field-declaration phase, which means there's still space to be part of something formative.
