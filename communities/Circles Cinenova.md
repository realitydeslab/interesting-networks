# Circles / Cinenova

The UK's pioneering feminist film and video distribution network. **Circles** was founded in November 1979 by **[[Lis Rhodes]]**, Annabel Nicolson, Felicity Sparrow, and Jo Davis (Joanna Davis), emerging directly from the feminist artists' withdrawal from the Arts Council's *Film as Film* exhibition. Grew from 30 titles (1980 catalogue) to 200+ titles before closing in 1991. Merged with Cinema of Women to form **[Cinenova](https://www.cinenova.org/)**, which continues to operate.

## Mission & Objectives
To distribute and screen women's films on their own terms. A membership organisation where all women whose films Circles distributed automatically became members with policy-making rights. Selection by open Viewing Committee with wide representation from women's groups in production and exhibition.

The founding statement, **"Women and the Formal Film"**, was published in the *Film as Film* Arts Council exhibition catalogue (1979) after feminist artists withdrew their research from the show, citing marginalisation of women's work in experimental film history. Circles then distributed the films by Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, Maya Deren, and Lois Weber that had been planned for the exhibition.

## Key People
- **[[Lis Rhodes]]** — Co-founder. Also distributed her own *Light Reading* (1978) through Circles.
- **Annabel Nicolson** — Co-founder. LFMC performance-film artist.
- **Felicity Sparrow** — Co-founder. Produced the first 1980 catalogue (printed by Only Women Press). Wrote the first touring programme notes.
- **Jo Davis (Joanna Davis)** — Co-founder. Collaborated with Rhodes on *Hang on a Minute* (1983).
- **June Givanni** — Member, advocated for Black women filmmakers; created *Black Women and Invisibility* programme (1987).
- **Pratibha Parmar** — Member, filmmaker.

## Key Publications & Output
- Circles catalogue (1980, printed by Only Women Press) — first edition, ~30 films
- Touring programme: "Her Image Fades as Her Voice Rises" (1980) — films by Joanna Davis, Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, Lis Rhodes (*Light Reading*)
- Final catalogue (1991): 200+ titles spanning 90 years of women's film production

## Key Contributions
- First feminist film and video distribution network in the UK
- Recovered and distributed the work of Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, Maya Deren, Lois Weber at a time when these were barely acknowledged in mainstream film history
- Foregrounded films by women of colour (including *Black Women and Invisibility* programme, 1987)
- The model: feminist infrastructure as political act. Not advocacy — making the thing exist.
- Legacy: Cinenova continues. Full Circles back-catalogue survives including Lis Rhodes's *Hang on a Minute* series.

## Funding & Closures
- 1987: Tower Hamlets council withdrew funding. Resolved by BFI replacement funding.
- 1991: Funding issues led to merger with Cinema of Women → Cinenova.

## Connected Institutions
- **[[London Film-Makers Co-operative (LFMC)]]** — origin network (Circles emerged from LFMC's feminist artists)
- **[[LUX]]** — current distributor for many LFMC/Circles-era works
- **Only Women Press** — printed the first Circles catalogue
- **British Film Institute** — emergency funding 1987

## Why Interesting
Circles/Cinenova is a case study in feminist infrastructure-building that is directly relevant to any conversation about what it means to build institutions for underrepresented practitioners. The founding act (withdrawal + statement → new institution) is a template. The survival question (funding, merger) is also instructive. Cinenova still operates as a resource.
