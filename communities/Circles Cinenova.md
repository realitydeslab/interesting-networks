# Circles / Cinenova

**Cinenova** is the UK's foundational feminist film and video distribution organisation, formed in 1991 from the merger of two pioneering distributors — **Circles** (1979–1991) and **Cinema of Women** (1979–1991). A volunteer-run charity based in London, Cinenova preserves and distributes 500+ titles of work by women, transgender, and gender non-conforming filmmakers from the 1910s to the present. It represents one of the longest-running examples of feminist cultural infrastructure in Britain, with an archive that is irreplaceable as a record of experimental, documentary, activist, and postcolonial filmmaking by women.

The story begins in **1979**, with a founding act that is itself a model of feminist institutional critique: a group of feminist filmmakers withdrew their research from the Arts Council's landmark *Film as Film* exhibition, published a manifesto about gender exclusion in experimental film history, and used that withdrawal as the founding energy for **Circles – Women's Film in Distribution** — the first organisation of its kind in Britain.

## History

### Founding Context: The "Film as Film" Rupture (1979)

In 1979, the **Arts Council of Great Britain** mounted *Film as Film: Formal Experiment in Film 1910–1975* — a major retrospective at the Hayward Gallery, London, intended as the canonical survey of avant-garde cinema. A group of feminist filmmakers had been involved in preparing research for the exhibition, compiling material on women's contributions to experimental film history: **Alice Guy** (who directed the first fiction film in cinema history), **Germaine Dulac**, **Maya Deren**, and **Lois Weber**.

When the curators proceeded with the exhibition without adequately integrating this research — effectively reproducing the marginalisation of women from avant-garde film history that the feminist researchers were documenting — the women withdrew. The group included: **Annabel Nicolson**, **Felicity Sparrow**, **Jane Clarke**, **Jeanette Iljon**, **Lis Rhodes**, **Mary Pat Leece**, **Pat Murphy**, and **Susan Stein**. They published their account and analysis as **"Women and the Formal Film"** — a statement that appeared in the official *Film as Film* exhibition catalogue (ed. Phil Drummond, Arts Council of Great Britain, 1979, p. 118), making their critique permanently visible within the very document it critiqued.

The withdrawal was not a defeat — it was the founding act. The energy channelled from the rupture became **Circles**.

### Circles – Women's Film in Distribution (1979–1991)

**Circles** was founded in November 1979 by [[Lis Rhodes]], [[Annabel Nicolson]], [[Felicity Sparrow]], and **Jo Davis** (Joanna Davis). Additional early members included [[Tina Keane]] and [[June Givanni]].

**Operational structure:**
- Initially ran from Felicity Sparrow's flat
- Women-only screenings and discussions at **Four Corners Film Workshops**, Bethnal Green
- Consensus-based governance: every filmmaker whose work was distributed had a vote
- Deliberately broad definition of distribution media: 16mm film, tape recordings, video tapes, slide/tape, and performance works — whatever format women's work existed in

**First catalogue (1980):** Printed by **Only Women Press**. Approximately 30 titles, including: Susan Stein, Pat Murphy, Tina Keane's *Clapping Songs* (1981), Annabel Nicolson's recordings of *Women and Creativity*. The catalogue was the organisation's manifesto in material form — the claim that these works existed and could be distributed.

**First touring programme (1980): "Her Image Fades as Her Voice Rises"** — curated by Felicity Sparrow. Included: Alice Guy's early films, Germaine Dulac, Joanna Davis, and Lis Rhodes's *Light Reading* (1978). The programme explicitly reclaimed the women whose work had been marginalised in *Film as Film*.

**Key milestones:**
- **1983–85:** Lis Rhodes and Jo Davis make *Hang on a Minute* — 13 one-minute feminist films commissioned by Channel 4 to air between programmes. Films on Greenham Common, domestic violence, uranium extraction, RTZ profits. Fully distributed by Circles.
- **1987:** [[June Givanni]] creates the *Black Women and Invisibility* programme — a touring programme specifically focusing on work by Black women filmmakers, expanding Circles' scope beyond its initial white feminist framing. This became a landmark in the organisation's history.
- **1987 funding crisis:** Tower Hamlets council withdrew funding. The crisis was resolved by emergency BFI funding, but the precarity was a warning of what was to come.
- By the late 1980s: 200+ titles, spanning 90 years of women's filmmaking, in multiple formats
- **Screen article (1987):** "Distribution Matters: Circles" — Caroline Merz and [[Pratibha Parmar]], *Screen* 28(4). The key academic account of Circles' model and significance.
- **1991:** BFI funding cut. Decision made to merge with Cinema of Women rather than close. Circles becomes **Cinenova**.

**Circles' model:** It pioneered the idea of distribution as feminist infrastructure — not just circulating films but constituting the political community around them. Women-only screenings were not merely logistical; they created a space for discussion that mainstream cinema culture structurally prevented. The consensus governance meant that artists' voices shaped the organisation. This was a blueprint for collective feminist cultural infrastructure that has had lasting influence.

### Cinema of Women (1979–1991)

**Cinema of Women** was founded in 1979 — the same year as Circles — with a more explicitly issue-based agenda. Where Circles focused on experimental and avant-garde film, Cinema of Women concentrated on documentary, activist, and narrative productions concerned with women's rights and queer-feminist themes. The two organisations were complementary: together they covered the breadth of feminist film and video production.

By 1991, both organisations were under BFI funding pressure. Their merger was a consolidation of resources, collections, and institutional experience rather than a crisis capitulation — both had been pursuing similar goals through different emphases for twelve years.

### Cinenova (1991–Present)

**Cinenova** — a portmanteau of *Cinema of Women* and a gesture toward feminist cinema generally — launched in 1991 as the successor organisation to both Circles and Cinema of Women. Registered as a charity (No. 1035742, Company No. 1730697), it became the centralised archive and distribution point for the combined collections.

**Collection scope (current):** 500+ titles, including:
- Artists' moving image and experimental film
- Narrative feature films
- Documentary and educational videos
- Rare historical works from the 1910s to the present
- Thematic range: oppositional histories, postcolonial and decolonial struggles, domestic and care work, gender, race, sexuality, reproductive rights, transnational feminist solidarity, ecological politics

**Landmark additions to collection include works by:** Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, Maya Deren, Lois Weber, Lis Rhodes, Annabel Nicolson, Tina Keane, Sandra Lahire, [[June Givanni]] (programme research), Pratibha Parmar, Kim Longinotto, Zoe Leonard, Heiny Srour, Sarah Turner, Sheffield Film Co-op, Sistren Theatre Collective, and hundreds more.

**Archive materials:** Cinenova also holds rare posters, catalogues, and ephemera related to feminist and queer filmmaking practices — including Circles' original distribution catalogues and touring programme materials.

**The Cinenova Working Group (founded 2010):** When Cinenova's professional staff was reduced, a volunteer collective took over operational stewardship. The Working Group has been the organisational engine since 2010, comprising artists, curators, researchers, and archivists:
- **Emma Hedditch** — artist, early member, wrote the LUX Online detailed history of Circles
- **Charlotte Procter** — archivist; Collection & Archive Director at LUX (London)
- **Irene Revell** — Director of Electra (sound/moving image arts organisation)
- **Marina Vishmidt** (1976–2024) — philosopher and art theorist; Goldsmiths, University of London; died April 26, 2024
- **Karolin Meunier** — artist and researcher
- **Sandra Schäfer** — curator and filmmaker
- **Kerstin Schroedinger** — artist and researcher
- **Louise Shelley** — artist
- **Cay Castagnetto** — artist
- Current Coordinator: **Reman Sadani**

**Operations:** All digitised titles can be previewed at LUX, London, or via Cinenova's online previewing service (£30 for a two-week research access). Hire fees range from £50 (1–5 mins) to £300 (over 120 mins). Orders must be placed six weeks in advance.

- **[[Lis Rhodes]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — Filmmaker, poet, teacher. Co-founder. - **[[Felicity Sparrow]]** ⭐⭐ — Circles' practical anchor. Circles ran from her flat. Produced first catalogue (1980). Curated first touring programme "Her Image Fades as Her Voice Rises." Co-signed "Women and the Formal Film."
- **Jo Davis (Joanna Davis)** — Co-founder. Co-directed *Hang on a Minute* series (1983) with Lis Rhodes; her films included in first Circles touring programme alongside Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, *Light Reading*.

Annabel Nicolson, Felicity Sparrow, Jane Clarke, Jeanette Iljon, Lis Rhodes, Mary Pat Leece, Pat Murphy, Susan Stein. All LFMC-connected feminist filmmakers. The statement appears in the *Film as Film* catalogue (ed. Phil Drummond, Arts Council, 1979, p. 118).

### Key Circles Members
- **[[June Givanni]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — Film curator and archivist. Circles member who created the landmark *Black Women and Invisibility* touring programme (1987), the first Circles programme specifically curated around Black women filmmakers. - **[[Pratibha Parmar]]** ⭐⭐ — Filmmaker, writer. Circles member. Co-authored "Distribution Matters: Circles" with Caroline Merz (*Screen* 28(4), 1987) — the key academic account of Circles. Multiple films in Cinenova collection. Known for *Reframing AIDS* (1988), *Warrior Marks* (1993, with Alice Walker), *A Place of Rage* (1991, featuring Angela Davis, June Jordan, Alice Walker).
- **Mary Pat Leece** — American artist, UK-based; co-signed "Women and the Formal Film." Co-researched *Running Light* (1996) with Lis Rhodes during 1985 visit to West Virginia documenting migrant farmworkers.

## Key Contributions

### 1. Feminist Film Distribution as Infrastructure (not Advocacy)
Circles' most important contribution was not arguing that women's film should be distributed — it was *distributing women's film*. The distribution catalogue was the political act. This model — building the missing infrastructure rather than lobbying existing structures — was a distinctive contribution to both feminist politics and independent film culture.

### 2. Consensus Governance / Artist Control
Every filmmaker whose work was distributed had a vote. This meant the organisation was shaped by the artists whose work it held, not by administrators or curators above them. This model of governance preceded and influenced later thinking about cooperative cultural infrastructure.

### 3. Broadening "Feminist Film"
The arc from Circles' founding (experimental film, LFMC-connected white feminist practice) → June Givanni's *Black Women and Invisibility* programme (1987) → Cinenova's expanded mandate (women/transgender/gender non-conforming makers; postcolonial, decolonial, queer content) represents a genuine expansion of what "feminist film" means, produced by internal pressure and political evolution rather than imposed from outside.

### 4. Preservation of an Irreplaceable Archive
Many of the 500+ titles in the Cinenova archive exist nowhere else in accessible form. The ongoing digitisation work (e.g., Sheffield Film Co-op's *A Woman Like You*, digitised by Open University funding; Sandra Lahire's films) is preserving work that would otherwise be inaccessible or lost. This archival function is perhaps Cinenova's most critical current contribution.

### 5. Historical Recovery
The founding act of reclaiming Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, Maya Deren, and Lois Weber from the margins of film history — for Circles' first touring programme — was an early example of feminist historiographic recovery that is now standard practice in film studies.

### 6. Pedagogical Resource
Cinenova functions as a unique pedagogical resource for educators and researchers — films unavailable elsewhere, at hire fees structured to be accessible (£50 for short works). It has been a key resource for film studies, feminist art history, and moving image arts education in the UK for 45+ years.

- **[[London Film-Makers Co-operative (LFMC)]]** ([lux.org.uk](https://lux.org.uk/)) — The origin community. Circles emerged from the feminist tendency within the LFMC. Key shared figures: Lis Rhodes (cinema curator), Annabel Nicolson (cinema programmer), Malcolm Le Grice (co-founder), Peter Gidal (theorist). The LFMC became LUX in 1999.
- **[[LUX]]** ([lux.org.uk](https://lux.org.uk/)) — Current primary distributor/exhibitor for much of the Cinenova archive. Charlotte Procter (Cinenova WG) is Collection & Archive Director at LUX. Digitised Cinenova titles available for preview at LUX.
- **[[Four Corners Film Workshop]]** ([fourcornersfilm.co.uk](https://www.fourcornersfilm.co.uk/)) — The venue for early Circles women-only screenings and discussions (Bethnal Green, 1979–80). Still operating as a creative media charity.
- **Only Women Press** — Feminist press that printed the first Circles catalogue (1980). Also publisher of early feminist theory and fiction in the UK. Key node in 1970s–80s London feminist infrastructure.
- **Cinema of Women** (1979–1991) — The parallel feminist film distributor that merged with Circles to form Cinenova. More overtly documentary/activist focus than Circles' experimental film emphasis.
- **British Film Institute (BFI)** — Key funding source for Circles' latter years. Emergency funding resolved the 1987 crisis; funding withdrawal in 1991 precipitated the Cinenova merger.
- **The Showroom, London** ([theshowroom.org](https://theshowroom.org/)) — Repeated Cinenova exhibition venue from 2011. Partnership for "Now Showing" series (ongoing since 2015).
- **Nottingham Contemporary** ([nottinghamcontemporary.org](https://nottinghamcontemporary.org/)) — Venue for 2019 Circles 40th anniversary event; also hosted *Lis Rhodes: Dissident Lines* exhibition.
- **Women's Film and Television Archive** — UK archive focused on women's contributions to film and TV. Closely related institutional mission to Cinenova.
- **Arsenal – Institut für Film und Videokunst, Berlin** ([arsenal-berlin.de](https://www.arsenal-berlin.de/)) — "The Work We Share" (2021-22) international partner.
- **[[Electra]]** — Organisation led by Irene Revell (Cinenova WG); curates/commissions projects across sound, moving image, performance.
- **June Givanni Pan African Cinema Archive (JGPACA)** ([junegivannifilmarchive.org](https://junegivannifilmarchive.org/)) — Founded by June Givanni, Circles member. Held at MayDay Rooms, London. Parallel feminist archive infrastructure in the Pan-African moving image tradition.

## Why Interesting

### Feminist Infrastructure as Historical Precedent
Circles/Cinenova is the case study in what it means to *build* feminist cultural infrastructure rather than advocate for inclusion in existing structures. The 1979 withdrawal from *Film as Film* → founding of Circles is a perfect sequence: (1) document the exclusion, (2) refuse to legitimise it by participating, (3) build the alternative. This happened within months. The resulting institution lasted 12 years as Circles, then 33+ years as Cinenova. The advocacy approach would have produced a footnote; the institution-building approach produced an archive.

### The Distribution Network as Political Technology
Circles was not a screening programme — it was a **distribution organisation**. The distinction matters enormously. Screenings are events; distribution is infrastructure. By making feminist film *available* rather than just *screening* it, Circles enabled feminist film to circulate through every art college, women's centre, film society, and community hall in Britain. The distribution catalogue was the political instrument.

### Postcolonial and Decolonial Expansion
The trajectory from Circles' founding (experimental/avant-garde, LFMC-connected) → June Givanni's *Black Women and Invisibility* (1987) → Cinenova's current mandate (including postcolonial struggles, decolonial discourse, Sistren Theatre Collective, Caribbean women's movement films) represents a genuine history of how an organisation can expand its political scope without losing its organisational identity. This is rare and worth studying.

### The Volunteer Collective as Durability Model
The Cinenova Working Group (founded 2010) has kept the organisation alive and active without professional staff for 15+ years. This is a model of organisational sustainability through collective commitment that is directly relevant to contemporary discussions of platform governance, cooperative digital infrastructure, and artist-run organisations.

### Connection to Amber's Research Taste
- **Feminist infrastructure:** This is the feminist infrastructure story, properly told. Not theory about feminist institutions — an actual feminist institution built from scratch, sustained through decades of funding precarity, producing a collection that is now an irreplaceable archive.
- **Experimental film + critical politics:** The Circles model explicitly combines formal rigour (LFMC experimental film aesthetics) with political content (feminist, anti-colonial, anti-capitalist) — exactly the synthesis Amber is interested in.
- **Institutional critique through building:** The withdrawal from *Film as Film* is institutional critique that doesn't end in critique — it ends in a distribution catalogue. This is the template for critical practice that produces infrastructure.
- **Archive as activism:** The ongoing digitisation work (preserving work that exists nowhere else) is archival activism — maintaining the conditions of possibility for feminist film culture.
