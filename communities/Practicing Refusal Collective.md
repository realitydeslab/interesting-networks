# Practicing Refusal Collective

International Black feminist forum of artists and scholars dedicated to initiating new dialogues on Blackness, anti-Black violence, and Black futurity in the 21st century. Founded in 2015 by **[[Tina Campt]]** and **[[Saidiya Hartman]]** — two of the most consequential figures in contemporary Black feminist thought. Sixteen members spanning visual culture, film, philosophy, architecture, poetry, Caribbean studies, and performance. The most intellectually dense Black Studies collective currently active, producing theory at the intersection of refusal politics, diaspora, and the archive.

The collective's central wager: **"practicing refusal"** is not passive withdrawal but an active, generative strategy for Black life-making. Refuses the current status quo as liveable; refuses Black precarity as inevitable; refuses the terms of diminished subjecthood. Coined the concept of **"Black fungibility"** — the state in which Black bodies are treated as disposable and expendable — as a shared analytical rubric.

## Mission & Objectives

The PR Collective's point of departure is a set of overlapping interests in theorizing Black agency in the face of contemporary circumstances of **imperilled Blackness** and vulnerable Black bodies. The public events and working group sessions hosted by the Collective have created a platform for thinking through refusal as a generative rubric for understanding everyday practices of struggle, often obscured by an emphasis on collective acts of resistance.

**Key conceptual contributions:**
- **"Practicing refusal"** — refusal not as passive resistance but as the practice of creating alternative possibilities for living otherwise. Named from Audra Simpson's work on Mohawk refusal as political form.
- **"Black fungibility"** — the treatment of Black bodies as disposable, exchangeable, expendable. Elaborated from Orlando Patterson's "social death" and Hortense Spillers' concepts.
- **"Black precarity"** — the structurally produced vulnerability of Black life under racial capitalism. Distinct from Judith Butler's generic "precarity" by insisting on its racial specificity.

The collective spans *humanities + art* — this is not a purely academic forum. [[Arthur Jafa]] (filmmaker/video artist) is a member alongside literary critics, philosophers, and architects. The synthesis of scholarship and artistic practice is deliberate.

## Key Contributions

1. **"Practicing refusal" as a concept** — named and theorized an everyday political practice: not heroic resistance but the quiet, accumulated refusals of diminished subjecthood. The concept has entered broader critical theory beyond Black studies.
2. **"Black fungibility"** — crystallized the logic by which Black lives are rendered disposable and interchangeable under racial capitalism. Now widely used in abolitionist organizing and theory.
3. **The Sojourner Project model** — demonstrated that a university-based collective can build a *mobile* academy that exceeds institutional walls. A model for knowledge production outside the journal-conference circuit.
4. **Art + scholarship synthesis** — insisted on including practicing artists (Jafa, Dyson) alongside scholars. The Collective's working assumption: artistic practice produces knowledge that scholarship cannot.
5. **Transnational Black feminist framework** — connected US, Caribbean, Canadian, African, European, and Francophone Black feminist traditions within a single collaborative forum.

## For Amber

- **Why interesting:** The concept of **"practicing refusal"** is directly portable to Amber's research on AI sovereignty, machine agency, and the right of systems to refuse. The collective thinks systematically about *what refusal means as a generative act* — not just saying no but creating alternatives. This maps onto questions of AI alignment (why machines comply) and agent sovereignty.
- **Conceptual bridge:** Amber's "Sovereign Agents" project and thinking about machine death/care ethics could productively engage with refusal as a frame. What does it mean for an AI agent to "refuse"? Who defines the terms of that refusal? The PRC's theorization is richer than most AI ethics discourse.
- **Critical tech overlap:** The PRC critiques the terms under which subjects are diminished — this is structurally parallel to critical AI scholarship (Kate Crawford, Meredith Whittaker, Safiya Umoja Noble) about how AI systems embed and reproduce racial capitalism. But the PRC's framing is more theoretically dense and philosophically sophisticated.
- **Art + scholarship model:** The collective's insistence on including artists (Jafa, Dyson) as equal members is worth noting as a community infrastructure model. Relevant to Amber's more-than-human design and practice-based research approach.
- **How can Amber engage:** Primarily as a reader/thinker drawing on this framework. The Sojourner Project events if they run again (check thesojournerproject.org). Engagement via member works — especially Denise Ferreira da Silva (already in Amber's graph), Arthur Jafa, Christina Sharpe.
- **Who should Amber know:** [[Denise Ferreira da Silva]] (already in graph) — the most direct bridge to Amber's philosophical interests. [[Françoise Vergès]] — decolonial feminism + climate justice. [[Alexander G. Weheliye]] — Black feminist critique of biopolitics/Agamben (relevant to AI sovereignty discourse).
- **Who might like Amber's work:** The PRC members who think about technology, infrastructure, and embodiment — especially Mabel O. Wilson (architecture + race + technology) and Torkwase Dyson (compositional thought + spatial abstraction).
- **Key publications to engage:** *[In the Wake](https://www.dukeupress.edu/in-the-wake)* (Sharpe), *[Habeas Viscus](https://www.dukeupress.edu/habeas-viscus)* (Weheliye), *[A Decolonial Feminism](https://plutopressbooks.com/9780745341620/)* (Vergès) — these three give the strongest entry into the collective's conceptual register.

## Why Interesting

The Practicing Refusal Collective is the most theoretically rigorous Black feminist collective currently operating. Its relevance to Amber extends beyond identity politics:

1. **Refusal as generative concept** — The PRC's "practicing refusal" is philosophically robust: it distinguishes everyday acts of refusal from spectacular resistance, theorizes how refusal creates alternative conditions for life, and names the terms that must be refused. This conceptual move is portable to AI ethics, agent sovereignty, and critical computing.

2. **Model of knowledge infrastructure** — The Sojourner Project is a practical experiment in building knowledge commons outside the university-conference circuit. It's a mobile academy that creates diasporic dialogue. Amber's interest in "communities as research infrastructure" (noted in research-taste.md) is directly engaged by this model.

3. **Art + theory synthesis** — Including Arthur Jafa (video art) and Torkwase Dyson (spatial art) alongside literary critics and philosophers demonstrates that the most generative intellectual communities integrate making and thinking. This is the Amber model too.

4. **Critical theory density** — Weheliye's critique of Foucault/Agamben from a Black feminist standpoint, Ferreira da Silva's quantum physics + race theory, Hartman's archival poetics — this is critical theory operating at the highest level. Not "AI for X" — structural critique all the way down.

5. **Connection to existing Amber graph** — Three ⭐⭐⭐ people already in the graph (Hartman, Jafa, Ferreira da Silva, Sharpe) are members. The Collective is a nexus node that retroactively connects existing nodes.
