# Digital Democracies Institute (DDI)

An interdisciplinary research institute at Simon Fraser University (SFU) in Burnaby, British Columbia, Canada. Founded and directed by [[Wendy Chun]] under her Canada 150 Research Chair in New Media. DDI works across humanities, social sciences, computer science, and data science to research and create "critical and creative responses to our data-filled world." Their focus: combating misinformation, echo chambers, discriminatory algorithms, and radicalization — by understanding the sociocultural, technical, and political roots of these phenomena, not just patching symptoms.

The institute sits inside SFU's School of Communication, Canada's top-ranked communication program, and draws collaborators from universities in the US, UK, Europe, New Zealand, Australia, and beyond. It is genuinely transdisciplinary — not just interdisciplinary lip service.

## Research Projects

### Current / Active

**1. Beyond Verification: Authenticity and Mis/Disinformation** ([link](https://digitaldemocracies.org/research/beyond-verification/))
The thesis: verification/fact-checking is insufficient because fake news reaches different audiences than corrections. DDI asks instead about *authenticity* — why do people find information true regardless of facticity? Six categories of authenticity developed: self-authentication, emotional intensity, transgression of conventions, culturally authenticating rhetoric, social validation, branding. Published as *Algorithmic Authenticity* (meson press, 2022) — [freely available](https://meson.press/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/978-3-95796-211-9_Algorithmic_Authenticity.pdf). Collaboration with [Goodly Labs](https://www.goodlylabs.org/). Also worked with McGill, U Toronto, and the Canadian Election Misinformation Project monitoring 44th federal election.

**2. Data Fluencies** ([link](https://digitaldemocracies.org/data-fluencies/))
Combining analytical techniques from arts/humanities with data science tools to understand our data-filled world. Four streams:
- Developing Data Fluency Methods and Tools
- Experimental Algorithmic Futures Working Group
- Expanding and Co-Creating Public Knowledge
- Scaling and Mainstreaming Data Fluencies
International partners: York University (CA), Canterbury University (NZ), USC, Emerson College, University of Kentucky, SSRC (USA). Currently in "Data Fluencies 2.0" phase.

**3. BRIDGE Research Consortium** ([link](https://digitaldemocracies.org/research/bridge-research-consortium/))
Cross-institutional consortium. Details on site.

**4. Cyberwar Topologies** ([link](https://digitaldemocracies.org/research/cyberwar-topologies-2/))
Led by Svitlana Matviyenko. Key sub-projects:
- *Technologies of Filtration and Deportation during Russia-Ukraine War* (2023–): documents biometric data collection in Russian "filtration camps" — rendering Ukrainian civilians as data subjects before forced deportation. Publication in *Sociologica* (2022).
- *Technologies of Russian Colonialism* working group (2023–): collective volume in progress (2025) on Russian colonialism and methods of translocal solidarity.
- *Soviet Technological Pasts in East-European Computer Networks* (2023–2026): exploring remnants of failed Soviet OGAS network in post-Soviet republics. Special issue *Internet Histories* (vol.10, issue 1, 2026).
- *War Diary: Dispatches from the Place of Imminence* — Matviyenko's autoethnographic book (Duke UP, Practices Series).
- Forthcoming edited collection: *Post-American Internet* (Matviyenko & Hilstob, eds). Contributors include Nick Dyer-Witheford, Tim Stevens, Anna Engelhardt, Niels ten Oever, GeocinemaU, and others.

**5. Media, Infrastructure, Environment** ([link](https://digitaldemocracies.org/research/media-infrastructure-environment/))
Explores connections between imperialism, colonialism, Black and Indigenous resistance, and how media infrastructures constitute "toxic territories of injustice." Working groups include: Pollution/Elemental Media/Weapon of War (Matviyenko + Rahul Mukherjee); Vernadsky Working Group (Biosphere/Noosphere studies).

**6. Imaginative Methods** ([link](https://digitaldemocracies.org/research/imaginative-methods/))
Led by Gillian Russell and Frédérik Lesage. Develops research practices to "imagine with people" — combining critical design with new materialist epistemologies and practice-based research. Working group (biweekly); workshop series "The Way We See It." Influences: Escobar, Haiven, Keeling, Khasnabish on "radical imagination."

## Why Interesting for Amber

**The homophily-machine-behavior connection is essential.** Chun's work on homophily — the "birds of a feather flock together" algorithm embedded in all social networks — is directly relevant to Amber's work on agent behavior and multi-agent simulation. Homophily as a model predates AI but now runs inside AI recommendation systems. Chun historicizes it (from 1950s urban sociology through network science to today's platforms) in a way that reveals what assumptions are baked into agent behavior by design.

**STS + computing history meets Amber's core themes.** Stephanie Dick at DDI is a must-engage — her work on automated theorem-proving and what AI was *supposed to* do reveals the conceptual gap between AI rhetoric and actual machine behavior. This maps directly onto Amber's machine behavior research and the question of what machines *actually do* vs. what they are claimed to do.

**Cyberwar Topologies is unexpectedly compelling.** Matviyenko's work on Russian filtration camps using biometric data collection — rendering bodies as data subjects — is a chilling instantiation of what happens when agent behavior is weaponized for state control. The concept of "producing the subject of deportation" through data resonates with Amber's thinking about agency, identity, and how systems define personhood.

**Data Fluencies bridges the gap between arts/humanities and data science** — exactly the space Amber inhabits. Their interdisciplinary course development work (Carina Albrecht's dissertation) on critical data pedagogy is the educational infrastructure question Amber cares about.

**The Wendy Chun → Yuk Hui connection is critical.** Both are thinking about technology through a non-Western philosophical lens. Chun moves through race and media; Hui through cosmotechnics. They met in Hong Kong (2022) and their trajectories are increasingly parallel. DDI is one of the few Western institutions where cosmotechnics meets critical media theory in institutional form.
