# Wellcome Trust (& Wellcome Collection)

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Deep research completed. Expanded from stub to full profile. Full history, funding schemes with 2026 deadlines, Wellcome Collection exhibitions, arts-health connection, Discovery Awards details.

The world's fourth-largest charitable foundation (endowment £39.9 billion, 2025), and the UK's largest non-governmental funder of scientific research. Founded in 1936 on the death of pharmaceutical entrepreneur Sir Henry Wellcome, who left his fortune to "improve human and animal health." Today operates two parallel tracks: a major biomedical research funder (£16 billion pledged 2022–2032) and a remarkable free museum + library (Wellcome Collection) that sits at the **intersection of art, medicine, and human experience**. The Collection is one of the most important art-science venues in Europe, just up the road from UCL, in the heart of London.

## Mission & Objectives

**Wellcome's mission:** "Support science to solve the urgent health challenges facing everyone."

**Four strategic programmes:**
1. **Discovery Research** — fundamental science across all disciplines, including humanities and social sciences. Deliberately broad, from cells to societies. £1 billion+ awarded since 2021 across 64 countries.
2. **Mental Health** — anxiety, depression, psychosis interventions; lived experience expertise central.
3. **Climate and Health** — £205 million awarded, research into distributed health impacts of climate change.
4. **Infectious Disease** — pandemic preparedness, global health equity.

**Philosophy:** Long-term, independent, willing to take risks. "If none of our funded projects fail, we're not being brave enough." Funds physical sciences, social sciences, humanities, clinical research — all under Discovery Research.

## History & Founding

**1853:** Sir Henry Solomon Wellcome born in Almond, Wisconsin, frontier log cabin. American-born entrepreneur, childhood interest in medicine and marketing. First product at 16: "invisible ink" (lemon juice).

**1880:** Co-founds [Burroughs Wellcome & Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burroughs_Wellcome_%26_Company) with Silas Burroughs. Introduces medicine in tablet form to England under trademark "Tabloid." Also pioneers direct marketing to doctors with free samples.

**1895:** Burroughs dies. Wellcome takes sole control. Company flourishes; he sets up research laboratories.

**1901:** Marries Gwendoline Syrie Barnardo (daughter of orphanage founder Thomas Barnardo). Marriage unhappy; they separate in 1909. Syrie later marries Somerset Maugham.

**1910:** Becomes British subject.

**1924:** Consolidates all activities into The Wellcome Foundation Ltd.

**1928:** Henry Wellcome amasses one of the world's largest private collections — medical artefacts, instruments, books, art from around the globe — displayed at the Wellcome Historical Medical Museum, Wigmore Street. A driven collector who once described himself as collecting "everything connected with the history of the healing art."

**1932:** Wellcome Building at 183 Euston Road completed (Portland stone). Wellcome knighted.

**1936:** Sir Henry Wellcome dies. In his will, leaves entire share capital of Wellcome Foundation to trustees, charged with spending income to "further human and animal health." The **Wellcome Trust** is established.

**1986:** Trust sells 25% of Wellcome plc to public. Begin of explosive financial growth.

**1995:** Divests all pharmaceutical interests — sells remaining stock to Glaxo plc, forming GlaxoWellcome. Then in 2000, GlaxoWellcome merges with SmithKline Beecham to form **GlaxoSmithKline (GSK)**. Wellcome name disappears from pharma; the Trust becomes pure funder.

**1992:** Funds the [Wellcome Sanger Institute](https://sanger.ac.uk/) at Hinxton, Cambridgeshire — a premier genomics centre that makes the largest single contribution to the Human Genome Project.

**1999–2002:** Runs the **Sci-Art Programme** — grants for collaborations between artists and scientists. This is the program that funded Stelarc's Extra Ear project (with John Hopkins surgeon). A foundational moment in UK art-science history.

**2004:** New administrative HQ — the glass Gibbs Building by Hopkins Architects at 215 Euston Road — opens next to the original Wellcome Building.

**2007 (June):** Original Wellcome Building reopened as [Wellcome Collection](https://wellcomecollection.org/) — free museum and library exploring health, medicine, and human experience. This is the public-facing, art-forward arm of the Trust.

**2015 (October):** £17.5m refurbishment; Wellcome Collection gains new public spaces.

**2019:** Melanie Keen becomes Director of Wellcome Collection. Permanent gallery *Being Human* opens — designed with disabled artists/activists using social model of disability. One of the world's most accessible galleries. Wellcome also opens Berlin office.

**2019:** Launches research culture initiative — explicitly critiques current incentive structures ("prioritising publications over wellbeing").

**2022:** Closes *Medicine Man* gallery permanently after 15 years, acknowledging "racist, sexist and ableist theories and language" in its framing. Sign of ongoing reckoning with colonial collecting history.

**2025:** Endowment £39.9 billion. Chair: Julia Gillard (former Australian PM). CEO: Dr. John-Arne Røttingen. Now 4th wealthiest charitable foundation globally.

## Key Contributions

- **UK art-science funding infrastructure:** The Sci-Art Fellowship programme (late 1990s–early 2000s) established the dominant template for UK art-science collaboration. Stelarc, Oron Catts, and many others funded under this.
- **Human Genome Project:** Wellcome Sanger Institute made largest single contribution.
- **Open access advocacy:** Pioneer of open access in biomedical research; launched Europe PubMed Central, partnered with NIH + Howard Hughes for Open Science Prize.
- **Wellcome Collection as cultural institution:** One of Europe's leading venues for art exploring medicine, health, and body politics. 403,000+ visitors/year. Free admission always. 40+ million digitised images available open access.
- **Research culture reform:** Actively pushing against publication-first culture; funds research into research practices themselves.

## Funding Schemes (For Researchers — Amber's Most Relevant)

Wellcome funds across a deliberately broad scope: "from cells to societies, physical sciences to humanities." Humanities and social science research **explicitly included** in Discovery Research. Arts-science collaboration has a precedent (Sci-Art Fellowship). Three main recurring schemes:

### 🔑 Wellcome Early-Career Awards
**URL:** [wellcome.org/research-funding/schemes/wellcome-early-career-awards](https://wellcome.org/research-funding/schemes/wellcome-early-career-awards)
- **Who:** Early-career researchers — PhD passed, up to 3 years postdoc (can be extended for career breaks, discipline changes)
- **Amount:** Salary + up to £400,000 research expenses
- **Duration:** Usually 5 years
- **Frequency:** Three times a year
- **2026 Deadlines:**
  - 21 July 2026 (decisions January 2027)
  - 10 November 2026 (decisions May 2027)
- **Key:** Must contribute 80% research time. Can change discipline. Humanities/social science explicitly funded.
- **Amber relevance:** **HIGH.** This is the primary pathway for Amber at current career stage. Research connecting computing, health, more-than-human systems, participatory design would be fundable under Discovery Research.

**URL:** [wellcome.org/research-funding/schemes/wellcome-career-development-awards](https://wellcome.org/research-funding/schemes/wellcome-career-development-awards)
- **Who:** Mid-career researchers, "already driving own research," ready to lead substantial programme
- **Amount:** Annual expenditure usually below £250,000 (excl. salary). Salary included if fixed-term contract.
- **Duration:** Usually 8 years
- **Frequency:** Three times a year
- **2026 Deadlines:**

**URL:** [wellcome.org/research-funding/schemes/wellcome-discovery-awards](https://wellcome.org/research-funding/schemes/wellcome-discovery-awards)
