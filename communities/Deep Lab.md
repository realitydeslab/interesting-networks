# Deep Lab

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-28:** Full deep research completed. Expanded all sections with members, events, publications, York Mediale 2018 residency.
- **2026-02-26:** Initial stub created during Kate Crawford deep research.

A congress of cyberfeminist researchers, artists, writers, engineers, and cultural producers. Founded in 2013–2014 by Addie Wagenknecht at the Studio for Creative Inquiry, Carnegie Mellon University. Active 2013–2020. One of the most significant explicitly feminist collectives operating at the intersection of digital rights, art practice, surveillance critique, and security culture. Post-Snowden in origin and urgency.

## Mission & Objectives

Critical assessments of contemporary digital culture, exploiting the potential for creative inquiry "lying dormant in the deep web." Research conducted independently and collaboratively, manifested through lecture series, publications, contemporary art, public programming, and performances. Positioned in lineage of cyberfeminist ancestors: [Cornelia Sollfrank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornelia_Sollfrank), [Netochka Nezvanova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Netochka_Nezvanova_(author)), and projects like [Anna Adamolo](http://www.digicult.it/digimag/issue-041/anna-adamolo-practical-critique-of-ideology/).

Core concerns: privacy, surveillance, anonymity, race, capitalism, code, big data, digital infrastructure, social hacking.

## Key Publications & Output

- **[*Deep Lab* book](https://studioforcreativeinquiry.org/pdf/deep_lab.pdf)** (2014) — 240-page publication produced in a 5-day book sprint at CMU. Free PDF, public domain. Covers privacy, surveillance, anonymity, art, hacking. The founding artifact.
- **[Deep Lab Lecture Series](https://vimeo.com/album/3167394)** (2014) — ten public presentations at CMU, December 8-11, 2014. All on Vimeo. Speakers: Varner, Cranor, Pourkazemi, Lowe, Holmes, Burrington, Burtch, Wagenknecht, Caruso, introduced by Levin.
- **[Deep Lab Documentary](https://vimeo.com/116314844)** (2015) — 18-minute film by Jonathan Minard. Interviews with participants.
- **"Can You Die If You Don't Exist?"** (2018, York Mediale) — 10.5-hour large-scale video work. Deep Lab read every name and non-name of the 34,361 refugees who died attempting to claim asylum in Europe since the early 1990s. Site-specific. Devastatingly political.

## Key Contributions
- One of the first explicitly cyberfeminist collectives to bridge hacker culture, critical theory, surveillance studies, and contemporary art — and to do it rigorously.
- The *Deep Lab* book (240 pages, CC-licensed) remains a key document of post-Snowden feminist digital critique.
- Connected digital rights/security culture to art institutions (NEW INC, MIT Media Lab, York Mediale).
- Mentored new generation of feminist technologists and critics.
- York Mediale commission was arguably their highest-impact single work — transforming refugee mortality statistics into a durational performance.

## For Amber

- **Why interesting:** Deep Lab is the gold standard for feminist-tech collectives that operate across art, activism, and critical computing. The membership roster is extraordinary — Simone Browne, Kate Crawford, Runa Sandvik, Claire L. Evans, Lorrie Cranor, Jillian York. This is *exactly* the kind of interdisciplinary community at the intersection of surveillance, digital rights, art, and critical theory that connects to Amber's interests in machine behavior and critical computing.
- **How can Amber engage:** The collective appears to have ended around 2020, but its members remain active. The key move is to know the individual members, not the collective. Several are at the top of their fields.
- **Who should Amber know?**
  - **Ingrid Burrington** — internet infrastructure as artistic and political subject; her book *Networks of New York* is canonical
  - **Simone Browne** — race and surveillance; *Dark Matters* is essential reading adjacent to machine behavior
  - **Joana Varon** — Brazilian tech-feminism, global south digital rights
  - **Claire L. Evans** — writes on tech history and futures, strong cultural theory + narrative
- **Who might like Amber's work?** Ingrid Burrington (infrastructure/systems thinking), Joana Varon (more-than-human + digital rights), Maral Pourkazemi (data visualization + feminist tech).
- **Key publications to be part of:** The *Dark Matters* / surveillance studies tradition; HyperAllergic; Motherboard; i-D (culture + tech intersection).
- **Opening opportunities:** Individual members publish widely; Lorrie Cranor (now Professor at CMU) could be a connection point; Jillian York (EFF) speaks at digital rights venues.

## Recent Events That Matter

- 2018 York Mediale commission ("Can You Die If You Don't Exist?") — shows the collective's capacity for high-stakes, durational, political performance. This is not just feminist tech hobbyism — it's serious artistic practice addressing refugee mortality.
- Claire L. Evans' *Broad Band* (2018) is the most visible cultural output from a Deep Lab member, reaching mainstream audiences.

## Why Interesting

Deep Lab represents a model that Amber should understand: a *collective structure* that gave feminist technologists and artists institutional legitimacy and the ability to produce ambitious work. It was structured as a temporary congress (not a permanent institution), which allowed it to operate with intensity without the bureaucratic overhead of an org. The 5-day book sprint → 240-page public-domain book is a particular model worth studying — radical productivity in a pressure-cooker format.

The connection to Amber's interests: machine behavior, surveillance, critical computing, and the political dimensions of digital infrastructure all converge here. Simone Browne's *Dark Matters* is essentially a machine behavior study of surveillance technologies and their racial dimensions. Ingrid Burrington's work on internet infrastructure makes the invisible physical layers of computation visible — relevant to Amber's interest in posthuman systems.

The collective is now dormant (2020), but the network it created persists and remains active. The real value for Amber is knowing the people.
