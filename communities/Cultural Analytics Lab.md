# Cultural Analytics Lab

Research lab at the CUNY Graduate Center founded by [[Lev Manovich]]. Pioneered computational analysis and visualization of **massive cultural visual datasets** in the humanities. Originally founded in 2007 as the **Software Studies Initiative** at UCSD; renamed Cultural Analytics Lab when Manovich moved to CUNY (2013, renamed 2016).

## Key People
- **[[Lev Manovich]]** — Founder and Director
- **Jeremy Douglass** — Former post-doctoral scholar; contributed to Manga Style Space, TimeLine, other projects; now at UCSB
- **Jay Chow** — Long-term collaborator; co-creator on Selfiecity, On Broadway, Phototrails, 144 Hours in Kyiv
- **Alise Tifentale** — Collaborator; Selfiecity; photo theory
- **Nadav Hochman** — Collaborator; Instagram city studies, Phototrails
- **Moritz Stefaner** — Data visualization designer; Selfiecity, SelfieSaoPaulo
- **Mehrdad Yazdani** — Collaborator; Selfiecity, 144 Hours in Kyiv

## Topics
- Computational analysis of large cultural visual datasets
- Data visualization as method and art
- Social media image analysis (Instagram, Twitter)
- Art history at scale (Time magazine covers, manga, photography collections)
- Cultural analytics methods

## Major Projects
- **[Selfiecity](http://selfiecity.net/)** (2014) — 3,200 Instagram selfies from 5 cities. **Golden Award, Best Visualization (2014)**. Commissioned by MoMA.
- **[On Broadway](http://www.on-broadway.nyc)** (2015) — Life along 13 miles of Manhattan's Broadway. **Silver Award, Best Visualization (2015)**. Commissioned by NYPL.
- **[Phototrails](http://phototrails.info/)** (2013) — 2.3 million Instagram photos from 13 cities.
- **[Visual Earth](http://visual-earth.net/)** (2017) — 270 million geotagged Twitter images worldwide.
- **[144 Hours in Kyiv](http://www.the-everyday.net/)** (2014) — Instagram during Ukraine revolution.
- **TimeLine** (2009) — All 4,535 covers of Time magazine (1923-2009). With Jeremy Douglass.
- **Manga Style Space** (2010) — 1 million manga pages. With Jeremy Douglass.

## Why Interesting
The Cultural Analytics Lab demonstrates that computational methods applied to cultural archives can produce genuinely revelatory results — aesthetic, cultural, and political. Their methods (large-scale image analysis, custom visualization software) are the quantitative complement to speculative/qualitative practices. For Amber, understanding how culture actually evolves at scale (through their Instagram studies, art history visualizations) provides grounding for speculative proposals.
