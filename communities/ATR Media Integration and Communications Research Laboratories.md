# ATR Media Integration and Communications Research Laboratories

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-27:** Full deep research completed. History, structure, key people, projects, and Amber connections documented.
- **2026-02-26:** Stub created via Christa Sommerer & Laurent Mignonneau deep research.

The Media Integration and Communications Research Laboratories (MIC, also known as MICRL) was a subsidiary R&D institution of [ATR International (Advanced Telecommunications Research Institute International)](https://www.atr.jp/), located in Kansai Science City, Seika-cho, Sorakugun, Kyoto, Japan. Founded in March 1995 as a 7-year project, it was one of the boldest institutional experiments in **art-science fusion** of the 1990s — bringing together engineers, computer scientists, and internationally acclaimed media artists under one roof, funded by Japan's telecommunications R&D ecosystem. Its mission: "Creating new multi-media communications."

## Mission & Objectives

MIC was established to push beyond conventional telecommunications research toward a radical vision of **communication as art and culture**. Its five departments spanned:
1. **Department 1:** Reconstruction and Creation of Communication Environments
2. **Department 2:** Communication Support (AIDE, C-MAP, Iamascope, InvenTcl, Meta-museum)
3. **Department 3:** Mental Image Expression and Transmission
4. **Department 4:** Human Communication Process
5. **Department 5:** Art & Technology Project

The fifth department — Art & Technology — was where artists-in-residence like Sommerer & Mignonneau operated, making MIC unique among Japanese research labs: art was not decoration, it was a research methodology.

MIC was the **7th independent R&D corporation** administered by ATR International. The lab ran until approximately 2002 (its 7-year charter), after which it was reorganized as **ATR Media Information Science Research Labs** (a separate entity, with Sommerer & Mignonneau as part-time artistic directors until 2003). The current ATR no longer has a Media Integration lab; its structure now covers Computational Neuroscience, Wireless Communications, Deep Interaction Science (including the famous Hiroshi Ishiguro Laboratories), and Life Science.

## Key People

### Leadership
- **Ryohei Nakatsu** — President of MIC. Prolific researcher in multimedia, emotion recognition, interactive arts, and Kansei computing. His academic output from this period spans: interactive cinema systems, multimodal emotion recognition, Kansei (感性/sensibility) communications, life-like agent design, and cultural computing. Published widely in IEEE Multimedia, ICASSP, MMCS. Later founded and led interactive media programs at multiple institutions. With 297 papers spanning 1976-2025, Nakatsu's MIC tenure (1995-2002) was his most generative and interdisciplinary phase.

### Artists-in-Residence (Art & Technology Department)
- **[Christa Sommerer](https://www.interface.ufg.ac.at/christa-laurent/)** — Artistic Director & Researcher, 1995–2001 (part-time until 2003). Austrian artist known for biologically-inspired interactive art.
- **[Laurent Mignonneau](https://www.interface.ufg.ac.at/christa-laurent/)** — Artistic Director & Researcher, 1995–2001 (part-time until 2003). French artist, Sommerer's collaborator.

Both came to ATR from NTT-ICC residencies (1994-95). Both simultaneously held positions at [[IAMAS — Institute of Advanced Media Arts and Sciences]] in Gifu (1997-99 as Associate Professors). Their works created at ATR became canonical new media art.

## Key Projects & Output

### Engineering/Research Projects
- **AIDE** — AI-assisted discussion environment for group thinking support
- **C-MAP** — Context-aware Mobile Assistant Project (early mobile contextual computing)
- **Iamascope** — Interactive Kaleidoscope (interactive installation)
- **InvenTcl** — Inventor + Tcl/tk integration for interactive prototyping
- **Meta-Museum** — Virtual/augmented reality museum environment (presented at VSMM'96 in Gifu; one of the first AR museum systems)
- **Life-like communication agent "MIC" and feeling session character "MUSE"** — early affective computing agents with emotion-sensing and expression
- **Interactive Movie System** — multi-person participatory narrative cinema (Nakatsu's recurring project throughout the MIC era)
- **Virtual Kabuki Theater** — human metamorphosis system using body tracking and virtual costume projection (shown at RO-MAN'96)
- **Robovie** — humanoid everyday robot (later developed with other ATR labs, by 2002)

## For Amber

**Why interesting:** MIC is a historical case study in *institutional art-science fusion at scale*, funded by telecommunications industry R&D budgets. Japan created a unique ecosystem in the 1990s where artists could do computational research for 6-7 years with full institutional support — producing work that became canonical (Sommerer/Mignonneau), theorized (Nakatsu's Kansei computing), and influential (ALife as medium). Understanding this model helps Amber think about *what institutional conditions enable the best art-science work* and how to build or find such conditions today.

**Connections to Amber's research:**
- **ALife as creative medium:** MIC's Art & Technology department pioneered using artificial life as artistic/communicative medium — directly relevant to Amber's ALife 2026 interests
- **Embodied communication:** MIC's focus on non-verbal, affective, body-based communication (Kansei, emotion recognition, multi-modal HCI) prefigures Amber's somaesthetic design research
- **Human-machine symbiosis:** Nakatsu's Kansei computing and the interactive agents (MIC/MUSE characters) are early thinking about human-machine communication beyond text — relevant to Amber's cyborg sociology
- **Proto-MR:** Meta-Museum (1996) and Virtual Kabuki Theater (1996) are early AR works — historical precedent for HoloKit-type thinking
- **Japanese research ecosystem:** MIC was part of a network including [[IAMAS — Institute of Advanced Media Arts and Sciences]] and [[NTT InterCommunication Center (ICC)]] — institutions Amber should understand when thinking about Tokyo/Kyoto collaboration opportunities
- **Institution-building model:** ATR funded 7-year themed labs with clear sunset clauses — a radical model of focused research sprint, not perpetual lab building

**How can Amber engage:**
- ATR itself no longer has a media arts program, but the current Hiroshi Ishiguro Laboratories and Interaction Science Laboratories carry forward the human-robot / human-communication lineage
- The intellectual legacy lives at IAMAS (still active), where Sommerer and Mignonneau taught during the MIC years
- Historical study: the MIC era (1995-2002) is underresearched academically and worth citing/referencing in papers about institutional art-science history

**Who should Amber know:**
- **Ryohei Nakatsu** — still active (2025 papers!), now working on immersive art spaces, art-fashion, and physiological evaluation of art. Potential to engage via art-science conferences
- **Christa Sommerer** — now Professor at Interface Cultures, University of Art and Design Linz; internationally active; intersects with ALife community
- **Laurent Mignonneau** — same institution as Sommerer; co-leads the Interface Cultures program

## Connected Institutions

- [[IAMAS — Institute of Advanced Media Arts and Sciences]] — Sommerer and Mignonneau were Associate Professors there (1997-99, 2001-04) while at ATR; IAMAS and ATR MIC were overlapping ecosystems
- [[NTT InterCommunication Center (ICC)]] — Sommerer & Mignonneau came from NTT-ICC before ATR; these three institutions formed Japan's 1990s art-tech triad
- [ATR International (current)](https://www.atr.jp/) — parent institution; now focused on robotics (Ishiguro), neuroscience, and wireless communications; no media arts program
- [Hiroshi Ishiguro Laboratories at ATR](https://www.atr.jp/) — carries forward the human-robot interaction lineage
- [Interface Cultures, University of Art and Design Linz](https://www.ufg.at/Interface-Cultures.200.0.html) — where Sommerer and Mignonneau are now professors (since 2004)

## Why Interesting

MIC represents a *historical peak* of art-science institutional fusion that has not been replicated at scale since. Japan's model — where telecom industry R&D money funded artists doing 6-year residencies alongside scientists — produced work (ALife art, affective agents, interactive cinema, AR museums) that was years ahead of its time. For Amber, MIC is a case study in: **what conditions enable the most ambitious art-science work?** It answers: institutional commitment (not individual grants), long time horizons (7 years, not 1-year residencies), co-location of artists and engineers, and a mission that treats art as methodology, not decoration.

The specific lineage — Japanese telecommunications research → art-technology → embodied interaction → human-robot communication → Kansei computing — is a direct ancestor of the territory Amber works in (somaesthetic design, cyborg sociology, more-than-human design). Knowing this history makes Amber's work legible in a longer arc.
