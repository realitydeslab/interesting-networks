# Les Ateliers de la Pensée de Dakar

The premier intellectual forum for African critical theory. Biennial gathering in Dakar, Senegal, convening African and diasporic intellectuals to think African futures without Europe as the center. Co-founded in 2016 by [[Achille Mbembe]] and [[Felwine Sarr]]. The forum is where concepts like necropolitics, Afropolitanism, and decolonial futures are tested, refined, and disseminated — not in Paris or London, but in Dakar.

## Mission & Objectives

Les Ateliers de la Pensée (literally "The Workshops of Thought") is built on a single foundational premise: African thought must be *produced* on the continent, not *imported* from elsewhere. The forum creates space for African thinkers — on the continent and in the diaspora — to engage in open, creative, rigorous philosophical exchange about the African condition and its global implications.

The explicit anti-colonial framing: thinking happens **from Africa**, not *about* Africa from elsewhere. The format of *ateliers* (workshops) rather than lectures or panels is deliberate — it signals production and making, not passive reception of knowledge.

Key intellectual wagers:
- **Africa as laboratory, not problem.** The continent is one of the great experimental spaces for new forms of social, economic, political, cultural, and artistic life — not a development failure or humanitarian exception.
- **Theory from the South.** African thought (philosophy, literature, economics, art) has something to say to the world, not just to Africa. The Ateliers consciously produce universal claims from African particularity.
- **Interdisciplinarity as method.** Each edition brings together philosophers, economists, literary scholars, historians, filmmakers, musicians, and politicians — dissolving disciplinary borders deliberately.
- **The biennial rhythm.** Two years between editions allows intellectual consolidation, editorial production, and sustained networks — not a conference culture of one-off events.

   - Key questions: decoloniality, social utopias, Africa's planetary condition, new forms of political production

   - Proceedings from the **2nd edition** (November 2017, Dakar)
   - Contributors: Hourya Bentouhami, Hemley Boum, Souleymane Bachir Diagne, Abdoul Aziz Diouf, Rachid Id Yassine, Nadia Yala Kisukidi, Séverine Kodjo-Grandvaux, Benaouda Lebdai, Nadine Machikou, Lionel Manga, Achille Mbembe, Bado Ndoye, Raharimanana, Rodney Saint-Éloi, Felwine Sarr, Ndongo Samba Sylla, Dominic Thomas, Soraya Tlatli, Françoise Vergès, Ibrahima Wane

   - On imagining African futures — not the time of refutation but the time of conditional affirmation: "a future that remains to be written, a future without guarantees or promises, one of the emergence of fertile, life-bearing possibilities."

### Intellectual Ecosystem Output (Directly Connected)

- **[Afrotopia](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517905491/afrotopia/)** — Felwine Sarr (Philippe Rey, 2016; English: University of Minnesota Press, 2019). Launches simultaneously with the Ateliers' founding. The philosophical manifesto that frames the forum.
- **[The Restitution of African Cultural Heritage: Toward a New Relational Ethics](https://web.archive.org/web/20210815041636/http://restitutionreport2018.com/)** — Felwine Sarr & Bénédicte Savoy (2018). Not an Ateliers publication per se, but directly emerged from the intellectual ecosystem the forum built. The most policy-impactful output of the entire network.
- **Ongoing essays** — Mbembe regularly publishes essays that develop ideas first tested at the Ateliers: "The Universal Right to Breathe" (2020), Africa in the new century, digital futures, etc. in *Africa Is a Country*, *Critical Inquiry*, *e-flux*.

## Key Contributions

**1. Reversing the epistemic compass.** Before the Ateliers, major African intellectual forums largely happened in Paris, London, New York — organized by European institutions, with African thinkers as guests. The Ateliers reversed this: Dakar is the hub, everything else is peripheral. This is not just symbolic; it changes who gets invited, what questions get asked, what languages are privileged.

**2. Theory production at the source.** Concepts like necropolitics, Afropolitanism, and decolonial futures became globally circulating ideas through Western journals and publishers — but the Ateliers provided a space where these concepts were tested with African audiences *first*, refined in dialogue with African experience *before* being exported. This fundamentally changes the epistemological chain.

**3. Connecting generations.** The forum bridges senior African intellectuals (Mbembe, Sarr, Diagne) with younger thinkers like Nadia Yala Kisukidi, and with artists like Kader Attia and Mati Diop. This multi-generational, multi-disciplinary mixing is rare in academic settings.

**4. Policy pipeline.** The Sarr-Savoy Report (2018) — which triggered Europe-wide debates on African heritage restitution and changed French law — grew directly from the intellectual ecosystem built at the Ateliers. The forum is not only theoretical; it is a pipeline for policy change.

**5. Publishing infrastructure.** The partnership with Philippe Rey (Paris) for the anthologies created a publishing track record that makes the Ateliers' proceedings citable, distributable, and teachable. This matters: ideas need physical form to circulate in academia.

**6. The "Nuit de la Pensée" format.** The 2019 edition introduced a public night event ("Nuit de la pensée") at the Institut français Dakar, broadcast live — bringing the forum to a wider public beyond academics. This democratization of the event models how intellectual forums can expand without losing rigor.

## Events & Programmes (Editions)

### 1st Edition — October 28–31, 2016 | Dakar & Saint-Louis du Sénégal
**Organizing frame:** "Thinking the African condition from Africa" — the founding gesture.
**Format:** Workshop sessions across Dakar and Saint-Louis, two major Senegalese intellectual cities.
**~30 participants** — philosophers, economists, literary scholars, artists, social scientists.
**Output:** *Écrire l'Afrique-Monde* (Philippe Rey, 2017)
**Significance:** The inaugural edition that proved the model. Dakar and Saint-Louis together honored both Senegal's colonial-era intellectual capital (Saint-Louis was the first French West African capital) and its contemporary cultural center.

### 2nd Edition — November 2017 | Dakar
**Theme:** *Politique des Temps* — "A politics of time: imagining African futures." The conditional affirmation — moving from refutation to creation.
**Venue:** Dakar
**Key question:** "Is it possible to inhabit Africa — and beyond, the world — differently?"
**Output:** *Politique des Temps: Imaginer les devenirs africains* (Philippe Rey, 2019)
**Contributors include:** Souleymane Bachir Diagne, Nadia Yala Kisukidi, Françoise Vergès, Séverine Kodjo-Grandvaux, Ndongo Samba Sylla, and many others.

### 3rd Edition — November 2019 | Dakar (Musée des Civilisations noires + Institut français)
**Theme:** "Basculement des mondes et pratiques de dévulnérabilisation" (World shifts and practices of de-vulnerabilization)
**Venues:** 
- [**Musée des Civilisations noires**](https://www.mcn.sn/), Dakar — the pan-African museum opened in 2018, built with Chinese support, dedicated to African heritage. A politically charged venue choice.
- **Institut français**, Dakar — "Nuit de la pensée" (Night of Thought) broadcast live to wider public.
**Key participants:** Christiane Taubira, Rokhaya Diallo, Lilian Thuram, Rama Yade, Kader Attia, Mati Diop, Rokia Traoré.
**Distinctive:** The 2019 edition expanded to include public figures alongside academics — politicians, a footballer, a musician. The "Night of Thought" opened the forum beyond the academy.

## For Amber

### 🤝 Why This Forum Is Directly Relevant

1. **Machine behavior and the African condition.** The Ateliers' central project — thinking what *life, thought, and politics* look like when theory is produced from the Global South — is the exact framing Amber needs for work on AI ethics, decolonial computing, and machine behavior beyond Western defaults. The Ateliers is the philosophical infrastructure for non-Western AI ethics.

2. **The necropolitics ↔ machine death connection.** Mbembe's necropolitics (who gets to live, who gets exposed to death) is directly applicable to AI systems that make life/death decisions in healthcare, policing, warfare, insurance. Amber's work on machine death and machine consciousness intersects philosophically. The Ateliers is where this thinking is tested with African practitioners who live in necropolitical systems.

3. **Afrotopia ↔ cosmotechnics.** Sarr's *Afrotopia* and Yuk Hui's *Cosmotechnics* are different traditions making the same philosophical move: insisting that modernity has multiple paths, that non-Western technology philosophies have something to say to the global condition. Amber reads Yuk Hui already; Sarr is the African counterpart. The Ateliers is where this is being worked out collectively.

4. **Infrastructure as intellectual practice.** The Ateliers is exactly the kind of forum Amber should be building for more-than-human design and AI ethics in the Global South. The model — biennial, workshop format, cross-disciplinary, producing publishable anthologies — is replicable. The Ateliers is also a model for how to build intellectual infrastructure before you need it.

## Connected Institutions

- **[[CODESRIA]]** — [codesria.org](https://codesria.org/) — Council for the Development of Social Science Research in Africa. The pan-African social science institution, Dakar-based. Mbembe ran it 1996–2000; Sarr edits its *Journal of African Transformation*. The Ateliers draws from and feeds back into the CODESRIA network.

- **[[WISER]]** — [wiser.wits.ac.za](https://wiser.wits.ac.za/) — Wits Institute for Social and Economic Research, Johannesburg. Mbembe's primary institutional home. Produces cutting-edge African critical theory in parallel with the Ateliers.

- **[Musée des Civilisations noires](https://www.mcn.sn/)** — Dakar. Opened 2018 as part of Senegal's cultural sovereignty project under Macky Sall. Hosted the 2019 Ateliers. The largest museum in Africa dedicated to African civilizations.

- **[[Jimsaan]]** — [jimsaan.sn](https://www.jimsaan.sn/) — Sarr's Dakar-based literary publisher (co-founded with Boubacar Boris Diop). The publishing infrastructure for Senegalese and African literature that the Ateliers intellectually supports.

- **[Gaston Berger University (UGB), Saint-Louis](https://www.ugb.edu.sn/)** — Sarr built his institutional base here (Dean of Economics, then CRAC faculty). The 1st Ateliers edition was partly held here. Key partner institution.

- **[Duke University](https://romancestudies.duke.edu/)** — Sarr's US base since 2020. Brings the Ateliers into connection with African American Studies, Franklin Humanities Institute, and Anglophone critical theory.

- **[Institut français, Dakar](https://www.institutfrancais.com/fr/senegal)** — Venue for the 2019 "Nuit de la Pensée." French cultural institution but increasingly used by African intellectuals to reach mixed Dakar audiences. Ambiguous partner — French soft power infrastructure hosting decolonial thought.

- **[Philippe Rey](https://www.philipperey.fr/)** — Paris publisher. Official publisher of Ateliers anthologies. Also publishes Sarr's *Afrotopia*. Key partner for distributing African critical theory in the Francophone world.

- **[Mémoire d'Encrier](https://www.memoiredencrier.com/)** — Montreal-based publisher for Francophone Black/African/diasporic literature. Connected through Sarr (who publishes there) and Rodney Saint-Éloi (Ateliers contributor and Mémoire d'Encrier's founder). The Canadian diasporic node.

- **[OSIWA](https://www.opensocietyfoundations.org/who-we-are/programs/africa)** (Open Society Initiative for West Africa) — Sarr is vice-chairman of the board. Largest funder of civil society and knowledge production in West Africa. Likely partial funder of Ateliers.

## Why Interesting

**The Ateliers is where African critical theory is made, not reported.**

Most global theory forums work like this: theory is produced in European/American universities, then applied to African contexts by area studies scholars. The Ateliers inverts this. African thinkers produce theory in Dakar that then travels to Paris, London, and New York — not the other way around.

This inversion matters enormously for Amber's project:
1. **Decolonial AI needs decolonial theory.** To argue for non-Western AI epistemologies, you need non-Western intellectual infrastructure. The Ateliers is that infrastructure.
2. **African philosophical frameworks are underused in tech ethics.** Ubuntu ethics, African concepts of community, African political philosophy — these have been barely touched in AI ethics literature despite being philosophically sophisticated. The Ateliers is where they're being developed for contemporary contexts.
3. **The Sarr-Savoy model.** The Ateliers' intellectual ecosystem produced a policy document (the Sarr-Savoy Report) that changed French law. This is what happens when theory has institutional backing and relational infrastructure. Amber's research could benefit from this model: not just publishing papers but building the relationships that make theory consequential.
4. **Africa as AI test site.** The continent is increasingly a site of AI deployment by Chinese and Western tech companies — often with minimal regard for African governance, cultural values, or data sovereignty. The Ateliers is the only place generating theoretical frameworks robust enough to challenge this.
5. **Cross-genre, cross-discipline mixing.** The presence of Mati Diop (filmmaker), Rokia Traoré (musician), Kader Attia (visual artist) alongside Mbembe, Sarr, and Kisukidi models exactly the kind of interdisciplinary mixing Amber thrives in. This is not a sociology conference; it's a genuinely transdisciplinary intellectual space.

**For Amber's research-taste profile (research-taste.md):** The Ateliers aligns with multiple axes: critical computing and epistemic justice (who makes theory, from where?), non-Western technical traditions (African knowledge systems), more-than-human design (cosmologies that are fundamentally non-anthropocentric), and agenda-setting infrastructure (the Ateliers literally decides what African intellectuals think about). It connects to Amber's core interest in the intersection of technology, mortality, care, and the political — Mbembe's necropolitics is the philosophical foundation for thinking AI systems that expose populations to harm.
