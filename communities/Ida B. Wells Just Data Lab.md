# Ida B. Wells Just Data Lab

Critical data research lab housed in Princeton University's **Department of African American Studies**, founded in 2018 by [[Ruha Benjamin]]. Named after Ida B. Wells-Barnett — the 19th-century investigative journalist who weaponized data to document and fight lynching in America. The lab brings together students, educators, activists, and artists to "rethink and retool data for justice." Runs an **Artist-in-Residence** program, **Tech Freedom School**, and mentors undergraduate student researchers across multiple cohorts per year.

The naming is strategic and political: invoking Ida B. Wells historicizes the project (data journalism as investigative justice tool goes back to the 1890s), gives it moral grounding well beyond "tech ethics," and asserts a lineage of Black intellectual leadership that predates Silicon Valley by over a century.

## Mission & Objectives

From the About page: *"The IDA B. WELLS Just Data Lab brings together students, educators, activists, and artists to develop a critical and creative approach to data conception, production, and circulation. Our aim is to rethink and retool the relationship between stories and statistics, power and technology, data and justice."*

Quote from Ida B. Wells-Barnett displayed on the site: *"The way to right wrongs is to turn the light of truth upon them."*

The lab is distinctly **activist-academic**: it produces research, yes, but also zines, data visualizations, community tools, artist installations, and policy-adjacent outputs. This is not just a research lab — it's an organizing infrastructure.

**Institutional home:** Princeton Department of African American Studies + Center for Digital Humanities + Lewis Center for the Arts. Major sponsors include Princeton's Office of Information Technology, Center for Digital Humanities, Lewis Center for the Arts, Department of African American Studies. Additional support from: Anthropology + VizE Lab, Carl Fields Center, Center for Health & Wellbeing, History, John H. Pace Jr. '39 Center for Civic Engagement, Politics, ProCES, School for Public & International Affairs, Sociology, Spanish and Portuguese Studies, and University Center for Human Values.

## Key Contributions

1. **Artist-in-Residence model** — demonstrated that artists can be full members of academic research infrastructure, not just outreach ornaments. Mimi Onuoha and Chanika Svetvilas produced serious work under this model.

2. **"New Jim Code" as framework** — via Ruha Benjamin's books, the JDL is the institutional home of the "New Jim Code" concept — now one of the most-cited framings in critical AI globally.

3. **Tech Freedom School** — summer pipeline program for Black college students working on AI bias; profiled nationally. Building the next generation of critical tech scholars from underrepresented backgrounds.

4. **Liberatory Technology / Digital Marronage** — the conceptual contributions of student researchers Payton Croskey and Kenia Hale have circulated internationally (UNDP AI conference, 4S/ESOCITE, Boston Review).

5. **Modeling what "data justice" looks like institutionally** — the JDL is proof-of-concept that academic departments can do activist research with real-world impact while maintaining scholarly rigor.

## Why Interesting

The JDL sits at a rare intersection: **academic rigor + activist purpose + art as method**. Most critical computing labs are either purely academic (papers, conferences) or purely activist (advocacy, policy). The JDL uses art residencies as a way to generate knowledge that wouldn't emerge from either mode alone.

For Amber specifically:
1. **Art-as-research model** — closest analog in the knowledge graph to what Amber wants to build. The Artist-in-Residence model treats artists as co-researchers, not illustrators.
2. **Data justice meets machine behavior** — Amber's work on machine behavior (ALIFE, agent ethology) mostly lacks the critical/justice framing. The JDL provides the vocabulary (New Jim Code, digital marronage, abolitionist technology) that would make Amber's work legible to social scientists and critical computing scholars.
3. **Princeton connects** — Amber's connections at Princeton (Wendi Yan is a Princeton AAS alum) provide potential introduction pathways.
4. **The naming strategy** — invoking Ida B. Wells isn't nostalgia; it's political positioning. Amber could learn from this: naming a lab or project after a historical figure who used the relevant methods for justice gives it immediate moral weight.
