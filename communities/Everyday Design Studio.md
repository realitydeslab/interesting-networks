# Everyday Design Studio

Design research studio at Simon Fraser University's [School of Interactive Arts and Technology (SIAT)](https://www.sfu.ca/siat.html). Founded by [[Ron Wakkary]], co-directed with [[William Odom]]. The institutional home of **more-than-human design** as a research field — producing the landmark book *Things We Could Design* (MIT Press, 2021), 100+ peer-reviewed publications, 16+ best paper awards, and the next generation of more-than-human design researchers. If you want to understand where more-than-human HCI comes from and where it's going, this is the lab.

## Mission & Objectives
EDS investigates the changing nature of interaction design in response to:
- The increasing role technology plays in mediating everyday practices
- New understandings of human-technology relations
- **Multispecies worlds** and posthumanism — designing WITH non-humans, not just FOR humans

The studio's approach is explicitly **reflective and generative**: researchers design new computational things and systems to ask critical questions, then study how those things interact with the world. Research through design is the core methodology.

From Ron Wakkary's framing: *"Often, design research draws on design's capacity to solve problems. However, using design to create problems, rather than solutions, may be the stranger but wiser choice."*

## Key Publications & Output

### Landmark Book
- [*Things We Could Design: For More Than Human-Centered Worlds*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262542876/things-we-could-design/) (Ron Wakkary, MIT Press, 2021) — The foundational text of the more-than-human design field. Argues that design must move beyond human-centered frameworks. Grounded in postphenomenology and philosophy of technology. **Winner of the Kyoto Design Award 2021** for design theory. Essential reading.

### Recent Key Publications (2023–2025)
- [Backyard Practices: A Liminal Approach to Designing in More-than-Human Worlds](https://doi.org/10.1145/3706598.3713291) (CHI 2025) — Wakkary, Oogjes, Tomico, Sakib, Kökel. Liminal approaches to more-than-human design through backyard ecologies.
- [Constituency as a Matter of Practice: Moving a Plant Studio](https://doi.org/10.1145/3706598.3713916) (CHI 2025) — Tomico, Oogjes, Wakkary. Multispecies design practice with plants. Literally designing with plants as co-constituents.
- [The Future of More-Than-Human Design: A Computing Practice in Crisis?](https://doi.org/10.1145/3737609.3747092) (Aarhus 2025) — Wakkary + 18 co-authors. Major 19-author coalition paper interrogating whether more-than-human design is in crisis. A territorial claim for the field.
- [Translating HCI Research to Broader Audiences](https://doi.org/10.1145/3706598.3713884) (CHI 2025) — Wakkary, MinYoung Yoo, Ppali, Odom, Zhuang et al. How HCI research reaches beyond academic audiences.
- [Unmaking and HCI](https://doi.org/10.1145/3689047) (ACM ToCHI 2024) — Wakkary, Katherine W. Song, Samar Sabie, Jackson, Lindström, Paulos, Ståhl. "Unmaking" as counter-practice to maker culture — designing for biodegradation, decay, disassembly.
- [Weaving Stories: Toward Repertoires for Designing Things](https://doi.org/10.1145/3491102.3517578) (CHI 2022) — Wakkary, Oogjes. **Best Paper Award.** 90+ citations. Influential framework for how designed things accumulate meaning through use.
- [Turner Boxes and Bees: From Ambivalence to Diffraction](https://doi.org/10.1145/3563657.3596020) (DIS 2023) — Wakkary, Oogjes, Sakib, Behzad. More-than-human design through insect observation and materiality.
- [Compost Kits: Bridging More-than-Human Theory with Design Practice through Vermicomposting](https://doi.org/10.1145/3757980.3757990) (MindTrek 2025) — Wakkary, Tiffany Wun. Literal compost as a design material.

### Earlier Landmark Papers
- [Morse Things: A Design Inquiry into the Gap Between Things and Objects](https://doi.org/10.1145/3064663.3064734) (CHI 2017) — Wakkary, Odom, Oogjes et al. Design exemplar: computational things that develop their own character through minimal, inscrutable communication. Classic EDS approach.
- [Designing for an Other Home](https://doi.org/10.1145/3196709.3196810) (DIS 2018) — Wakkary, Oogjes, Fung. **Best Paper Award.** Speculative domestic design for non-normative living arrangements.
- [From Research Prototype to Research Product](https://doi.org/10.1145/2858036.2858606) (CHI 2016) — Odom, Wakkary, Lim, Desjardins, Hengeveld, Banks. Key contribution to research through design methodology.

**Total output:** 100+ peer-reviewed publications at CHI, DIS, ToCHI, Ubicomp, CSCW, Design Issues, C&C, and others. **16+ best paper and honourable mention awards.**

## Key Contributions
What EDS has given the world:
1. **More-than-human design as a discipline** — not just a metaphor, a fully theorized research framework with methods, exemplars, and a growing community. The EDS approach (design artifacts as critical questions, not solutions) has spread to labs worldwide.
2. **Research through design legitimization** — demonstrating through 20+ years of work that making things can be first-class HCI research. The lab's outputs include artifacts, methods papers, and theoretical frameworks all treating making as inquiry.
3. **Slow technology and temporal design** (via Odom's work) — the counterpressure to attention-maximizing technology. Designing for reflection, time, and long-term relations rather than engagement.
4. **Unmaking** as a design concept — designing for biodegradation, decay, and disassembly as sustainability practice. This is a genuinely novel contribution to sustainable HCI.
5. **Everyday design** theory — how people improvise, appropriate, and redesign the computational things in their lives. Reframes users as co-designers.

## Why Interesting
EDS is not just a lab — it's the institutional origin of a research field that is directly relevant to Amber's agenda. More-than-human design asks: what if the things we design are not passive tools but active participants in a world that exceeds human control and meaning? That's precisely the question Amber's work raises from the computational side — what if AI agents are not passive tools but entities with something like behavior, agency, and death?

The EDS approach — using design artifacts to create productive problems rather than solve existing ones — is a methodology Amber can import. The lab's 20+ year track record demonstrates that this approach produces sustained, rigorous research with real field-level impact.

The **TU Eindhoven connection** is also significant: it puts EDS's intellectual framework within a short flight of Amber's Oxford base. The Future Everyday cluster (Andersen, Oogjes, Tomico, Yoo, Wakkary) is a concentration of more-than-human researchers rare in Europe.
