# MIX NYC

**The world's longest-running platform for queer experimental film.** Founded 1987 by author [Sarah Schulman](https://www.sarahschulman.net/) and filmmaker [Jim Hubbard](https://jimhubbardfilm.com/) during the AIDS crisis. Now a NYC non-profit running the annual New York Queer Experimental Film Festival (MIX FEST) and year-round programming including "One-Night Stands" experimental screenings. Nearly four decades of queer avant-garde cinema — the institution that premiered Todd Haynes, Paris Is Burning, Jonathan Caouette's Tarnation, and launched satellite festivals across Latin America, Europe.

Key connection to Amber's network: [[Shari Frilot]] was co-director 1993–1996 and shaped MIX's multicultural, installation-focused era. Also linked via [[Nonny de la Peña]] through the Sundance/queer media orbit.

## Mission & Objectives

MIX NYC is a 501(c)(3) non-profit dedicated to platforming, promoting, and supporting LGBTQIA2S+ experimental filmmakers and artists. Their mandate:
- **Anti-mainstream**: showcase films that challenge conventional filmmaking practices — formally inventive, not just thematically queer
- **Community infrastructure**: function as incubator for creativity, fostering collaboration and dialogue
- **Accessibility**: tiered submission fees, no exclusion based on ability to pay (founding principle of Schulman & Hubbard, maintained today)
- **Preservation**: Jim Hubbard's ongoing effort to preserve 2,000+ hours of AIDS-era film and video, now [free at NYPL](https://www.nypl.org/)
- **Artist-first**: all makers paid equally regardless of work length (policy since founding)

Origin story: In 1987, newly emerging gay film festivals weren't including formally inventive work, and experimental film venues were marginalizing gay/lesbian content. MIX was founded precisely in this gap — at the intersection of queer underground and avant-garde cinema.

## Key People

- **[Sarah Schulman](https://www.sarahschulman.net/)** — Co-founder (1987–1991). Novelist, playwright, HIV/AIDS activist. Also co-initiated the [ACT UP Oral History Project](http://www.actuporalhistory.org/) with Hubbard (2003). Key figure in lesbian feminist and AIDS activism writing.
- **[Jim Hubbard](https://jimhubbardfilm.com/)** — Co-founder (1987–present as preservationist). Filmmaker, archivist. Preserved 2,000+ hours of AIDS film/video at NYPL. Continued organizing ACT UP Oral History Project.

Premiered Thomas Allen Harris's *Vintage* and her own *Black Nations/Queer Nations* (1995). Later became major curator at [Sundance Film Festival](https://www.sundance.org/). *(Full note exists)*

## Key Contributions

1. **First LGBTQ+ experimental film festival**: Created the infrastructure where formally inventive queer cinema could exist outside both the mainstream gay festival circuit and the avant-garde film world.
2. **Premiere launchpad**: First screenings of *Paris Is Burning* (on dual system), Todd Haynes' thesis film, Christine Vachon's first film, Jonathan Caouette's *Tarnation* — works that defined queer cinema.
3. **Black queer representation**: First film festival to include a dedicated focus on films by and about Black gay men (from founding).
4. **AIDS preservation**: Jim Hubbard's 2,000+ hour archive, free at NYPL, is irreplaceable historical record.
5. **ACT UP Oral History Project** (2003-ongoing): [www.actuporalhistory.org](http://www.actuporalhistory.org/) — interviews with ACT UP NY members, Ford Foundation-funded, documenting effective activist methodology.
6. **Global satellite network**: MIX Brasil (1993, São Paulo), MIX México (1996, Arturo Castelan), MIX Milan (2008), MIX Copenhagen (2011) — a transnational queer experimental film network.
7. **Immersive format innovation**: Under Jusick/Burgess/Eckhardt (2006–2015), pioneered immersive warehouse environments for film festivals — long before "immersive" became fashionable.
8. **Whitney / Berlin crossover**: MIX work regularly contributed to [Whitney Biennial](https://whitney.org/exhibitions/biennial/) and [Berlinale](https://www.berlinale.de/), bridging underground and institutional cinema.

## Events & Programmes

### Annual
- **[MIX FEST](https://www.mixnyc.org/festivals/mix-fest-2024)** — Annual New York Queer Experimental Film Festival. Week-long. Screenings + installations + performances. Historically November. 2024 revival at [Quad Cinema](https://quadcinema.com/).
- **One-Night Stands** — Year-round experimental screening series (relaunched 2024). Single-night events, community-building function.

### Historical / Notable
- **Museum of Mix** (2025) — Curated by Jessica Dunn Rovinelli + angelray (Aster Ryan). MoMA archive partnership. Secret Brooklyn location. Installations + 16mm prints.
- **MIXtv** (2004–2005) — Weekly TV program on [Manhattan Neighborhood Network](https://www.mnn.org/)
- **A Different Take** (2005–) — Youth film production workshop in partnership with NYC Parks & Recreation. BIPOC queer youth. Screenings at [Le Petite Versailles Community Garden](https://alliedproductions.org/)
- **Community Screenings** (2005–) — Five-boroughs outreach. Partners: [BAAD! Bronx Academy of Arts and Dance](https://baad.org/), UnionDocs (Brooklyn), Collective Unconscious (Manhattan), Bent Pages (Staten Island)
- **ACT UP Oral History Project** (2003–ongoing) — [www.actuporalhistory.org](http://www.actuporalhistory.org/)
- **Naked Eye Celebrity Camera** (2006) — Benefit auction; disposable cameras exposed by Laurie Anderson, Gus Van Sant, Alec Soth, 100+ others
- **Memorizing MIX** (1999–) — Archival preservation initiative for past works

### Submission
- Annual film submissions via [FilmFreeway](https://filmfreeway.com/), approximately May–August
- Tiered fees; queer filmmakers/concepts/subjects at all career stages

## Why Interesting

MIX is a **model of autonomous cultural infrastructure** built from scratch with no institutional funding, sustained through community commitment and box office. It demonstrates:

1. **Community-as-institution**: Schulman & Hubbard built a major cultural institution on artist fees, hand-pasted posters, and no grants — the inverse of how most institutions work
2. **Formal radicalism + identity politics as unified project**: Not just "queer content" but "formally inventive queer cinema" — the insistence that HOW you film is as political as WHAT you film
3. **Preservation as activism**: The AIDS archive is as important as the festival — MIX understood that queer cultural production was being lost and built infrastructure to save it
4. **Satellite model**: MIX Brasil / México / Milan / Copenhagen — an early model of networked cultural institutions with shared values but local leadership
5. **Immersive as political**: The 2006–2015 era's move to warehouses and installations (before "immersive" was an art market category) was a deliberate rejection of cinema as passive spectatorship

For Amber, the most interesting angle is: **MIX as a model for how community-centered experimental practice builds lasting infrastructure**. The ACT UP Oral History Project especially — using the festival organization to document activist methodology — is a model for thinking about how art communities generate knowledge.
