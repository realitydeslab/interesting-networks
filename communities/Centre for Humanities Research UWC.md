# Centre for Humanities Research (CHR), University of the Western Cape

The Centre for Humanities Research (CHR) at the [University of the Western Cape (UWC)](https://www.uwc.ac.za/), Cape Town, South Africa is one of Africa's most intellectually ambitious humanities research institutes — and arguably the world's leading institutional site for decolonial critical humanities. Founded at a university born out of apartheid resistance, it has become a major Global South node for postcolonial/decolonial critical theory, archival practices, memory studies, affect theory, and fundamental questions about what the university should be.

Directed by [[Premesh Lalu]] (Professor of History, founding director). Since November 2024, [[Erich Hörl]] holds an Extraordinary Professorship here — creating a distinctive German media philosophy–South African postcolonial theory axis that is theoretically generative and institutionally unusual.

The CHR is funded through the **DST-NRF Flagship for Critical Thought in African Humanities**, a major South African national research initiative giving it sustained capacity for doctoral fellowships, postdoctoral positions, and international visiting researcher programmes.

## Mission & Objectives

**University history:** UWC was established in 1960 during apartheid as a university designated for "Coloured" people under the apartheid classification system. Rather than becoming a compliant institution, it transformed into a site of resistance, radical thought, and anti-apartheid activism — earning the nickname "the People's University." The 1985 student movement and the Trojan Horse Massacre (when apartheid police hidden in a truck killed student protesters in Athlone) are constitutive events in this history. This heritage profoundly shapes the CHR's intellectual orientation.

**CHR founding and mission:** The Centre emerges from this resistance tradition. Its mission is explicitly about rethinking the university and knowledge from the Global South — not merely applying existing theoretical frameworks but asking what critical thinking looks like from the postcolony, from Africa, from a place systematically excluded from the supposed universality of Western knowledge production.

**DST-NRF Flagship for Critical Thought in African Humanities:** South Africa's Department of Science and Technology (DST) and National Research Foundation (NRF) designated CHR as one of a small number of "Flagship" research projects, giving it sustained funding for:
- Doctoral fellowships (**Next Generation Scholars** programme)
- Postdoctoral fellowships (**Early Career Fellows**)
- Infrastructure for convening scholars internationally
- Mellon Foundation co-funding (African Humanities Programme)

This model — a humanities research institute with real funding power to form cohorts and attract international visitors — is unusual in the Global South humanities landscape.

**The Hörl axis (2024-):** The appointment of [[Erich Hörl]] as Extraordinary Professor in November 2024 is a structurally significant development. Hörl brings: German media philosophy (Simondon, Stiegler lineage), the concept of **general ecology** (the becoming-environmental of sense and milieu), **environmentality** (power operating through environments, not subjects), and current work on **disruption theory**. Lalu brings: South African postcolonial theory, apartheid archives and memory, the university question, the technical constitution of the human. Their 2024 *Theory, Culture & Society* paper ("Unseating Mastery") is the inaugural product — arguing that the university must be rethought beyond "mastery" toward openness to the genuinely new.

## Key Contributions

The CHR has contributed several important intellectual formations to global humanities:

1. **"Becoming Technical of the Human"** (Lalu) — a concept for how bodies encounter technical systems as a site of political constitution. The apartheid state's use of technology (weaponized trucks, surveillance systems) constituted certain bodies as killable. This extends to all encounters between human bodies and technical systems — including AI.

2. **"Unseating Mastery"** (Hörl & Lalu) — the argument that the university must be reconstituted against the tradition of "mastery" (colonial + academic) to open to genuinely new knowledge from non-canonical positions.

3. ***Remains of the Social*** — a body of post-apartheid social theory that engages Derrida, Butler, Arendt, Lacan, Foucault from specifically post-apartheid standpoints, generating new postcolonial theory rather than applying European frameworks.

4. **General Ecology × Decolonial Theory** — the new Hörl-Lalu synthesis (2024-): thinking "general ecology" (the becoming-environmental of sense, power, capital) from the standpoint of the postcolony, where technics arrived as colonial violence.

5. **Archive politics** — CHR's work on digitizing liberation struggle archives (Aluka project) and the power dynamics of who controls what data is preserved, whose past is legible, is a precursor to current debates about AI training data and historical bias.

## Why Interesting

The CHR represents one of the most intellectually serious institutional experiments in the Global South humanities. It is not "African studies" in the traditional area-studies sense — it is genuinely engaged with the most difficult questions of continental philosophy, critical theory, and political thought, while insisting these questions must be asked from the specific position of the postcolony.

**What makes it distinctive:**
1. **The university question as practice** — CHR is not theorizing the university from outside; it IS the university experiment. UWC's apartheid origins and post-apartheid transformation are the raw material of the research. The embedded, embodied quality this gives CHR is rare.
2. **The Hörl-Lalu axis** — Genuinely unusual. German media philosophy and African decolonial theory are rarely connected. "General ecology" thought from the standpoint of the postcolony — where technics arrived as colonial violence — could generate new concepts.
3. **"Unseating Mastery"** — The 2024 TCS paper is a significant intervention: "mastery" has both academic valence (transmitting established knowledge) and colonial valence (the master/slave relation). Arguing the university must "unseat mastery" connects epistemology to decolonization philosophically.
4. **Archive as political AI infrastructure** — CHR's work on who controls colonial and apartheid-era archives is a direct precursor to AI training data politics. The political economy of what gets digitized, by whom, for whose use, is a live debate.
5. **Critical Times** — CHR is a founding voice of one of the most rigorous new global critical theory journals. This is institutional infrastructure that matters.
