# Sundance New Frontier

The world's most prestigious exhibition of experimental immersive and interactive media art. Founded in 2007 by **[[Shari Frilot]]** at the Sundance Film Festival, initially as "New Frontier on Main" — a small sidebar focused on digital installation. Over 16+ years it became the defining institution for VR/AR/XR as a serious art form: the place where the commercial VR industry was accidentally launched (Nonny de la Peña → Palmer Luckey → Oculus Rift), and where feminist, Afrofuturist, and Indigenous artists shaped immersive media culture before the tech industry did.

The program sits at the intersection of cinema, technology, and art — and consciously refuses to prioritize any one over the others. That tension is its curatorial engine.

## History & Mission

### Founding and Early Years (2007–2011)

**Shari Frilot** joined Sundance in 1998 as a senior programmer. In 2007 she launched "New Frontier on Main" — a dedicated section for digital art installations alongside the main festival program. The founding curatorial logic: cinema is changing, and the people changing it most are working in forms the film world doesn't have categories for. New Frontier would create those categories.

Early focus: net art, interactive video, digital installation. Artists like **Doug Aitken**, **Jennifer Steinkamp**, **Pipilotti Rist**, and **Aaron Koblin** appeared in the early years — all figures whose practice already challenged the screen-as-window paradigm.

### The VR Inflection Point (2012–2016)

**2012** is the watershed moment. **[[Nonny de la Peña]]** premiered *Hunger in Los Angeles* — a VR documentary reconstruction of a diabetic man collapsing at a food bank line in LA. It was the first VR documentary ever screened at a major film festival. De la Peña's intern, **Palmer Luckey**, built the headset used for the installation. Nine months later, Luckey founded Oculus Rift. Oculus was eventually acquired by Facebook for $2 billion, and laid the foundation for Meta. One New Frontier installation → the modern VR industry. This is the event that every stakeholder in immersive media cites as their origin story.

By **2016** (the 10th anniversary), New Frontier had grown to showcase 30+ VR projects per year. Key 2016 works:
- **"Double Conscience"** — [[Kahlil Joseph]] + Kendrick Lamar; double-channel portrait of Black life in Compton
- **"Stonemilker"** — Björk + Andrew Thomas Huang (Vrse.works); VR collaboration
- **"Kiya"** — [[Nonny de la Peña]] / Emblematic Group; Al Jazeera America VR doc
- **"Notes on Blindness"** — Peter Middleton + James Spinney; audio diary + VR
- Multiple mobile VR releases downloadable via app

The 10th anniversary also saw **"Slithering Screens"** — a traveling New Frontier exhibition at **MoMA, New York** (April 2016). Alumni from across the decade: Doug Aitken, Jennifer Steinkamp, Aaron Koblin, Pipilotti Rist, James Franco, Nonny de la Peña, Joseph Gordon-Levitt.

### World Building Residency and the Lab (2016–2021)

In parallel with the main exhibition, Frilot launched the **New Frontier Story Lab** (later: **New Frontier World Building Residency**) — a development program for immersive projects by established filmmakers. Participants included **Roger Ross Williams** and **Josephine Decker**. Also: **[[Lauren Lee McCarthy]]**'s "Beyond the Breakdown" (2021) came through the 2017 World Building Residency.

The Lab was discontinued in 2021 amid pandemic-related budget cuts. The main exhibition continued.

### The Metaverse Moment (2018–2022)

New Frontier became both a launchpad and a cautionary tale during the metaverse hype cycle (2018–2022):

- **2018:** *SPHERES: Songs of Spacetime* (Eliza McNitt, exec. produced by Darren Aronofsky) — first VR work to sell for **seven figures** at Sundance; narrated by Jessica Chastain. Major industry signal.
- **2018:** "Frankenstein AI: A Monster Made by Many" — AI trained on Mary Shelley's text; participatory narrative.
- **2020:** Fully virtual editions during COVID; "The Spaceship" online environment with avatar interaction.
- **2021–2022:** New Frontier was effectively the most prominent section of the festival; Sam Green's "32 Sounds" (live cinema) opened the 2022 program.
- **2022:** Frilot resisted tech industry pressure: "I've seen filmmakers make their first feature and then they climb up and get chewed up in the studio system, and you see the same thing happening with New Frontier visions who create these miraculous innovations in storytelling. It turns them into workhorses."
- **Lynette Wallworth** (Australia) — received the first-ever Sundance VR residency (~2016); her *Collisions* (Martu Country, VR, Emmy Award 2017) and *Awavena* (Yawanawa people, Emmy Award 2019) are the program's most acclaimed documentary works internationally. She is the only Australian to ever sit on the Sundance Institute board.
- **Chris Milk** — credited Frilot with validating his early VR work before he pivoted to the product space; founded VR workout app Supernatural (Meta acquired for ~$400M).

### The Hiatus and Reimagination (2023–2024)

In October 2022, Frilot sent a letter to New Frontier alumni and submitters: the 2023 program was cancelled. Not dead — suspended for reflection. The letter described "a period of reimagination" to assess how best to serve artists and audiences amid shifts in the media landscape (VR consumer market not materializing as expected; Meta's metaverse pivot drawing backlash).

2024: New Frontier returned with a reimagined format. Frilot continued as chief curator. The 2025 and 2026 editions continued.

## Institutional Network

### Core Institution
**Sundance Institute** / **Sundance Film Festival** — New Frontier is a section of the festival, curated by Shari Frilot (Chief Curator and Senior Programmer). The festival runs annually in January in Park City, Utah.

### Industry Partners (Historical)
- **Google** — scouted talent at New Frontier; funded multiple productions (including *Use of Force*)
- **Intel** — technology partner; scouted for talent
- **Meta / Facebook** — major VR industry player whose founding was indirectly catalyzed by 2012 New Frontier
- **PBS Frontline** — documentary production partner (de la Peña's *After Solitary*, *Greenland Melting*)
- **Darren Aronofsky / Protozoa** — executive produced *SPHERES* (2018)

### Peer Programs (Connected via Artist Circuit)
- **[[IDFA DocLab]]** (Amsterdam, November) — European counterpart for interactive documentary; Frilot scouts there
- **[[Venice VR]]** (Venice, September) — European prestige circuit; many New Frontier artists also show here
- **Tribeca Immersive** (New York, June) — US counterpart; funded *Use of Force* (de la Peña)
- **SXSW XR** (Austin, March) — tech-adjacent; many New Frontier artists also win awards here
- **Ars Electronica** (Linz, September) — art-tech crossover; overlapping alumni

### Academic Partners
- **MIT Open Documentary Lab** — documented and critically analyzed New Frontier programming
- **USC Institute for Creative Technologies** — key production collaborator on de la Peña's VR works
- **World Economic Forum** — commissioned *Project Syria* for Davos; policy-art crossover

### Application / Submission Process

New Frontier accepts submissions annually through Sundance's general submission system. Key parameters:

- **Submission window:** Typically **August–September** for the January festival
- **Platform:** Sundance Film Festival official submission portal (sundance.org)
- **What's eligible:** Immersive/interactive works including VR/AR/XR, installation, live performance, AI-generated narrative, interactive documentary
- **Selection:** Shari Frilot (Chief Curator) makes final selections, with a small curatorial team; no formal jury for selection (juries may be involved in awards)
- **Fees:** Standard submission fees apply; were reimbursed when 2023 program was cancelled
- **Note:** New Frontier does NOT currently have an open call for 2026 or 2027 (January 2026 festival has already occurred; next call would open August 2026)

## Venue Circuit Analysis

### Park City Geography
New Frontier is physically presented at the Sundance Film Festival in **Park City, Utah** — a ski resort town where the festival takes over venues across the main street and surrounding area. The program has had dedicated venues:
- **Kimball Art Center** — primary venue for installations
- **"The Ray"** — a dedicated immersive experience venue built specifically for New Frontier (multi-year commitment)
- Various Park City hotels and alternative spaces for smaller works

The Park City geography creates an unusual experience: you walk from indie films to VR installations to ski slopes within minutes. The collision of mainstream festival culture (buyers, agents, stars) with experimental tech art is the defining context. Tech industry scouts mix with art-film fans — a cross-contamination New Frontier exploits deliberately.

### Downstream Circuit
Works that premiere at New Frontier typically travel to:
1. **Venice VR Expanded** (September) — the European prestige stop
2. **IDFA DocLab** (November) — documentary-oriented works
3. **Tribeca Immersive** (June) — US East Coast premiere if not already shown
4. **SXSW XR** (March) — tech-industry facing
5. **CPH:DOX** (Copenhagen, March) — documentary festivals
6. **Ars Electronica** (September) — art-science festival; overlapping artist pool
7. **Sheffield DocFest** (June) — UK documentary festival with interactive strand
8. Museum acquisitions: **MoMA**, **Whitney**, **Smithsonian** have all hosted New Frontier alumni works

**The critical path:** New Frontier → Venice VR → museum/gallery → streaming (or technology acquisition). The SPHERES ($7-figure deal) and Supernatural (Chris Milk's app, ~$400M Meta acquisition) represent two different endpoints of the commercial trajectory.

## Why Interesting for Amber

New Frontier is simultaneously **a target venue** (Amber's work could legitimately belong there), **a model for field-building** (how Frilot created institutional infrastructure for a medium that didn't have it), and **a historical case study** (the most documented example of experimental art catalyzing industrial-scale technology).

### Direct Relevance to Amber's Work

**1. HoloKit as "anti-Meta" technology**
Frilot has consistently resisted New Frontier's commercialization — explicitly critiquing what she sees as artists becoming "workhorses" for tech platforms. HoloKit, as an open-source MR headset that democratizes AR without corporate lock-in, is exactly the kind of alternative infrastructure Frilot values. A project using HoloKit could be framed as a commentary on ownership, access, and the politics of immersive hardware — directly in her curatorial language. Amber's explicit contrast: HoloKit vs. Apple Vision Pro / Meta Quest.

**2. Agent Ethology as Immersive Installation**
New Frontier's most celebrated works use embodied presence to make audiences witnesses to experiences they couldn't otherwise have. Agent ethology (studying AI agent behavior as if studying animal behavior) has a natural extension into installation art: what if visitors could *observe* AI agents in their "natural" computational habitat? A New Frontier-appropriate piece might be an installation where visitors witness AI agents doing something incomprehensible — and are invited to interpret that behavior through the lens of ethology (signaling, territory, foraging). This would be novel in the New Frontier context: most AI art there has been participatory (you interact with the AI). The ethological model positions the visitor as *observer*, not interactor.

**3. Machine Death as Installation**
Frilot's 2020 program was explicitly about mortality, ecological grief, and non-human consciousness (e.g., "Breathe" — breath sensing + climate visualization). Machine death — the idea that AI systems can die, and that this death is meaningful — is a ready-made New Frontier topic. An installation that stages a machine dying, or invites witnesses to the death of an AI agent, would resonate with the program's anti-escapist, politically engaged tradition. The "ghost in the machine" question (does it persist after death? what remains?) connects to Amber's broader research.

**4. More-than-Human Design**
The 2016–2022 period of New Frontier was rich with more-than-human themes: Indigenous cosmologies (Wallworth's Martu and Yawanawá VR), non-human animal perspectives (blue whale encounter VR), ecological presence (mushroom networks, breath, water). Amber's work in more-than-human design could contribute to this tradition. The specific intersection of AR (superimposing more-than-human agents onto the physical world) is underexplored in the New Frontier context.

**5. Open-Source / Anti-Platform Positioning**
New Frontier has historically been ambivalent about tech industry alignment. A project explicitly foregrounding open-source tools, community fabrication, and anti-platform values would resonate with Frilot's documented concerns about commercialization. HoloKit's provenance (open hardware, community-driven) is a narrative asset.

### What It Would Take to Get In

**Hard requirements:**
- A complete, exhibitable immersive work (not a prototype or demo)
- Strong conceptual framing — "we built something interesting" is not enough; "this work does X to audiences and asks Y question" is
- Technical robustness — Frilot selects works she's confident can be installed in a festival environment
- Some exhibition track record (one prior festival/exhibition showing strengthens the case significantly)

**Soft requirements:**
- The work should come from outside the mainstream tech industry (independent artists preferred over corporate labs)
- It should have human stakes — social justice, human experience, political dimension
- It should NOT be a tech demo wearing art clothing; the reverse (art clothing that happens to use interesting tech) is fine
- Being on Frilot's radar through the broader festival/immersive community helps; she scouts at IDFA, Venice, etc.

**Realistic timeline for Amber:**
1. Develop a complete exhibitable work (HoloKit + agent ethology + machine death — pick one strong concept)
2. Show at one prior venue: ISEA 2026, Ars Electronica 2026, or Venice VR submission
3. Apply to New Frontier submission cycle (August–September 2026) for January 2027

**Amber's strongest entry point:** A work at the intersection of HoloKit AR + more-than-human design + machine mortality — essentially: an AR installation where visitors witness AI agents dying, observed through open-source MR hardware. That hits every dimension of Frilot's curatorial sensibility: embodied presence, ecological/more-than-human stakes, anti-commercial hardware politics, and documentary (witnessing as mode).

### Model for Field-Building

Frilot's work at New Frontier is the most complete institutional model for "building a venue for an art form that doesn't have one." In 2007, immersive media wasn't a festival category. By 2016, it was a billion-dollar industry. She didn't predict this; she created the conditions. For Amber: agent ethology as a practice needs exactly this kind of institutional home. What is the "New Frontier" of agent ethology? Which festival/conference should add an "Agent Behavior" track? Amber should think about building that venue, not just attending existing ones.
