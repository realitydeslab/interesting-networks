# Laboria Cuboniks

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Deep research complete. Expanded from stub to full profile with history, manifesto analysis, all six members, theoretical context, and Amber connections.

Xenofeminist working group. Founded 2014 as an international feminist collective of six theorists, artists, and practitioners. Named as an anagram of "Nicolas Bourbaki" — the collective pseudonym used by French mathematicians — signalling that individual authorship is a political act, not just a stylistic choice. Best known for the "Xenofeminist Manifesto: A Politics for Alienation" (2015), a foundational text proposing a radical feminist engagement with technology, rationalism, and alienation. Operates as a distributed collective across multiple disciplines and continents: art, design, architecture, philosophy, music, writing, evolutionary algorithms.

## Mission & Objectives

**Core claim:** Xenofeminism (XF) is anti-naturalist, technomaterialist, and gender-abolitionist. It argues that feminist politics must scale beyond the local and micropolitical to operate at systemic levels — using technology, law, and infrastructure as feminist tools. Against romantic returns to nature. For the radical reworking of what "universal" means.

**Three pillars:**
1. **Anti-naturalism** — "Nature" is not a refuge but a justification for injustice. The argument that things are "natural" is always already political. XF refuses nature as political justification.
2. **Technomaterialism** — Technology is not inherently progressive or regressive. It is a contested terrain. Feminists must engage it strategically, not fear it or celebrate it uncritically.
3. **Gender abolitionism** — Not the erasure of sexuate diversity, but the dismantling of the grid by which gender asymmetrically distributes power. "Let a hundred sexes bloom!"

**Manifesto opening:**
> "XF seizes alienation as an impetus to generate new worlds. We are all alienated — but have we ever been otherwise? It is through, and not despite, our alienated condition that we can free ourselves from the muck of immediacy."

**Name etymology:** "Laboria Cuboniks" is an anagram of "Nicolas Bourbaki" — the collective pseudonym adopted by a group of French mathematicians in the 1930s who published under a single fictitious name. The XF collective inverts this: a fictitious name that obscures many bodies rather than one. The choice affirms "abstraction as an episto-political necessity for twenty-first century claims on equality."

## History & Founding

**2014:** Founded as a working group. Six members met across overlapping academic, art, and theory circuits in Europe and beyond. The collective emerged from conversations about the limits of existing feminist frameworks — particularly the retreat into localism, identity politics, and the uncritical celebration of "nature" in some ecofeminist and queer theory traditions.

**June 2015:** Publication of "Xenofeminism: A Politics for Alienation" on [laboriacuboniks.net](https://laboriacuboniks.net/manifesto/xenofeminism-a-politics-for-alienation/). The manifesto went viral within feminist theory, accelerationist, and art-theory circuits. Available in 18+ languages on the website.

**2015–2019:** Collective appearances, talks (e.g., Question of Will in Bratislava, 2017), and interviews (DIS Magazine, aCCeSsions, Kunsthalle Wien, Zineseminar/Seoul). Members also published individually on XF-adjacent topics.

**2019:** Special issue of [*Angelaki*](https://www.tandfonline.com/journals/cang20) (Vol. 24) dedicated to xenofeminist theory, with individual essays by Helen Hester, Patricia Reed, and Diann Bauer. Formalizes academic reception.

**2022:** [*Nuevos Vectores del Xenofeminismo*](https://laboriacuboniks.net/books/nuevos-vectores-del-xenofeminismo/) (Spanish, Zazie Ediciones, ISBN: 978-84-123170-6-0, 256 pp.) — collection of individually-authored essays by all six members, translated by Toni Navarro and Federico Fernández Giordano. Only book-length collection to date from the collective.

**Status (2026):** The collective appears to have reduced collective activity post-2020. Members continue to publish individually. Helen Hester is most publicly active as a xenofeminist theorist.

## Key People

### The Six Members

**[[Helen Hester]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — Professor of Media and Communication, University of West London. Based in 🎯 London. The most publicly recognizable face of xenofeminism, author of *Xenofeminism* (Polity, 2018) — the definitive academic book. Co-authors with [[Nick Srnicek]] on post-work politics. Also connected to [[Antikythera]]. Key node for Amber.

**[[Patricia Reed]]** ⭐⭐ — Artist, designer, and theorist. Based in Berlin, Germany. Works across art, design, and philosophy. Known for essays on "post-anthropocentric cosmologies," "platform cosmologies," and feminist universalism. Regular contributor to [e-flux](https://www.e-flux.com/) and [Glass Bead Journal](https://www.glass-bead.org/). Engages seriously with Sylvia Wynter, Donna Haraway, Reza Negarestani. The most philosophically technical of the members. Has presented at Haus der Kulturen der Welt (HKW), Berlin.

**[[Amy Ireland]]** ⭐⭐ — Theorist, writer, poet. Australian. Connected to accelerationist and CCRU genealogies as well as xenofeminism. Published in [e-flux #80](https://www.e-flux.com/journal/80/100016/black-circuit-code-for-the-numbers-to-come/) ("Black Circuit: Code for the Numbers to Come"). External affiliate of [[AI & Culture Research Center]] at NYU Shanghai. Co-editor (with [[Benjamin Bratton]], [[Anna Greenspan]], [[Bogna Konior]]) of *[Machine Decision is Not Final](https://www.urbanomic.com/book/machine-decision-is-not-final/)* (Urbanomic, 2025). Bridges XF and accelerationist theory.

**[[Diann Bauer]]** ⭐⭐ — Visual artist and theorist. UK-based. Runs [diannbauer.net](https://diannbauer.net/). Works in sculpture, video, installation. Connected to CREAM research centre at University of Westminster (Deep Field Project). Also part of Alliance of the Southern Triangle (A.S.T.) collaborative. In the collective interviews, often provides the most art-field-specific analysis of systemic inequity.

**Olivia Lucca Fraser** (formerly Lucca Fraser) — Philosopher, software engineer, writer. Canadian. Runs [feralmachin.es](https://feralmachin.es/) (blog on philosophy, fiction, mathematics, evolutionary computation). Research interests span theoretical philosophy, computer virology, computational creativity. Wrote the most practically-minded sections of the manifesto regarding digital networks and coevolution. Became Olivia Lucca Fraser (name update reflects gender transition).

**[[Katrina Burch]]** — Musician, performer, theorist. UK-based. Involved in digital music production and performance. Collaborated with Helen Hester on the CTM Festival performance "The (Dia)grammatology of Space" (Berghain, Berlin, 2016). Less publicly active individually than other members.

## Key Publications & Output

## Key Contributions

**To feminist theory:**
- Revived and updated cyberfeminism for the post-2010 era, explicitly engaging platform capitalism, trans politics, and post-work futures
- Introduced **"constructive alienation"** as feminist concept — alienation is not to be overcome but deployed strategically
- Proposed **generic universalism** as alternative to both liberal universalism and particularist identity politics
- Demonstrated that feminist politics requires systemic/infrastructural thinking, not just local resistance

**To political theory:**
- The manifesto's code-like structure (hexadecimal sections, register flags as headings) performs its own argument — technology and abstraction as feminist tools
- Coalition paper as territorial claim — the manifesto's collective authorship builds a movement, not just an academic position

**To practice:**
- Multiple language translations (18+) as political act — making feminist theory accessible across language barriers
- Pseudonymous collective authorship as feminist strategy — no individual brand, no star system

## Theoretical Context & Relationships

### Intellectual Lineage
**Cyberfeminism (1990s):** XF emerges from cyberfeminism (VNS Matrix, Sadie Plant, Donna Haraway's Cyborg Manifesto) but critiques its romantic anarchism and celebration of indeterminacy. Cyberfeminism "waned" when it couldn't scale.

**Accelerationism:** XF shares vocabulary and sensibility with left-accelerationism ([[Nick Srnicek]], Alex Williams, [[Nick Land]]'s CCRU genealogy — but through a feminist critique of that tradition). The manifesto's language of "hypertition," "memes as political tools," and systemic transformation echoes accelerationist discourse. Amy Ireland is the most directly connected to CCRU/accelerationist genealogy.

**Rationalism:** XF makes a strong claim that reason is not inherently patriarchal — it just happens to be dominated by patriarchal practitioners. The response is not to reject reason but to practice it better. Explicit engagement with Reza Negarestani's work on rationalism.

**Intersectionality / Universalism:** XF engages and critiques the limits of intersectional politics. Not against intersectionality, but argues it must be a *movement* not a *taxonomy*. Proposes "generic universalism" from below.

**Post-work / Social reproduction:** Helen Hester and Nick Srnicek's collaboration connects XF to post-work politics, automation, and the feminist economics of care work.

## For Amber

### Why Interesting
Xenofeminism occupies a unique position at the intersection of **feminist theory**, **critical computing**, and **speculative futures** — three of Amber's core threads. It's not just feminist theory: it's feminist theory that takes seriously the question of how to use computation, abstraction, and technology as tools of political transformation. This resonates deeply with Amber's critical computing interests and her broader orientation toward more-than-human politics.

The collective is also a **model for practice**: six people from different fields (art, design, philosophy, music, architecture) producing collective theory under a pseudonym. This is unusual in academic contexts and represents a productive alternative to individual career-building.

## Why Interesting
**XF is one of the most rigorous feminist interventions in technology studies of the past decade.** It doesn't moralize about technology or retreat into tech pessimism. It thinks strategically about how to use technology for feminist ends — and it's willing to be abstract, universal, and weird in doing so. That combination (rigor + radicalism + technological engagement) is rare.

For Amber specifically: XF's "generic universalism" — the idea that feminist politics must be scalable without becoming homogenizing — parallels questions Amber faces in any "more-than-human" design work: how do you design for a broader constituency without imposing a particular view of what "more" includes?

The collective authorship model is also genuinely interesting as **organizational innovation** — a way of doing theory that builds a movement rather than careers.
