# Emergence Magazine

The leading literary venue at the intersection of ecology, culture, and spirituality. An initiative of [[Kalliopeia Foundation]], published ad-free and paywall-free since 2016. Produces long-form essays, poetry, fiction, films, audio/podcast, and an annual print edition. The home base for ecological humanities — where scientific rigour meets Indigenous knowledge, spiritual tradition, and literary craft. Key contributors include [[Robin Wall Kimmerer]], Robert Macfarlane, Merlin Sheldrake, Terry Tempest Williams, Joanna Macy, Amitav Ghosh, Monica Gagliano, and many others.

## Mission & Objectives

> "We believe in sharing stories that have the potential to shift ways of thinking and being in our relationship to the living world."

- **Slow consumption** ethos: long-form, evergreen, depth over clicks
- Not ad-supported, not paywalled — fully funded by Kalliopeia Foundation's philanthropic mission (ecology × culture × spirituality)
- Produces work that is simultaneously literary, scientific, spiritual, and activist — rare intersection
- Thematic focus: **ecology**, **spirituality**, **more-than-human intelligence**, **Indigenous knowledge**, **grief and beauty in the Anthropocene**

## Submission Themes (Currently Open)

### "SEASONS"
Three movements: **Requiem** (grief for what's lost), **Invitation** (attending to ecological transitions), **Celebration** (rituals and renewal). Both human and **more-than-human** perspectives invited.

### "ECOLOGY OF INTELLIGENCE"
Three sub-themes:
1. **Expanding the definition of intelligence** — beyond human-like cognition, sensory landscapes
2. **Embracing more-than-human intelligences** — on their own terms, collaborative knowledge-making between species
3. **Ecologizing artificial intelligence** — *"If we recognize that our technologies are continuous with the natural world—that our machines are made of and from the Earth—how does that change the way we construct them? Instead of envisioning an AI-driven future that further insulates the human from the natural, can we instead build technologies to reflect, and help us recognize, other kinds of intelligence?"*

**This is a direct editorial invitation for Amber's work on more-than-human design, agent ethology, machine behavior, and machine death.**

Accepts: essays, op-eds, poetry, films, photography, multimedia, audio stories. Unpublished or online premieres only.

## Why Interesting

Emergence is doing something rare: **marrying ecological science with spiritual depth and literary quality**, at scale, for free. This is the venue for the "slow" ecological humanities conversation that most culture runs away from.

The **"Ecology of Intelligence" submission theme** is essentially an open invitation for Amber's research framing. The question they're asking — "if our technologies are made of the Earth, how should we build them differently?" — is precisely what Amber's work on machine behavior, machine death, and more-than-human design answers.

The connection to **[[Robin Wall Kimmerer]]** shows that Emergence doesn't just publish writing about indigenous ecological knowledge — it publishes the indigenous scientists themselves, and seeds ideas that become books read by millions.

Emmanuel Vaughan-Lee's **Sufi background** infusing the whole project is unusual and interesting: it's spirituality not as soft add-on but as the epistemological frame. This bridges to cosmotechnics thinking (Yuk Hui) and to Amber's more-than-human design interests.

The **film studio** (Go Project Films) with NYT, New Yorker, PBS distribution channels is an underappreciated dimension — Emergence has serious visual media reach, not just text reach.
