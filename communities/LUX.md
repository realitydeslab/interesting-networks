# LUX

The UK's leading organisation for artists' moving image — production, distribution, exhibition, education, and scholarship. Based in Waterlow Park Centre, Highgate, London. Registered charity and not-for-profit company. Collection of **6,000+ works by 1,500+ artists** spanning the 1920s to the present. Founded 2002 from the merger of the historic **London Film-Makers' Co-operative** (LFMC, 1966) and **London Electronic Arts** (LEA). The single most important institutional home for experimental, essay, and artists' film in the United Kingdom.

## Overview

LUX is London's — and arguably the UK's — central institutional infrastructure for artists' moving image. It operates simultaneously as:

1. **Distribution agency** — the go-to point for hiring experimental, essay, and artists' film works by hundreds of artists globally
2. **Exhibitor** — hosts exhibitions, screenings, events at its Waterlow Park space
3. **Scholarship and publishing platform** — commissions critical writing, essays, online publications, and in-depth studies of artists' practices
4. **Support organisation** — artist development, residencies, forums, education programmes
5. **Archive** — holds and preserves a significant collection of historical and contemporary works

## Mission & Objectives

LUX supports and promotes visual artists working with the moving image. Its mission encompasses:

- **Production support** — developing and commissioning new works
- **Distribution** — making works available to hire for exhibitions, screenings, festivals internationally
- **Exhibition** — hosting shows and events at the Waterlow Park Centre
- **Education** — courses, study days, workshops, academic partnerships
- **Scholarship** — commissioning critical writing, New Artist Focus essays, publishing research
- **Artist development** — Critical Forum groups, residencies, professional support

## History

### Predecessor Organisations

**London Film-Makers' Co-operative (LFMC)** was founded in **1966**, inspired by New York's Film-Makers' Cooperative and Anthology Film Archives. It operated as an artist-run film distribution and production collective. Key figures included **Malcolm Le Grice** (committee member 1969–1976, Chair 1970–73), **Lis Rhodes**, **Jayne Parker**, **Nina Danino**, **Peter Gidal**, and many others. In 1969, Le Grice and Fred Drummond physically built the Co-op's first workshop prototype at the Robert Street Arts Lab — establishing an infrastructure for experimental filmmaking in the UK that shaped generations of artists. The LFMC held its own screenings, distribution catalogue, workshop, and study centre/archive.

**London Video Arts (LVA)** and later **London Electronic Arts (LEA)** ran parallel to the LFMC as the UK distribution organisation for video art.

The **LFMC and LEA merged in 2002** to form LUX, with **Benjamin Cook** appointed as founding Director. Cook led LUX for 22 years until 2025 — an extraordinary run that built the organisation's international standing and comprehensive collection.

### Key Moments
- **1966** — London Film-Makers' Co-operative founded
- **1978** — Margaret Raspé screens "camera-helmet" films at the Co-op (one of earliest presentations of her work)
- **2002** — LUX formed from merger of LFMC and London Electronic Arts; Benjamin Cook becomes Director
- **2002–2024** — Benjamin Cook's 22-year directorship; builds LUX into UK's leading artists' film organisation
- **2014** — LUX Scotland established (based at Trongate 103, Glasgow); pilot support from Creative Scotland and Glasgow Life; Isla Leaver-Yap as Project Director LUX Scotland
- **2024** — Malcolm Le Grice (1940–2024) passes; LUX publishes major tribute marking LFMC history
- **2025 (April)** — **Ali Roche** appointed new Director

### Key Historical Figure

**Malcolm Le Grice** (1940–2024) — Central figure in the LFMC, one of LUX's predecessor organisations. Trained as a painter at Slade. Committee member 1969–76, Chair 1970–73. Built the LFMC workshop infrastructure. Films include *Berlin Horse*, *Horror Film 1*. Wrote the original blueprint for the LFMC's structure: "screenings, distribution, workshop, and a study centre or archive." Teacher at Goldsmiths, St Martins, Harrow. Wrote and theorised extensively on experimental film.

## Historical & Intellectual Significance

### The LFMC and British Experimental Film

LUX's lineage through the **London Film-Makers' Co-operative** places it at the centre of the British experimental film tradition. The LFMC was to British experimental cinema what Anthology Film Archives was to American: a self-organised infrastructure that enabled a generation of artists to work. Malcolm Le Grice, Lis Rhodes, Jayne Parker, Sarah Pucill, Peter Gidal — these are the canonical figures of the British structural/materialist film tradition. LUX now distributes their works and maintains the archive.

The LFMC model — **screenings + distribution + workshop + study centre** — was explicitly political: it meant artists could control production, distribution, and exhibition. This artist-run model is built into LUX's DNA.

### Black Audio Film Collective and the Essay Film

**John Akomfrah** and the **Black Audio Film Collective** represent a crucial strand in LUX's collection — the Black British Arts Movement of the 1980s. BAFC's works (*Handsworth Songs*, 1986; *The Last Angel of History*, 1996) are essay films using archive, music, narration, and theory in highly non-classical ways. Akomfrah is now one of the most internationally exhibited artists in the world; LUX distributes his key early and recent works.

**[[The Otolith Group]]** (Anjalika Sagar + Kodwo Eshun) — distributed by LUX — are the explicit intellectual successors to this tradition. Their Turner Prize nomination (2010) and international exhibition circuit draw directly on the intellectual legacy of Stuart Hall, Kodwo Eshun's Afrofuturism theory, and the Black British Arts Movement. LUX is the distribution home that makes this lineage institutionally continuous.

### The Essay Film Tradition

The essay film lineage LUX represents:
- **Chris Marker** — French essay film grandfather (LUX has helped introduce his work in UK/US/Europe)
- **Harun Farocki** — German essay filmmaker distributed by LUX
- **Black Audio Film Collective** (Akomfrah) — British essay film from the Black Arts Movement
- **The Otolith Group** (Sagar + Eshun) — contemporary continuation
- Numerous other artists working with voice-over, archive, and essay form

### The *Nightcleaners* Legacy

**Nightcleaners** (1975) by the Berwick Street Collective is a foundational political documentary in the LUX collection. Made during a strike by women nightcleaners in London's office buildings, it is one of the most discussed and debated films in British film history — a work that asks how political cinema should function. Its 50th anniversary in 2025 has prompted current programming around it.

## Why Interesting

LUX is interesting for several interlocking reasons:

1. **Unique depth of collection** — 6,000+ works by 1,500+ artists spanning 100 years of moving image practice. Nowhere else in the UK has this breadth and depth for artists' moving image specifically.

2. **Living lineage** — LUX isn't just an archive; it actively develops the field. The lineage from LFMC (1966) → LUX (2002) connects living artists to their historical predecessors through direct distribution relationships.

3. **Essay film infrastructure** — LUX is the institutional home of the essay film tradition in the UK. If you want to understand how moving image practice intersects with theory, postcolonial thought, feminist politics, ecological thinking — LUX's collection is the most concentrated resource.

4. **New commissions** — LUX continues to add artists and commission new works. The Collection Engagement Residency and New Artist Focus are active development pipelines.

5. **Black British Arts** — The distribution of BAFC (Akomfrah), The Otolith Group, and other artists makes LUX the most important institutional home for Black British moving image practice and its continuities.

6. **Accessible** — Unlike many art institutions, LUX is directly accessible: the library is free to use, many screenings are free/affordable, the Critical Forum is open application, the distribution catalogue is hireable.

7. **Research ecology** — The combination of library + collection + education + critical writing + forums creates an unusually dense research ecology for anyone working with or studying moving image.
