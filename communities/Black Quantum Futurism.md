# Black Quantum Futurism

**Black Quantum Futurism (BQF)** is an interdisciplinary artistic collective and theoretical framework founded by **Camae Ayewa** (aka [Moor Mother](https://www.moormother.net/)) and **Rasheedah Phillips**, two queer Black women based in Philadelphia. Founded around 2014 (growing from The AfroFuturist Affair, started 2011), BQF synthesizes quantum physics with Afrodiasporic ontologies of time, space, ritual, text, and sound — yielding a framework for counterhistories and alternate futures.

The core claim: quantum mechanical interpretations of time, spacetime, and causality are more congruent with Afrocentric understandings than with Western linear ones. BQF uses this to challenge colonialist, Eurocentric structures of reality — arguing that domination is as much about controlling time (futures) as space (land).

## Mission & Objectives

BQF proposes "a new approach to living and experiencing reality by way of the manipulation of space-time in order to see into possible futures, and/or collapse space-time into a desired future in order to bring about that future's reality." Core outputs:

- Theoretical framework (quantum physics × African diasporic time ontologies)
- Community memory and oral history practice (Community Futures Lab)
- Experimental music, immersive installations, films, zines, performances
- Direct community anti-gentrification work (North Philadelphia)

The core philosophical move: **time is political**. Manifest Destiny was about claiming the future, not just land. Eviction cuts Black communities off from their futures. BQF opposes linear Western time with cyclical, interactive Afrocentric temporality that connects past-present-future.

## Key Contributions

1. **Temporal theory as liberation practice** — BQF made time a political/activist concept before it became fashionable in speculative design circles
2. **Quantum-Afrodiasporic synthesis** — unusual bridging of physics and Black cultural theory (peers with Sylvia Wynter in scope of ambition)
3. **Community Futures Lab model** — template for community-embedded speculative futures work
4. **Afrofuturism infrastructure** — The AfroFuturist Affair as early internet-era platform for Black speculative writers
5. **Art-activism integration** — housing law work and art practice as inseparable; rare in media art circuits

## Why Interesting

BQF sits at an unusual intersection: **rigorous theoretical framework × activist community practice × high-prestige international art circuit**. The temporal theory is genuinely philosophical (engaging Karen Barad, quantum physics, Indigenous time concepts) while being grounded in housing law and North Philadelphia gentrification. This rare combination of abstract + embodied makes BQF interesting beyond Afrofuturism as a genre category.

For Amber's research taste: the work asks *what time even is* from non-Western ontological standpoints — directly relevant to cosmotechnics, more-than-human design, and the posthumanist critique of Western technological modernity. BQF is doing the cultural theory equivalent of what Amber does in design.
