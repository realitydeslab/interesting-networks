# Feral Feminisms

An open-access feminist journal and community. Tagline: "a provocative call to untaming, queering, and radicalizing feminist thought and practice today." Takes the feral — escape from domestication — as central political metaphor. Sits at intersection of feminist theory, queer theory, critical animal studies, and anticolonial thought.

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-02:** Stub created during Feral Dynamics deep research.

## Mission & Objectives
Publishing feminist theory that refuses "domestication" — domesticated feminism = feminism that has been tamed by institutional acceptance. Feral feminism = feminism that has escaped, gone wild, become ungovernable.

## Key People
- **Kelly Struthers Montford** — co-editor, critical animal studies, University of Alberta
- **Chloë Taylor** — co-editor, feminist philosophy, Foucault, University of Alberta
- **Fiona Probyn-Rapsey** — "Five Propositions about Ferals"; critical animal studies, Australian context
- **Dinesh Wadiwel** — *The War Against Animals* (2015); animal ethics, political theory; dialogue with Taylor
- **Jack Halberstam** — cited frequently; "going wild" concept parallels ferality

## Key Publications & Output
- Special issue "Feral Theory" (Issue 6, 2016) — most theoretically rich issue. Guest editorial: "Feral Theory" by Montford & Taylor. Articles by Belcourt, Magnone, Rowan & Timmins, Miranda Johnson.
- Open access: <https://feralfeminisms.com/>

## Key Contributions
- Theorized "feral" as feminist-political concept: escape from patriarchal domestication
- Connected ferality to queer theory (Halberstam's "going wild"), anticolonial politics, critical animal studies
- Showed that the feral is ambivalent: both liberation and abandonment/vulnerability

## Why Interesting
The feminist theory of ferality as *resistance to domestication* maps powerfully onto AI contexts. If AI domestication = training/alignment/control, then "feral AI" = AI that has escaped that domestication. The feminist framing asks: who benefits from domestication? Who performs it? What is lost and what is freed in the escape? These are exactly the questions Amber's agent ethology work needs to engage.

Proximity: Montford and Taylor are at University of Alberta — not Oxford, but the journal is online/open.
