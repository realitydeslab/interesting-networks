# DAVRA Collective

Research group and artistic collective of 19 artists from Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, and Uzbekistan — founded by [[Saodat Ismailova]] in Tashkent in 2021. The first pan-Central Asian contemporary art institution dedicated to researching, documenting, and disseminating the culture and knowledge of Central Asia. "Davra" (دایره) refers to a *circular gathering or assembly* — the form in which traditional knowledge is shared across generations. Defined by Indigenous knowledge reclamation, collective ritual practice, and non-Western cosmological frameworks against the backdrop of post-Soviet erasure.

## Mission & Objectives

DAVRA exists to fill a structural void: Central Asian contemporary art has had no dedicated research institution, no collective voice, and no infrastructure for knowledge transmission across its four major post-Soviet republics (Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan, Uzbekistan). The collective's name — *davra*, meaning circular assembly — encodes its epistemological commitment: knowledge is transmitted through gathering, not through hierarchical archive.

**Three interlocking mandates:**
1. **Research** — excavating Central Asian spiritual traditions, oral histories, women's knowledge systems, mythology, and material cultures at risk of erasure under Soviet and post-Soviet modernization
2. **Documentation** — preserving and making legible what is being lost (traditional practices, languages, ecology, shamanistic and Sufi knowledge)
3. **Dissemination** — presenting this research to international audiences on Central Asian terms, not as exotic folklore but as living contemporary practice

The intellectual focus is on **chilltan** — spirits from Central Asian folklore described as 40 invisible holy entities (hence: 40 bodies, no fixed gender, shapeshifters) that appear in times of crisis to guide, heal, and protect. As knowledge objects, the chilltan represent non-Western, non-binary, non-anthropomorphic forms of being — philosophically interesting beyond their folkloric context.

## Key Contributions

### documenta fifteen — 40-Day Programme (Kassel, June 25 – August 4, 2022)
DAVRA enacted a 41-day public programme in the Fridericianum basement, Kassel, timed to the *chilla* — the 40 hottest days of the Central Asian summer, a traditional period of intensified spiritual practice. The programme included:
- Film screenings
- Collective cooking sessions
- Discussions and readings
- Dance and workshops
- Music performances
- Children's programme

All activities organized around shared research on chilltan spirits. The format was processual and participatory — a live research practice, not a static exhibition. **Supported by Goethe-Institut Kazakhstan and Uzbekistan, and documenta 15.**

This was the most significant public debut of Central Asian contemporary art in the European art world — and it happened on the artists' own terms, within the lumbung solidarity framework.

### EYE Film Player curatorial programming (Amsterdam, 2023)
Three curated film programmes presenting Central Asian cinema across three generations. Curated by Dilda Ramazan. This was not Ismailova's solo work — it was DAVRA functioning as a curatorial collective, making Central Asian film history visible to European audiences.

### Tate Modern programme presence (London, May 2023)
During the *Dislocation Blues: Saodat Ismailova* screening programme at Tate Modern, DAVRA members performed live readings of Ismailova's poetry in their native languages — Tajik, Kazakh, Kyrgyz, Uzbek. Language plurality as artistic medium.

- **Why interesting:** DAVRA is a working model of **research infrastructure built from within an underserved cultural territory** — analogous to what Amber might need to do in HCI/ALife from a non-Western perspective. They didn't wait for Western institutions to "discover" Central Asian art. They built the collective that could carry, document, and disseminate it.

- **Non-Western cosmological frameworks for technology:** The chilltan concept — 40 shapeshifting entities with no fixed gender, appearing in times of crisis — is a powerful non-Western framework for thinking about *non-anthropomorphic agency*. This maps directly onto Amber's work on machine behavior, ALife, and more-than-human design. Chilltan are not gods (anthropomorphic), not animals (biological), not objects (passive) — they are something else. So are the agents Amber studies.

## Why Interesting

DAVRA is theoretically interesting for Amber on three levels:

**1. Organizational epistemology.** The name "davra" (circular assembly) encodes a theory of knowledge: distributed, circular, not hierarchical. This is structurally similar to the lumbung model of ruangrupa — a rice barn where surplus is shared collectively. Both DAVRA and lumbung are making an argument *about* knowledge organization through their organizational form. This rhymes with Amber's interest in decentralized AI and agentive commoning.

**2. The chilltan as non-anthropomorphic agent concept.** Chilltan are 40-bodied shapeshifters with no fixed gender, appearing in times of crisis. From an ALife/machine behavior lens: this is a folk ontology of emergent, swarm-like, crisis-responsive, non-binary entities. Not gods, not monsters, not animals. They exist *between* categories. That's philosophically where Amber's agents live too.

**3. Infrastructure-building as artistic practice.** DAVRA doesn't just make art *about* cultural erasure — it is the infrastructure that resists erasure. The collective is itself the archive, the research protocol, and the transmission vehicle. This model — where the institution IS the artwork — is exactly what Amber should be building in the HCI/ALife space.

**The ruangrupa connection** also matters: DAVRA participated in the most politically significant contemporary art event of the 2020s (documenta fifteen) through the lumbung model. Understanding lumbung as an alternative to the commercial art market's extractive logic is directly relevant to thinking about post-commercial AI development ecosystems.
