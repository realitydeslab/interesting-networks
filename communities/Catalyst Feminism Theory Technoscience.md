# Catalyst: Feminism, Theory, Technoscience

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-28:** Full deep research. Expanded all sections — editorial team, history, key people, special issues, connections to Amber's interests.
- **2026-02-26:** Stub created during Karen Barad deep research.

The *prestige* open-access journal for feminist science and technology studies (STS). Peer-reviewed, interdisciplinary, activist-aligned. Covers feminist technoscience from critical AI to disability studies to bioscience to postcolonial science critique — all through a feminist, decolonial, and queer theoretical lens. Founded 2015. Advisory board of ~100 scholars; [[Karen Barad]] is on it. Affiliated with [4S (Society for Social Studies of Science)](http://4sonline.org/).

## Mission & Objectives

From the journal's own statement:

> *Catalyst: Feminism, Theory, and Technoscience serves the expanding interdisciplinary field of feminist science and technology studies (STS) by supporting theoretically inventive and methodologically creative scholarship incorporating approaches from critical public health, disability studies, sci-art, technology and digital media studies, history and philosophy of science and medicine, and more.*

Key distinctives:
- **Open access** — all articles free to read (runs on [Open Journal Systems](https://pkp.sfu.ca/ojs/))
- **Polyphonic** — includes senior scholars AND undergraduates, artists AND scientists
- **Multimodal** — publishes text, image-text, video works, sound projects
- **Accessibility-first** — accessibility as ongoing political project, not afterthought
- **Takes its name from chemistry** — "reagentive", designed to promote reflexive constitution of the field

The journal covers: feminist science studies, critical AI, more-than-human thinking, bioscience, disability studies, ecological critique, postcolonial science, queer technoscience, data justice, environmental justice.

## History & Founding

- **Conceived:** 2011 by Lisa Cartwright + David Serlin (UC San Diego) and Deboleena Roy + Elizabeth Wilson (Emory University)
- **Inaugural issue:** 2015 (Vol. 1 No. 1)
- **10th Anniversary:** 2025 (Vol. 11 No. 1 / No. 2)

The journal was born out of feminist STS scholarship at UCSD and Emory, two key nodes of the field. Elizabeth Wilson (Emory) and Deboleena Roy remain important figureheads of feminist bioscience theory.

## Key Contributions

What Catalyst has given feminist STS:
1. **A polyphonic, open-access home** for feminist technoscience — replacing the need for paywall journals
2. **Multimodal scholarship** — normalizing image-text works, video, sound as legitimate academic forms
3. **Accessibility as politics** — building disability politics into journal infrastructure itself
4. **Bridge building** — linking senior STS scholars with junior scholars, artists, scientists, activists
5. **10 years of infrastructure** for a field that now connects feminist STS, critical AI, disability studies, postcolonial science
