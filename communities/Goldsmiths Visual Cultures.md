# Goldsmiths Visual Cultures

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Deep research completed. Full profile: history, faculty, research centres, alumni, relationship to CCRU/accelerationism, Forensic Architecture, engagement paths for Amber.

**One of the world's most intellectually alive art-and-theory departments.** Founded 2002 by Irit Rogoff in the School of Art at Goldsmiths, University of London (New Cross, South East London). The inheritor of the CCRU diaspora, the incubator of Forensic Architecture, and the only institutional home of theory-fiction as academic praxis. Not a traditional art history department — it operates explicitly at the intersection of philosophy, critical theory, politics, technology, and contemporary art practice. **London-based: directly accessible for Amber.**

## Mission & Objectives

Visual Cultures understands itself as the study of **the intersection of visual forms with social, philosophical, political, and aesthetic concerns** — but with a militant edge. Research is guided by four principles explicitly framed as political commitments:

1. **Decentering, decolonizing** — anti-racist, feminist, queer, anti-ableist, decolonial approaches to art and visual culture worldwide
2. **Philosophical depth** — rigorous sustained engagement with continental philosophy and critical theory; concepts are as important as images
3. **Political approaches to aesthetics** — visual culture as inherently political; examining distributions of power within society
4. **Experimentation and innovation** — new research forms: investigative aesthetics, forensic architecture, fictioning, advanced practices

The department was consciously conceived as something new: not art history, not cultural studies, not media studies — but a transdisciplinary space where none of the rules of traditional disciplines applied. **Theory and practice deliberately entangled.**

## History & Founding

**1989–2002:** Irit Rogoff teaches at UC Davis, where she merges graduate programs in Art and Critical Theory under "Visual Culture." This becomes the template.

**2002:** Rogoff founds the Department of Visual Cultures at Goldsmiths. The timing is not accidental — the CCRU at Warwick had dissolved/imploded around 1999–2001, leaving a diaspora of theorists looking for institutional homes. Several CCRU-adjacent figures would find theirs at Goldsmiths Visual Cultures.

**2004–2005:** The department formally opens for undergraduate/postgraduate programs. Quickly builds an international reputation as a site where theory and contemporary art practice operate at the highest level of rigour.

**2010:** Eyal Weizman launches **Forensic Architecture** within the Centre for Research Architecture — a unit of Visual Cultures. This becomes one of the most globally significant research agencies of the 2010s, winning a Prix Ars Electronica (2017), Turner Prize nomination (2018), Peabody Award (2021).

**2020s:** The department now sits within the School of Art (merged with Fine Art and Curating programmes). Visual Cultures remains distinct — with its own research clusters, public programme, and PhD pathways. Luciana Parisi's departure to Duke creates a gap in the computational-speculative theory axis, but the department remains highly active. The department is currently in an especially interesting moment around **Digital Visual Cultures** (AI, algorithms, data, politics of digital image) — a cluster explicitly engaging with computational aesthetics and power.

## Research Centres Within Visual Cultures

### [Centre for Research Architecture (CRA)](https://www.gold.ac.uk/architecture/)
Founded by Eyal Weizman. Uses spatial practices to investigate politics, media, ecology, and human rights. Home to **Forensic Architecture** — the globally significant research agency that:
- Investigates state violence and human rights violations using architectural and media analysis
- Has produced evidence for UN, ICC, Amnesty International, international prosecutors
- Won Prix Ars Electronica 2017, Turner Prize nomination 2018, Peabody Award 2021
- Current director: Eyal Weizman; Centre Director: Susan Schuppli
- Runs its own MPhil/PhD programme with unique practice-research methodology

### [Centre for Art and Ecology](https://www.gold.ac.uk/research/centres-units/research-centre/centre-for-art-and-ecology/)
Supports practice-based interdisciplinary research, fostering new approaches to ecological issues. Active in [Critical Ecologies](https://critical-ecologies.org/) research stream. Responds to climate, Anthropocene, multispecies concerns.

### [Goldsmiths CCA (Centre for Contemporary Art)](https://goldsmithscca.art/)
Free public art gallery on campus. World-class exhibitions by international artists. Designed by Turner Prize winners Assemble — in a converted Victorian bathhouse. Creates feedback loop between student work, research, and international contemporary art.

## Research Clusters (Current)

Visual Cultures research is organised into six clusters:

1. **Situated Knowledges** — performativity, subjectivities, affects; anti-racist, anti-ableist, decolonial, feminist, queer, and trans scholarship; making of race, gender, sexuality, embodiment
2. **Spatial Practices and Architecture** — delivered via Centre for Research Architecture; legal frames, territorial divisions, environmental conditions, technoscience; field-work + digital tools (data visualisation, media mapping, terrain modelling, remote sensing)
3. **Planetary Aesthetics and Ecologies** — visual practices addressing climate change, environmental crises, ecological sustainability, human and non-human geographies; interdisciplinary arts-sciences
4. **Digital Visual Cultures** — art, media, and visual culture in relation to computation, algorithms, data, AI; financialisation and assetization of digital culture; digital labour; how digital technology reproduces racism; gender and sexuality in media/technology. **Most directly relevant to Amber's interests.**
5. **Curatorial and Institutional Practices** — curatorial practices shaped by institutional and social contexts; museum histories; exhibition-making; curatorial as philosophical thought
6. **Radical Archives: Memory, Narrative, Care** — memory, residual cultures, counter-history, resistance; how archives shape visual and other narratives

## Key Contributions

What Goldsmiths Visual Cultures has given the world:

1. **Forensic Architecture** (2010–) — arguably the most globally significant art-research agency of the 2010s; redefined how art intersects with human rights law, journalism, and politics
2. **Software Studies** as a discipline — Matthew Fuller's MIT Press series, with Lev Manovich, established the field
3. **Theory-fiction as academic form** — building on CCRU, institutionalising the genre
4. **Afrofuturism's academic legitimacy** — Eshun's "Further Considerations on Afrofuturism" (2003) gave the field its theoretical foundation
5. **Mark Fisher's Capitalist Realism** — the most widely read political-theoretical text of the 2010s emerged from Goldsmiths' orbit
6. **Decolonising the curriculum** in art history/visual culture — Goldsmiths Visual Cultures was among the first departments to make this structural rather than ornamental
7. **Curatorial/Knowledge PhD** — Rogoff's programme made curating a serious research methodology, influencing institutions globally

## CCRU Connection: The Living Legacy

The department is, in significant part, the institutional outcome of the CCRU diaspora. After the CCRU dissolved at Warwick (~1999–2001), its key members scattered:

- **Kodwo Eshun** → Goldsmiths Visual Cultures (teaching since ~2002)
- **Luciana Parisi** → Goldsmiths Visual Cultures (later → Duke)
- **Mark Fisher** → Goldsmiths-adjacent orbit → k-punk → Zero Books → Repeater Books
- **Steve Goodman (Kode9)** → [[Kode9]] → Hyperdub, music production, separately published *Sonic Warfare* (MIT Press, 2010)
- **Robin Mackay** → [[Robin Mackay]] → [[Urbanomic]], *Collapse* journal

The CCRU's core obsessions — accelerationism, theory-fiction, hyperstition, Afrofuturism, non-linear temporality, technology as philosophical problem — all found homes in Goldsmiths Visual Cultures. The department is not simply influenced by the CCRU; it is where the CCRU's intellectual project was academically institutionalised and domesticated (for better or worse).

**For Amber:** This means Visual Cultures is simultaneously one of the most theoretically rigorous and most politically alive departments in the UK. The theory-fiction genre, Afrofuturism, speculative computation (Parisi's work), and investigative aesthetics are all directly relevant to her research.

## Why Interesting

**Goldsmiths Visual Cultures is the institutional crystallisation of the most interesting intellectual current in British art-theory from the 1990s to present.** It represents a model of how to build a department that is simultaneously theoretically serious, politically committed, aesthetically adventurous, and institutionally powerful.

The **Digital Visual Cultures** cluster suggests it is actively re-orienting toward the questions that matter most right now.
