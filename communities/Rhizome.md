# Rhizome

The premier American nonprofit for born-digital and net art. Founded 1996 in Berlin by [[Mark Tribe]] as an email list for digital culture; now the field's most important institutional infrastructure — commissioning, exhibiting, criticizing, and preserving art that lives at the intersection of technology and culture. Located at 235 Bowery, New York; affiliated with the [[New Museum]] since 2003. Uniquely double-tracked: equal parts cultural organization and open-source software builder (Webrecorder, Conifer, OldWeb.Today). Currently turning 30.

## History

### 1996–1999: Email List to Nonprofit
- **February 1, 1996:** Mark Tribe, living in Berlin, starts the Rhizome email list (hosted by Desk.nl, Amsterdam). First subscribers include people he met at [[Ars Electronica]]. Vision: "Artforum meets AltaVista — a bottom-up alternative to the top-down art world." Not a curated magazine; a community discussion list.
- **August 1996:** Website launched. Significant readership by 1998 within internet art community.
- **1998:** Becomes a proper nonprofit organization (.org). Originally registered as a business.
- **1999:** **ArtBase** launched — first online archive of net art. Rachel Greene becomes Executive Director.

### 2000–2009: Institutionalization
- **2001:** **Commissions Program** founded to support artists working with technology.
- **2003:** Rhizome becomes affiliated with the New Museum of Contemporary Art, NYC. Moves to the New Museum building at 235 Bowery. The affiliation enables New Museum resources but creates institutional tensions.
- **2005:** **[[Lauren Cornell]]** becomes Executive Director. Co-curates "ArtBase 101" with Rachel Greene — first major New Museum exhibition, 40 works from ArtBase. Artists include John F. Simon Jr., 0100101110101101.org, Young-Hae Chang Heavy Industries, Cory Arcangel.
- **2007:** Lauren Cornell also becomes Adjunct Curator at New Museum, beginning dual role.
- **2008:** Commissions Program scope expands from internet-only art to the full range of new media art: performance, video, installation, sound.

### 2010–2015: Seven on Seven Era
- **2010:** **Seven on Seven** conference launched. Co-founded by Lauren Cornell, Fred Benenson, John Borthwick, and Peter Rojas. Inspired by Experiments in Art and Technology (E.A.T.) — Billy Klüver and Robert Rauschenberg's Bell Labs artist-engineer collaborations (1967). Annual event at New Museum.
- **2011:** Joanne McNeil becomes editor.
- **2012:** Lauren Cornell steps down as Executive Director (July 2012) to co-curate New Museum's Triennial. Joanne McNeil edits *The Best of Rhizome 2012* (LINK Editions). Heather Corcoran briefly becomes Executive Director. Zachary Kaplan becomes Executive Director; Michael Connor becomes Artistic Director.
- **2014:** Colloq social media archiving tool developed by Ilya Kreymer and [[Dragan Espenschied]]. First use: Amalia Ulman's "Excellences and Perfections" Instagram project. In 2015, used to archive the influential art blog VVORK.
- **2015:** OldWeb.Today launched (December) — browse archived webpages inside emulated legacy browsers (Mosaic, Netscape Navigator, IE, Firefox, Chrome). Site relaunched with design by Wieden+Kennedy.

### 2016–2020: Webrecorder and Net Art Anthology
- **2016:** Net Art Anthology launched (October) — 2-year online exhibition restaging 100 key artworks from net art history, one per week. Opening panel at New Museum with Olia Lialina, Martha Wilson, Mark Tribe, Ricardo Dominguez. Major editorial/curatorial project.
- **2016 (August):** Webrecorder.io public release — free browser-based web archiving tool. Funded by $600K Andrew W. Mellon Foundation grant. Symmetrical web archiving: same software records and plays back. Records network traffic as user browses; captures interactive features unlike traditional crawlers.
- **2016 (September):** [[Aria Dean]] appointed Assistant Curator of Net Art and Digital Culture.
- **2017:** Webrecorder receives $1 million from Andrew W. Mellon Foundation.
- **2018:** Commissions Program has awarded 100+ commissions total.
- **2020 (June):** Webrecorder.io renamed to **Conifer**. Ilya Kreymer splits **Webrecorder** off as separate entity (webrecorder.net) — open-source software toolkit for web archiving.
- **2020 (November):** Board member Seth Stolbun resigns in protest over New Museum workplace culture (NYT report on toxic environment, verbal harassment, financial opacity). Rhizome board chair Greg Pass defends the affiliation.

### 2021–present: Transition
- **2021 (January):** Aria Dean steps down as Curator and Editor.
- **2022:** Seven on Seven returns after COVID hiatus.
- **2025 (December 15):** Rhizome announces Conifer service discontinuation in June 2026. Significant shift — pulls back from hosting infrastructure, may focus more on software development via the Webrecorder project.
- **2026:** Rhizome's 30th anniversary year.

## Mission & Objectives
"Rhizome supports born-digital art and culture through commissions, preservation, criticism, and community engagement."

What makes Rhizome unusual:
1. **Preservation as art:** Net art is technically difficult to preserve (software rots, browsers change, platforms die). Rhizome doesn't just archive — it builds tools to *replay* the original experience.
2. **Artist-submitted archive:** The ArtBase is self-submitted by artists themselves, not curated by Rhizome alone. This creates a community record.
3. **Critical writing at a high level:** Rhizome publishes actual criticism, not press releases.
4. **Software as mission:** Building open-source infrastructure (Webrecorder) is core to the mission, not a side project.

## Key Programmes & Initiatives

### ArtBase
<https://rhizome.org/art/artbase/>

The field's most important archive of net art. Founded 1999. Contains **2,110+ artworks** (as of 2016 count; growing). Artists self-submit. Materials: software, code, websites, moving image, games, browsers, interdisciplinary works with online elements.

Key features:
- Works submitted and described by artists themselves
- Rhizome adds conservation work: updating obsolete code, emulation, documentation
- Scope expanded beyond internet-only to include games, software art, browser art
- ArtBase 101 exhibition (2005) was a landmark attempt to show net art in gallery context

### Seven on Seven
<https://rhizome.org/sevenonseven/>

Annual conference (held since 2010) pairing seven leading contemporary artists with seven technology innovators. Challenge: create something new in one day. Results premiered at New Museum. Not a hackathon — the products range from artworks to prototypes to polemics.

**Format:** 24-hour collaboration, presentation at New Museum on the day.

**Founding:** Co-founded by Lauren Cornell, Fred Benenson (Kickstarter), John Borthwick (Betaworks), Peter Rojas (Engadget/GDGT). Inspired by E.A.T. (Experiments in Art and Technology), the Bell Labs artist-engineer programme (1967).

**Sponsors:** Ace Hotel, HTC, Deutsche Bank, GIPHY, Wieden+Kennedy (founding partner), Kickstarter, Phillips, Spring Place.

**Notable pairings and outcomes (selected):**
- **2012:** Taryn Simon (artist) + Aaron Swartz (programmer/activist) → **Image Atlas** — aggregate search engine comparing image results from 17 countries' search engines for the same term. Exposed how search engines filter the internet differently by geography/censorship. Now haunted by Swartz's suicide (Jan 2013).
- **2012(?):** Monegraph created — an early NFT-like system for registering digital work ownership on Bitcoin blockchain. Significant precursor to the NFT market.
- **2013(?):** Laura Poitras (filmmaker) + ??? → short documentary for The New York Times.
- **2016:** Miranda July (filmmaker/artist) + Paul Ford (writer/developer); Jennifer Steinkamp (artist) + Rana El-Kaliouby (Affectiva/emotion AI); Claire Evans (YACHT, musician/writer) + Tracy Chou (Pinterest engineer/diversity advocate); Jenna Wortham (NYT) + JUNGLEPUSSY (rapper); Ingrid Burrington (artist) + Meredith Whittaker (Google Open Research/later AI Now Institute co-founder).

**All known artist participants:** Evan Roth, Aaron Koblin, Monica Narula, Ryan Trecartin, Tauba Auerbach, Marc Andre Robinson, Kristin Lucas, Michael Bell-Smith, Ricardo Cabello (mr.doob), Liz Magic Laser, Zach Lieberman, Rashaad Newsome, Ryder Ripps, Camille Utterback, Emily Royston, Aram Bartholl, Xavier Cha, LaToya Ruby Frazier, Naeem Mohaiemen, Jon Rafman, Taryn Simon, Stephanie Syjuco, Miranda July, Jennifer Steinkamp, Claire Evans, Jenna Wortham, JUNGLEPUSSY, Ingrid Burrington.

**All known technologist participants:** Jeff Hammerbacher, Joshua Schachter, Matt Mullenweg, Andrew Kortina, Hilary Mason, Ayah Bdeir, David Karp, Andy Baio, Ben Cerveny, Jeri Ellsworth, Kellan Elliott-McCrea, Bre Pettis, Chris Poole (moot), Erica Sadun, Jeremy Ashkenas, Blaine Cook, Michael Herf, Charles Forman, Aaron Swartz, Grant Olney Passmore, Khoi Vinh, Anthony Volodkin, Paul Ford, Rana El-Kaliouby, Tracy Chou, Meredith Whittaker.

**Reading the participant list as a cultural signal:** The technologist list reads as early-2010s NYC/SF tech elite (moot/4chan, David Karp/Tumblr, Matt Mullenweg/WordPress, Bre Pettis/MakerBot, Andrew Kortina/Venmo). Shifted by 2016 to include critical voices (Meredith Whittaker, later AI Now Institute). Artist participants span net art canon (Evan Roth, Aram Bartholl) and contemporary expanded practice (Miranda July, Taryn Simon). Seven on Seven is not "tech meets art for marketing" — it's a genuine cultural experiment.

### Webrecorder / Conifer / OldWeb.Today
<https://webrecorder.net/>

Rhizome's preservation technology suite:

**Colloq** (2014–2015): Social media interface archiving. Replicates the visual experience of social media at time of capture — not just the content but the UI. Built by Ilya Kreymer and Dragan Espenschied. First use: Amalia Ulman's "Excellences and Perfections" (Instagram project, 2014). Used to archive VVORK art blog (2015).

**Webrecorder.io → Conifer** (2016–2026): Browser-based web archiving. Symmetrical approach: same software records and replays. Records network traffic while user browses, capturing dynamic/interactive content. Funded by Andrew W. Mellon Foundation ($600K in 2016, $1M in 2018). Renamed Conifer in June 2020. Being discontinued June 2026.

**OldWeb.Today** (<https://oldweb.today/>): Browse archived webpages inside emulated legacy browsers — Mosaic, Netscape Navigator, Internet Explorer, Firefox, Chrome, Ruffle (Flash). "Emulation as a Service." Launched December 2015. Still maintained by Webrecorder project.

**Webrecorder** (independent entity, 2020–): Ilya Kreymer split off as separate open-source organization. Tools: WACZ file format (portable web archives), ReplayWeb.page (play back archives anywhere). Open-source community, used by libraries, archives, journalists.

Technical significance: Rhizome identified that conventional web crawlers miss interactive content (JavaScript, Flash, social media, authentication-required content). Their approach — record as you browse — is technically distinct and more complete for art preservation.

### Commissions Programme
<https://rhizome.org/commissions/>

Founded 2001. 100+ commissions as of 2016. Scope expanded 2008 from internet-only to full new media range (performance, video, installation, sound, web).

**Selected commissioned artists:** Heba Amin, Aleksandra Domanović, Aram Bartholl, Knifeandfork (Brian House + Sue Huang), Mendi & Keith Obadike, [[Trevor Paglen]], Jon Rafman, Tao Lin, Tristan Perich, Angelo Plessas, Brody Condon, Jona Bechtolt, Kristin Lucas, Evan Roth, Rafaël Rozendaal, eteam, Steve Lambert, Zach Lieberman, Ryota Matsumoto, Porpentine (game designer).

Commission sizes not publicly specified but consistent with a small nonprofit budget.

### Net Art Anthology (2016–2018)
<https://anthology.rhizome.org>

Two-year online exhibition restaging 100 key artworks from net art history — one per week. Used Rhizome's own preservation tools to make each work accessible again. Major editorial/scholarly project; each artwork presented with contextual essays. Launched October 27, 2016 at New Museum with panel: Olia Lialina, Martha Wilson, Mark Tribe, Ricardo Dominguez.

This is the closest thing to a net art canon — 100 works selected as historically significant, with essays explaining why.

### Critical Writing / Editorial
<https://rhizome.org/editorial/>

Rhizome publishes original critical writing on digital art and culture at a high level. Key voices include Michael Connor (Artistic Director). Has published writing by [[Aria Dean]] on race and internet culture ("Poor Meme, Rich Meme"; "Notes on Blacceleration" context). The editorial platform distinguishes Rhizome from purely archival/exhibition institutions.

Annual publication: *The Best of Rhizome* (Joanne McNeil edited 2012 edition, published LINK Editions).

## For Amber

### Direct Research Relevance

**1. Seven on Seven as Model**
This is the most directly relevant thing for Amber's work. Seven on Seven is a sustained, 14-year experiment in what happens when artists and technologists collaborate with *no predetermined outcome*. Some results are artworks; some are prototypes; some are just provocations. The format (two people, one day, something new) removes the scaffolding of institutional expectation. Compare to what Amber studies: how do AI agents behave when embedded in social situations without scripts?

**2. Meredith Whittaker Connection**
Whittaker participated in Seven on Seven (2016, paired with Ingrid Burrington). Subsequently co-founded AI Now Institute — which is about machine behavior, surveillance, power, and labor exactly in Amber's critical computing territory. Rhizome created the context that connected an artist and a tech critical researcher. Worth noting: this is how Rhizome functions as infrastructure — it doesn't just show art, it creates conditions for intellectual emergence.

**3. Preservation as Critical Practice**
Rhizome's approach to preservation (you can't just crawl; you have to experience and record) raises questions directly relevant to AI art: what does it mean to preserve an AI-generated work? A generative system? An agent-based art piece? Rhizome has been working on this longer than anyone, and their conclusions (emulation, symmetrical recording, WACZ format) are relevant to anyone thinking about long-term digital art.

**4. Aria Dean's Writing on Race and Technology**
Dean's essays "Poor Meme, Rich Meme," "Notes on Blacceleration" (e-flux), and collection *Bad Infinity* (MIT Press, 2023) represent serious critical writing on the intersection of Blackness, technology, and media culture. This is not adjacent to Amber's research on agents — it's the exact frame of critique that matters when thinking about who agents serve, whose behavior they model, and what cultural logics they embed.

**5. Net Art as History of "Agents on the Internet"**
Net art 1995–2005 is essentially the history of autonomous digital agents interacting with people and systems. Many works in the ArtBase involve scripts, bots, automated processes, procedural systems. The ArtBase is a historical record of what artists imagined machines could do before the age of ML. Rhizome's preservation work keeps this history accessible. This is field-defining archaeology for AI art history.

**6. Critical Tech Connections**
The Seven on Seven alumni network includes significant critical tech voices: Meredith Whittaker (AI Now → Signal), Tracy Chou (diversity in tech), Hilary Mason (data scientist who became thoughtful about ethics), Ayah Bdeir (LittleBits, making electronics accessible). These are the people who bridge technical ability with critical awareness — exactly the profile Amber is interested in.

## Why Interesting

Rhizome is irreplaceable as:
1. **The net art institution** — the ArtBase is where this history lives
2. **The serious art-tech critical platform** — not breathless cheerleading, actual criticism
3. **Software infrastructure for digital preservation** — Webrecorder is used by institutions globally
4. **Career infrastructure** — the alumni pathways (Cornell → CCS Bard; Dean → Greene Naftali; McNeil → book authorship) show that Rhizome is a genuine career accelerator for people at the art-tech intersection
5. **Seven on Seven as cultural signal** — the participant list each year is a readout of who Rhizome thinks matters; attending or being invited is significant

The Conifer shutdown (June 2026) is a moment of organizational stress — watch what Rhizome does next. The software-heavy phase (2014–2025) may be ending; a return to pure commissioning and criticism, or a pivot toward something new.
