# Karrabing Film Collective

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-27:** Stub created during EYE Filmmuseum deep research. EYE Art & Film Prize winner 2021.

Indigenous media collective founded in 2008, based in Northern Territory, Australia. Approximately 30 members, primarily from the Emmiyengal and neighboring language groups. Self-described grassroots activist collective; creates films and video installations as a form of **political self-organization and cultural survival**. The word "karrabing" means "low tide" in Emmiyengal — a form of collectivity outside government-imposed structures of clanship or land ownership.

## Mission & Objectives

- Keep members' own lands strong by strengthening family, environmental, and totemic relations
- Create films that represent their lives, land, and culture from the inside — not through outsider ethnographic gaze
- Challenge Australian government land and welfare policies through satirical and political films
- Develop "improvisational realism" as an artistic/political methodology

## The "Improvisational Realism" Methodology

Their films are shot on handheld cameras and phones, featuring collective members as actors in improvised scenarios drawn from their actual lived experiences with government and corporate entities. Stories blend:
- Fictional and documentary
- Past and present
- Indigenous mythology and contemporary political reality
- Satire and genuine political urgency

The result bypasses the fiction/documentary binary entirely. "Improvisational realism" as a concept is a genuine contribution to film theory — not just a description of their process but a critique of both Hollywood narrative and traditional ethnographic documentary.

## Why Interesting

**For Amber's more-than-human + critical computing + decolonial threads:** Karrabing is doing something radical — building a media practice that explicitly refuses Western film grammar while using the tools of cinema (cameras, editing, screenings). Their "improvisational realism" is a critique of representation itself, from within an indigenous ontology that treats land, ancestors, and totemic beings as agents.

The "low tide" concept (collective living outside government-imposed structures) is a useful model for thinking about non-state, non-corporate collectives organizing around shared resources and care — adjacent to Amber's interest in democratic design and participatory systems.

Their work is **not technologically experimental** in the new media art sense, but politically and philosophically experimental — which makes it a useful counterpoint to Amber's computational practice.
