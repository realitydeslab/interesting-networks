# Soma Design Research Community

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Full deep research. Expanded from stub to complete profile. History, methodology, global network, key people, Amber's direct connection through "Somaesthetic Realities" at CAA.
- **2026-02-26:** Created stub during Kia Höök research. International network working on somaesthetic interaction design.

The world's most developed body-centered design research community. Founded and led by [[Kia Höök]] at KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm, the soma design community translates Richard Shusterman's philosophical somaesthetics into concrete design practice — methods, projects, papers, and a flourishing international network. The core provocation: interaction design has been dominated by cognition, screens, and instrumentality; soma design insists the body and its felt sense must be the primary design material.

**Amber's direct connection:** Amber teaches "Somaesthetic Realities" at China Academy of Art — a course whose title and content are directly in the soma design lineage. This community is not adjacent to Amber's work — it is a methodological parent of it.

---

## Mission & Objectives

Soma design foregrounds **felt bodily experience** as both the material and method of interaction design. Against the cognitive-instrumental tradition of HCI (where bodies are input devices and computers are tools), soma design insists:

1. **The body knows things the mind doesn't** — interoception, felt sense, proprioception are legitimate forms of knowledge
2. **Designers must inhabit the body** — somatic practice (feldenkrais, pilates, yoga, dance) is research methodology
3. **Interaction is somatic encounter** — when humans engage with computational systems, the whole body is implicated
4. **Design from the inside out** — not "designing for the body" from external ergonomic criteria, but designing *with* the body from within felt experience

The community sits at the intersection of philosophy (Shusterman's somaesthetics), design research (HCI, DIS, TEI), and embodied practice traditions (movement arts, martial arts, mindfulness). It is simultaneously a theoretical framework, a research methodology, and a design practice.

## History

### The Philosophical Ancestor: Shusterman's Somaesthetics
[**Richard Shusterman**](https://www.richardshusterman.com/) (Florida Atlantic University), American pragmatist philosopher, introduced somaesthetics in his 1992 book *Pragmatist Aesthetics* and expanded it in *Body Consciousness: A Philosophy of Mindfulness and Somaesthetics* (Cambridge University Press, 2008). The core insight: the living body (*soma*) is not merely a physical object but a site of sensory appreciation and self-cultivation. Somaesthetics is both a *descriptive* discipline (studying somatic experience) and a *meliorative* one (improving it).

Shusterman distinguishes three forms:
- **Analytic somaesthetics** — theory of somatic experience
- **Pragmatic somaesthetics** — comparative evaluation of somatic disciplines (pilates, feldenkrais, yoga, etc.)
- **Practical somaesthetics** — hands-on somatic practice as philosophy

This philosophical framework gave Höök the vocabulary for what she was already doing computationally — designing systems that engage and cultivate bodily awareness.

### The HCI Predecessor: Dourish's Embodied Interaction
[[Paul Dourish]]'s *[Where the Action Is: The Foundations of Embodied Interaction](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262541787/)* (MIT Press, 2001) brought Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology into HCI. Dourish argued that interaction is inherently embodied — computing happens in a world that we inhabit with our bodies, not just our minds. This opened the theoretical space for soma design, but Dourish's work remained primarily theoretical. Höök's soma design project is the methodological extension: not just *understanding* embodied interaction philosophically, but developing concrete *design methods* for creating embodied interaction systems.

### Kia Höök and SICS: Origins (1990s–2000s)
Kia Höök received her PhD from Stockholm University in 1996 (thesis: "A Glass Box Approach to Adaptive Hyper-Media") and joined the Swedish Institute of Computer Science (SICS), where she won the [ERCIM Cor Baayen Fellowship](https://www.ercim.eu/corfellowship) in 1997 — Europe's most prestigious young researcher award in informatics. Her early work focused on personalized and social navigation in information space, affective computing, and adaptive systems.

Her path into soma design traces through **affective computing** — she became increasingly interested in how emotion and bodily experience are implicated in human-computer interaction. The "Affective Diary" project (early 2000s) collected physiological signals (heart rate, accelerometer) and used them as materials for diary-keeping and self-reflection — early evidence of her direction.

By the mid-2000s, Höök was developing what would become soma design: systems that engage with and cultivate bodily awareness rather than just measuring bodies as data sources.

### KTH and Field Formalization (2012–2018)
Höök joined **KTH Royal Institute of Technology** as Professor in 2012, in the Media Technology and Interaction Design (MID) department. This gave her an institutional home for the soma design community.

Key papers establishing the framework:
- **"Strong Concepts"** (Höök & Löwgren, 2012) — introduced the notion of "strong concepts" as intermediate-level design theory: between concrete design examples and high-level philosophical theory. This became the methodological vocabulary for soma design's theoretical contribution mode.
- **"Somaesthetic Appreciation Design"** (CHI 2016) — first major publication explicitly naming the somaesthetic design approach, with Jonsson, Ståhl, and Mercurio.

**[*Designing with the Body: Somaesthetic Interaction Design*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/designing-with-body)** (MIT Press, 2018) consolidated the field. This is not a methods manual or design handbook — it is a synthesis of philosophy, design research, and practice-based knowledge that declares a new subdiscipline within HCI. The book has become the definitive text: any interaction designer working with embodiment and the body must engage with it.

### Recognition and Expansion (2019–present)
- **2020:** Höök elected to the [ACM SIGCHI CHI Academy](https://sigchi.org/about/sigchi-academy/) — the highest honor in HCI research
- **2022:** Honorary Doctorate from University of Gothenburg
- **2025:** [ACM SIGCHI Lifetime Service Award](https://sigchi.org/people/sigchi-awards/) — alongside this, TWO KTH soma design community members received 2025 SIGCHI Special Recognitions: [[Nadia Campo Woytuk]] (intimate body-sensing, feminist HCI) and **[[Jooyoung Park]]** (feminist HCI, haptic technology, embodied care). Two Outstanding Dissertation Awards went to researchers now at KTH: Katerina Stepanova and Anupriya Tuli. The 2025 SIGCHI awards were a soma design sweep.

## Soma Design Methodology: What It Actually Is

Soma design is not a style or an aesthetic — it is a **first-person, practice-based design research methodology** with specific methods:

### 1. First-Person Somatic Practice
Designers must cultivate their own somatic awareness as a research tool. This means practicing somatic disciplines — feldenkrais, pilates, yoga, dance, martial arts, contact improvisation. Not as fitness, but as investigation of how movement, breath, posture, and sensation constitute bodily knowledge. You cannot design for the body without inhabiting your own body more fully.

### 2. Felt Sense as Design Material
"Felt sense" (a concept from philosopher Eugene Gendlin) — the diffuse, bodily sense of a situation, before it becomes explicit emotion or thought — is treated as a designable property. Soma design systems are judged by whether they cultivate, disturb, or enrich the user's felt sense.

### 3. Strong Concepts
Soma design research often produces **strong concepts** — not just working prototypes, but conceptual contributions with generative power across many design contexts. Example: "breathing as interaction" is a strong concept that applies to many system designs beyond any specific breathing interface.

### 4. Design Exemplars as Theory
Individual design projects are not just demonstrations — they are theoretical claims made material. The "Soma Mat" (a textile that tracks and responds to breathing and body position) is simultaneously a product, a performance, and an argument about what interaction can be when the body is centered.

### 5. Annotated Portfolios
Collections of design examples, annotated to reveal their conceptual core. The annotation practice is itself a research methodology — it surfaces implicit design knowledge. (This method connects to [[DIS Community]]'s Research Through Design tradition.)

### 6. Collaboration Across Body-Mind Boundary
Soma design research regularly collaborates with somatic practitioners — feldenkrais teachers, dance choreographers, movement therapists — treating their expertise as legitimate research knowledge, not mere inspiration.

### Key Canonical Examples
- **Soma Mat** — touch-responsive textile that maps body position and breathing; designed to cultivate somatic awareness during movement practice
- **Breathing Light** — ambient installation where room lighting responds to the user's breathing pattern; shifts attention from visual-cognitive to somatic-respiratory
- **Affective Diary** — early project using physiological signals (heart rate, galvanic skin response) as diary entries; pioneer in treating bodily data as expressive material
- **Horse-rider interaction** — research using equestrian interaction as a model for subtle, somatic communication between bodies (human and animal) — anticipates more-than-human design directions

## Key Contributions

What soma design has given the field of HCI:

1. **A methodology for first-person bodily design research** — not just philosophical theory of embodiment (Dourish) but *how to design* from within embodied experience. Feldenkrais as research method.

2. **Strong concepts as design theory** — the "strong concept" framework enables soma design to contribute theory that is neither pure philosophy nor pure engineering. It operates at an intermediate level: applicable across contexts, generative of new designs.

3. **Felt sense as design criterion** — the shift from "usability" and "user experience" to "felt sense" introduces a fundamentally different evaluative vocabulary. Does this system cultivate interoceptive awareness? Does it disturb or enrich felt sense?

4. **Somatic practice as research** — treating feldenkrais, pilates, movement arts as legitimate epistemic tools for design research. This is radical within the HCI tradition and productively imports knowledge from movement sciences.

5. **Body-material interaction design** — designing textiles, surfaces, and ambient systems that engage the body's proprioceptive and tactile channels. The Soma Mat lineage.

6. **Feminist extension** — the newer generation of KTH soma design researchers (Campo Woytuk, Jooyoung Park) have brought feminist theory to soma design: whose body counts? How are gendered and racialized bodies included or excluded in somatic design?

## Network Analysis: Soma Design's Position in the Ecology

### Within HCI
Soma design is a **sub-community within HCI** (not a separate field) that has successfully claimed intellectual territory through:
- Methodological distinctiveness (first-person somatic practice)
- Institutional anchoring (KTH MID department)
- Definitive text (MIT Press 2018 book)
- Conference presence (CHI, DIS, TEI)

### Relationship to Embodied Interaction (Dourish)
Soma design extends Dourish's embodied interaction in a specific direction: **from philosophy to methodology**. Dourish tells us WHY the body matters in computing; Höök tells us HOW to design *for and with* the body. The genealogy: Heidegger + Merleau-Ponty → Dourish (phenomenological HCI) → Shusterman (somaesthetics) → Höök (soma design methodology). Soma design is the design-practice layer that phenomenological HCI theory lacked.

### Relationship to [[TEI Community]]
TEI (Tangible, Embedded, Embodied Interaction) is soma design's natural hardware/materials venue. TEI papers tend to feature concrete systems (tangible interfaces, e-textiles, haptic devices). Soma design is well-represented at TEI because its design exemplars (Soma Mat, Breathing Light) are physical systems. But TEI is a venue, not a methodology — soma design as methodology spans across TEI, CHI, and DIS.

### Relationship to [[DIS Community]]
DIS is soma design's natural methodology/theory venue. Within DIS, the Soma Design sub-community is part of the Research Through Design (RtD) track. DIS's Pictorials format is ideal for documenting soma design projects visually. The DIS ethos (design as critique, design as inquiry) aligns with soma design's refusal of pure instrumentality.

### Relationship to More-than-Human Design
Soma design and more-than-human design are convergent trajectories within HCI. Soma design: body as more-than-cognitive agent. More-than-human design: world as more-than-human agent. The horse-rider interaction work in soma design is explicitly more-than-human (animal bodies in interaction). The feminist turn in soma design (Campo Woytuk, Jooyoung Park) brings in consideration of whose body, raising questions parallel to more-than-human design's "whose world?"

### Relationship to [[Design+Posthumanism Network]]
The newest KTH soma design researchers (Campo Woytuk, Søndergaard) overlap significantly with the D+PH Network — feminist posthumanist design that asks whose body, whose senses, whose fluids are centered in design. Soma design is moving toward posthumanism through its feminist turn.

## Why Interesting

Soma design represents a **methodological bet** that has paid off: that taking the body seriously as a site of knowledge — not just a sensor array — would generate better interaction design. Höök's bet has been vindicated by the 2025 SIGCHI awards sweep (Lifetime Service Award + two Special Recognitions from KTH soma design) and by the fact that embodied design is now mainstream in the best design conferences.

But the more interesting question for 2026 is: **where does soma design go next?**

The feminist turn (Campo Woytuk, Jooyoung Park) is actively extending soma design to include whose body, in whose terms, caring for what. The more-than-human extension (horse-rider research, animal bodies) pushes beyond human soma. And Amber's own "Somaesthetic Realities" course at CAA is an instance of soma design crossing into Chinese art education — an unstudied geography for soma design.

**The edge of chaos:** Soma design is not new anymore (the 2018 book consolidated the field), but the feminist and more-than-human extensions are. The convergence of soma design + feminist posthumanism + more-than-human design is the hot new territory, and the KTH community is right on it.

For Amber, this is not a community to observe — it is a community to join.
