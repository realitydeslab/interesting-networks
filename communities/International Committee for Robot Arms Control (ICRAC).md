# International Committee for Robot Arms Control (ICRAC)

International civil society organization of academics, activists, and legal advisors advocating for international prohibition on autonomous weapons systems ("killer robots"). Founded by Peter Asaro, Noel Sharkey, and others. Works in parallel with the UN Stop Killer Robots Campaign and participates in the UN's Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons (CCW).

## Mission & Objectives
Advocates for international prohibition on the development, deployment, and use of autonomous weapons systems capable of lethal targeting without meaningful human control. Works through UN policy channels, academic publishing, and public advocacy.

## Key People
- **[Peter Asaro](https://www.icrac.net/members/peter-asaro/)** — Chair, Co-Founder. New School, New York. Media philosopher specializing in AI ethics and autonomous weapons.
- **[Noel Sharkey](https://www.icrac.net/members/noel-sharkey/)** — Emeritus Chair and Co-Founder. University of Sheffield roboticist.
- **[[Lucy Suchman]]** — **Vice-Chair**. Lancaster University. HCI/STS foundational figure. Links HCI expertise (situated action, human judgment) to weapons policy.
- **[Jürgen Altmann](https://www.icrac.net/members/jurgen-altmann/)** — Vice-Chair and Co-Founder. Technische Universität Dortmund. Physics/arms control.

## Why Interesting
ICRAC represents the intersection of critical HCI/STS scholarship with live international weapons policy. It's a case where academic theory (Suchman's situated action critique of AI planning) directly informs arms control advocacy. The argument against autonomous weapons (that they can't make situated, contextual judgments about human life) is exactly the argument of *Plans and Situated Actions*, applied to the highest possible stakes. For Amber, this shows one trajectory for critical AI/HCI scholarship — into policy.
