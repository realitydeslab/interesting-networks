# International Committee for Robot Arms Control (ICRAC)

An international civil society organization of academics, activists, engineers and legal advisors — founded 2009 at the University of Sheffield — advocating for the prohibition and regulation of autonomous weapons systems ("killer robots"). ICRAC operates at the intersection of technical expertise and political advocacy, influencing UN arms control processes through expert testimony, formal statements, and academic publishing. A co-founding member of the broader [Stop Killer Robots](https://www.stopkillerrobots.org/) campaign. The intellectual home for the argument that machines cannot make situated moral judgments — connecting foundational HCI/STS theory (Suchman's *Plans and Situated Actions*) to live international law debates.

## Mission & Objectives

ICRAC's founding mission (Sheffield Declaration, 2009):
- **Prohibit** the development, deployment and use of armed autonomous unmanned systems — machines should not be allowed to decide to kill people
- **Limit** the range and payload of "man in the loop" unmanned systems
- **Ban** arming unmanned systems with nuclear weapons
- **Prohibit** the development of robotic space weapons

The Berlin Statement (2010) added: autonomy in warfare "may further the indiscriminate and disproportionate use of force and obscure the moral and legal responsibility for war crimes."

ICRAC engages primarily through:
1. **UN Convention on Certain Conventional Weapons (CCW)** — expert panel participation, Geneva meetings
2. **UNGA 1st Committee** — formal statements on lethal autonomous weapons (most recently May 2025)
3. **Submissions to UN Secretary-General** — in response to resolutions on AI and military domain
4. **Academic publishing** — special journal issues, books, papers
5. **Stop Killer Robots Campaign** — ICRAC is a co-founding member, with members serving as Campaign Liaisons

## Key Contributions

1. **Founding the academic wing of the killer robots prohibition campaign** — ICRAC was established 2009, four years before the Stop Killer Robots Campaign (2013), providing intellectual legitimacy and technical expertise.
2. **Translating STS theory into arms control arguments** — Lucy Suchman's situated action critique (originally about photocopiers and office work) became the core argument against autonomous targeting: machines cannot exercise the contextual, situated judgment required by international humanitarian law.
3. **Expert access to UN CCW processes** — ICRAC members participate as NGO observers and expert panelists at the CCW Group of Governmental Experts, the primary UN forum on LAWS.
4. **Project Maven resistance** — Laura Nolan's prominent resignation from Google over Project Maven brought ICRAC's arguments into the tech worker ethics movement.
5. **Interdisciplinary coalition-building** — uniquely bridges roboticists, philosophers, IR scholars, legal experts, and NGO activists in one organization.

## Why Interesting

ICRAC is one of the clearest existing examples of **academic research with direct, live policy consequence**. The intellectual argument at its core — that autonomous systems cannot exercise the situated, contextual judgment required for lawful and ethical action — is structurally identical to arguments in Amber's own research territory (machine behavior, sovereign agents, more-than-human AI). 

Specifically: ICRAC's central claim is that meaningful human control cannot be delegated to machines because machines lack the situated understanding of context that humans exercise in moral/legal decisions. This is the *Plans and Situated Actions* argument applied at the highest stakes. Amber's work on agent ethology, machine behavior, and sovereign agents is directly relevant — empirical study of how autonomous agents actually behave in the wild is exactly what arms control debates need but currently lack.

ICRAC also represents a community type that's worth knowing: **expert NGO as research infrastructure** — a small group of academics who maintain policy relevance through a combination of credibility (peer-reviewed publication) and access (UN accreditation). Not a lab, not a conference, not a university — but a self-organized civil society organization that creates its own influence.

For Amber's ERA fellowship trajectory and existential risk-adjacent work, ICRAC sits directly in the AI safety / AI governance space with a specifically arms-control and international law focus.
