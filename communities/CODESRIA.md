# CODESRIA — Council for the Development of Social Science Research in Africa

The oldest and most important pan-African social science institution. Founded 1973 in Dakar, Senegal. Produces the theory, fellowships, journals, and intellectual infrastructure that anchors serious African social science globally. Receives funding from Ford Foundation, Carnegie, Norad (Norway), Sida (Sweden), and the Mastercard Foundation. Unusually, CODESRIA insists on including North Africa in its definition of "Africa" — most organizations ignore the Maghreb. Won the [Prince Claus Award](https://princeclausfund.org/) in 1997.

Connection to decolonial AI, epistemic justice, and Global South knowledge infrastructure makes it peripherally but genuinely relevant to Amber's critical computing work. CODESRIA is also where [[Achille Mbembe]] built his pan-African institutional base as Executive Secretary (1996–2000) — the period that shaped his engagement with postcolonial thought at continental scale.

## Mission & Objectives

Founded in 1973 with a mandate to promote, facilitate, and disseminate social science research across Africa — and to create an intellectual community that transcends barriers of language, country, age, and gender. CODESRIA operates from a foundational conviction: a sound knowledge base about Africa, produced *by* African scholars, is indispensable for addressing the continent's challenges.

Key institutional commitments:
- **Pan-African, not regional.** All five African regions (North, West, East, Central, South) are represented equally — correcting the tendency in international organizations to treat sub-Saharan and North Africa as separate intellectual worlds.
- **Multilingual.** Operates in English and French across all programmes; some work extends to Portuguese and Arabic.
- **Epistemological independence.** Research agenda set by African scholars, not by donor interests. This is the ideological core: Africa produces knowledge about Africa.
- **Three internal tendencies.** CODESRIA historically houses at least three intellectual orientations: (1) an older Marxist-inflected political economy tradition (Samir Amin, Claude Ake); (2) a younger generation aligned with post-structuralism and decolonial feminism; and (3) a women's studies / civil society strand. These tensions produce live intellectual debate.

## Key Contributions

1. **Building pan-African social science as a discipline.** Before CODESRIA, African social science was fragmented by colonial language boundaries and dependent on Western funding and agenda-setting. CODESRIA created cross-regional, cross-linguistic networks that allowed Anglophone, Francophone, Lusophone, and Arabic-speaking African scholars to collaborate and build a shared intellectual tradition.

2. **Training generations of African scholars.** The fellowship and institute programmes have trained thousands of African researchers since 1973. Many of the continent's most prominent social scientists passed through CODESRIA's working groups, gender institutes, or methodological workshops.

3. **Publishing infrastructure for African social science.** CODESRIA's journals (*Africa Development*, *CODESRIA Bulletin*) provided peer-reviewed publishing venues at a time when African scholars had almost no African journals to publish in. This infrastructure reduced dependency on Western journals.

4. **Gender Institute (since early 1990s).** The Annual Gender Institute has been a major site for feminist scholarship in Africa, training gender researchers and producing feminist theory grounded in African contexts. Runs continuously — one of the oldest Africa-focused feminist academic programmes.

5. **Conceptual contributions.** Multiple major African intellectual frameworks first circulated through CODESRIA: dependency theory (via Samir Amin), African political economy, postcolonial theory (via Mbembe's executive tenure), decolonial feminism. The institution is not just a conveyor belt but an intellectual generator.

6. **Prince Claus Award (1997).** Received from the [Prince Claus Fund](https://princeclausfund.org/award-recipients/council-for-the-development-of-social-science-research-in-africa) for its contribution to knowledge production and cultural exchange in Africa. Recognized by an international jury as the continent's most important social science infrastructure.

### Flagship Annual Programme

#### MRI (Meaning-Making Research Initiative)
- Introduced 2017 as CODESRIA's principal research support tool. Merged National Working Groups, Multinational Working Groups, Comparative Research Networks, Transnational Working Groups, and Postdoctoral Grants.
- **Grants:** USD 10,000 (individual) / USD 25,000 (group of 3–5 researchers)
- **Duration:** 16 months per grant cycle
- **Thematic priorities 2023–2027:** State and democratization; economic transformation; indigenous and alternative knowledge; gender; migration; climate and ecology; higher education; digital Africa.
- **Special call for female scholars** — runs alongside regular MRI call.
- **Advanced Senior Research Fellowship (ASRF)** — for scholars with 15+ years post-doctoral experience; produces manuscripts for CODESRIA Monograph Series.
- **URL (general):** <https://codesria.org/meaning-making-research-initiatives-mri-2026/>

#### Diaspora Project (African Academic Diaspora Support to African Universities)
- Connects African academics in the diaspora with African universities — visiting lectureships, curriculum development, joint research. Addresses brain drain by creating mechanisms for diaspora scholars to contribute without permanently returning.

#### CODESRIA College of Mentors
- Pan-African mentorship programme for African universities. Mentors include [Abdul Karim Bangura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdul_Karim_Bangura), [Mshai Mwangola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mshai_Mwangola), and others. Strengthens research capacity at African universities that lack senior research faculty.

## For Amber

### Why Interesting

**1. AI from the African south — CODESRIA is entering AI discourse.** The recent *AI and Higher Education* book signals CODESRIA's move toward AI ethics and governance from an African epistemological standpoint. This is exactly the intellectual infrastructure that decolonial computing needs — not just Western critiques (FAccT, AI Now) but African frameworks grounded in African institutional life. Watch this space.

**2. The necropolitics pipeline.** CODESRIA (1996–2000) shaped [[Achille Mbembe]]'s thinking at continental scale. His necropolitics — who is exposed to death, what systems govern life and death — is directly applicable to AI systems in healthcare, policing, and surveillance that are deployed disproportionately in Africa. CODESRIA is the institutional root of the theory Amber needs for machine-death research.

**3. Epistemic justice as critical computing.** CODESRIA's entire project is epistemic: who produces knowledge, from where, in whose language, under whose agenda? This is the foundational question of decolonial computing. CODESRIA operationalizes epistemic justice at institutional scale — it runs journals, funds research, builds mentorship networks. It's not theory; it's infrastructure.

**4. Indigenous knowledge × AI.** The AFRIAK programme and the 2026 Gender Institute both engage indigenous/endogenous knowledge systems with sophisticated theoretical framing (not naive celebration). The question "what can AI learn from non-Western knowledge architectures?" connects directly to Amber's work on more-than-human design and alternative epistemologies.

**5. Global South research positioning.** CODESRIA has relationships with SSRC (Social Science Research Council), Ford Foundation, Carnegie, and Nordic funding bodies. Understanding its network is useful for building credibility in Global South research spaces.

## Why Interesting

CODESRIA is not directly in Amber's field (HCI, AI, design) but it is the **institutional infrastructure for African critical theory** — the social science counterpart to the philosophical work of Mbembe, Sarr, and Diagne. Three reasons it stays in the graph:

1. **The AI turn.** CODESRIA is visibly entering AI discourse (the AI + Higher Education book; AFRIAK's framework around indigenous knowledge and AI). Within 2-3 years this may become a significant site for African AI ethics.

2. **Necropolitics' institutional home.** Mbembe built his pan-African intellectual identity here. Understanding CODESRIA is understanding the institutional conditions that produced necropolitics — which Amber needs for machine-death and AI ethics work.

3. **The Dakar hub.** CODESRIA, [[Les Ateliers de la Pensée de Dakar]], [[Jimsaan]], and the Senegalese cultural scene around [[Felwine Sarr]] form a cluster of African intellectual infrastructure that is producing theory directly relevant to Global South AI ethics. Dakar is increasingly a node in the global theory network, not just a regional hub.
