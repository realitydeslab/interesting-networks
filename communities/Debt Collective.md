# Debt Collective

The first debtors' union in US history. Born from Occupy Wall Street's Strike Debt working group (2012), co-founded by [[Astra Taylor]], Thomas Gokey, and others. Organizes debtors collectively to dispute, resist, and abolish predatory debt — student, medical, housing, probation, and consumer. Operates a digital debt dispute toolkit (the first tool that made debt disputes collectively actionable at scale), organized the first US student debt strike since the 1970s (Corinthian Colleges, 2015), and has helped abolish over $100 billion in debt through campaigns and legal victories. Model of participatory design embedded in a social movement: a digital tool that only works within a collective action framework.

## Mission & Objectives
"You are not a loan." Core insight: debt is not personal failure but a political structure — and can be resisted collectively, like labor organizing. Three strategic pillars:

1. **Building economic power** — campaigns for mass debt cancellation and policy change, combining debt disputes, debt strikes, media, legal, and direct action
2. **Building multiracial working-class power** — membership-based debtors' union; online + local organizing, political education, technology tools
3. **Building coalitions for alternative economic systems** — mutual aid, worker ownership, socialized finance, community credit institutions

Mission: "We are not in debt because we live beyond our means, but because we are denied the means to live."

## Key Contributions
- **First US debtors' union** (organizational model innovation — applying labor union logic to creditor-debtor relations)
- **Rolling Jubilee** (2012–2013, revived 2020–2024): crowdfunded debt purchases + cancellations totalling $32M+; demonstrated debt's arbitrary market value publicly; cancelled probation debt, ambulance debt, tuition debt, abortion debt, utility debt, school meal debt
- **Corinthian 15** (2015): First student debt strike since 1970s; 15 students publicly refused loan repayment, triggering precedent-setting "defense to repayment" process; eventually led to $6B+ Corinthian cancellation (2022 Biden administration)
- **New Economy Project collaboration**: 120,000 judgement debts ($800M) forfeited and retired via legal case
- **Policy impact**: Nearly 5 million people got federal student loans cancelled 2020–2025; put free public college on mainstream political agenda
- **Total debt abolition:** Over $100 billion in student debt canceled through movement pressure; substantially more via Rolling Jubilee and legal cases

## For Amber
- **Participatory design at the limit:** The Debt Collective's dispute toolkit is one of the cleanest examples of a *designed artifact that only works embedded in collective action*. The software isn't the product — the union is the product; the software enables union tactics. This is what Amber's work on "agentive commoning" or participatory systems could look like at political economy scale.
- **Democratic design meets adversarial design:** Their model combines Scandinavian participatory design tradition (design *with* people) with adversarial/critical computing (design *against* power structures). The dispute toolkit is both participatory AND a weapon.
- **Rolling Jubilee as design provocation:** Buying debt on the secondary market to cancel it was a *speculative design move* — it made visible how arbitrary debt "value" is. This is design fiction made real: demonstrate the system's absurdity through action.
- **Organizing technology as research method:** The Shuttleworth Fellowship that funded the Debt Collective's tech is a relevant funding model for Amber's participatory tech work — it funded a tool embedded in social change, not a standalone product.

## Why Interesting
Debt Collective sits at an intersection that's deeply resonant with Amber's interests: **participatory design + political economy + adversarial systems thinking**. What makes it interesting as a design object:

1. **The union as the design artifact.** The dispute toolkit is secondary. The primary innovation is applying labor union logic — collective bargaining, solidarity, coordinated action — to the debtor-creditor relation. This is institutional design, not software design.

2. **Making the invisible visible.** Rolling Jubilee was a design provocation: it revealed that debts sold at $0.01 on the dollar "worth" their face value is a legal fiction. The campaign was an *epistemic intervention* — changing what people know about the system they're inside.

3. **From shame to solidarity.** Debt is designed to be private, shameful, isolating. The Debt Collective's design goal was to make debt *legible as a shared political condition* rather than personal failure. This is almost exactly what Amber's work on "agentive commoning" does with AI behavior — making invisible collective dynamics visible and actionable.

4. **Platform design for adversarial contexts.** The dispute toolkit had to work within a hostile legal-financial system while empowering people who didn't understand that system. This is human-centered design under adversarial constraints — design for people with low power against institutions with high power.

5. **David Graeber connection.** *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* is a foundational text for the movement. Graeber's anthropological approach — treating debt as a social relation rather than a financial fact — is structurally similar to Amber's "machine behavior" approach: studying AI systems the way you'd study societies.
