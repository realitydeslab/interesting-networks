# London Film-Makers' Co-operative (LFMC)

The foundational institution of British avant-garde and structural-materialist cinema. Founded in **1966** by [[Stephen Dwoskin]], [[Malcolm Le Grice]], and others — inspired directly by [[Jonas Mekas]]'s [[Film-Makers' Cooperative]] in New York. Operated on an integrated cooperative model: 16mm production workshop, distribution catalogue, and exhibition programme all under one roof and non-hierarchical governance. Home to the most formally radical and politically committed British film practice from the mid-1960s through the 1990s. The LFMC gave the world structural-materialist theory, Annabel Nicolson's legendary *Reel Time*, Lis Rhodes's *Light Music*, the feminist rupture that founded [[Circles]], and a training ground for four decades of British experimental filmmakers. Merged in 2002 with London Electronic Arts to form [[LUX]], which now holds its distribution archive.

## Overview

The LFMC was simultaneously:
1. **A production workshop** — providing 16mm camera, processing, and editing facilities to artists at a time when film equipment was otherwise inaccessible
2. **A distribution agency** — maintaining a catalogue of experimental films for hire nationally and internationally
3. **An exhibition space** — screening films with no other venue, including world premieres of radical work
4. **An intellectual community** — generating and contesting structural-materialist theory, feminist critique, and expanded cinema practice

At its peak in the 1970s, the Co-op represented the most vigorous structural-materialist film scene in Europe. It was simultaneously a workshop, a distributor, a debating club, and a political entity — one that fractured productively in 1979 when feminist members withdrew from the Arts Council's *Film as Film* exhibition and founded [[Circles]], the UK's first feminist film distribution network.

The LFMC's model — artist-run, collectively governed, combining production infrastructure with distribution and exhibition — became a template for artist-run moving-image organisations worldwide.

## History & Timeline

### 1961 — The New York Model
[[Jonas Mekas]] founds [[The Film-Makers' Cooperative]] in New York with [[Andy Warhol]], [[Stan Brakhage]], [[Shirley Clarke]], and others. The principle: distribute experimental films on a non-exclusive basis, with the cooperative open to any filmmaker. This model directly inspires the LFMC.

### 1964–1965 — The American Arrives
[[Stephen Dwoskin]], a disabled New York graphic designer and filmmaker embedded in the Mekas underground scene, arrives in London on a Fulbright Scholarship in 1964 and stays. He brings firsthand knowledge of the NY cooperative model, energy from the American underground, and connections to Jonas Mekas, Carolee Schneemann, and the avant-garde scene he left behind.

### 1966 — Foundation
The **London Film-Makers' Co-operative is founded in 1966**. Co-founders include [[Stephen Dwoskin]], along with others in London's emergent counterculture film scene (sources also cite critic [[Raymond Durgnat]] as involved). [[Malcolm Le Grice]] becomes involved from the late 1960s onwards, quickly becoming its central shaping force.

The Co-op initially operates without fixed premises, screening at arts labs and alternative spaces — part of the wider London Arts Lab scene centred around [[Jim Haynes]]'s Drury Lane Arts Lab and, later, the Robert Street Arts Lab.

**Inaugural purpose:** A cooperative distribution and exhibition collective for avant-garde and experimental film — works that had no other venue or distributor in Britain.

### 1966–1969 — Early Years
The early Co-op is eclectic. Dwoskin screens his own work — intimate, body-focused films that deal with gaze and desire in ways deeply connected to his disability experience. [[Sally Potter]] joins the Co-op as a teenager, making her first experimental films (*Jerk*, 1969; *Play*, 1970) before training as a dancer and choreographer.

The 1967 Knokke experimental film festival in Belgium becomes a key moment of international exposure for the Co-op's artists. Dwoskin's films gain international recognition there.

### 1969 — Building the Workshop
**Malcolm Le Grice** and **Fred Drummond** physically construct the Co-op's first 16mm processing workshop prototype at the **Robert Street Arts Lab**. This is a defining moment: the LFMC becomes not just a distribution and exhibition entity but a **production infrastructure**. Artists can now process their own film.

Le Grice joins the LFMC committee in 1969 and serves until 1976 (Chair 1970–73). He also introduces experimental film to fine art students at **Saint Martin's School of Art** and **Goldsmiths**, building a pipeline of new experimental filmmakers.

### 1969–1970 — Structural Film Arrives
P. Adams Sitney coins the term "structural film" in his 1969 essay in *Film Culture* magazine, describing American avant-garde filmmakers' focus on predetermined form, fixed camera, flicker effects, and loop printing. This formulation reaches London and sparks both engagement and counter-formulation.

The British variant becomes **structural/materialist film**: more explicitly politically inflected, focused on **film-as-material-substance** rather than film-as-form. The debate centres on whether to adopt Sitney's American vocabulary or forge a distinctly British-Marxist theoretical framework.

**Malcolm Le Grice** makes *Berlin Horse* (1970), one of the defining British structural-materialist films: a dual-screen work using found footage of a horse pulling a plough, rephotographed and colour-shifted to expose the grain, the optical printing process, and the temporal structure of cinema itself.

### 1968–1972 — Peter Gidal Arrives, Theory Sharpens
[[Peter Gidal]] moves to London from the US in July 1968 to study at the **Royal College of Art**. He joins the LFMC and rapidly becomes the Co-op's most rigorous theoretical voice. Gidal is concerned with American imperialism during the Vietnam War; his filmmaking and writing are explicitly political.

Gidal develops a position distinct from Sitney's structural film: his films have **no predetermined shape**, no "goal achieved," no overall structure that could be exploited for symbolic content. The aim is to frustrate narrative, refuse illusion, and force the viewer into awareness of their own perceptual labour. *Room Film 1973* is considered the culmination of his mature practice.

**Key theoretical text:** Gidal's **"Theory and Definition of Structural/Materialist Film"** (1974) — the foundational document of British structural-materialist film theory. Published first in essay form; Gidal edits the **Structural Film Anthology** (BFI, 1976), one of the earliest book-length accounts of British avant-garde cinema.

### 1972–1977 — Workshop Maturity; The Reel Time Moment
**[[William Raban]]** (born 1948, painting BA from St Martins 1971, MA from Reading 1974) becomes **Manager of the LFMC Workshop 1972–76**. Under Raban's management, the workshop operates as a genuine production facility. Filmmakers process their own 16mm footage, build optical printing equipment, and develop new technical approaches to the medium.

**[[Annabel Nicolson]]** (born 1946, studied Hornsey, Edinburgh, St Martins post-graduate film 1970-71) makes the legendary **expanded cinema performance *Reel Time*** (1973). Nicolson sews through a loop of 16mm film with a domestic sewing machine while simultaneously projecting it — the holes appear as light events on screen, merging the material trace of production (the sewing machine's path through the film strip) with the projected image. A perfect synthesis of structural-materialist principle and feminist attention to domestic labour. One of the most celebrated performances in British avant-garde history.

**[[Lis Rhodes]]** is **Cinema Curator at the LFMC 1975–76**. She makes *Light Music* (1975): two 16mm films projected side-by-side, the soundtracks generated by the optical soundtrack of abstract drawn marks. Image and sound arise from the same material marks — sound *is* image, image *is* sound. Tate Modern later exhibits it (2012) as "an iconic work of expanded cinema." 

**Gill Eatherley** — experimental filmmaker, LFMC member, collaborates with Le Grice (later co-editing *Like a Fox*, 1988).

**Mike Dunford** and **Roger Hammond** — LFMC filmmakers associated with structural-materialist tendency.

**Fred Drummond** — technician and filmmaker who built the workshop with Le Grice.

[[Malcolm Le Grice]] writes **Abstract Film and Beyond** (Studio Vista/MIT, 1977) — the first book-length history of experimental cinema by a British practitioner. Sets the LFMC's structural-materialist practice in international context.

### 1975–1979 — Feminist Tension Grows
Women at the LFMC — working in an organisation dominated by a certain kind of theory (Gidal's hard-structural position), certain dominant personalities (Le Grice, Gidal), and a historically male-controlled exhibition environment — begin to organise around a different set of concerns. Their work is formally experimental but refuses the austerity of pure materialism. They are interested in the body, language, pleasure, and the gendered position of filmmaker and viewer.

[[Lis Rhodes]], [[Annabel Nicolson]], **Jo Davis**, **Felicity Sparrow**, **Mary Pat Leece**, **Jane Clarke**, **Jeanette Iljon**, **Susan Stein**, **Pat Murphy** form the core of this feminist experimental tendency.

### 1979 — The Feminist Rupture: Film as Film / Women and the Formal Film

The **Arts Council of Great Britain** organises a major exhibition and touring programme on experimental film: **Film as Film: Formal Experiment in Film 1910–1975** (Hayward Gallery, 1979; Phil Drummond editor of the catalogue). Women on the exhibition committee undertake painstaking research into women's contribution to avant-garde film — Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, Maya Deren, Lois Weber, and others — only to find their work marginalised in the final curatorial frame.

**The women withdraw from the exhibition** and issue an explanatory statement — **"Women and the Formal Film"** — which is published in the *Film as Film* catalogue as a manifesto (authored by Annabel Nicolson, Felicity Sparrow, Jane Clarke, Jeanette Iljon, Lis Rhodes, Mary Pat Leece, Pat Murphy, Susan Stein). The statement documents a gendered erasure from avant-garde film history and demands a politics of representation internal to the avant-garde itself.

**The research and energy from this withdrawal becomes the cornerstone of Circles.** In 1979, [[Lis Rhodes]], Jo Davis, Felicity Sparrow, and [[Annabel Nicolson]] co-found **[[Circles]]** — the UK's first feminist film distribution network. Circles publishes its first catalogue in 1980 with ~30 films. Eventually grows to 200+ titles spanning 90 years of women's film production. A membership organisation where all distributed filmmakers have policy rights.

This is one of the most productive institutional ruptures in British art history. The women didn't leave the field — they built parallel infrastructure that ultimately had "an impact far out of proportion to its small size" (Holland and Harris).

Circles operates until 1991, when funding crises (Tower Hamlets funding withdrawn 1987; BFI replacement funding; eventual collapse) lead to merger with Cinema of Women → forming **[[Cinenova]]**, which carries the Circles catalogue forward.

### 1970s–1990s — Film Workshops & Channel 4 Era
The LFMC's workshop model influences the broader **Film Workshop movement** in Britain. [[Four Corners Film Workshop]] in East London operates as an allied radical collective. The founding of **Channel 4** in 1982 with its brief to serve minority communities and commission innovative work provides new distribution possibilities and revenue for the independent sector.

The LFMC adapts: it continues to distribute 16mm experimental work while also engaging with video and the new broadcast context. But the ideological cohesion of the structural-materialist moment dissipates as the 1980s progress.

**[[Annabel Nicolson]]** returns as cinema programmer at the LFMC in 1992/93, bridging the early moment and the later period.

### 1990s — Toward LUX
By the 1990s, the LFMC's institutional form has become problematic. The 16mm film workshop is expensive to maintain; the distribution catalogue needs professionalisation; the broader landscape of artists' moving image has expanded dramatically into video and installation.

**London Video Arts (LVA)**, later renamed **London Electronic Arts (LEA)**, has operated in parallel as the UK distribution organisation for video art.

### 2002 — Dissolution and Formation of LUX
The **LFMC and London Electronic Arts merge in 2002** to form **[[LUX]]**, with **Benjamin Cook** appointed as founding Director. LUX absorbs the LFMC's distribution catalogue, carries forward its collection, and builds it into the UK's leading artists' moving image organisation (6,000+ works by 1,500+ artists, including the full 16mm experimental archive).

**LUX Online** (2004–2009) later creates a web resource for exploring the LFMC's historical collection online.

## The Structural-Materialist Theory

The LFMC's most significant intellectual contribution is the **structural/materialist theory of film** — a distinctly British response to American structural film, inflected by European Marxism and psychoanalytic film theory.

**Core claim:** Film is not a transparent window onto the world. It is a physical substrate — celluloid, emulsion, light, projection mechanism — with its own material properties. Avant-garde film must foreground these properties rather than use them to create narrative illusions. The viewer must be forced into awareness of their own perceptual and cognitive labour.

**Distinction from American Structural Film:** P. Adams Sitney's "structural film" (1969) identified four formal properties (fixed camera, flicker, loop printing, rephotography) as defining an American tendency centred on form. Peter Gidal's "structural/materialist" film (1974) adds an explicitly political dimension: representation itself is ideologically loaded; the refusal of conventional representation is a political act. Gidal's films have no predetermined shape — they resist the symbolic exploitation that even Sitney's structural film could allow.

**Key theoretical texts:**
- **Peter Gidal**, "Theory and Definition of Structural/Materialist Film" (1974) — first published essay; reprinted in the *Structural Film Anthology* and subsequent anthologies
- **Peter Gidal** (ed.), [*Structural Film Anthology*](https://monoskop.org/images/6/65/Gidal_Peter_ed_Structural_Film_Anthology.pdf) (British Film Institute, 1976) — the key primary text; collected essays by LFMC filmmakers defining their practice
- **Malcolm Le Grice**, [*Abstract Film and Beyond*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262620208/) (Studio Vista / MIT, 1977) — the first book-length history of experimental cinema by a British practitioner; situates LFMC in international context
- **Peter Gidal**, *Materialist Film* (Routledge, 1989) — the definitive theoretical statement
- **Michael O'Pray** (ed.), *The British Avant-Garde Film 1926–1995: An Anthology of Writings* (University of Luton Press, 1996) — essential anthology including primary texts
- **Malcolm Le Grice**, *Experimental Cinema in the Digital Age* (BFI, 2001/2011) — collected essays across decades

## Key People

### Founders & Shaping Figures

**[[Stephen Dwoskin]]** (1939–2012) — Brooklyn-born, trained at Parsons (under de Kooning and Albers), graphic designer at CBS and Epic Records, embedded in the Mekas circle. Contracted polio at nine; used crutches throughout life, later wheelchair. Arrived in London on Fulbright 1964, stayed permanently. Co-founded LFMC 1966. His work is closely linked to gaze theory and [[Laura Mulvey]]; his films explore desire, the body, and the politics of looking — made more complex by his own disabled subjectivity. Also a key figure in **The Other Cinema** (radical distribution collective). Made films for German broadcaster ZDF. Films: *Times For* (1970) with [[Carolee Schneemann]], *Dyn Amo* (1972, shot in a Soho strip club), *Behindert* (1974, first to deal explicitly with his disability). Archive at University of Reading; films distributed by LUX.

**[[Malcolm Le Grice]]** (1940–2024) — Born Plymouth, Devon. Studied painting at Plymouth and Slade School of Fine Art. Central figure of the LFMC from the late 1960s: committee member 1969–76, Chair 1970–73. Physically built the Co-op's workshop with Fred Drummond. Established film at Saint Martin's School of Art and Goldsmiths. Called "probably the most influential modernist filmmaker in British cinema" (BFI). Films: *Castle 1* (1966), *Berlin Horse* (1970), *Horror Film 1* (1971, shadow-play performance film), *After Lumière* (1974), *Blackbird Descending* (1977). Later moved extensively into video and digital installation. Wrote two major books. Professor Emeritus, University of the Arts London. Died December 2024.

**[[Peter Gidal]]** (born 1946) — Grew up in Mount Vernon, NY and Switzerland. Studied theatre, psychology, literature at Brandeis 1964–68; University of Munich 1966–67. Moved to London 1968 to study at Royal College of Art. Joined LFMC immediately. Developed the theory of structural/materialist film as distinct from American structural film — focused on non-illusionist image-making, refusal of representation, political anti-illusionism. Films include *Room Film 1973*, *Condition of Illusion* (1975), *Kopenhagen 1930* (1977). Remains the most uncompromising theorist of the tendency.

### Key Artists

**[[Lis Rhodes]]** (born 1942, Cornwall) — Studied Royal College of Art. Cinema Curator at LFMC 1975–76. Key works: *Dresden Dynamo* (1972), *Light Music* (1975, sound generated from optical soundtrack of drawn marks — image and sound arise from same material), *Light Reading* (1978). Co-founded [[Circles]] 1979 with Jo Davis, Felicity Sparrow, and Annabel Nicolson. Also member of [[Four Corners Film Workshop]]. Arts Adviser to Greater London Council 1982–85. Lecturer at Slade School of Fine Art from 1978. Won Freelands Award 2017. Work exhibited at Tate Modern, ICA, Courtisane Film Festival. Lives and works in London.

**[[Annabel Nicolson]]** (born 1946) — Studied Hornsey College of Art 1964–65, Edinburgh College of Art (Drawing and Painting) 1965–69, St Martins post-graduate film 1970–71. Cinema programmer at LFMC 1974, 1976–77, 1992/93. Co-founder of [[Circles]] 1979. Editorial contributor to *Musics* magazine 1976–79; co-editor of *Readings* magazine 1977. John Brinkley Fellowship, Norwich School of Art 1980–81. Key work: *Reel Time* (1973) — loops 16mm film through a sewing machine while projecting it; holes appear as light-events on screen; production trace made visible. Her work is in collections of the Belgian National Film Archive, BFI, and Canterbury University. Quote: "It is what happens to things when they are not being looked at that puzzles me."

**[[Sally Potter]]** (born 1949, London) — Began making amateur films at 14. Joined LFMC in the late 1960s. Made *Jerk* (1969) and *Play* (1970). Then trained as dancer/choreographer; founded Limited Dance Company with Jacky Lansley. Made *Thriller* (1979, hit on international festival circuit), *The Gold Diggers* (1983, with Julie Christie). Later directed *Orlando* (1992, with Tilda Swinton) and became internationally known. Member of the **Feminist Improvising Group**. The LFMC is the origin point of her career.

**[[William Raban]]** (born 1948, Fakenham) — BA painting, Saint Martins 1971; MA Fine Art, Reading University 1974. Manager of LFMC Workshop 1972–76. Structural filmmaker; later video artist. Published bi-monthly *Filmmakers' Europe* 1977–81. Part-time senior lecturer in Film at Saint Martin's School of Art 1976–89. Reader in Film at University of the Arts, London. Member of editorial board, *Vertigo* film magazine.

**[[Gill Eatherley]]** — Structural filmmaker, LFMC member. Later collaborated with Le Grice on *Like a Fox* (1988).

**[[Mike Dunford]]** — Filmmaker, listed in the structural/materialist tendency.

**[[Roger Hammond]]** — Filmmaker, LFMC structural tendency.

**[[Fred Drummond]]** — Technician and filmmaker; built the LFMC workshop prototype with Le Grice at Robert Street Arts Lab. Also published *Filmmakers' Europe* (bi-monthly journal, 1977–81).

**[[Raymond Durgnat]]** — Film critic and theorist; cited as involved in co-founding the LFMC alongside Dwoskin.

- **Lis Rhodes**, *Light Reading* (1978) — text and image working on the position of women in language.

## The Circles Rupture and Feminist Legacy

The 1979 feminist withdrawal from the *Film as Film* exhibition is the LFMC's most productive institutional moment — not because it was the end, but because it generated a parallel feminist infrastructure that survived the LFMC itself.

**The feminist analysis:** The structural-materialist tendency at the LFMC had a politics of form but was weak on the politics of gender. The hard-structural position (Gidal's most extreme formulation: no representation, no narrative, no image that could carry ideological meaning) could theoretically apply to all filmmakers equally — but in practice, male filmmakers set the terms. Women's work dealing with embodiment, language, desire, and the gendered gaze was evaluated against an aesthetic standard that implicitly coded those concerns as insufficiently rigorous.

**"Women and the Formal Film"** (Annabel Nicolson, Felicity Sparrow, Jane Clarke, Jeanette Iljon, Lis Rhodes, Mary Pat Leece, Pat Murphy, Susan Stein, 1979) — the statement published in the *Film as Film* catalogue. It documents: the painstaking research women did for the exhibition; the marginalisation of that research in the final programme; and the historical erasure of women's contributions to experimental film from Alice Guy forward. It acts as a founding manifesto.

**[[Circles]]** (1979–1991):
- Founded by Lis Rhodes, Jo Davis, Felicity Sparrow, Annabel Nicolson
- Distributed films by Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, Maya Deren, Lois Weber — the recovered history the *Film as Film* exhibition would have excluded
- Membership organisation: all distributed filmmakers automatically become members and have policy rights
- By closure: 200+ titles spanning 90 years of women's film production
- Organised women-only screenings at Four Corners cinema, Bethnal Green
- 1987: Tower Hamlets withdraws funding → BFI replaces it; 1991: final funding collapse
- **[[Cinenova]]** (1991–present): merger of Circles and Cinema of Women; Circles catalogue continues to circulate

## International Network Connections

**New York Film-Makers' Cooperative** — the direct model; [[Jonas Mekas]] (Lithuanian-American; co-founded with [[Andy Warhol]], [[Stan Brakhage]], [[Shirley Clarke]], 1961). Mekas's principle of non-exclusive open-membership cooperative distribution is directly borrowed by the LFMC. Dwoskin was embedded in this scene before arriving in London.

**Canyon Cinema** (San Francisco, 1961) — the West Coast American cooperative model; the Film-Makers' Cooperative notes Canyon Cinema and the LFMC as the two primary international imitators of its structure.

**Anthology Film Archives** (New York, 1970) — founded by Mekas and others as an exhibition venue for avant-garde cinema including structural films. Close relationship to the NY coop; occasional programming overlap with LFMC.

**European structural film connections:**
- **[[Kurt Kren]]** (Austria) — key figure in European structural film; flicker films; influenced the British scene
- **[[Peter Kubelka]]** (Austria) — radical film theorist; metric film; shown at LFMC
- **[[Birgit and Wilhelm Hein]]** (Germany) — German structural/materialist filmmakers; active at the same historical moment
- **Knokke experimental film festival** (Belgium, 1967) — the key European venue where Dwoskin gained international recognition after the LFMC's formation

**British connections:**
- **[[Arts Lab]] scene** (Jim Haynes's Drury Lane Arts Lab; Robert Street Arts Lab) — the LFMC's early home and counterculture context
- **[[Four Corners Film Workshop]]** — allied radical collective in East London; overlapping membership (Lis Rhodes was a member)
- **[[The Other Cinema]]** — radical distribution collective for British independents, Third World filmmakers, European films; Dwoskin was associated
- **[[Saint Martin's School of Art]]** — Le Grice established film there; many LFMC filmmakers trained here
- **[[Goldsmiths]]** — Le Grice also taught here

## Why Interesting for Amber

**🔧 The Artist-Run Infrastructure Model:**
The LFMC is the canonical example of artist-run infrastructure in British art: built by artists, for artists, combining production, distribution, and exhibition in a non-commercial, non-hierarchical structure that lasted 36 years. For Amber's interest in how artistic communities sustain themselves and build collective resources, the LFMC is the deep historical case study. What made it work? What made it eventually transform? The merger into LUX is not failure — it's a specific kind of institutional evolution.

**⚡ Film as Material Investigation:**
Structural-materialist film treats film stock as a physical substrate — something to be worked, processed, chemically altered, looped, scratched, sewed through. This is **material practice as epistemology**: the medium is not transparent but opaque, and making it opaque is a political-aesthetic act. This has direct resonance with any practice that treats its technical medium as a site of investigation rather than a tool to be used. For Amber's interest in the phenomenology and material reality of technological systems, structural-materialist film offers a complete methodological example — decades before the material turn in HCI.

**👁️ Feminist Experimental Practice:**
The LFMC's feminist strand (Rhodes, Nicolson, Potter, the Circles founders) shows how feminist politics can work *within* a formally rigorous avant-garde tradition — and how the formal-political synthesis can be more productive (and more durable) than either pole alone. The feminist criticism wasn't that structural film was too rigorous; it was that structural film was not rigorous enough about its own ideological assumptions. This is a model for how to work within, critique, and transform an aesthetic tradition from within.

**🏛️ The 1979 Rupture as Institutional Template:**
The withdrawal from *Film as Film* and the founding of Circles is a masterclass in productive institutional rupture. When marginalised practitioners within a community are excluded or erased, they don't just leave — they build parallel infrastructure that carries the work forward on their own terms. Circles lasted 12 years and influenced British feminist film culture for decades. The lesson: rupture can be more generative than accommodation.

**🎬 Historical Canon:**
The LFMC produced the canonical films of British structural-materialist practice — Le Grice's *Berlin Horse*, Nicolson's *Reel Time*, Rhodes's *Light Music* — works now held in major museum collections and shown at Tate Modern, MOMA, and internationally. These are not obscure historical footnotes but active, shown works. Understanding them means understanding a major strand of 20th century experimental film.

**🌐 Network Effects:**
The LFMC → LUX lineage connects directly to the current distribution infrastructure Amber would engage if working in artists' film in the UK. Understanding the LFMC means understanding how LUX got its collection, its politics, and its institutional identity.
