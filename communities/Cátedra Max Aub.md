# Cátedra Max Aub — Transdisciplina en Arte y Tecnología

**Cátedra Max Aub, Transdisciplina en Arte y Tecnología** is UNAM's named research chair and institutional home for critical art-technology inquiry in Mexico. Its public-facing manifestation is **El Aleph Festival de Arte y Ciencia** — an annual festival held in Mexico City, typically in May. The Cátedra also organizes seminars, co-produces events with [[MUAC]], and generates academic publications. Coordinator: **Tania Aedo**.

The website `catedramaxaub.unam.mx` is currently not resolving (as of Feb 2026). The festival website is https://festivalelaleph.com (2026 edition: May 8–17).

## Who Is Max Aub?

The choice of namesake matters. Max Aub Mohrenwitz (Paris, June 2, 1903 – Mexico City, July 22, 1972) was a **Spanish-Mexican experimental novelist, playwright, screenwriter, poet, and cultural organizer** — one of the great figures of Republican exile culture, and one of the most radically transdisciplinary artists of the 20th century.

**Key biographical facts that frame the Cátedra's ethos:**

- Born in Paris to a French-Jewish mother and German father; raised in Valencia, Spain from 1914. Identity permanently between nations and languages.
- **Spanish Civil War**: Republican cultural attaché to Paris. In 1937, he personally arranged the placement of **Picasso's Guernica** at the International Exposition — an act of art-as-political-weapon at a world stage. Also organized the **Second Congress of Anti-Fascist Writers** that year.
- Worked on **André Malraux's film _L'espoir_** (1938), writing the screenplay — a rare crossover between avant-garde literature, cinema, and political resistance.
- Imprisoned 1940-42 at Camp Vernet (France) and Djelfa (Algeria forced labor camp) after being denounced to Vichy as "communist" and "German Jew." Escaped with help from Don Gilberto Bosques, Mexican Consul-General in France.
- Settled in Mexico City; became a Mexican citizen 1955. Formed close working friendship with Luis Buñuel. Wrote for major newspapers. Taught at the Film Academy.
- **Jusep Torres Campalans** (1958): his most conceptually radical work — a fake biography of a fictional Cubist painter, complete with fake paintings, fake reviews, fake interviews. Presented at an exhibition in Mexico with actual "paintings" by this nonexistent artist. An elaborate conceptual hoax that predated many Art & Language and institutional critique gestures. Max Aub understood fiction as a machine for producing reality.
- **El laberinto mágico** (1943–1968): major cycle of novels about the Spanish Civil War. Six novels, written across 25 years, from exile.
- In 1965, founded **Los Sesenta** literary periodical with Jorge Guillén and Rafael Alberti.
- 1972: **Chevalier de l'Ordre des Arts et Lettres** (France), the year of his death.

Why does this namesake matter? The Cátedra takes its character from Aub: **anti-fascist, cross-disciplinary, politically committed, uncomfortable with disciplinary purity, committed to fiction as a mode of knowledge production, and operating from the perspective of the Global South exile.** This is not a tech-celebratory institution. The "transdisciplina" is not marketing language — it's a political position rooted in a specific intellectual genealogy.

## Mission & Structure

The Cátedra is a formal **named research chair (cátedra)** within UNAM — the largest and most prestigious university in Latin America. It operates as an institutional node connecting:

- **Academic research** (UNAM faculty, Instituto de Investigaciones Estéticas ecosystem)
- **Public cultural programming** (El Aleph Festival, MUAC joint events, seminars)
- **Critical publishing** (co-produced publications, conference proceedings)
- **International networks** (Latin American art-technology scholars, European counterparts)

The "Transdisciplina en Arte y Tecnología" framing is explicitly not an uncritical celebration of tech. The 2024 forum, organized with [[MUAC]], was titled **"Museo digital. Inteligencias y artificios"** — "artificial intelligences" but also "artifices/tricks," a deliberate ambiguity. The event centered **data colonialism** as a key critical frame, with keynote contributions from **Ulises A. Mejias** (co-author with Nick Couldry of the seminal "Data Colonialism" framework). Tania Aedo moderated the panel "Respuestas culturales al colonialismo de datos" (Cultural responses to data colonialism).

## Key People

### Tania Aedo — Coordinator
The central figure. Tania Aedo coordinates the Cátedra and its programming, including El Aleph Festival. She is the critical linchpin connecting MUAC, UNAM research infrastructure, international guests, and the broader Mexican art-technology scene. Her work positions her squarely at the **art-technology-decoloniality** nexus in Mexican academic culture. Presented at/moderated the 2024 "Museo digital" forum at MUAC.

### Regular Collaborators & Invited Voices (2024 forum)
- **[[Ulises A. Mejias]]** — Professor, SUNY Oswego; co-founder, Non-Aligned Technologies Movement and Tierra Común network. Co-author (with Nick Couldry) of **"Data Colonialism: Rethinking Big Data's Relation to the Contemporary Subject"** (Television & New Media, 2018 — ~50K views, ~1,670 citations). Keynote/panelist at 2024 "Museo digital" forum. Contributed chapter "The Museum as a Cultural Response to Data Colonialism" to the 2024 publication (UNAM/MUAC + Fundación Telefónica).

- **[[Federico Pérez Villoro]]** — Mexican artist, programmer, educator. Participated in 2024 forum; case study titled "Sobre políticas vegetales" (On vegetable politics) — exploring computation, non-human agency, and artistic practice from an ecological angle.
- **[[Amanda de la Garza]]** — Former MUAC director (now Director of DGAV); presented at 2024 forum.
- **[[Cuauhtémoc Medina]]** — IIE-UNAM researcher and former MUAC chief curator; intellectual backbone of UNAM's critical art infrastructure.

## El Aleph Festival de Arte y Ciencia

The festival is the Cátedra's most visible output — an annual multi-day event in Mexico City bringing together artists, scientists, theorists, and technologists. Named after **Borges's story "El Aleph"** (a point in space that contains all other points), perfectly framing the festival's transdisciplinary ambition.

- **2026**: May 8–17 (festivalelaleph.com — currently "coming soon")
- Previous editions focused on: art + AI, ecology, computation, body/technology, social/political dimensions of digital culture

The festival format typically includes: performances, installations, talks, workshops — spread across UNAM cultural venues (possibly including MUAC, Centro Cultural Universitario, and other spaces).

- **Forum**: March 6–7, 2024 at MUAC
- **Co-organizers**: Cátedra Max Aub (Tania Aedo) + MUAC + Fundación Telefónica Movistar México
- **Published as book** by UNAM (free download; also on MUAC site as "descarga gratis")

## Why Interesting for Amber

**Institutional archetype**: The Cátedra Max Aub is a model for what a politically committed, critical art-technology research unit can look like within a major national university. Not a tech-celebratory innovation lab. Not pure academia. A third thing — transdisciplinary, historically grounded, decolonially oriented, with a public festival format.

**Intellectual alignment**:
- "Transdisciplina" resonates with Amber's work across ALife, somaesthetic design, speculative design, more-than-human computing
- **Data colonialism** framing is relevant to Amber's work on decentralized AI, machine behavior, and trustworthy agentic web — all of which have colonial/decolonial dimensions worth thinking through
- **Federico Pérez Villoro's** work on "vegetable politics" and computation-ecology is near-exact overlap with Amber's ALife/more-than-human interests
- **Max Aub's Jusep Torres Campalans** — the fake-artist hoax — is an interesting historical precedent for speculative/fictional research methods (relevant to Amber's speculative design practice)

**Access point**: El Aleph Festival (May 2026) is the best immediate entry point. Reaching Tania Aedo through MUAC channels would open doors to festival invitations, Cátedra seminars, and the UNAM research network.

**Global South node**: The Cátedra represents Mexico City as a serious art-technology research center — counterweight to Ars Electronica / transmediale / ZKM axis. Important for Amber's global network building beyond European/Anglo-American institutional contexts.
