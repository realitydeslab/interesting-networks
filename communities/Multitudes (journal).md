# Multitudes (journal)

**Multitudes** is a French political philosophy, art, and social theory journal published in Paris since 2000. Subtitle: *revue politique, artistique, philosophique*. Founded as a direct continuation of *[Futur Antérieur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Futur_ant%C3%A9rieur)* (1990–1998). Published quarterly — currently on issue **101 (Hiver/Winter 2026)**. Available in print and digital via [Cairn](https://www.cairn.info/) and [Scopalto](https://www.scopalto.com/). Bilingual: all articles published in French with English abstracts.

The journal sits at the intersection of **post-operaismo (post-workerism), Spinozist political theory, Deleuzian/Guattarian philosophy, commons, biopolitics, feminism, ecology, and contemporary art**. It is the primary publication venue for the Franco-Italian radical left that coalesced around the Negri/Hardt project (*Empire*, *Multitude*, *Commonwealth*). Structured around three recurring sections: **Majeure** (thematic dossier), **À chaud** (hot takes on current events), and **Icônes** (an artist feature with original artwork per issue).

## Mission & Objectives

Multitudes exists to sustain a radical theoretical tradition across the Franco-Italian intellectual left — **autonomist Marxism, Spinozism, post-Fordism, and biopolitics** — while continuously engaging with contemporary political emergencies (tech capitalism, ecological crisis, migration, race, war). Its distinctive contribution is linking high theory to concrete political analysis without losing rigor at either end.

Core commitments:
- **The common/commons** as a political and ontological category (vs. both state and market)
- **Biopolitics** (Foucault × Negri) — power over life itself
- **Cognitive capitalism** (Moulier Boutang's term) — how knowledge/creativity is the new site of value extraction
- **Immaterial/digital labor** (Lazzarato) — the new working class is cognitive workers and the indebted
- **Multitude** as political subject — pluralist, non-identitarian alternative to both the masses and the people
- **Art-politics** interface — each issue features an artist (Icônes section), taking art seriously as political thought

The journal deliberately publishes at the intersection of French theoretical tradition (Deleuze, Guattari, Foucault) and Italian workerist/autonomist tradition (Negri, Virno, Berardi), making it a unique crossover venue.

## Key People

### Founding Editorial Board (2000–)
- **[[Antonio Negri]]** — Italian political philosopher, *Empire* (2000) co-author with [[Michael Hardt]]. The central intellectual figure. Based in Paris for many years after leaving Italy (extradition from political trials). Post-operaismo, Spinozism, constituent power.
- **[[Maurizio Lazzarato]]** — Italian-French sociologist/philosopher. Key theorist of *immaterial labor*, *debt economy* (*The Making of the Indebted Man*, 2012), and *signs and machines*. Based in Paris. Very active contributor.
- **[[Yann Moulier Boutang]]** — Economist, co-founder. Coined *cognitive capitalism* — the post-Fordist economy where knowledge/creativity is the primary source of value. Strong voice on universal basic income.
- **[[Judith Revel]]** — French philosopher, Foucault specialist, author of *Foucault: A Conceptual Vocabulary* (2002). Regular editorial presence.
- **[[Giuseppe Cocco]]** — Italian-Brazilian political theorist. Bridge between Latin American social movements and European theory. Frequent contributor (wrote key piece on Ukraine/war in #98, 2025).

### Regular Contributors
- **[[Erik Bordeleau]]** — philosopher, researcher at [SenseLab](https://www.senselab.ca/), wrote on "cosmo-finance" and blockchain-as-art-patronage in #100 (2025). Interesting at the intersection of speculative finance and art commons.
- **[[Sandra Laugier]]** — philosopher of ordinary language, leading care ethics theorist at Paris 1 Panthéon-Sorbonne.
- **[[Pascale Molinier]]** — psychologist and care theorist, CNAM Paris. Care/ethics of care.
- **[[Mohamad Amer Meziane]]** — philosopher, wrote "Amazigh: On the Concept of Multitudes" in #100 — decolonial reframing of the journal's key concept.
- **[[Allan Deneuville]]** — geographer, mapping the journal's global geographies in #100.
- **[[Behrang Pourhosseini]]** — commons theorist.
- **[[Gaëtane Lamarche-Vadel]]** — art historian, wrote on the Icônes art section in #100.
- **[[Bayo Akomolafe]]** — Nigerian-Indian philosopher/activist, Yoruba cosmology × critical race theory. Published "Homo Icarus" in #101 (2026).

## Key Contributions

1. **Theorized the "multitude"** as an alternative political subject to People/Masses — pluralist, non-identitarian, post-workerist. This concept is the journal's central gift to political theory.
2. **Cognitive capitalism** — Moulier Boutang's framework for understanding how knowledge/creativity became the site of capitalist extraction in the digital age. Precedes most "platform capitalism" theory by a decade.
3. **Immaterial labor / digital labor** — Lazzarato's concept (adopted from earlier Italian autonomists) became foundational for understanding cultural and knowledge workers' exploitation.
4. **Commons theory in French** — key venue for commons discourse in France, bridging Ostrom (resource commons) with political philosophy.
5. **Art-theory interface** — the Icônes section has featured artists from across the world for 25 years, treating art as political thought rather than illustration.
6. **Decolonial turn** — recent issues (96–101) show an active integration of decolonial, critical race, and Global South perspectives into the autonomist tradition.
7. **Care ethics integration** — through Sandra Laugier, Pascale Molinier, and others, integrating feminist care ethics into the radical left tradition.

## Why Interesting

Multitudes represents the most intellectually serious sustained attempt in French to think through **what post-Fordist capitalism does to collective life** — and to develop political theory adequate to it. The journal's central concepts (multitude, cognitive capitalism, immaterial labor, the common) have quietly shaped how the left thinks about platform economies, digital labor, open source, and AI governance — often without direct citation.

For Amber specifically:
1. **Agentive Commoning** — the autonomist theory of the commons is the strongest intellectual foundation for Amber's agentive commoning concept. Reading Multitudes is reading the primary source tradition.
2. **Cognitive capitalism** (Moulier Boutang) — reframes AI not just as a technology but as a site of value extraction from collective intelligence. The concept of *general intellect* (from Marx, via Virno/Negri) is directly relevant.
3. **Biopolitics + AI** — Negri/Foucault tradition on power over life maps powerfully onto AI governance and AI systems that shape human behavior at scale.
4. **Art-philosophy interface** — the Icônes section treats art as political thought, directly compatible with Amber's speculative design and reality design practice.
5. **The decolonial turn in autonomism** — recent issues show the tradition actively grappling with its Eurocentric blindspots, opening space for Amber's non-Western × Western bridging.

The journal is in **French only** — this is a genuine barrier, but also means the English-speaking world consistently underestimates it. Getting published here would be distinctive.
