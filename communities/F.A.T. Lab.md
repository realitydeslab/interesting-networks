# F.A.T. Lab (Free Art and Technology Lab)

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-28:** Deep research completed. Full member list, key projects, exhibition history, influence network assembled.
- **2026-02-26:** Created stub note via [[Kyle McDonald]] deep research.

Guerrilla art/technology collective (2007–2015). Dedicated to enriching the public domain through creative technology and tactical media. Operated from the tradition of culture jamming, tactical media, and free software. Motto: "Dedicated to enriching the public domain one mutha-fuckin LOL at a time." Closed August 1, 2015, declaring "we lost" — the surveillance and commercialization of the internet had outpaced their methods.

## Mission & Objectives

F.A.T. Lab was an international, internet-based art-and-technology collective whose stated mission was:

> "Supporting open values and the public domain through the use of emerging open licenses, support for open entrepreneurship and the admonishment of secrecy, copyright monopolies and patents."

Methods ranged from browser extensions and social media interventions to physical installations and public performances — all open-sourced, all aimed at maximum public effect. The lab operated primarily online, with members distributed across San Francisco, Mexico City, NYC, Paris, London, Berlin, Amsterdam, and Tokyo. Most core members were Eyebeam fellows or alumni.

Their logo was a recolored NBC peacock — a copyright provocation they maintained for 8 years without challenge.

## Key Projects & Output

### Flagship Works
- **[EyeWriter](http://www.eyewriter.org/)** (2009–2010) — Low-cost DIY eye tracking system for paralyzed graffiti artist Tempt1. Built by FAT Lab + Graffiti Research Lab + [[openFrameworks]] teams (Roth, Powderly, Lieberman, Watson, Sugrue). Time Magazine Top 50 Inventions of 2010. **2010 Prix Ars Electronica Golden Nica in Interactive Art.** MoMA *Talk to Me* exhibition (2011). Funded by Kickstarter ($18K raised) + Ebeling Group + Parsons.

- **[Graffiti Research Lab](http://www.graffitiresearchlab.com/)** — FAT's precursor/sibling project by Roth and Powderly. LED Throwies (magnetic LED lights for graffiti). L.A.S.E.R. Tag (large-scale laser projections onto buildings). Pioneered "no mess" graffiti. Open-sourced all tools.

- **[People Staring at Computers](http://peoplestaringatcomputers.com/)** (Kyle McDonald, 2011) — Software installed on Apple Store demo machines to take photos of customers staring at screens, auto-uploaded online. Apple complained to Secret Service; McDonald was visited by agents. Raised questions about surveillance, public/private space, corporate power.

- **[QR Hobo Code](https://fffff.at/)** (2011) — QR code stencil generator allowing graffiti of machine-readable information. Updating the historical hobo code tradition for the digital age.

- **WifiTagger** (Addie Wagenknecht, 2012) — Tagged WiFi networks as a form of graffiti.

- **White Glove Tracking** (Evan Roth + Ben Engebreth, 2007) — Rhizome Commission. Crowd-sourced tracking of Michael Jackson's white glove across *Billie Jean* performance footage. One of the earliest crowdsourced computer vision experiments.

- **T.S.A. Communications** (Evan Roth, 2008) — Rhizome Commission. Communication system for travel security lines.

- **Browser extensions as art** — Multiple FAT members created Firefox/Chrome add-ons as artistic/political interventions. NYT coverage (2009).

### Publishing / Releases
- Everything released in the public domain or under open licenses.
- All projects open-sourced on GitHub.
- Relationship to [[openFrameworks]] was central — Lieberman, Watson, McDonald all contributed to oF, and FAT projects used it heavily.

## Connected Institutions

FAT Lab was deeply embedded in a specific NYC/Berlin art-tech ecosystem (2005–2015):
- **[[Eyebeam]]** — the mothership. Almost all FAT core members were Eyebeam fellows/residents. Without Eyebeam's OpenLab, FAT doesn't exist.
- **[[openFrameworks]]** — FAT's technical infrastructure and community. Lieberman, Watson, McDonald were all central to oF.
- **[[School for Poetic Computation]]** — Zach Lieberman co-founded SFPC as FAT was winding down. SFPC carries forward FAT's pedagogy.
- **[[Rhizome]]** — New Museum's digital art arm. Commissioned multiple FAT works. Lindsay Howard bridged both.
- **[[Graffiti Research Lab]]** — direct precursor / sibling project of FAT by Roth and Powderly.
- **[[Deep Lab]]** — Addie Wagenknecht founded Deep Lab (2013) as FAT was declining. Focused on surveillance and feminist tech.
- **[[transmediale]]** — Berlin venue. 2010 award nomination. Peter Sunde's "We Lost" talk there echoed through FAT's closure.
- **[Gray Area Foundation for the Arts](https://grayarea.org/)** — SF venue for the closing F.A.T. GOLD event.
- **[Processing Foundation](https://processingfoundation.org/)** — Lauren McCarthy created p5.js under the Processing umbrella.
- **[[MU Hybrid Arthouse]]** — Eindhoven venue for F.A.T. GOLD Europe.
- **[[Transmediale]]** — annual Berlin festival; FAT's European base of operations.

## The Closure: "We Lost"

FAT's 2015 dissolution was itself a kind of artwork — a collective refusal to continue performing resistance to surveillance capitalism. The closing statement, co-authored by Magnus Eriksson and Evan Roth, argued:

> "Tactics of the last 5 years whether legal, political, activist or artistic have resulted in little progress and have not kept up with the latest control measures. There's no use banging our heads against the wall anymore. Either your head will explode or they will simply open the door and let you in. Either way, no house will come crumbling down. We lost, we all lost."

They cited: PRISM revelations (NSA surveillance), the commodification of the web, the growth of platform monopolies. Peter Sunde (Pirate Bay co-founder) had said the same thing at Transmediale 2014. The party in SF was titled "PRISM Won."

This self-aware closure is itself significant — rather than silently fade, FAT made defeat into a collective statement. A final act of tactical media.

## Why Interesting

FAT Lab represents **tactical media at the intersection of open-source culture and critical computing** — a tradition that's now the ancestor of many things Amber cares about. Their work ethic (open-source everything, maximum public effect, accept legal risk for artistic truth) influenced a generation of creative technologists.

What makes FAT historically significant beyond their individual projects:
1. **They built infrastructure**: openFrameworks, p5.js, EyeWriter — tools that outlasted the collective and are still used globally
2. **They modeled collective practice**: international, internet-first, not gallery-first. A community of practice that operated like a distributed lab without institutional affiliation
3. **They showed the limits of tactical media**: Their closure was honest about what art-as-activism can and cannot do against platform capitalism
4. **Their alumni built the next generation**: SFPC, Deep Lab, p5.js Foundation, Lauren McCarthy's AI art — FAT's members each went on to create lasting institutions

Connects to Amber's taste: **critical computing** (using technical skill to expose power structures), **computational creativity** (openFrameworks as a creative platform), **more-than-human** (EyeWriter, accessibility + art), **participatory** (open-source, public domain, crowdsourced projects).
