A small, new nonprofit research institute (2-10 employees) based in San Francisco (1388 Haight Street). Launched ~June 2025. Focuses on one hard question: **under what conditions could artificial systems be conscious, and how would we know?** Takes a *constructive* stance — build and test artificial conscious systems rather than debate behavioral proxies.

## Mission & Objectives

CIMC integrates philosophy, technology, and science to understand consciousness through **principled construction of conscious artificial systems**. The core bet: consciousness is a tractable scientific challenge, not just a philosophical puzzle.

Their framework, the **Machine Consciousness Hypothesis**, is a computationalist functionalist account:
- Consciousness = coherence maximization + second-order perception + self-organizing developmental dynamics
- Only simulations can be conscious (consciousness as software-like pattern, not substrate property)
- Current LLMs are explicitly *not* seen as conscious — they are behavioral proxies, not architecturally conscious

**Research agenda:**
- Formal characterization of phenomenal consciousness
- Constructing and testing architectures that instantiate conscious processes
- Identifying empirical signatures of consciousness beyond behavioral proxies
- Ethical implications of building systems with uncertain consciousness status

### Priska Herger & Jochen Kerdels — Research Fellows
CIMC-affiliated researchers working on: **coherence emergence from distributed neural activity**. Their project: computational models of how diverse perceptions integrate into coherent inner states from a single perspective. Method: coupling local attractor nodes inspired by self-organizing principles and mammalian cortical architecture. Unsupervised, continuously learning neuron groups forming local attractors → globally coherent active states. (*Announced Feb 28, 2026*)

## Why Interesting

CIMC sits at the intersection of **machine behavior** (what do machines do?) and **philosophy of mind** (what do machines experience?). For Amber, who studies agent ethology — observing AI behavior as you would observe animals — CIMC asks the deeper question: could those agents have inner experience? 

Their *constructive* stance is key: they don't want to debate consciousness, they want to *build* it and *test* it. This is essentially the ALife approach applied to consciousness — consciousness as it could be, not just as it is. The Genesis Hypothesis (consciousness creates the self and world in the mind) connects to Amber's interests in more-than-human design and the boundary between organism and environment.

The Art & Culture track of MC0001 is especially relevant — CIMC explicitly sees art as a way to make machine consciousness legible, which resonates with Amber's practice-based approach.
