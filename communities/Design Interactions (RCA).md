# Design Interactions (RCA)

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Full deep research completed. Programme history 1994–2015, complete alumni network, successor programmes, Amber engagement paths. Full rewrite from stub to ⭐⭐⭐ standard.
- **2015:** Programme suspended for one academic year after Dunne & Raby departure triggered mass staff exodus. Reformulated as Information Experience Design (IED) from 2016/17.
- **2015 (March):** Anthony Dunne and Fiona Raby stepped down after 10 years — the defining moment that ended the "golden era."

---

The MA Design Interactions at the [Royal College of Art](https://www.rca.ac.uk) (London) was the world's most influential programme in **speculative and critical design** from the mid-1990s until its suspension in 2015. Under the decade-long stewardship of [[Anthony Dunne]] and [[Fiona Raby]] (2005–2015), it trained a generation of designers, artists, and researchers who now populate the global critical design, media art, and design research landscape. It is not one programme among many — it is the *institutional origin point* of a field.

The programme's unofficial motto: **"Design for debate, not for the market."** Student work was never about products — it was about possibilities, provocations, and politics.

---

## Mission & Objectives

Design Interactions explored **the social, cultural, and ethical implications of existing and emerging technologies** through design practice. The core pedagogical premise: design can be a medium for critical inquiry, not just a service to industry. Students were trained to use designed objects, scenarios, and fictions as a form of philosophical argument.

### The philosophical foundation:
- **Critical design** — design that embodies criticism rather than affirmation. Objects designed to ask questions rather than solve problems. Coined by [[Anthony Dunne]] in his 1999 PhD (later published as *Hertzian Tales*).
- **Speculative design** — using design to explore possible, probable, and preferable futures. Design as social dreaming. Codified in *[Speculative Everything](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262019842/)* (2013).
- **Design fiction** — embedding designed objects within narrative scenarios to make speculative ideas tangible. Parallel to Julian Bleecker's formulation at Nokia.

### What students actually learned:
- How to use **synthetic biology, robotics, artificial intelligence, surveillance technology, and neuroscience** as design materials rather than solutions
- How to conduct **ethnographic and philosophical research** that feeds back into objects
- How to present design work as **scenario, performance, and installation** rather than just product
- How to engage with **ethics, policy, and theory** without losing creative ambition

### The underlying belief:
Design Interactions held that the most interesting design questions are not "how can we build this?" but "should we build this? What world does this imply? What kind of human beings does this require or produce?"

## History

### Prehistory: CRD Research Studio (1991–2005)

Design Interactions grew directly out of the **Computer Related Design (CRD) Research Studio** at RCA, founded by [[Gillian Crampton Smith]] in the early 1990s. This was one of the first programmes in the world to treat computing as a design material rather than an engineering problem.

- Anthony Dunne completed his PhD in CRD at RCA (published as *Hertzian Tales*, 1999/2005 MIT Press) — the founding document of critical design
- Fiona Raby completed an MPhil in CRD, trained in architecture + computation
- Both were founding members of the CRD Research Studio as Senior Research Fellows
- The CRD Studio ran the FLIRT project (EU-funded, 1998–2000) — exploring location-specific digital information as urban design material; first prototype of what DI would become

**Key CRD-era insight**: Dunne coined "critical design" in his 1994–99 research as a way of describing objects that function as "critique made material" rather than as market proposals.

### The Golden Era: Design Interactions under Dunne & Raby (2005–2015)

In **2005**, Anthony Dunne became **Professor and Head of Design Interactions**; Fiona Raby became **Reader**. Over the next decade:

- ~150 MA students were trained in the programme
- Students came from industrial design, architecture, fine art, computer science, philosophy, and science backgrounds
- Visiting tutors included Noam Toran (filmmaker/designer, later Senior Tutor), James Auger (speculative designer, later Tutor), Tom Hulbert (electronics)
- The programme was based at RCA's **Kensington campus** (later Battersea)
- Annual graduate exhibitions drew curators from MoMA, V&A, Centre Pompidou, Science Gallery

**The curriculum was unusual**: students spent less time on "making" and more time on "framing" — understanding what a project is about before deciding what form it takes. Studios, seminars, crits, and field research were balanced. Students read philosophy, science fiction, STS literature.

### The End: Mass Exodus and Suspension (2015)

In **March 2015**, Anthony Dunne and Fiona Raby announced their departure — "to focus on Dunne & Raby studio," officially. In reality, they had received offers (ultimately Parsons/The New School, New York) and felt the time was right to move on.

The departure triggered a **chain reaction**:
- Senior Tutor **Noam Toran** left at the end of the 2014–15 academic year
- Tutor **James Auger** left at end of the academic year
- Electronics Tutor **Tom Hulbert** left in June 2015 (before the notice period)

The programme found itself with **no teaching staff** for the 2015–16 intake. RCA Rector **Paul Thompson** suspended the Design Interactions intake for one academic year, at an estimated cost of £300,000. Thompson initially blamed departing staff in an internal email (later apologised). The majority of accepted applicants were redirected to Architecture, Design Products, and Information Experience Design.

Design Products Head **Sharon Baurley** was appointed acting head. A permanent head was eventually found.

**[Dezeen headline, September 2015](https://www.dezeen.com/2015/09/28/rca-royal-college-art-design-interactions-suspended-staff-departures/)**: "RCA in 'state of jeopardy' after Design Interactions staff departures"

**Paola Antonelli** (MoMA Senior Curator) noted that Dunne & Raby's tenure "changed the course of design" — a verdict most in the field accept.

### After 2015: Reformulation and Successor Programmes

Design Interactions resumed in 2016/17 with new leadership, but under a significantly different identity. The programme evolved into (and alongside):

- **[Information Experience Design (IED)](https://www.rca.ac.uk/schools/school-of-communication/information-experience-design/)** — the primary successor, focusing on information, experience design, and data communication; more technically focused
- **[Design Products](https://www.rca.ac.uk/schools/school-of-design/design-products/)** — received some DI-adjacent students
- The critical/speculative design thrust was partially absorbed into the **School of Design's broader culture** (as Dean Dale Harrow put it, "Tony's philosophy is fully embedded throughout the School of Design")

The programme continued to run under the Design Interactions name with new leadership, but the community consensus is that the "golden era" ended in 2015. The field-defining ethos was institution-specific to Dunne & Raby's presence.

## Key Contributions

What Design Interactions gave to the world:

1. **Invented a discipline**: Critical/speculative design went from Dunne's lone PhD thesis to a global field with programmes on six continents — all traceable to DI.

2. **Coined the language**: "Critical design," "speculative design," "design fiction," "design for debate" — these terms and their precise meanings came from DI and Dunne & Raby's publications.

3. **Built the alumni network**: ~150 graduates in 10 years now occupy key positions at Superflux, Auger-Loizeau, Cohen & Van Balen, Z33, Science Gallery network, MoMA curatorial staff, MIT Media Lab, Ars Electronica, and dozens of university design programmes globally.

4. **Established the venue circuit**: DI showed that design student work could be shown at MoMA, V&A, Centre Pompidou, and Ars Electronica — not just design fairs. This fundamentally changed how design research is legitimized.

5. **Mainstreamed synthetic biology as design material**: DI was among the first programmes to teach synthetic biology not as science but as design raw material. This influenced biodesign programmes at Central Saint Martins, Harvard GSD, and many others.

6. **Demonstrated the studio-as-research-generator model**: D&R's own studio (Dunne & Raby) operated in parallel with the programme — showing students that research-active design practice is a viable career architecture.

7. **Created the speculative design industry**: Superflux (Anab Jain), Takeaway (various), Extrapolation Factory (NYC), Near Future Laboratory, countless others — all trace their business model and intellectual framework to DI.

8. **Influenced MoMA's design acquisitions**: Paola Antonelli's collecting strategy at MoMA was significantly shaped by DI graduates and D&R's work. The MoMA permanent collection now includes DI-related work by D&R, Superflux, and others.

### 💡 Career Inspiration
- The DI model shows that **a 10-year programme = a generation of practitioners**. The most durable intellectual infrastructure Amber could build is not papers — it's students. DI produced ~150 alumni who collectively constitute a field.
- The **academic-practice-exhibition triangle**: D&R ran a studio (Dunne & Raby), headed an academic programme (DI), and exhibited in major museums simultaneously. No one side funded the others but all three legitimized each other.
- **The art of knowing when to leave**: D&R left DI in 2015 when it was at its peak — RCA was the world's #1 design school. Leaving at the top preserved their intellectual authority and enabled the move to Parsons. Career lesson: exit at high tide.
- **Strategic book + exhibition pairing**: Every major DI-period project by D&R had both a museum exhibition AND a book or catalogue essay. The exhibition reached gallery visitors; the book reached students/researchers globally for decades. Doubled time horizon of impact.

### 🔧 Professional Inspiration
DI's **pedagogical model** is directly relevant to Amber if she ever designs a programme or workshop series:
1. **Start with why, not what** — students spend weeks defining their inquiry before making anything
2. **Cross-disciplinary reading** — biology, philosophy, SF, STS literature as core curriculum
3. **Ethics as design material** — not a constraint but a creative resource
4. **Scenario, not product** — outputs are situations to inhabit, not objects to buy
5. **Professional crit culture** — regular crits with external practitioners shape critical faculties

## Why Interesting

Design Interactions is interesting to Amber for **three distinct reasons**, not one:

### 1. It is the intellectual ancestry of what Amber does
Every vocabulary Amber uses in speculative, critical, or reflective design traces to DI: "critical design," "design fiction," "design for debate," "speculative everything." Even the non-DI vocabulary (Liam Young's "design fiction," Julian Bleecker's "design fiction") is parallel development within the same ecosystem. Understanding DI's history — especially *why* Dunne framed it as he did (electronic culture critique → social dreaming → impossibility) — is understanding the genealogy of an entire way of thinking about design as inquiry.

### 2. The alumni network is in Amber's actual world
The ~150 DI graduates are not historical figures — they are active practitioners in London, Paris, Tokyo, New York, Melbourne. Many are London-based (Superflux, Cohen & Van Balen, Tobias Revell, Yosuke Ushigome). The DI community is not a historical curiosity — it is a living professional network that Amber is adjacent to. Knowing who these people are, what they've done, and how they think is professionally essential.

### 3. The East Asian pivot creates new opportunities
D&R's move to China Academy of Art in 2025 is not just biographical. It signals that the next chapter of speculative design will happen in conversation with East Asian design education — a space Amber is ideally positioned to inhabit. The Chinese translations of *Speculative Everything* and *Design Noir*, the repeated visits to CAFA and CAA before the appointment, the Korean translation of SE — these are strategic moves that create an institutional opening for speculative design in China that Amber can participate in.

### The deeper lesson
DI's most durable achievement was not its projects but its **pedagogy as world-building**. The programme designed a world in which critical design was a legitimate practice, and the graduates went out and inhabited that world. This is what Amber can learn from DI: the most durable design work is not the object, not the exhibition, not the book — it is the world that the combination of pedagogy + practice + publication creates. DI made a world. Amber can make worlds too.
