# Cybernetic Imagination Residency

Annual creative residency program embedded within the [[ANU School of Cybernetics]] (Canberra, Australia). Platform for "innovative storytellers, thinkers, tinkerers, and creatives as well as critical doers to develop credible, hopeful, and unexpected stories about the future which imagine and instigate positive change." Each annual residency cohort develops a specific project over the year, culminating in public output (exhibitions, digital art, installations, performances). Curated and invitation-based — not open application.

## Mission & Objectives

The residency is the public-facing creative arm of the [[ANU School of Cybernetics]], established by Distinguished Professor [[Genevieve Bell]] in 2021. It operationalizes the School's belief that art and storytelling are necessary tools for navigating technological transformation — not decorative additions but epistemological contributions. Each residency year focuses on a single artist-researcher or collaborative duo, allowing deep development of one project rather than short productive sprints.

**Core intellectual agenda:**
- Futures imagination as a form of research and advocacy
- Technology neither dystopian nor naively utopian — but *credible* and *hopeful*
- Indigenous knowledge systems as legitimate frameworks for understanding and designing technology
- Art as the medium for ideas that survive beyond academic papers

The residency sits at the intersection of [[speculative-design]], [[more-than-human]], systems theory, and cultural practice — doing in practice what most STS/cybernetics discourse does in theory.

**[[Ambelin Kwaymullina]]** ⭐⭐ — *Resident, 2023*
[ANU profile](https://cybernetics.anu.edu.au/people/Ambelin-Kwaymullina/)
Palyku writer, artist, and academic. Practitioner of [Indigenous Futurisms](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/walking-the-clouds) (term coined by Anishinaabe academic Grace Dillon). Her 2023 residency applied Indigenous futurist thinking to AI through acrylic painting. Core argument, deeply subversive: in Indigenous knowledge systems, there is *no such thing* as "artificial" intelligence — intelligence is relational, embedded in Country, not produced by machines. Her paintings explored non-linear time, Aboriginal epistemologies of knowing (heart and mind inseparable), and decolonising pathways where Aboriginal knowledge-ways join other knowledge-ways for sustainable technologies. Series installed at Birch Building until December 2024. Palyku writer of Young Adult fiction (Catching Teller Crow series) with nephew Jay Boochani. Academic at University of Western Australia. ## Key Publications & Output

- **[Address to the Nations](https://cybernetics.anu.edu.au/projects/tomorrows-pasts/)** (2025, briggsnpledger) — evolving digital art archive of moving-image portraits of senior First Nations figures in alternate Australian history. Uses GenAI + dramaturgical tools. Part of larger multi-platform project [Tomorrow's Pasts](https://notyet.com.au/projects/tomorrows-past).
- **Indigenous Futurisms paintings** (2023, Ambelin Kwaymullina) — acrylic on canvas series. Exhibited Birch Building Level 3, ANU Canberra, until December 2024. Not a book or paper — the paintings *are* the research output, which is the point.
- **Ongoing VR/XR and immersive film work** (Lynette Wallworth) — her residency at ANU contributes to positioning her work within cybernetics and systems thinking, informing her metaverse governance and *Edge of Life* documentary.

## Key Contributions

- **Indigenous epistemology × AI** — the residency has consistently platformed Indigenous thinkers (Kwaymullina, Wallworth's Indigenous collaborations, briggsnpledger) applying non-Western frameworks to technology. This isn't tokenism — each project makes a substantive philosophical argument about the nature of intelligence, history, and future.
- **Credible futures imagination as institutional practice** — models how a university research unit can use residencies to generate futures knowledge that peer-reviewed papers cannot.
- **GenAI + First Nations storytelling** — briggsnpledger's *Address to the Nations* is one of the earliest serious artistic projects using GenAI as a collaborator in Indigenous history-making, raising important questions about who benefits from AI tools in cultural production.
- **Anti-disciplinary practice in the academy** — by hosting NYID's David Pledger and immersive filmmaker Wallworth, the School signals that anti-disciplinary practice is a legitimate research methodology, not a public engagement add-on.

## Why Interesting

Three things make this residency genuinely interesting rather than just "arts and AI" box-ticking:

1. **Indigenous epistemology as a serious philosophical framework.** Kwaymullina's argument — that there is no "artificial" intelligence in Indigenous worldviews — is not a cultural add-on; it's a radical critique of the foundational assumptions of the AI field. briggsnpledger's alternate history project asks "what if" at the civilizational level. This is the kind of philosophical ambition most AI-art residencies don't attempt.

2. **The residency format actually works.** One artist, one year, one project. The outputs (a painting series, a digital art archive, an ongoing immersive film practice) are substantive, not "we participated in a symposium." The commitment is real.

3. **The institutional container matters.** Being embedded in a School of Cybernetics gives residents a systems-theory vocabulary and a research community. Wallworth's cybernetics residency visibly shapes her metaverse governance work. This is different from an artist residency at a tech company.

Connection to research-taste.md: weird + rigorous, art-science bridging, non-Western + Western perspectives bridged (Indigenous epistemology × AI × cybernetics), more-than-human, speculative design, edge of chaos (AI × First Nations futures is genuinely fast-evolving). The residency's insistence on *hope* (not dystopia or utopia but credible positive change) is philosophically interesting — adjacent to Amber's interest in life-affirming AI framings.
