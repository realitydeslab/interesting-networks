# Future Histories Studio

Research lab and art studio founded by [[Stephanie Dinkins]] at [Stony Brook University](https://www.stonybrook.edu/), Department of Art. The studio investigates **equitable AI ecosystems** through community collaboration, speculative design, oral history, and storytelling — by building AI systems alongside communities of color rather than about them. One of the few university labs in the U.S. that is simultaneously a critical AI research unit, an art production space, and a community organizing platform.

## Mission & Approach

Future Histories Studio operates at the intersection of transmedia storytelling, AI building, community organizing, and cultural analysis. Its central thesis: communities of color must be participants — not just subjects — in the design of AI systems that affect their lives. The studio's methodology involves:

- **Community co-creation**: Workshop-based participatory design where Black, Indigenous, and communities of color author AI systems using their own knowledge
- **Cultural AI training**: Building AI systems trained on Black oral history, family memory, and vernacular knowledge rather than hegemonic datasets
- **Art as research vehicle**: Using aesthetic practice to make algorithmic systems legible, contestable, and imaginable by non-technical publics
- **Speculative worldbuilding**: Imagining futures where AI reflects cultural diversity, not just Western/corporate values

## Projects

### N'TOO — Not the Only One (2018–present)
*The studio's flagship project.* An experimental, multigenerational memoir of one Black American family, told from the "mind" of an AI of evolving intellect. N'TOO is a **voice-interactive AI robot** that:
- Is trained using a recursive neural network (deep learning) on data gathered from members of Dinkins' family — oral histories, stories, recipes, knowledge
- Serves as a "physically embodied artificially intelligent agent that senses and acts on its world"
- Is explicitly designed, trained, and aligned with the needs and ideals of Black and brown people who are underrepresented in the tech sector
- Prompts critical questions about memory, identity, and who controls AI's learning

Exhibited at: Whitney Museum of American Art, de Young Museum (SF), Philadelphia Museum of Art, Museum of Contemporary Photography, ICP (NYC), Herning Kunstmuseum (Denmark), The Barbican (London), Queens Museum, and many others. Supported by [Creative Capital grant](https://creative-capital.org/projects/not-the-only-one/).

### Project al-Khwarizmi / PAK (2017–present)
Named after medieval Persian mathematician Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi who introduced algebra to the West. A **series of pop-up workshops** for communities of color that:
- Use art as a vehicle to help citizens understand how algorithms and AI impact their lives
- Gather artists, youth, elders, and computer scientists to collaboratively design algorithms and chatbots
- Teach algorithmic thinking through vernacular examples (e.g., "the talk" Black parents give children about police encounters as a practical algorithm)
- Support communities in subverting or exploiting algorithmic biases

Venues: Eyebeam (Brooklyn), Recess (Brooklyn), Google NYC, Duke University.

### AI.Assembly
A series of public conversations asking the question: **"What does AI need from you?"** — framing AI development as a social contract requiring input from all communities, not just technologists.

### Conversations with Bina48 (2014–present)
Long-running photographic and video documentation of Dinkins' conversations with Bina48, a humanoid AI robot. The project probes race, gender, and bias in AI systems — revealing how even an AI modeled after a Black woman was shaped by white, male creators.

### Okra Continuum
A project exploring the entanglement of African diaspora cultural knowledge with emerging technologies. Draws on oral tradition, plant life, and food knowledge as carriers of ancestral intelligence.

### #WhenWordsFail
A project exploring the limits of language and AI in expressing human experience.

## Why Interesting for Amber

**The making lab model.** Future Histories Studio is one of the clearest examples of a university lab that builds AI systems *alongside* communities rather than studying them. Not a critique lab — a creation lab with political commitments. This is the model Amber may want to build: where Stony Brook is to equitable AI, Oxford could be to trustworthy agentic AI systems built with (not for) underrepresented communities.

**Methodology overlap.** The studio's approach to community co-design with algorithmic systems connects to Amber's interest in agentive commoning, more-than-human design, and participatory futures. PAK's "algorithm as vernacular practice" framing is a powerful pedagogical model.

**N'TOO and machine memory.** The N'TOO project directly resonates with Amber's interest in machine death and machine consciousness — what does it mean for an AI to carry family memory? For AI to inherit oral tradition? This is a precursor to questions about AI mortality and continuity of self.

**Art circuits.** Dinkins shows at serious contemporary art venues (Whitney, Barbican, de Young) while maintaining research credibility. This hybrid positioning — artist + researcher + community organizer — is exactly the kind of cross-disciplinary identity Amber may be building.

**Institutions to watch.** Eyebeam, Recess, and Data & Society are all allies in this orbit — possible collaborators or venues for Amber's work.
