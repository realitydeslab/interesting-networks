# UCL Interaction Centre (UCLIC)

World-leading HCI research centre at University College London, lodged in the heart of Bloomsbury. Jointly housed between UCL's Department of Computer Science and the Division of Psychology and Language Sciences — this dual-disciplinary structure is its defining feature. ~90 researchers: 22 faculty, 26 postdocs, 48 PhD students. Directed by [[Yvonne Rogers]], who coined the "research in the wild" methodology that now shapes how the global HCI field thinks about in-situ technology design. Most cited HCI centre in the UK. Immediate neighbour to the British Library, British Museum, and the broader London tech/art ecology. For Amber: directly accessible from Oxford (1hr by train), one of the few places where rigorous CS and social science traditions genuinely integrate.

## Mission & Objectives

UCLIC exists to understand how people and technology can work better together — but with a commitment to empirical rigor and real-world deployment that distinguishes it from purely design-led or purely engineering labs. Core method: "in-the-wild" studies — deploying novel technologies in actual settings (homes, schools, hospitals, streets) and studying what happens when they meet the messiness of real life. This contrasts with lab-based usability testing and gives their work an ecological validity that most HCI labs lack.

The Centre draws on two traditions: **Computer Science** (systems, sensors, interaction architectures) and **Human Sciences** (psychology, cognitive science, linguistics, behavioral economics). This dual heritage produces research that can both build things and explain why they work — or why they don't.

Eleven research themes:
1. **Affective Computing** — emotion-aware interfaces, body movement as data, pain and wellbeing
2. **Ageing and Technology** — digital equity for older people, designing for cognitive change
3. **Artificial Intelligence & HCI** — how generative AI changes interaction; novel AI-HCI frameworks
4. **Behavioural Change** — technology for habit formation, nudging, self-regulation
5. **Digital Health** — designing and evaluating health/wellbeing tech interventions
6. **Disability Interactions** — assistive tech, accessible design (home of the Global Disability Innovation Hub)
7. **Educational Technologies** — interactive learning, e-lectures, computer-based assessment
8. **Future of Work** — new models for distributed/hybrid labour, cognitive tools
9. **Future Technologies** — next-gen interfaces: wearables, tangibles, large installations, cross-device
10. **Interactions in the Wild** — in-situ studies, participatory design, ethnographic methods
11. **Multisensory Experiences** — touch, smell, taste, sound: embodied and multisensory interfaces

## Key People

### Core Faculty

**[[Yvonne Rogers]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — Director of UCLIC. Professor of Interaction Design. Co-author of *Interaction Design: Beyond Human-Computer Interaction* (Wiley, now 6th ed.) — the most widely used HCI textbook in the world, translated into multiple languages. Coined "research in the wild" as both a methodology and a philosophical stance. Former Director of the Pervasive Interaction Lab at the Open University. CHI Academy member (the HCI equivalent of the Royal Society). Key ideas: ambient displays, distributed cognition, external cognition, scaffolding. Very connected to industry (Apple, Microsoft, Google). See stub: [[Yvonne Rogers]].

**[[Ann Blandford]]** ⭐⭐ — Professor of Human-Computer Interaction, Vice Dean (Equality, Diversity & Inclusion) at UCL Faculty of Brain Sciences. Former Director of UCLIC. Digital health specialist; seminal work on human factors in healthcare settings — how clinicians interact with complex systems (EHRs, infusion pumps, monitoring equipment). Methodologically rigorous, mixed-methods. Co-authored influential work on "interactive critical systems." Highly respected figure in European HCI. [UCL profile](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/computer-science/people/professor-ann-blandford).

**[[Anna Cox]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — Professor & HCI MSc Deputy Programme Director. Digital wellbeing, self-regulation, smartphone overuse, screen time. Known for empirical work on "design frictions" — intentional friction in interfaces to prompt reflection and reduce mindless use. Strong connection to the [[Oxford Internet Institute]] / wellbeing-tech policy world. Increasingly relevant as AI agents become ambient. Key figure in the emerging "digital wellbeing" sub-field that now has policy weight (UK government, WHO). [UCL profile](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/uclic/people).

**[[Nadia Bianchi-Berthouze]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — Professor, Academic Director of the [Global Disability Innovation Hub (GDI Hub)](https://www.disabilityinnovation.com/). Affective computing via body movement — using posture, gesture, and movement patterns to infer emotional/pain states. Cross-disciplinary bridge between HCI, clinical research, and AI. Works on chronic pain recognition through movement analysis; also VR/games for pain management. The GDI Hub is the WHO Collaborating Centre for Assistive Technology. Extraordinary reach into humanitarian/global health contexts. [UCL profile](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/uclic/people).

**[[Nicolai Marquardt]]** ⭐⭐ — Associate Professor & MSc Programme Director. Physical computing, near-surface interaction, large-scale displays, cross-device interaction, sensing and visualization for creative exploration (Sensor Sandbox). Known for elegant prototyping of novel interfaces. [Personal site](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/uclic/people) / [UCL profile](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/uclic/people).

**[[Paul Marshall]]** ⭐⭐ — Professor & Deputy Director of UCLIC. Tangible and physical interaction; embodied cognition and collaborative learning environments. Works on how physical objects and spatial arrangements shape thinking and collaboration. Co-led "tangible tabletops" research. [UCL profile](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/uclic/people).

**[[Duncan Brumby]]** ⭐⭐ — Professor & Deputy Director of UCLIC. Interaction science — attention, multitasking, procedural errors, driver distraction. Evidence-based approach to interface design. Faculty Graduate Tutor. [UCL profile](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/uclic/people).

**[[Jon Bird]]** ⭐⭐ — Professor. Physical computing with a philosophical edge — interested in minimal technology, slow technology, sustainable interaction. Background in cognitive science and philosophy of mind. Related to somaesthetic approaches. [UCL profile](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/uclic/people).

**[[Catherine Holloway]]** ⭐⭐ — Professor, Director of MSc Disability, Design and Innovation, Co-Director of GDI Hub. WHO Collaborating Centre for Assistive Technology. Works at the intersection of inclusive design, global health equity, and assistive technology deployment in low/middle-income countries. [GDI Hub](https://www.disabilityinnovation.com/who-we-are/partners/ucl).

**[[Rowanne Fleck]]** ⭐ — Lecturer in Digital Health. Focuses on technology-mediated mental health and digital therapeutics. [UCL profile](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/uclic/people).

## Key Contributions

1. **"Research in the Wild" methodology** — Yvonne Rogers' framework for deploying and studying technology in situ. Influenced an entire generation of HCI researchers. Counter-movement to lab-based usability testing; reclaimed ecological validity.
2. **HCI Textbook hegemony** — The Rogers/Preece/Sharp textbook has defined HCI education globally for two decades. Having the leading textbook = curriculum control = field-shaping power.
3. **Global Disability Innovation Hub** — First dedicated AT research hub, with WHO Collaborating Centre status. Policy reach into global health (COVAX, LMICs).
4. **Digital Wellbeing as a field** — Anna Cox and collaborators essentially created "digital wellbeing" as a legitimate research area, with policy consequences (UK government screen time guidance, App Store wellbeing tools).
5. **Affective computing via body movement** — Bianchi-Berthouze's work on non-facial emotion/pain recognition is a significant AI+HCI advance.
6. **Interdisciplinary model** — UCLIC demonstrates that housing HCI jointly between CS and Psychology actually works, producing research neither could do alone.

## Why Interesting

Three reasons UCLIC is a ⭐⭐⭐ for Amber:

**1. Intellectual fit is exact.** The "Interactions in the Wild" methodology is what Amber should be doing with AI agents — deploying them in real settings and studying what actually happens. UCLIC wrote the book (literally, Yvonne Rogers did) on how to do this rigorously. Anna Cox's digital wellbeing work is the empirical counterpart to Amber's more philosophical/design-led approach to AI agent welfare. They're doing the science; Amber could bring the speculative design and more-than-human framing they're currently missing.

**2. Geography = opportunity.** Oxford to London is 1 hour. UCLIC is the closest world-class HCI centre to Amber. This is the place where Amber can show up at seminars, meet faculty, get invited back. Academic visibility comes from physical presence, and UCLIC makes that possible at low cost.

**3. Policy+research bridge.** UCLIC has unusual reach into UK government (digital wellbeing, disability, health tech). Ann Blandford is a Vice Dean. GDI Hub has WHO status. This matters for Amber whose work on AI agents and machine behavior has policy implications. UCLIC is one of the few HCI places that has both research credibility AND policy access.

What's slightly less interesting: UCLIC's dominant mode is empirical/applied, sometimes at the expense of theoretical depth or speculative ambition. They rarely do the kind of weird, boundary-pushing work that Amber gravitates toward (that's more RTD, DIS, Antikythera territory). The most interesting research in the group is the work that gets uncomfortable — Anna Cox's critique of design patterns that exploit users, Jon Bird's minimal/slow technology stance, anything touching posthumanism or more-than-human design. Follow those threads.
