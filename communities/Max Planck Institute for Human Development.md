The global home of **machine behavior** as a scientific discipline. Two centers define MPIB's relevance to Amber: the **Center for Humans and Machines** (CHM), where Iyad Rahwan is building the science of AI behavioral ecology, and the **Center for Adaptive Rationality** (ARC), heir to Gigerenzer's tradition of ecological rationality and fast-and-frugal heuristics. Together they represent the most intellectually rigorous intersection of AI behavior, human decision-making, and societal implications outside of Amber's own HCI/design field.

## Mission & Objectives

MPIB is one of 86 institutes of the Max Planck Society — Germany's most prestigious research organization, equivalent to the CNRS in France or the National Academies in the US. Founded in **1963** in West Berlin, it was originally focused on the psychology of human development and education. Today it spans developmental psychology, neuroscience, decision science, and the emerging science of human-AI interaction.

**Core interdisciplinary identity:** Psychologists, computer scientists, biologists, neuroscientists, philosophers, economists, and physicists working together on how humans develop, decide, and now — increasingly — how they coexist with artificial agents.

**Key research questions:**
- How do people make effective decisions under time pressure and information overload?
- How does digitalization reshape the human mind?
- What is machine behavior, and how can it be understood scientifically?
- How do human minds interact with online environments?

## Centers & Key People

### Center for Humans and Machines (CHM)

**Director:** [Iyad Rahwan](https://www.mpib-berlin.mpg.de/chm)  
**URL:** [https://www.mpib-berlin.mpg.de/chm](https://www.mpib-berlin.mpg.de/chm)

**The flagship group for Amber's field.** CHM conducts interdisciplinary science to understand, anticipate, and shape major disruptions from digital media and AI on how we think, learn, work, play, cooperate, and govern. Rahwan coined the term **"machine behavior"** (in the landmark 2019 *Nature* paper) — framing AI systems as behavioral entities whose actions in society can be studied empirically, just as ethologists study animal behavior.

**Research Agenda:**
- Machine behavior as a scientific discipline (AI as behavioral subject, not just tool)
- How AI shapes and corrupts human morality, culture, and cooperation
- Moral decision-making at the population scale (the Moral Machine experiment)
- Science fiction as a method for predicting AI social impacts
- Agentic AI and the delegation of unethical behavior
- Machine culture — how culture is mediated and generated by machines

**Key CHM People:**
- **Iyad Rahwan** — Director. Syrian-born computational social scientist. Former MIT Media Lab professor. Pioneer of machine behavior. Coined the field; runs the CHM conference. One of the most cited researchers in AI ethics globally.
- **Nils Köbis** — Behavioral economist at CHM. Dishonesty, corruption, AI agents and moral behavior. Co-author on the "Delegation to AI" Nature paper.
- **Jean-François Bonnefon** — CHM researcher. Moral cognition, autonomous vehicles, how people reason about AI agents' decisions. Co-author on Moral Machine.
- **Stephan Lewandowsky** — CHM collaborator. Misinformation, cognition, online environments, democracy.

### Center for Adaptive Rationality (ARC)

**Director:** [Ralph Hertwig](https://www.mpib-berlin.mpg.de/research/research-centers/adaptive-rationality)  
**URL:** [https://www.mpib-berlin.mpg.de/research/research-centers/adaptive-rationality](https://www.mpib-berlin.mpg.de/research/research-centers/adaptive-rationality)

Heir to **Gerd Gigerenzer**'s foundational work on ecological rationality, fast-and-frugal heuristics, and the "adaptive toolbox." ARC continues and extends this tradition, now incorporating computational methods, online cognition, and collective intelligence.

**Research Areas:**
1. **Simple Heuristics** — How people infer and decide using minimal information by exploiting environmental regularities
2. **Search and Learning** — When to stop searching and act (optimal stopping problems in the wild)
3. **Collective Intelligence** — When do groups outperform individuals? Social information exploitation
4. **Lifespan Development of Decision Making** — How decision-making develops from childhood to old age
5. **Boosting Decision Making** — Empowering people to make better decisions (not just nudging)
6. **Cognition in Online Environments** — How human minds interact with digital environments; implications for democracy

**Key ARC People:**
- **Ralph Hertwig** — Director. Ecological rationality, risk communication, boosting. Successor to Gigerenzer.
- **Gerd Gigerenzer** — *Emeritus*. Founder of the heuristics tradition. His *Simple Heuristics That Make Us Smart* (1999) and *Rationality for Mortals* (2008) define the field. Still influential.
- **Thorsten Pachur** — Senior researcher at ARC. Risk perception, decision under uncertainty.
- **Wouter Kool** — ARC researcher. Computational models of decision making, effort, cognitive control.

### Center for Developmental Psychology (Lifespan Development)

Studies how cognition, memory, and brain function change across the human lifespan — from infancy through old age. Director: **Ulman Lindenberger**. Relevant context for MPIB's overall intellectual culture.

### Environmental Neuroscience

Studies how the physical environment (urban vs. natural) affects brain and behavior. Director: **Simone Kühn**. The [Max Planck UCL Centre for Computational Psychiatry and Ageing Research](https://www.mps-ucl-centre.mpg.de) is co-run with UCL.

## Key Publications & Output

### Canonical CHM Papers (All Amber-Relevant)

- Rahwan, I. et al. (2019). **Machine Behaviour**. *Nature*, 568, 477–486. [DOI: 10.1038/s41586-019-1138-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-019-1138-y) — The paper that coined the field. Argues for studying AI systems as behavioral entities using tools from animal behavior science and social science. **Amber's closest intellectual ancestor paper.**

- Awad, E. et al. (2018). **The Moral Machine Experiment**. *Nature*, 563, 59–64. [DOI: 10.1038/s41586-018-0637-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-018-0637-6) — 40M+ responses from 233 countries on moral trade-offs for autonomous vehicles. Scale play + participatory method. Probably the most impactful AI ethics experiment ever run.

- Acerbi, A., Tennie, C., & Mesoudi, A. (2023). **Machine Culture**. *Nature Human Behaviour*, 7, 1523–1524. [DOI: 10.1038/s41562-023-01742-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-023-01742-2) — Explores whether AI systems can generate and transmit culture; what "machine culture" means.

- Köbis, N., & Mossink, L. D. (2021). **Bad Machines Corrupt Good Morals**. *Nature Human Behaviour*, 5, 1038–1045. [DOI: 10.1038/s41562-021-01128-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41562-021-01128-2) — AI agents corrupt human moral behavior.

- Crandall, J. W. et al. (2018). **Cooperating with Machines**. *Nature Communications*, 9, 233. [DOI: 10.1038/s41467-017-02597-8](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-017-02597-8) — Machine learning algorithms that cooperate with humans and other machines at human-level.

- Rahwan, I. et al. (2025). **The Science Fiction Science Method**. *Nature*. [DOI: 10.1038/s41586-025-09194-6](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-025-09194-6) — Using sci-fi to predict social/behavioral impacts of future technologies before they're built. **Direct methodological overlap with Amber's speculative design work.**

- Köbis, N. et al. (2025). **Delegation to AI Can Increase Dishonest Behaviour**. *Nature*. [DOI: 10.1038/s41586-025-09505-x](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-025-09505-x) — Delegating tasks to AI agents facilitates unethical behavior. Key paper on agentic AI risks.
