# Stanford HCI Group

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Deep research completed. Expanded from stub to full profile with history, faculty, alumni, key contributions, and connections to Amber's interests.

The world's most influential human-computer interaction research group, operating out of Stanford University's Gates Computer Science Building. Founded in the 1980s around Terry Winograd's vision of designing computing technology that genuinely serves human needs, the group defined what HCI *is* as a field — and is now redefining what it becomes in the era of LLMs and social AI. The birthplace of Generative Agents, Flash Teams, Street-Level Algorithms, and dozens of other field-defining concepts. If you want to understand the dominant US paradigm for human-centered AI, this is the center of gravity.

**Personal connection:** Amber is a Stanford MS CS alumnus — this is her intellectual home institution, and many researchers here are potential direct collaborators or entry points into US HCI/AI networks.

## Mission & Objectives

**Mission:** "How do we design computing systems effectively and responsibly?" Research advances systems, designs, and models for human-computer interaction, with deep attention to how computing platforms reshape human behavior — social dynamics, democracy, labor, and collective action.

**Core philosophy:** Computing platforms are architectures of behavior. As Winston Churchill said, "We shape our buildings; thereafter they shape us." The Stanford HCI Group takes this seriously: changing the design of a social media feed, a labor platform, or a simulation environment changes how people think, relate, and act. Design is ethics.

**Current focus (2022–present):** The group is at a historic pivot point — from studying how humans interact with software, to studying how AI systems interact with society. LLM social simulation, AI-enhanced work, algorithmic governance, and human-AI alignment are now central themes.

## History

**1970s origins:** Stanford's engagement with HCI predates the group's formal founding. [Stuart K. Card](https://www.parc.com/person/stuart-card/), working at nearby Xerox PARC, co-authored [*The Psychology of Human-Computer Interaction*](https://www.amazon.com/Psychology-Human-Computer-Interaction-Stuart-Card/dp/0898598028) (1983) with Thomas Moran and Allen Newell — the book that popularized the term "HCI" and established it as a field. Card would later join Stanford as consulting professor.

**1973: Terry Winograd arrives at Stanford.** After creating [SHRDLU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SHRDLU) (a landmark natural language understanding program) at MIT, Winograd moved to Stanford and experienced a profound shift in thinking. Encountering [Hubert Dreyfus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubert_Dreyfus)'s critique of cognitivism and working with Chilean philosopher [Fernando Flores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fernando_Flores), he shifted away from classical AI toward understanding computing as a design discipline. This philosophical turn — from AI as intelligence simulation to computing as human practice design — became the intellectual foundation of the Stanford HCI approach.

**1986:** Winograd and Flores published [*Understanding Computers and Cognition: A New Foundation for Design*](https://www.amazon.com/Understanding-Computers-Cognition-Foundation-Design/dp/0201112973) — applying phenomenology (Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty) to computing design. Radical claim: computers are not thinking machines but tools embedded in human practices. Influenenced design practice, not just theory.

**1991:** Winograd formally founded the "Project on People, Computers and Design" — the direct ancestor of the Stanford HCI Group. Published [*Bringing Design to Software*](https://www.amazon.com/Bringing-Design-Software-Terry-Winograd/dp/0201854910) (1996), which became foundational design education.

**1990s: The d.school seed.** Winograd worked closely with [David Kelley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Kelley_(designer)) (founder of IDEO) on product design and HCI. This collaboration seeded what would become Stanford's [d.school (Hasso Plattner Institute of Design)](https://dschool.stanford.edu/) — now one of the world's foremost design education programs. Winograd helped found the d.school.

**1995–1998: Google is born.** Winograd served as adviser to Stanford PhD student [Larry Page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Larry_Page), who was building a web search engine. Page (with [Sergey Brin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergey_Brin)) took leave in 1998 to co-found Google. Winograd is thus the academic grandfather of Google — an extraordinary piece of Stanford HCI heritage. (Winograd spent a sabbatical at Google in 2002 as visiting researcher.)

**2000s: Expanding faculty, defining CSCW.** As the web matured, the group expanded its interests to computer-supported cooperative work (CSCW), social software, and online communities. Major new faculty hires; the group became increasingly interdisciplinary — connecting CS, psychology, sociology, design.

**2012: Michael Bernstein joins.** Fresh from his MIT PhD (Crowd-Powered Systems, George M. Sprowls Award for best CS PhD thesis at MIT), Bernstein joined Stanford faculty. Over the next decade he would become the group's intellectual center of gravity, defining three consecutive research arcs: crowd computing, critical algorithm studies, and LLM social simulation.

**2023: Generative Agents.** PhD student Joon Sung Park, advised by Michael Bernstein, published [Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03442) (UIST 2023, Best Paper). The paper launched a new field. It's the paper that made the rest of the world realize: LLMs don't just generate text, they can simulate people. The Stanford HCI Group is now at the center of AI's most consequential shift since deep learning.

## Key Contributions

The Stanford HCI Group's intellectual contributions are large enough to constitute a significant portion of modern HCI's foundational concepts:

### From the Winograd Era
- **Language/Action Perspective on Computing** — Winograd + Flores: computers facilitate *actions* and *commitments* between people, not just information processing. Shifted HCI from cognitive models to social practice.
- **Design as a discipline** — Helping establish that software design is distinct from engineering and analysis. Birthed the d.school.
- **Google** — Indirectly: Winograd advised Larry Page.
- **SHRDLU** — Winograd's PhD thesis at MIT (1970): one of the most famous AI programs in history, a natural language understanding system. Shows the group's long AI lineage.

### From the Card/PARC Era
- **Model Human Processor** — Stuart Card et al.: theoretical framework for predicting human performance with computers. Foundational cognitive engineering.
- **Information Visualization** — Card, Mackinlay, Shneiderman: invented many core InfoViz techniques and the field itself.
- **Fitts's Law for the Mouse** — Card's characterization of mouse pointing became the scientific basis for mouse design and evaluation.

### From the Bernstein Era
- **Crowd Computing** — Flash Teams, Soylent, Turkoption, Dynamo: defined how human labor can be computationally organized, coordinated, and empowered.
- **Street-Level Algorithms** — Critical framework for analyzing algorithmic decision-making as discretionary bureaucratic practice. Most cited critical computing paper of the 2010s.
- **Generative Agents** — The paper that launched the LLM agent simulation field. Architecture (memory + reflection + planning) is now the blueprint for virtually all LLM agent research.
- **Social Simulacra** — Predecessor to Generative Agents: using LLMs to simulate populated online communities for design prototyping.
- **LLooM** — Inductive concept discovery from large text corpora using LLMs (CHI 2024).
- **Alexandria** — AI-powered social media reranking for pluralistic values (ICWSM 2026).
- **Jury Learning** — Redesigning ML annotation to honor disagreement rather than enforcing consensus.
- **Ethics and Society Review** — Bernstein's institutional design for ethics review of AI research proposals (now piloted at Stanford).
