# Center for Humanities Research (CHR), University of the Western Cape

The Center for Humanities Research (CHR) at the [University of the Western Cape (UWC)](https://www.uwc.ac.za/), Cape Town, South Africa is one of Africa's most intellectually ambitious humanities research institutes. Founded at a university born out of apartheid resistance, it has become a major Global South site for postcolonial/decolonial critical theory, archival practices, memory studies, and fundamental questions about what the university should be. Directed by [[Premesh Lalu]] (Professor of History). Since November 2024, [[Erich Hörl]] holds an Extraordinary Professorship here — creating a distinctive German media philosophy–South African postcolonial theory axis that is theoretically generative and institutionally unusual.

The CHR is funded through the **DST-NRF Flagship for Critical Thought in African Humanities**, a major South African national research initiative. This gives it PhD fellowships (Next Generation Scholars), postdoctoral positions, and international visiting researcher capacity.

## Background

**University history:** UWC was established in 1960 during apartheid as a university designated for "Coloured" people under the apartheid classification system. Rather than becoming a compliant institution, it transformed into a site of resistance, radical thought, and anti-apartheid activism — earning the nickname "the People's University." This heritage profoundly shapes the CHR's intellectual orientation.

**CHR founding and mission:** The Center for Humanities Research emerges from this resistance tradition. Its mission is explicitly about rethinking the university and knowledge from the Global South — not merely applying existing theoretical frameworks but asking what critical thinking looks like from the postcolony, from Africa, from a place that was systematically excluded from the supposed universality of Western knowledge production.

**DST-NRF Flagship for Critical Thought in African Humanities:** This is the main funding mechanism. South Africa's Department of Science and Technology (DST) and National Research Foundation (NRF) designated CHR as one of a small number of "Flagship" research projects, giving it sustained funding for doctoral fellowships ("Next Generation Scholars"), postdoctoral researchers ("Early Career Fellows"), and infrastructure for convening scholars internationally. This created an unusual model — a humanities research institute with real funding power to form cohorts and attract international visitors.

**The Hörl axis (2024-):** The appointment of [[Erich Hörl]] as Extraordinary Professor in November 2024 is a distinctive development. Hörl brings: German media philosophy, the concept of "general ecology" (from Simondon, Stiegler, Guattari), the "technological condition," and current work on disruption theory. Lalu brings: South African postcolonial theory, archives and memory, the university question, the Trojan Horse as site of political thought. Their 2024 *Theory, Culture & Society* paper ("Unseating Mastery") is the intellectual product of this axis — arguing that the university must now be thought from the standpoint of disruption, decolonisation, and the "promise of the new."

## Topics

The CHR works at the intersection of several major intellectual problems:

1. **The University Question** — What is the university for? What would a decolonial, post-apartheid university look like? This is not just a policy question but a philosophical one. Lalu's *Critical Times* (2019) paper and the Hörl-Lalu "Unseating Mastery" (2024) are the key texts.

2. **Archives, Memory, and the Postcolony** — Archives as sites of power, violence, and counter-memory. The CHR has been central to debates about apartheid-era archives, liberation struggle archives, and what it means to digitize them ("virtual stampede for Africa" — Lalu 2007). Key question: who controls the past?

3. **Post-Apartheid Social Theory** — What is "the social" after apartheid? *Remains of the Social* (2017) is the major intervention: engaging with Derrida, Butler, Arendt, Lacan, Foucault from a specifically post-apartheid standpoint.

4. **The Becoming Technical of the Human** — Lalu's concept: the student movements and political struggles in the Western Cape were also moments in which humans encountered the technical (the Trojan Horse massacre: the state weaponized a truck). This connects to Hörl's interest in technical becoming and the "technological condition." Their intellectual alignment is real.

5. **Critical Thought in African Humanities** — The flagship programme's guiding question: what does critical thought look like from Africa? How do African intellectual traditions (including but not limited to anti-apartheid thought, Ubuntu, precolonial knowledge systems) inform critique?

6. **General Ecology + Decolonial Theory (new axis, 2024-)** — The Hörl-Lalu collaboration opens new territory: can "general ecology" (the becoming-environmental of sense, power, capital) be thought from the postcolony? Does the technosphere look different from Cape Town than from Lüneburg?

### Books (CHR-affiliated)

- *The Deaths of Hintsa: Postapartheid South Africa and the Shape of Recurring Pasts* — Premesh Lalu. UC Press. Lalu's major monograph. On a 19th-century Xhosa king whose skull was taken by British colonizers and whose death recurs as a site of post-apartheid mourning and contestation.

### Key Papers

- **["Unseating Mastery: The University and the Promise of the New"](https://doi.org/10.1177/02632764241289621)** — Erich Hörl & Premesh Lalu. *Theory, Culture & Society* 41(7-8), 2024, pp. 219-238. The intellectual manifesto of the Hörl-Lalu axis. Argues that the university in the present "disruptive condition" must be rethought beyond mastery (the traditional model of transmitting already-known knowledge) toward an openness to the genuinely new — a decolonial and philosophical intervention simultaneously.

- **"What is the university for?"** — Premesh Lalu. *Critical Times* 2(2), 2019. Lalu's contribution to the global debate about university crisis, from the specific position of UWC and the post-apartheid.

- **"The Trojan Horse and the 'Becoming Technical of the Human'"** — Premesh Lalu. In *Remains of the Social*, 2017, pp. 249-274. On the 1985 Trojan Horse Massacre (Athlone, Cape Town) — when apartheid police hid in a truck and killed three students. Lalu reads this as a site where politics and technics intersect.

- **"Where Does Sadness Come From? Politics, Potentiality and a Possible Humanities"** — Premesh Lalu. *Journal of Asian and African Studies* 47(1), 2012. A Spinozist engagement: what role can the humanities play?

- **"The virtual stampede for Africa: Digitisation, postcoloniality and archives of the liberation struggles in Southern Africa"** — Premesh Lalu. *Innovation* 2007. On the politics of digitizing apartheid-era archives.

- **"Apartheid's University: Notes on the Renewal of the Enlightenment"** — Premesh Lalu. *Journal of Higher Education in Africa* 5(2-3), 2007. Early paper on the university question.

- **"Empire and Nation"** — Premesh Lalu. *Journal of Southern African Studies* 41(3), 2015.

- **"Medical Anthropology, Subaltern Traces, and the Making and Meaning of Western Medicine in South Africa: 1895–1899"** — Premesh Lalu. *History in Africa* 25, 1998, pp. 133-159.

## Why Interesting

The CHR represents one of the most intellectually serious institutional experiments in the Global South humanities. It's not just "African studies" in the traditional area-studies sense — it's genuinely engaged with the most difficult questions of continental philosophy, critical theory, and political thought, while insisting that these questions must be asked from the specific position of the postcolony.

**What makes it distinctive:**

1. **The university question as practice** — The CHR is not just theorizing the university from outside; it IS the university experiment. UWC's apartheid origins and post-apartheid transformation are the raw material of the research. This gives CHR a kind of embedded, embodied quality that elite Western humanities institutes lack.

2. **The Hörl-Lalu axis** — This is genuinely unusual. German media philosophy and African decolonial theory are rarely connected. The concept of "general ecology" (milieu, environment, technosphere) is potentially very productive when thought from the standpoint of the postcolony — where technics arrived as colonial violence, where the environment has been shaped by extractive capitalism, where the human was constituted through racial technologies.

3. **"Unseating Mastery"** — The 2024 TCS paper is a significant intellectual intervention. "Mastery" is a concept with dual valence: academic mastery (transmitting established knowledge) and colonial mastery (the master/slave relation). Arguing that the university must "unseat mastery" and open to "the promise of the new" connects epistemology to decolonization in a philosophically serious way.

4. **Critical Times journal** — Lalu published in *Critical Times* (Cambridge UP/Duke UP), which is one of the most rigorous venues for critical theory with global reach. This is not provincial scholarship.

5. **Archive as site of political thought** — The CHR's work on archives and memory (apartheid-era records, liberation struggle documents, colonial archives) is a politically urgent form of humanistic research. As AI tools for mass archive digitization and processing emerge, this becomes even more important.
