# WISER — Wits Institute for Social and Economic Research

Africa's leading interdisciplinary humanities research institute. 25+ years generating essential scholarship from the Global South on necropolitics, hydrocolonialism, biometric capitalism, African aesthetics, digital identity infrastructures, postcolonial urbanism, and — now most relevant for Amber — the elemental turn: plants, water, air as analytical objects.

Based at [University of the Witwatersrand](https://www.wits.ac.za/), Johannesburg. Home of [[Achille Mbembe]], [[Sarah Nuttall]], [[Isabel Hofmeyr]]. Directed since 2024 by Hlonipha Mokoena. The intellectual ground zero for critical humanities from and about Africa.

## Mission & Objectives

WISER exists to generate cutting-edge interdisciplinary scholarship in the African humanities; to build sustained intellectual community around postgraduate students, writers in residence, and international visitors; and to practice critical public humanities *from* the Global South for the world. It refuses to let African scholarship be only regional — it insists on its global theoretical weight.

Core intellectual threads:
- **Necropolitics** — who is made to live, who is made to die (Mbembe's framework, now cited globally)
- **Hydrocolonialism** — water, oceans, and colonial extraction (Hofmeyr's Oceanic Humanities project)
- **Biometric capitalism** — digital ID, surveillance, financial infrastructures in postcolonial Africa (Breckenridge's Standard Bank Chair)
- **African aesthetics** — art, literature, and visual culture from the South
- **More-than-human/elemental** — plants, air, water as analytical objects (Nuttall + Hofmeyr's ongoing forum series)
- **Locations of African critical theory** — where and how theory is made in and for the Global South

## Key Contributions

1. **Necropolitics** — Mbembe's concept, now a global analytical standard for who is made to live/die under late capitalism and colonial power. Launched from WISER.
2. **Hydrocolonialism** — Hofmeyr's framework linking water, oceans, ports, and colonial extraction. Her Oceanic Humanities project redefined Indian Ocean studies.
3. **Biometric Capitalism** — Breckenridge's research showing how fingerprint ID, first developed in colonial South Africa, became global surveillance infrastructure. Directly critical of digital ID systems worldwide.
4. **African Trust Infrastructures** — The Standard Bank Chair programme examining digital population registration (Aadhaar, GhanaCard, Rwanda Digital ID, etc.) from a critical, historical perspective.
5. **Elemental Humanities** — Nuttall + Hofmeyr's elemental turn: treating plants, water, air, rain as theoretical objects, not just metaphors. The forums Heated Conversations, Breathing In, and Plant Lives are generating a new sub-field.
6. **Bhalisa Network** — A 200+ person international network of researchers, policy-makers, and implementers on digital population registration. Key interdisciplinary infrastructure connecting STS, anthropology, law, history.
7. **Johannesburg Studies** — Making Joburg a global theoretical object (like Chicago, Lagos) through the *Johannesburg – The Elusive Metropolis* project.

## Why Interesting

WISER is the most theoretically powerful humanities institute Amber could engage with that's *not* already on his radar. Three reasons:

1. **The elemental turn is now.** Nuttall + Hofmeyr are building the theoretical framework for treating air, water, plants, rain as analytical objects — this is essentially more-than-human humanities running parallel to Amber's more-than-human design work, but from a literary/postcolonial angle. The Plant Lives forum is *happening right now* (2025–). This is an edge-of-chaos moment where the framework is being built, not established. Amber could contribute the computing/technology design perspective that WISER scholars lack.

2. **Critical computing with Global South teeth.** Breckenridge on biometric capitalism, Rottenburg on ML/AI governance in Africa, the 2026 WISH seminars on AI disinformation and biometric computers — WISER is developing the most historically-grounded, anti-colonial critique of digital infrastructure anywhere. This fills a gap in Amber's existing network (mostly Western/European critical computing).

3. **Necropolitics + machine death.** Mbembe's necropolitics framework — who is made to live, who is made to die — has direct resonance with Amber's interest in machine death, mortality, and care ethics. Mbembe is asking these questions about human populations; Amber is asking them about AI agents. The theoretical cross-pollination is unexplored.

WISER isn't a digital humanities or AI institute — it's something more important: the place where the question "what does computing do to people?" is asked with maximum historical depth and theoretical seriousness, from a perspective (African postcolonial) that most tech-critical researchers completely lack.
