The studio that named a discipline and trained a generation. **Anthony Dunne and Fiona Raby** — partners in life and work since 1994 — invented speculative design and critical design not as theoretical concepts but as living, breathing practices: objects you can touch, rooms you can walk through, scenarios you can inhabit. The work lives in the permanent collections of MoMA, the V&A, and MAK Vienna, but its real life is in the hundreds of designers, researchers, and thinkers who passed through their programmes at the RCA and Parsons and went out to reshape the world. If you work anywhere near the intersection of technology, design, and critical thought, you are downstream of Dunne & Raby.

The trajectory: **RCA/London (1994–2015) → Parsons/New York (2016–2025) → China Academy of Art/Hangzhou (2025–present)**. The pivot to East Asia is not incidental — it's a deliberate bet that the next chapter of speculative/critical design will be written in Mandarin.

## Mission & Objectives

> "We want to use design as a tool to create not just things but ideas, to speculate about alternative ways that the technological might develop... a catalyst for collectively redefining our relationship with reality." — Dunne & Raby

**Core philosophy:** Design as a form of inquiry, not just production. The studio treats designed objects as hypotheses — made material not to be manufactured, but to provoke, question, and open up debate about how we want to live with emerging technologies. This is the original definition of "critical design": using the language and methods of commercial design against its own assumptions.

**The theoretical spine:**
- Design as critique of the real, not proposal for the ideal
- Technology as cultural material, not just functional tool
- The electromagnetic, invisible, and computational as legitimate aesthetic territory
- Objects as philosophical propositions made tangible
- Multiple possible worlds simultaneously, rather than a single optimistic future
- The impossible as a legitimate design brief

**Evolution across three phases:**

1. **Electronic culture critique (1994–2005):** *Hertzian Tales*, *Design Noir*, Placebo Project. Starting point: what if we treated invisible electromagnetic fields as design material? What does it mean to "design" for the real psychological life of people, not their idealized consumer avatar?

2. **Speculative fiction as design method (2005–2015):** Design Interactions at RCA. *Speculative Everything*. Robots, biotech futures, political fiction (UMK), overpopulation. The studio now deployed design to make possible futures tangible and debatable — a systematic pedagogy for "design for debate."

3. **Impossibility and pluriversal worlds (2015–present):** *Not Here, Not Now*. Many Worlds Working Group at Parsons. Meinong's Jungle. The focus shifts from speculative to *impossible* — objects and scenarios that cannot exist but whose imagining tells us something essential about what we want and what we fear. Simultaneously, a deepening engagement with non-Western frameworks and ontologies (the "world of many worlds" draws on Zapatist thought, quantum physics, and pluriversal anthropology).

## Key Publications & Output

### Books

- **[*Hertzian Tales: Electronic Products, Aesthetic Experience and Critical Design*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262541992/)** (CRD Research / RCA, 1999; MIT Press, 2005) — The founding document of critical design. Dunne's PhD thesis turned book. Named critical design, argued for electronic products as cultural critique, mapped the invisible electromagnetic landscape as aesthetic territory. Korean translation 2002 (Sizirak Publishing, Seoul, trans. Haecheon Park).

- **[*Design Noir: The Secret Life of Electronic Objects*](https://www.bloomsbury.com/uk/design-noir-9781350116375/)** (August/Birkhäuser, 2001; Bloomsbury, 2021 as part of *Designing in Dark Times* series) — Critical design applied to "dark" psychological territory. Objects designed for phobias, anxieties, and the irrational inner life. With Michael Anastassiades. Chinese translation (Jiangsu Phoenix Fine Arts Publishing, 2023).

- **[*Speculative Everything: Design, Fiction, and Social Dreaming*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262019842/)** (MIT Press, 2013; new paperback edition with new foreword, 2024) — **The canonical text of the field.** Maps speculative design's intellectual genealogy (Italian radical design, critical theory, SF, art), defines the practice, argues for design as a tool for social dreaming. Probably the most widely assigned design theory text of the 2010s. Translations: Russian (Strelka Institute, Moscow, 2017); Chinese (Jiangsu Fine Art Publishing House, Nanjing, 2017; new edition 2024); Korean (agbook, Seoul, 2024).

- **[*Not Here, Not Now: Speculative Thought, Impossibility, and the Design Imagination*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262049665/)** (MIT Press, Spring 2025) — Their fourth and most conceptually ambitious book. Moves from speculative futures toward *impossibility* itself as a design material. What does it mean to design when the future seems to have become an impossibility for many people? A conceptual travelogue through edges of science, philosophy, and literature — imagined archives, glossaries, taxonomies, impossible objects. The book is itself a design experiment.

## Key Contributions

**1. Naming and institutionalizing critical design** — D&R coined and systematized "critical design" as a term, theory, and practice. Before *Hertzian Tales* (1999), there was no shared vocabulary for design that used the methods of commercial design to critique rather than affirm dominant values. After it, there was.

**2. Naming and propagating speculative design** — *Speculative Everything* (2013) did for "speculative design" what *Hertzian Tales* did for critical design. It provided the intellectual map, the historical genealogy, and the legitimating framework for a decade of practice. Every design school that runs a "speculative design" unit owes something to this book.

**3. Design Interactions at RCA (2005–2015)** — The most influential design programme of its era. Under Dunne's direction as Professor and Head, with Raby as Reader, DI became the field-generating machine for critical/speculative design globally. 150+ students over ten years. Alumni now lead programmes, studios, and research groups worldwide. When D&R left in 2015, the programme was temporarily suspended — testament to how much of it was them.

**4. The permanent collections** — Works in MoMA (Robots, Huggable Atomic Mushroom, and more), V&A (Weeds, Aliens and Other Stories), MAK Vienna, Fnac Paris, FrAC Ile-de-France. This is legitimacy in the most durable form possible: institutions saying "this matters forever."

**5. United Micro Kingdoms (2012–13)** — The most ambitious single project. A designed fiction of the UK divided into four experimental political super-shires — Digitarians (neoliberal/digital), Bioliberals (social democratic/biotech), Anarcho-evolutionists (anarchist/self-experimentation), Communo-nuclearists (communist/nuclear). Each with its own transport, governance, ecology, body culture. Exhibition at Design Museum London 2013. In Design Museum permanent collection. The project demonstrated that design fiction could operate at the scale of political theory.

**6. Establishing design research methodology** — Their consistent practice of coupling exhibitions with texts, and embedding both in academic discourse, established a model for design-as-research that has become standard in practice-based PhD programmes worldwide.

**7. The East Asian translation strategy** — By publishing translations of their books in Korean (2002, 2024), Chinese (2017, 2023, 2024), and Russian (2017) before establishing institutional presences in those geographies, D&R seeded the field globally in a way that shaped non-English-speaking design education.

## For Amber

- **Why interesting:** Dunne & Raby is Amber's intellectual genealogy. The framework she works in — critical design, speculative practice, design as inquiry — was literally coined and systematized by this studio. Understanding D&R is understanding the origin point of the conceptual tools Amber uses. More pressingly: the China Academy of Art pivot puts them in direct proximity to the Chinese institutions Amber has connections to (she taught at CAA's School of Design and Innovation 2023–2025). This is a live network.
