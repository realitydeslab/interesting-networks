# General Ecology (Serpentine Galleries)

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-27:** Initial deep research. Full profile created from Serpentine website, programme archives, event pages.
- **2024–2025:** Programme concluded as "General Ecology" and was institutionalised as Serpentine Ecologies department (opened 2023). Lucia Pietroiusti departed Serpentine (no longer on team page as of early 2026).
- **July–September 2024:** Infinite Ecologies Marathon (final major event under Lucia Pietroiusti's leadership).
- **November 2022:** Fifth and final edition of The Shape of a Circle in the Mind of a Fish series, in Porto.

Serpentine's landmark 2018–2023 ecological programme, founded by [[Lucia Pietroiusti]], which pioneered a model of art-institutional ecology work: not a silo programme about "green art" but a structural attempt to embed ecological thinking across exhibitions, infrastructure, networks and research. It spawned Back to Earth, the Infinite Ecologies Marathon, and ultimately transformed into the Serpentine Ecologies department. The programme ran the most rigorous interspecies / more-than-human art-science symposia in the UK art world — particularly *The Shape of a Circle in the Mind of a Fish* series (5 editions, 2018–2022).

---

## Mission & Objectives

From the Serpentine site: "General Ecology is the Serpentine's long-term, cross-organisational, multi-disciplinary and cross-media project dedicated to embedding ecological principles and commitments throughout the Galleries' programmes, networks and infrastructure."

This wording is key — not a standalone ecology "strand" but a commitment to **restructuring the entire institution** around ecological principles. Lucia Pietroiusti's approach was explicitly organizational transformation, not just ecological curating.

The programme had three interconnected goals:
1. **Research-led** — original symposia, publications, and investigations into more-than-human thinking, interspecies consciousness, environmental justice
2. **Campaign-led** — supporting artists doing actual climate/environmental work (Back to Earth)
3. **Structural** — making the gallery itself ecologically accountable (infrastructure, travel policy, procurement)

## Key Programmes & Events

### The Shape of a Circle in the Mind of a Fish (2018–2022)
A multi-year interdisciplinary festival and research project, part of General Ecology. Five editions exploring intelligence, sentience, and existence across more-than-human worlds. Curated by Lucia Pietroiusti and Filipa Ramos. Visual identity by Giles Round.

**Named after:** A puffer fish (*Torquigener albomaculatus*) that makes elaborate geometric sand circles on the sea floor — rediscovering that what looks like "artwork" may be mating behaviour, or perhaps both.

**Ed. 1 — Language** (May 2018)
- Venue: ZSL London Zoological Society
- Theme: Animal, human and artificial consciousness; language; interspecies communication
- Key participants: Peter Gabriel, Chris Watson, Ted Chiang, Andrew Adamatzky, Vint Cerf

**Ed. 2 — We Have Never Been One** (December 2018)
- Venue: Ambika P3, University of Westminster, London
- Theme: Interior multitude, swarming organisms, symbiosis, entanglements
- Name references: Donna Haraway's "We Have Never Been Modern" (Latour) reframed for multispecies thinking

**Ed. 3 — PLANTSEX and With Plants** (April/May 2019)
- Venues: Cinéma Lumière (French Institute), EartH Hackney, London
- Theme: Plant sentience, intelligence, vegetal communication; eroticism, mysticism, healing
- Key participants: Michael Marder (philosopher of plants), [[Anna Tsing]]

**Ed. 4 — The Understory of the Understory** (December 2020)
- Venue: Online, at dedicated website **themind.fish** (designed by Giles Round) — moved online due to COVID
- Theme: Soil, ground, land, Earth; teeming entangled life; distributed intelligence of ground systems
- Follow-up: Podcast episode by **Future Ecologies** (futureecologies.net) "composted" the event into a choral, poetic essay (Feb 2021)
- Partners: Future Ecologies podcast (hosted by Mendel Skulski and Adam Huggins)

**Ed. 5 — The Shape of a Circle in the Dream of a Fish** (November 26–27, 2022)
- Venue: Galeria Municipal do Porto, Porto, Portugal (+ online)
- Theme: Dream life across species boundaries; scientific advances in animal/plant consciousness; shamanic/mystical encounters
- Participants: Yussef Agbo-Ola (Olaniyi Studio, architect), Federico Campagna (philosopher), Nicola S. Clayton (psychologist/cognition researcher), Onome Ekeh (artist/writer), Cru Encarnação (artist/performer), Alex Jordan (behavioural ecologist), Sophie Lunn-Rockliffe (religious historian), Nahum Mantra (artist/musician), Hatis Noit (vocal performer), Giles Round (artist), Rain Wu (artist/architect)
- Film screenings: Mariana Caló & Francisco Queimadela, Rosalind Fowler, Derek Jarman (*A Journey to Avebury*, 1971), Dominique Knowles, Ben Rivers, Himali Singh Soin
- Co-presented with Galeria Municipal do Porto

**Previous Fish participants (across all editions) included:**
- Musicians: Peter Gabriel, Chris Watson (sound recordist/field recording)
- Writers: Ted Chiang (sci-fi), Michael Marder (plant philosophy)
- Scientists: Andrew Adamatzky (unconventional computing), Nicola S. Clayton (animal cognition), Alex Jordan (behavioural ecology)

## Theoretical Framework

General Ecology drew on several intellectual traditions:

1. **"General ecology"** as a concept — opposed to "restricted ecology" (studying only biological systems); seeks to understand the full complexity of all systems at once. The term likely draws on Félix Guattari's *Three Ecologies* (environmental, social, mental) and Timothy Morton's "dark ecology."

2. **More-than-human thinking** — porous boundaries between animal/human/vegetal/fungal/mineral/artificial consciousness. Direct influence: Donna Haraway, Anna Tsing, Michael Marder. The Fish series was the institutional operationalisation of this theoretical tendency.

3. **Interspecies communication & epistemological plurality** — treating indigenous knowledge, mysticism, science, and art as equal epistemologies rather than hierarchically ordering them. Fish Ed. 3 brought plant mysticism and plant biology into the same room.

4. **Environmental justice over environmental aesthetics** — Back to Earth was explicitly about justice, not just "green art." Artists were meant to be doing actual campaigns with real-world impact.

5. **Organisational transformation** — the most distinctive thing about General Ecology was its insistence on institutional change (not just producing ecology-themed programming). This is a model for thinking about how institutions can actually change.

## For Amber

**Why this is essential for Amber:**

The General Ecology programme is **the reference model** for institutional ecology work in the UK art world. It pioneered:
- Art-science interspecies research at institutional scale
- Environmental work that isn't superficial "green art" but structural/justice-oriented
- Multi-year durational symposia format (Fish series) that Amber's own interests align with perfectly

**Why now relevant:**
The programme has ended/transformed. Lucia Pietroiusti has left. This creates an *opening* — the networks she built are now dispersed and potentially available. The next generation of ecology-focused curators and researchers is forming.

**Amber's direct research overlaps:**
- More-than-human thinking (Fish series = canonical more-than-human event series)
- Interspecies communication and consciousness (Fish series, direct overlap with Amber's machine behavior work — both ask about non-human agency and language)
- Art-science methodology (Fish brought CS people like Vint Cerf, Andrew Adamatzky into art contexts — exactly the kind of bridge Amber needs)
- Computational creativity (Adamatzky's unconventional computing presented at Fish Ed. 1 alongside artists)

**Engagement paths:**

1. **[[Kostas Stasinopoulos]]** — still at Serpentine as Curator, Live Programmes. He's the institutional memory of General Ecology. If Amber has work relevant to Ecologies, this is the contact. He's been there through all of it.

2. **[[Lucia Pietroiusti]]** — now independent (post-Serpentine). She'll likely be starting something new. Follow her. She's one of the most interesting ecological curators in the UK, and now has freedom. An independent Lucia Pietroiusti is potentially more exciting than a Serpentine-institutionalised one.

3. **[[Filipa Ramos]]** — writer/editor who co-curated Fish. She edits Art-Agenda, writes for e-flux. Potential venue for Amber's writing if it touches ecology/more-than-human territory.

4. **Serpentine Ecologies** — the department continues. With no named head, there may be openings for external collaborators or new curatorial partnerships. Watch for new appointments.

5. **Future Ecologies podcast** (futureecologies.net) — co-produced content with Serpentine. Small, rigorous, good format. Potential podcast/essay venue for Amber's work.

6. **Fish series as model** — If Amber wants to run her own interdisciplinary event series on machine behavior/more-than-human/AI agency, the Fish series is the direct model to study and adapt. Five editions, each tackling a different angle of the same core question, durational, mixing disciplines, with dedicated publication/web presence.

**Who should Amber know?**
- Lucia Pietroiusti (most important)
- Filipa Ramos (writer/editor for ecology × art)
- Kostas Stasinopoulos (Serpentine, still active)
- Federico Campagna (philosopher, appeared in Fish series, writes for Fitzcarraldo Editions)

**What Amber can learn from the model:**
1. **Cross-institutional embedding** — how to make your research matter institutionally, not just academically
2. **Multi-edition symposium design** — running a 5-year research question as a series, each edition probing a different facet
3. **Publication strategy** — pairing each programme with a media output (website, podcast, book)
4. **Disciplinary mixing that works** — Fish succeeded at bringing Vint Cerf and Hatis Noit and Federico Campagna into the same room. How? Topic-led rather than discipline-led.
5. **From research to campaign** — the transition from Fish (pure research) to Back to Earth (artist campaigns) shows how to move from ideas to action without losing intellectual rigour

## Why Interesting

General Ecology is important to the knowledge graph for three reasons:

1. **It's the institutional model for what Amber is trying to do at the research/art intersection.** Not just making ecology-flavored work, but actually restructuring how institutions think about their purpose. The General Ecology approach to "what is a gallery doing in an ecological crisis?" is the question Amber should be asking about "what is a research lab doing in an AI crisis?"

2. **The Fish series is the most rigorous art-science interspecies event in the UK.** It brought together cognitive scientists, internet pioneers, indigenous knowledge holders, plant philosophers, and sound artists to ask the same question from different angles. That's the format. That's the ambition level. Amber's machine behavior work inhabits the same territory.

3. **The network is currently dispersed.** Lucia left. The programme ended. The people who ran it are available. This is the moment to connect with this world before the next thing crystallises.
