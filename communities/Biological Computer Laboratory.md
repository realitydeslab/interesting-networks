# Biological Computer Laboratory (UIUC, 1958–1975)

The most consequential interdisciplinary research laboratory of the mid-20th century that nobody talks about enough. Founded by **[[Heinz von Foerster]]** on January 1, 1958, within the Department of Electrical Engineering at the [University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign (UIUC)](https://illinois.edu/). The institutional home of **second-order cybernetics** — the radical proposition that the observer cannot be excluded from the observed system. Active for 17 years, BCL produced autopoiesis, radical constructivism, conversation theory, bio-inspired computing, and the epistemological foundations for complexity science, ALife, and embodied cognition. Closed 1974 when von Foerster refused to redirect research toward militarily useful outputs. 

**Rating: ⭐⭐⭐** — directly foundational to every field Amber works in (ALife, machine behavior, agent ethology, more-than-human design, open-endedness). The BCL is the intellectual ancestor of ALIFE, the Santa Fe Institute, and the machine behavior program.

## Mission & Objectives

BCL's formal focus was **systems theory and self-organizing systems, bionics, and bio-inspired computing** — analyzing, formalizing, and implementing biological processes using computers. But the actual mission, under von Foerster's intellectual leadership, was far broader: to create a space where biologists, physicists, mathematicians, philosophers, linguists, and engineers could think together about the foundational questions of life, mind, computation, and knowledge.

Von Foerster ran BCL less as a conventional research lab and more as a **curated intellectual environment** — a kind of sustained, interdisciplinary residency where the right people were brought together and allowed to collide productively. There was no single research agenda; there was a shared epistemological commitment to taking circular causation, self-reference, and the observer seriously.

The BCL was inspired by the ideas of **[[Warren McCulloch]]** and the **[[Macy Conferences]]**, extending the cybernetics conversation into the next generation and the next set of questions.

**In the first decade**, BCL was primarily a non-teaching research lab. Students could work there, but they were not formally trained — the focus was on visiting researchers exchanging ideas.

## Key People

### Director
- **[[Heinz von Foerster]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — founder and director (1958–1975). Austrian-American physicist. Developed second-order cybernetics. Ethical Imperative: *"Act always so as to increase the number of choices."* Editor of the Macy Cybernetics Transactions (Vols. 6–10). Retired rather than redirect BCL toward military applications.

### Regular Professors
- **[[W. Ross Ashby]]** ⭐⭐ (since 1961) — British psychiatrist-cybernetician. *Design for a Brain* (1952), *Introduction to Cybernetics* (1956). Developed the concept of **homeostasis** and **requisite variety** — a system must have at least as much variety as the disturbances it regulates. Original member of the **Ratio Club** (UK's Macy equivalent). His homeostasis thinking directly prefigures open-endedness and adaptive systems research.
- **[[Gotthard Günther]]** ⭐⭐ (since 1967) — German-American philosopher. Developed **polycontexturality** and non-Aristotelian logic — a formal framework for thinking about multiple observer positions simultaneously. Argued that formal logic built on two values (true/false) couldn't adequately model reflexive, self-referential systems. His work is underread and ahead of its time.

### Key Visiting Researchers (partial list, pre-1965)
- **[[Gordon Pask]]** ⭐⭐ (England) — developed **Conversation Theory** and **cybernetics of education** at BCL. His theory: learning is a conversation between participant and environment — a feedback loop, not a transmission. Later collaborated with [[Roy Ascott]] on cybernetic aesthetics; involved with [[Cedric Price]] and [[Joan Littlewood]] on the Fun Palace. Key collaborator with von Foerster on *"A Predictive Model for Self-Organizing Systems"* (1961).
- **[[Humberto Maturana]]** ⭐⭐ (Chile) — developed the concept of **autopoiesis** partly at BCL. Chilean biologist. With Varela, showed that living systems are defined not by their components but by the circular organization that continuously produces those components. His stricter reading of autopoiesis (molecular biology only, not social) distinguished him from Varela's more expansive application.
- **[[Francisco Varela]]** ⭐⭐⭐ (Chile) — co-developed autopoiesis at BCL (with Maturana). Later founded **enactivism** and **neurophenomenology**. Co-founded the [[Mind and Life Institute]] with the Dalai Lama. His *The Embodied Mind* (1991, with Thompson & Rosch) is a key text for more-than-human design and embodied interaction. BCL connection: PhD student of Maturana's; visited BCL during the autopoiesis development.
- **[[Ernst von Glasersfeld]]** ⭐⭐ (Austria) — developed **radical constructivism** — the position that reality is *constructed* by the observer, not discovered. BCL was the intellectual birthplace of this epistemology. His work influenced educational theory, psychotherapy, and constructivist pedagogy globally.
- **[[Stafford Beer]]** ⭐⭐ (England) — developed **management cybernetics** and the **Viable System Model** (VSM). Applied cybernetics to organizations and governance. Most famous for **Project Cybersyn** (1971–1973) in Allende's Chile — an attempted cybernetic economic management system. BCL visitor; remained in contact with von Foerster.
- **[[Lars Löfgren]]** (Sweden) — worked on formal systems and self-reference. Remained in constant contact with BCL after his visiting professorship.
- **[[John C. Lilly]]** (USA) — dolphin cognition and consciousness research. Explored mind expansion; later controversial figure. BCL connection represents the range of exploratory thinking von Foerster welcomed.
- **W. William Ainsworth** (England) — speech recognition research
- **Alex Andrew** (England) — cybernetics and robot control
- **Dan Cohen** (Israel) — formal systems

### Key Extended Network (influenced by BCL, not necessarily in-residence)
- **[[Ranulph Glanville]]** ⭐⭐ — British cybernetician and design theorist. Studied under Gordon Pask at Brunel. Extended second-order cybernetics into design theory: "cybernetics is the theory of design and design is the action of cybernetics." President of the [American Society for Cybernetics](http://www.asc-cybernetics.org/) (2009–2014). Directly relevant to Amber — cybernetics as design practice.
- **[[Warren McCulloch]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — the intellectual father-figure; chair of the Macy Conferences; invited von Foerster to the US; his neural net thinking preceded BCL and inspired it.
- **[[Gregory Bateson]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — anthropologist; Macy Conferences participant; his ecology-of-mind thinking deeply aligned with BCL's systemic epistemology.
- **[[Margaret Mead]]** ⭐⭐ — anthropologist; Macy participant; her 1967 ASC keynote address directly inspired second-order cybernetics.

- **[*An Unfinished Revolution?: Heinz von Foerster and the Biological Computer Laboratory / BCL 1958–1976*](https://www.edition-echoraum.at/)** (Albert Muller, Karl Muller eds., 2007, Edition Echoraum) — the definitive historical account of BCL

- **Maturana & Varela, "Autopoiesis: The Organization of Living Systems"** (1974, *BioSystems*, with Ricardo Uribe) — [DOI: 10.1016/0303-2647(74)90031-8](https://doi.org/10.1016/0303-2647(74)90031-8) — the original autopoiesis paper

## Key Contributions

### What BCL Gave the World

**1. Second-Order Cybernetics**
BCL was where the epistemological shift happened: from studying systems from outside (first-order cybernetics) to acknowledging that the observer is always *inside* the system. This move — observer-inclusion — is now foundational to complexity science, constructivism, and critical theory. Niklas Luhmann's social systems theory draws on it; complexity economics draws on it; posthumanism draws on it.

**2. Autopoiesis**
The theory that living systems are self-producing, self-maintaining networks — that life is defined by *organization*, not by *substance*. Maturana and Varela developed autopoiesis at and around BCL. It became foundational for:
- Artificial Life (Chris Langton's ALife program explicitly engages autopoiesis)
- Embodied Cognition (*The Embodied Mind*, Varela/Thompson/Rosch)
- Complex Systems theory
- Second-generation constructivism

**3. Radical Constructivism**
Von Glasersfeld's development: knowledge is not representation of an independent reality but an instrument for coping with experience. This epistemology, seeded at BCL, reshaped educational theory, systemic psychotherapy, and contributed to the philosophy of science critique of objectivism.

**4. Conversation Theory (Gordon Pask)**
Learning as conversation — between student and environment, between system and context. This influenced educational technology, human-computer interaction, and constructivist pedagogy. Pask's conversation theory is an underexplored ancestor of contemporary human-AI interaction design.

**5. Bio-Inspired Computing / Bionics**
BCL was one of the earliest systematic research programs on computing inspired by biological systems. The **Numa-Rete**, claimed to be the first parallel computer, was built at BCL. This bio-computing lineage leads to neural networks, evolutionary algorithms, and ALife computing paradigms.

**6. The BCL Model of Interdisciplinary Research**
Von Foerster's curation model — attracting the right minds, creating an environment for encounter, refusing instrumentalization — is itself a contribution. BCL demonstrated that genuinely transdisciplinary research requires institutional autonomy and long time horizons. Its closure, when military funding demanded militarily useful results, is a cautionary lesson about how research environments get destroyed.

**7. The Ethical Imperative**
Von Foerster's aphorism — *"Act always so as to increase the number of choices"* — emerged from BCL's epistemological tradition. This is a constructivist ethics: since reality is constructed through observation, the ethical act is to preserve alternative constructions. This framing is directly applicable to AI systems design.

## Events & Programmes

### Historical (Closed)
BCL ran internal seminars and hosted extended visiting scholar programs. The lab produced a series of **BCL Technical Reports** circulating research in progress — a Macy-Transactions-equivalent for the next generation.

**Key historical moment:** The 1974 publication of *Cybernetics of Cybernetics* as a BCL course book — the moment second-order cybernetics was consolidated as a teachable program. The course analyzed cybernetic texts using cybernetic principles — a recursive, self-referential pedagogy in action.

### Legacy Events (Active)
The BCL legacy lives through:
- **[ALIFE Conference](https://alife.org/)** — annual artificial life conference; direct descendant of BCL's computational biology thinking
- **[American Society for Cybernetics (ASC)](http://www.asc-cybernetics.org/)** — annual conferences; keeps second-order cybernetics alive; publishes *Cybernetics & Human Knowing*
- **[International Journal of Systems and Cybernetics](https://www.kybernetes.co.uk/)** — *Kybernetes* — the journal most connected to the BCL tradition
- **[IFSR (International Federation for Systems Research)](https://www.ifsr.org/)** — hosts biennial conversations following the Macy format

## For Amber

### 🔗 Why BCL Matters to Amber's Research

**Agent Ethology / Machine Behavior:**
BCL's central epistemological move — *observer-inclusion* — is precisely what machine behavior research must eventually confront. Rahwan's "Machine Behavior" (2019, *Nature*) program studies AI systems observationally, but BCL's second-order cybernetics adds the crucial next step: the researcher studying machine behavior is *entangled* with the machines being studied. Second-order cybernetics gives Amber philosophical vocabulary for the reflexive dimension of agent ethology. BCL → second-order cybernetics → observer-inclusion → the researcher IS part of the ecological niche being studied.

**ALife / Open-Endedness:**
Autopoiesis (Maturana/Varela, developed at BCL) is foundational to ALife. The concept that "life is organization, not substance" enables the ALife program: if life is pattern, it can emerge in non-biological substrates. This is the conceptual foundation of Chris Langton's ALIFE conference (1987), which Amber participates in directly. Joel Lehman's open-endedness research is downstream of this BCL thinking.

**More-than-Human Design:**
Von Foerster's Ethical Imperative — *increase the number of choices* — is a design principle that extends naturally to more-than-human design. If "choices" includes the choices of non-human agents (animals, machines, environments), you arrive at more-than-human design. BCL's systemic, relational thinking prefigures the decentering-of-the-human that more-than-human design performs.

**Conversation Theory (Pask) → Human-AI Interaction:**
Gordon Pask's conversation theory frames interaction as a cybernetic conversation — a feedback loop between system and participant. This is an underread theoretical foundation for thinking about human-AI interaction design. The "conversation" framing is everywhere (LLMs, chatbots), but Pask's rigorous cybernetic version — where conversation is about mutual construction of knowledge, not just information exchange — is almost never cited. Amber could position her work in this tradition.

**Embodied Interaction:**
Varela/Thompson/Rosch's *The Embodied Mind* (1991) — direct BCL lineage — is foundational for embodied interaction and somaesthetic design (Kia Höök explicitly references Varela). BCL → autopoiesis → enactivism → embodied mind → somaesthetics. Amber's embodied interaction work sits in this lineage.

### 💡 Career Inspiration: The BCL Model
Von Foerster ran BCL as **intellectual hospitality** — he attracted interesting people, created conditions for encounter, and let ideas emerge from collision. This is a model for how Amber might build her Oxford research group: not organized around a single agenda but around a shared epistemological commitment and a curated set of interlocutors.

Key lessons from BCL as institution:
- **Curation over control** — the director's job is to attract the right people, not dictate the agenda
- **Visitor programs** as research infrastructure — structured exchange beats static research teams
- **Resistance to instrumentalization** — BCL closed rather than became a military R&D shop; knowing when to end a project is a rare strategic skill
- **Interdisciplinarity requires long time horizons** — the autopoiesis insight took years of informal conversation to crystallize

## Why Interesting

BCL is the laboratory where second-order cybernetics — one of the 20th century's most radical epistemological proposals — was forged. The observer-inclusion move (you cannot step outside the system you study) is philosophically foundational to:
- Machine behavior as a science of situated observation
- Agent ethology (the researcher is entangled with the agents)
- More-than-human design (the designer is inside the designed system)
- Open-endedness research (what counts as novelty depends on who/what is watching)
- Posthumanism and critical AI studies

BCL produced autopoiesis, radical constructivism, and conversation theory. These are not historical curiosities — they're active frameworks being used in ALife, embodied cognition, and design research today.

The BCL is also relevant as a *model for how to do research*: curated interdisciplinarity, long time horizons, resistance to instrumentalization, visitor programs as intellectual infrastructure. This is the antithesis of the grant-driven, output-measured contemporary university research lab — and it was more productive precisely because of that.
