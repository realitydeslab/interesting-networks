# Xerox PARC (Palo Alto Research Center)

The most consequential private research laboratory of the late 20th century, founded by Xerox in 1970 in Palo Alto, California. Invented the graphical user interface, Ethernet, laser printing, WYSIWYG text editing, object-oriented programming, and the mouse — essentially the entire infrastructure of personal computing. Became independent of Xerox in 2002; acquired by SRI International in 2022.

## Why Interesting
PARC is the historical node where the modern computing paradigm was invented — and where [[Lucy Suchman]]'s foundational critique of that paradigm was simultaneously developed. Suchman spent 22 years there (1979–2001) as the first anthropologist embedded in a computing lab. Her video ethnographies of users failing to operate Xerox copiers were done with PARC resources, using PARC colleagues (including Allen Newell, founding father of AI) as subjects. The paradox: the institution that most needed to believe in AI planning also housed the person who showed it was wrong.

Other key PARC figures relevant to this knowledge graph:
- **[[John Seely Brown]]** — PARC Director 1990–2000; championed social/anthropological research as central to innovation
- **[[Lucy Suchman]]** — Principal Scientist; Work Practice and Technology research group
- **Julian Orr** — anthropologist; *Talking About Machines* (workplace ethnography of service technicians)
- **Austin Henderson** — computer scientist; co-made the original copier videos with Suchman
