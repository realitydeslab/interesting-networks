# Cambridge Cognitive Architecture (CCA)

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-13:** Initial deep research. Created full community note.

A small, focused research group at the University of Cambridge Department of Architecture, led by Assistant Professor Dr. Michal Gath-Morad. CCA fuses architectural design and cognitive science to decode how built environments shape human cognition, behaviour, and social dynamics. The group uses VR experiments, IoT sensors, agent-based modelling, and psychophysiological measures to produce evidence-based design tools. Methodologically rigorous and deeply interdisciplinary — sits at the intersection of architecture, cognitive science, neuroscience, and computer science.

## Mission & Objectives

CCA's mission is to decode how architectural design of physical environments affects human cognition, behaviour, and social dynamics. Three core research questions:

1. **Impact of Design on Spatial Cognition** — How does architectural/urban design impact inhabitants' cognition, behaviour, and social dynamics?
2. **Developing Evidence-Based Design Tools** — How can empirical findings about cognition and behaviour be integrated into architectural design practice?
3. **Impact of Evidence on Design Cognition** — What happens when architects employ evidence-based knowledge and tools? How does it affect design cognition, communication, and outcomes?

The group aspires to create humane built environments that prioritise legibility, inclusivity, and well-being.

### Tools & Platforms
- **Experiments As Code (ExaC)** — Open-source platform for reproducible VR behavioural experiments
- **DesignMind Toolkit** — Four modules: evidence-based design flashcards, topological/geometric analysis, agent-based simulation, cognitive walkthrough in VR
- **cogARCH** — Agent-based simulation of wayfinding by architecture in multilevel buildings
- **Architectural Cognition Cards** — Card-based method for introducing spatial cognition research into design process

## Key Contributions

1. **Reproducible VR experimentation for architecture** — ExaC framework published in Nature Scientific Reports makes VR-based behavioural experiments auditable and scalable
2. **Spatial cognition in complex buildings** — Rigorous empirical work on how visibility, spatial configuration, and signage affect wayfinding and navigation
3. **Evidence-based design integration** — Bridging the gap between cognitive science findings and architectural practice through tools like DesignMind
4. **Healthcare architecture optimisation** — EVIDENT project studying how spatial configuration of emergency departments affects face-to-face interactions and care outcomes
5. **NeuroCīvitās** — Cross-disciplinary initiative linking mental health and built environment research at Cambridge

## For Amber

### Why Interesting
CCA sits at a genuinely interesting intersection: **architecture × cognitive science × VR/spatial computing × embodied cognition**. Several threads connect to Amber's work:

1. **Spatial computing / VR overlap:** CCA's core method is VR-based behavioural experiments in architectural spaces. Their ExaC platform creates reproducible VR experiments. Amber's HoloKit does AR/MR spatial computing. There's a methodological bridge: could CCA's evidence-based cognitive insights inform MR/AR spatial design? Could HoloKit be used for architectural cognitive walkthroughs in mixed reality instead of pure VR?

2. **Embodied cognition in built environments:** CCA studies how physical space shapes cognition — this is embodied cognition applied to architecture. Simon Ladouce's mobile EEG/brain-body imaging work measures neural responses during real-world movement through spaces. This connects to Amber's interest in somaesthetic design and embodied experience, though CCA approaches it from a cognitive science rather than phenomenological angle.

3. **Cambridge proximity:** CCA is in Cambridge's Architecture Department. Amber is an ERA Fellow in Cambridge. Physical proximity for talks, seminars, informal meetings is high.

4. **More-than-human design (tangential):** CCA's framing is human-centred rather than more-than-human, but their work on how built environments shape behaviour has implications for thinking about environments that support human-AI-environment ecologies.

5. **BeMoBIL connection:** Gath-Morad's upcoming Humboldt Fellowship at TU Berlin's Mobile Brain/Body Imaging Lab (2026-2028) means she'll be working on brain imaging during bodily movement in real environments — directly relevant to embodied cognition and potentially to understanding human experience in AR/MR.
