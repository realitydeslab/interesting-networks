# Heated Conversations Forum

Online research forum convened by [[Sarah Nuttall]] and [[Isabel Hofmeyr]] at [[WISER]] (Wits Institute for Social and Economic Research), University of the Witwatersrand, Johannesburg. Ran for 6 months in 2023. **First** in a trilogy of elemental online forums generating new work from the Global South — followed by **Breathing In: Air and Atmospheres** (2024) and **Plant Lives** (2025–). Currently reaching publication as a book/special issue.

## Mission & Objectives

The forum operated on a deliberate **double meaning** of "heated":

1. **A literally heating planet** — climate crisis, thermal politics, the materiality of heat in the Global South, where temperature increase is not an abstraction but a daily survival condition in cities like Johannesburg, Dakar, Lagos, and Delhi
2. **Overheated pedagogies** — the crisis conditions of knowledge production in the Global South; universities under austerity, student protests, decolonization demands, and the systemic defunding of African academic life — a kind of epistemic fever

This dual framing is the methodological signature of the WISER elemental humanities approach: **elements are never just physical; they are always simultaneously political and epistemic**. Heat is not a backdrop to the climate crisis — it *is* a political condition. Overheated pedagogy is not a metaphor — it *is* an experience of material thermal pressure in overcrowded lecture halls, bodies stressed by rising temperatures, minds stressed by defunded institutions.

The forum was widely subscribed, open-access, and online — democratizing access to cutting-edge Global South scholarship.

## Intellectual Framework

### Heat as Elemental Object

The Heated Conversations Forum established the prototype for WISER's elemental humanities method — treating heat not as a physical constant but as:

- **A political condition** — who sweats, who is air-conditioned; whose bodies bear the thermal cost of colonial extraction
- **An epistemic state** — "overheated" thinking, emergency modes of knowing, crisis pedagogy
- **A material agency** — heat as infrastructure; urban heat islands as legacies of apartheid urban planning (townships built without trees); industrial heat as environmental racism
- **A temporal marker** — the Anthropocene as a thermal event; global warming as accumulated colonial extraction finally becoming felt

This conceptual move — treating elements as multi-register analytical objects — was then applied to air (Breathing In, 2024) and plants (Plant Lives, 2025), establishing a cumulative elemental curriculum.

## Key People

**[[Sarah Nuttall]]** — Co-convener. Professor of Literary and Cultural Studies at WISER, Director 2013–2023. Fifteen books. The architect of the elemental humanities turn at WISER. Her forthcoming book *On Pluviality: Rain Worlds and Elemental Reading in Southern African Literature* extends the elemental method to rain as political-temporal-epistemic object. ⭐⭐⭐

**[[Isabel Hofmeyr]]** — Co-convener. Professor Emeritus at Wits + Distinguished Scholar in Residence at NYU. Pioneer of hydrocolonialism scholarship. Author of *Dockside Reading: Hydrocolonialism and the Custom House* (Duke UP, 2022) and co-editor of *Reading for Water: Materiality and Method* (Routledge). Heat connects to her oceanic/hydraulic framework — water and heat are inseparable in climate science and colonial history. ⭐⭐⭐

**[[Charne Lavery]]** — Associate Professor at University of Pretoria; Co-director of the Oceanic Humanities for the Global South project at WISER. Co-editor of *Reading for Water* with Nuttall and Hofmeyr. The methodological bridge between the oceanic humanities and the elemental humanities; her framework on water and method directly informs how heat is treated in this forum. ⭐⭐

**[[Achille Mbembe]]** — Not named co-convener but WISER's intellectual anchor and Nuttall's spouse. His *necropolitics* framework — who is made to die, by what systems — provides the political grammar for thermal politics: who dies of heat, whose body is thermally expendable. ⭐⭐⭐

## Topics Explored

Based on the forum's stated mission and the broader WISER intellectual context:

- **Climate as colonial inheritance** — the Global South bears the thermal cost of Northern industrial extraction; temperature rise is geographically unjust
- **Urban heat islands** — apartheid-era township planning (no trees, no shade, heat-absorbing materials) as continued thermal violence; Johannesburg's differentiated thermal geography
- **Overheated pedagogies** — what does it mean to produce knowledge in crisis? Decolonization protests (Fees Must Fall, 2015–2016 at Wits) as pedagogical heat; the emotional temperature of learning under austerity
- **Heat as racialized condition** — farmworkers, outdoor laborers, informal settlement dwellers; which bodies bear thermal risk
- **Thermal justice** — access to air conditioning, cool spaces, climate infrastructure as a political question
- **Decolonization and temperature** — colonial architecture built for cool European bodies; the thermal politics of curricula ("cool rationality" vs. "heated emotion")
- **Climate crisis and knowledge production** — how do universities in the Global South respond to a crisis they didn't create?
- **Africa's thermal futures** — projections for West Africa (potentially uninhabitable by 2100 without intervention); southern African drought and temperature increases
- **Heat as element and metaphor** — fire as elemental force in African cosmologies; heat as spiritual register (fever, purification, transformation)

## Format & Method

**6-month online forum (2023).** Widely subscribed internationally. Open access. Speakers and participants primarily from the Global South (Africa, South Asia, Latin America, Caribbean). Sessions likely bi-weekly with pre-circulated papers — following WISER's Print Seminar model where papers are read before the session and discussion is primary.

Key methodological innovation: the forum **commissions new work** rather than showcasing existing scholarship. Participants write new papers for the forum — generating original contributions specifically from Global South perspectives. This is WISER's distinctive curatorial move: not a conference, not a reading group, but a commissioned-work-generating platform that then reaches publication.

## For Amber

### Why This Matters

**Three direct connections to Amber's research:**

1. **Thermal computing and data center heat.** The Heated Conversations framework — treating heat as simultaneously physical, political, and epistemic — maps directly onto Amber's interest in computing infrastructure. Data centers are *thermal actors*: they expel massive heat into local environments, consume enormous water for cooling, and concentrate their thermal impacts in specific geographies (often near cheap energy and labor). The "overheated" frame could productively describe AI training infrastructure itself — GPUs running hot, cooling systems straining, the literal and figurative heat of accelerated AI development.

2. **Overheated pedagogies → overheated AI production.** The concept of "overheated pedagogy" (knowledge production under crisis conditions) maps onto AI research culture: the frantic pace of publication, the devaluation of careful empirical work, the emotional temperature of an accelerating field. Amber's work on machine behavior and machine death inhabits this overheated zone — critical computation operating at crisis pace.

3. **Thermal justice and the Global South.** The forum's emphasis on who *bears* thermal risk — whose bodies are expendable to climate heat, whose institutions operate at insufficient temperature — resonates with Amber's interest in AI ethics from Global South perspectives (Abeba Birhane, Alex Hanna). Extending "thermal justice" to computing: whose GPUs are cooled while whose bodies overheat?

### Specific Conceptual Connections
- **Heat as infrastructure** — server farms as thermal actors in urban environments; the climate cost of AI
- **Overheated systems** — framing AI development as an overheated pedagogical/epistemic condition
- **Thermal bodies** — non-human machines that generate heat as a byproduct; heat as computational waste
- **Decolonial computing** — connecting Global South thermal injustice to the geography of AI labor and data extraction
- **Elemental design** — more-than-human design that takes heat as a material agency, not just an environmental parameter

## Why Interesting

The Heated Conversations Forum inaugurated one of the most methodologically significant projects in contemporary humanities: the systematic treatment of *elements* (heat, air, plants) as full analytical objects with political histories, material agencies, and epistemological weight — from the specific vantage of the Global South. This is elemental humanities before the term was fully established, forged by two of Africa's most important scholars at Africa's most important humanities institute.

For a researcher like Amber working at the intersection of more-than-human design and computing infrastructure, the thermal frame is particularly generative: data centers *are* thermal actors; AI training is a thermal event; the cooling of computation is a water and heat justice issue. The WISER framework gives Amber theoretical vocabulary from the African humanities to bring to computing systems that haven't been analyzed this way.

The forum also models a distinctive scholarly format — commissioning new work from the Global South for online audiences — that itself is a response to the "overheated" publication economy: slower, more deliberate, more communal than conference papers, more open-access than journal articles.
