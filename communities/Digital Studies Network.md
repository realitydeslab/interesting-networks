# Digital Studies Network

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-28:** Full deep research. Website (digital-studies.org) currently down (database error). Network was active 2012–~2020, now appears dormant following Bernard Stiegler's death (August 2020). Intellectual legacy continues through Collectif Internation, Association Épokhè, and Yuk Hui's Research Network for Philosophy and Technology.
- **2026-02-26:** Created stub during [[IRI (Institut de recherche et d'innovation)]] deep research.

International research network launched in 2012 by [[IRI (Institut de recherche et d'innovation)]] at the **Centre Pompidou** (Paris) to create a global scholarly platform for "digital studies" — the study of digital technologies not merely as technical artifacts but as epistemological, cultural, and political transformations of knowledge itself. Rooted in [[Bernard Stiegler]]'s pharmacological framework. Brought together scholars from many countries through seminars, workshops, and a contributory website. **Currently dormant** following Stiegler's death in August 2020, though its intellectual framework remains highly influential.

## Mission & Objectives

From the network's own homepage (archived):

> *"Today every academic discipline is affected by digital technologies, but we do not possess sufficient theoretical models to study how they influence knowledge, on an epistemological level (in the sense of Gaston Bachelard). We believe that this issue should be analyzed across all academic disciplines, each of which should maintain their differences whilst also recognizing common appointments, and hence the consequences of this at an epistemic level (in the sense of Michel Foucault). This idea is the basis of what we call 'digital studies'."*

**Core thesis:** Digital technologies produce **epistemological ruptures** (Bachelard) that transform not only what is known but *how* it is possible to know — the very structures of knowledge (*épistémè*, Foucault). Each discipline must undergo its own reckoning with this rupture while recognizing shared consequences.

**Operating mode:** Informal scholarly network. Members participate through seminars, workshops, and publishing to the contributory website (digital-studies.org). All members could publish text, video, audio. Contact was through IRI at Centre Pompidou.

**Key distinctions from mainstream digital humanities:**
- Not instrumentalist — not asking "how can digital tools serve humanities?" but "how do digital technologies transform the very conditions of humanistic knowledge?"
- Not neutral — rooted in pharmacological critique: every digital technology is simultaneously poison and remedy, requiring active care (*soin*)
- Not discipline-specific — explicitly cross-disciplinary, each field maintains its specificity while engaging shared digital transformations

## Founding Story

**Context:** IRI (Institut de recherche et d'innovation) had been running its annual [Entretiens du Nouveau Monde Industriel (ENMI)](https://iri-ressources.org/collections/collection-2.html) since 2007 — annual two-day conferences at Centre Pompidou co-organized with Cap Digital and Les Ateliers (ENSCI) on digital technology and society. The 2012 ENMI was the launching platform for the Digital Studies Network.

**Launch:** The Digital Studies Network was launched by [[IRI (Institut de recherche et d'innovation)]] during the **2012 Talks on the New Industrial World** (Entretiens du Nouveau Monde Industriel). This was the capstone event of IRI's 6 years of building institutional partners and theoretical frameworks.

**Philosophical anchor:** [[Bernard Stiegler]]'s organological theory. For Stiegler, technics (*techne*) is constitutive of human temporality and consciousness — not a mere tool but the very condition of human knowledge production. Digital technologies represent a new epistemic epoch requiring a new organology: mapping how human organs, technical organs, and social organizations co-individuate in the digital age.

**Second anchor:** [Gaston Bachelard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaston_Bachelard)'s epistemology — the idea that scientific knowledge advances through "ruptures" (*coupures épistémologiques*), not gradual accumulation. Digital technologies constitute such a rupture requiring explicit theoretical treatment.

**Third anchor:** [Michel Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Foucault)'s concept of *épistémè* — the underlying structure of knowledge that determines what can be said, thought, and known in a given historical period. Digital technologies are transforming the *épistémè* itself.

## Key People

- **[[Bernard Stiegler]]** (1952–2020) — Founding philosopher and director of IRI. The intellectual and institutional architect of the entire network. His pharmacological framework and general organology provide the theoretical substrate for "digital studies" as a discipline. Died 5 August 2020.

- **[Alexandre Monnin](https://fr.linkedin.com/in/alexandremonnin)** — Ran Digital Studies / Metadata Studies seminars at IRI in 2012–13. Philosopher of the Web, focused on the ontology of URLs, hyperlinks, and web resources as philosophical objects. Co-developer of the concept "RedWeb" and now working on "Closing Worlds Design" (design for reducing technical systems responsibly in the Anthropocene context). Currently at **ESDES Lyon School of Management**. Key intersection: philosophy of the Web, protocol studies, and responsible AI.

- **[[Yuk Hui]]** (b. Hong Kong) — PhD student under Stiegler at Goldsmiths (one of IRI's institutional partners). His dissertation became *On the Existence of Digital Objects* (2016), prefaced by Stiegler — the most significant book to emerge from the IRI/Digital Studies intellectual orbit. Developed **cosmotechnics** and **technodiversity** concepts. Now at Erasmus University Rotterdam and City University of Hong Kong. Visiting professor at [[China Academy of Art]] (where Amber has taught). Runs the [Research Network for Philosophy and Technology](https://www.researchnetworkphilosophyandtechnology.com/) since 2014 — the most active intellectual successor to the Digital Studies Network.

- **Caroline Stiegler** — Bernard Stiegler's wife, originally his lawyer. Contact person at IRI for the Digital Studies Network. Co-founded Ars Industrialis with Bernard in 2005. Key organizer of pharmakon.fr.

- **Ariane Mayer** — IRI contact person (2012-2016 period) for the Digital Studies Network.

- **[[Barbara Stiegler]]** — Bernard's daughter, philosopher and professor at Université Bordeaux-Montaigne. Continues Stieglerian critical engagement with digital capitalism and biological metaphors in politics. Author of *"Il faut s'adapter"* (2019).

- **[Dan Ross](http://www.daross.org/)** — Australian philosopher, principal English translator of Stiegler's work, co-director of documentary *The Ister* (2004). Key link between Stiegler's French-language work and anglophone audiences. Translated Stiegler's Hangzhou lectures (spring 2018).

## Key Publications & Output

**The network produced primarily:**
1. Seminar and workshop recordings (hosted on [iri-ressources.org](https://iri-ressources.org))
2. Contributory web content on digital-studies.org
3. Videos of ENMI conferences (Talks on the New Industrial World)

**Key books emerging from the network's intellectual orbit:**

- [*On the Existence of Digital Objects*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/on-the-existence-of-digital-objects) — [[Yuk Hui]], pref. Bernard Stiegler, University of Minnesota Press, 2016. The most rigorous philosophical analysis of digital objects — URLs, files, data — as a new ontological category. Bridges Simondon's individuation philosophy with Stiegler's technics and web architecture. **Essential for protocol studies and digital ontology.**

- [*Technics and Time* (3 vols.)](https://www.sup.org/books/title/?id=2555) — [[Bernard Stiegler]], Stanford University Press, 1998–2011. Foundation of the entire network's theoretical apparatus. Argues technics is constitutive of human temporality; every technical epoch produces a new relationship to memory, attention, and knowledge.

- [*For a New Critique of Political Economy*](https://www.polity.co.uk/book.asp?ref=9780745647159) — Bernard Stiegler, Polity, 2010. Key transitional text applying pharmacological framework to the digital economy. Introduces concept of "general proletarianization" (loss of knowledge and savoir-vivre).

- [*States of Shock: Stupidity and Knowledge in the 21st Century*](https://www.polity.co.uk/book.asp?ref=9780745671345) — Bernard Stiegler, Polity, 2015. Directly addresses digital epistemology crisis.

- Alexandre Monnin, Diego Landivar, Emmanuel Bonnet, *Héritage et Fermeture: une écologie du démantèlement* (2021) — extends pharmacological thinking into "responsible closure" of digital systems, degrowth design.

**Key seminars:**
- **Pharmakon.fr seminars (2010–2020)** — Stiegler's annual philosophy school at Épineuil-le-Fleuriel, parallel running at Centre Pompidou. Series: Organology (2010-2016), Exorganology I-III (2017-2020). All archived at iri-ressources.org.
- **IRI Digital Studies / Metadata Studies seminars (2012–13)** — run by Alexandre Monnin.
- **ENMI (Entretiens du Nouveau Monde Industriel) 2007–2012** — annual flagship event.

## Key Contributions

1. **"Digital Studies" as a discipline** — One of the first and most rigorous attempts to frame digital technology studies as a philosophically grounded discipline, not just tool-use or cultural observation.

2. **Metadata as philosophical problem** — The network (particularly through Monnin's work) elevated metadata from technical artifact to epistemological object: the organizational schemas of data determine what knowledge is possible.

3. **Pharmacological critique of digital capitalism** — The framework of poison-and-remedy, applied to platforms, recommendation algorithms, and social media: these systems both enable and destroy attention, knowledge, and psychic individuation.

4. **Digital object ontology** — Through Yuk Hui's work, established that digital objects (URLs, files, databases) are genuinely new ontological categories requiring philosophical treatment, not mere digitizations of existing things.

5. **Contributing model of web research** — The network itself embodied a theory about how collaborative digital knowledge production should work — contributory, not extractive.

## For Amber

**Why interesting:**
Amber works at the intersection of digital technology, critical philosophy, and protocol studies — exactly the terrain Digital Studies Network was trying to theorize. The pharmacological framework (every technology simultaneously remedy and poison) is one of the most sophisticated tools available for analyzing Amber's core concerns: agentic AI systems, trustworthy web protocols, machine behavior, and the ethics of autonomous systems.

**Key intellectual resonances:**
1. **Protocol studies** — Monnin's work on the ontology of URLs and web resources directly concerns what "the web" *is* philosophically. Essential context for Amber's trustworthy agentic web research.
2. **Pharmacological frame for AI governance** — Every AI system is simultaneously remedy (new capabilities, coordination, knowledge) and poison (proletarianization, attention capture, loss of savoir-faire). This is exactly the frame needed for agentic AI ethics without naive techno-optimism or naive technophobia.
3. **General organology for machine behavior** — Stiegler's co-individuation framework (human organs ↔ technical organs ↔ social organizations) is philosophically adjacent to Amber's machine behavior research. How do humans and AI systems co-individuate? What new forms of knowledge and attention do agentic systems enable and destroy?
4. **Cosmotechnics** — Yuk Hui's extension of this framework into non-Western tech philosophy connects to Amber's interest in Chinese/Eastern epistemologies of technology (Amber taught at China Academy of Art where Hui is visiting professor).
5. **Metadata studies** — The claim that metadata schemas determine epistemic possibility is powerful for thinking about knowledge graphs, AI training data curation, and protocol design.

**Engagement paths:**
- **Primary:** Read [[Yuk Hui]]'s *On the Existence of Digital Objects* — the most direct gateway into this intellectual tradition relevant to Amber's work.
- **Secondary:** Engage with Yuk Hui's [Research Network for Philosophy and Technology](https://www.researchnetworkphilosophyandtechnology.com/) — the living successor.
- **Tertiary:** Track Alexandre Monnin's "Closing Worlds Design" work — fascinating intersection of pharmacology + degrowth + digital infrastructure responsibility.
- **Archive:** The pharmakon.fr seminars (2010–2020, ~10 years) on iri-ressources.org are a substantial intellectual archive for the Stieglerian tradition.

**Who should Amber know:**

## Why Interesting

The Digital Studies Network represents the most philosophically rigorous attempt to establish digital technology studies as a genuine discipline — grounded in continental philosophy (Bachelard, Foucault, Simondon, Derrida) rather than either positivist computer science or naive cultural studies. Its pharmacological framework is one of the few theoretical tools sophisticated enough to address the genuine complexity of AI systems without reducing them to either pure tools or autonomous agents.

For Amber, this tradition provides:
1. **Historical depth** — understanding where the discourse on digital epistemology came from
2. **Philosophical toolkit** — pharmacology, organology, and technodiversity as analytical frames for agentic AI
3. **A network to trace** — the people who came through IRI/DSN (especially Yuk Hui) are now at key institutions globally

The network itself is dormant, but its intellectual framework is very much alive and increasingly relevant as AI systems become embedded in knowledge production, attention, and social organization — exactly the dynamics Stiegler spent his career analyzing.
