# Ars Industrialis

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-27:** Full deep research completed. Historical organization (2005–2020), now archived. Succeeded by [[Association Épokhè]].
- **2026-02-26:** Created stub during [[Bernard Stiegler]] research.

Political-philosophical association founded in 2005 by Bernard Stiegler and four colleagues. Full name: *Association Internationale pour une Politique Industrielle des Technologies de l'Esprit* ("International Association for an Industrial Politics of Technologies of the Spirit"). Active until Stiegler's death (August 2020), when it transitioned into [[Association Épokhè]]. The definitive Stieglerian platform: where his philosophy became political program.

---

## Mission & Objectives

"Industrial politics of spirit" — the slogan encapsulates the project: to develop a political and cultural response to what Stiegler called the "disadjustment" (*désadjustement*) of industrial capitalism. The core diagnosis: capitalism's drive for productivity and profit is systematically destroying the *psychic and collective individuation* that sustains culture, knowledge, and democratic life. Technologies of the spirit (writing, cinema, internet, etc.) have become tools of *proletarization* — stripping workers, consumers, and citizens of knowledge, attention, and care.

The response was not Luddism but a **pharmacological** approach: technology is both poison and cure (*pharmakon*). Ars Industrialis argued for an alternative *industrial politics* that would orient technological development toward what Stiegler called *neganthropy* (producing new differences, knowledge, and care) rather than entropy.

Key concepts developed through Ars Industrialis:
- **Pharmacology of technics** — technology as pharmakon (poison/remedy)
- **Hypomnémata** — external memory systems (from Foucault/Derrida, extended by Stiegler)
- **Contributive economy** — alternative to attention economy, based on knowledge contribution
- **Neganthropy** — producing difference and knowledge as counter to systemic entropy
- **Exorganology** — study of technical organs (tools, institutions, networks) as constitutive of human experience

## Key People

- **[[Bernard Stiegler]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — founder, primary theorist. Philosopher of technics, former director of IRI (Institut de Recherche et d'Innovation) at Centre Pompidou. Died August 2020.
- **Marc Crépon** — co-founder, philosopher (CNRS/ENS Paris), specialist in ethics and political philosophy. Works on translation, violence, and responsibility.
- **George Collins** — co-founder, philosopher. Less publicly prominent but part of original five.
- **Catherine Perret** — co-founder, art theorist and philosopher. Works on aesthetics and politics of art.
- **Caroline Stiegler** — co-founder, Bernard Stiegler's wife. Continued with Épokhè after his death.

**Associated researchers/participants (Épokhè continuation):**
- **Michel Blanchut** — member, worked on Pharmakon seminars (Exorganologie III: Remondialisation)
- **Victor Chaix** — member, co-edited Pharmakon hypertexte seminars

**Thinkers extensively engaged:**
- **Gilbert Simondon** — mechanology and individuation (theoretical cornerstone)
- **Gaston Bachelard** — phenomenotechnics (referenced in ENMI 2017)
- **Frédéric Kaplan** — "capitalisme linguistique" (cited in ENMI discussions)

## Events & Programmes

### Entretiens du Nouveau Monde Industriel (ENMI)
Annual conferences held primarily at **Centre Pompidou, Paris** (Grande salle). Key editions:

- **ENMI 2017** — *Bêtise et intelligence artificielles* ("Stupidity and Artificial Intelligence"), Dec 19-20, Centre Pompidou. In collaboration with: European project NextLeap, ANR project Epistémè, Chaire de Recherche Contributive Plaine Commune. Theme: epistemic costs of algorithmic black boxes, data economy, prolétarisation through AI.
- **ENMI 2018** — *L'intelligence des villes et la nouvelle révolution urbaine* ("Intelligence of Cities and the New Urban Revolution"), Dec 18-19. Smart city critique.

### Pharmakon Summer Academies
Annual intensive programs, held in France. Themes included:
- **2017** — *Modeler l'avenir dans l'Anthropocène: Pharmacologie de la localité* ("Modeling the Future in the Anthropocene: Pharmacology of Locality")

### Public meetings and seminars
Regular *réunions publiques* (public meetings) — Maison des Associations de Paris, TGP de Saint-Denis, MSH Paris Nord.

### Plaine Commune Collaboration
Stiegler and Ars Industrialis worked closely with **Plaine Commune** (Seine-Saint-Denis territorial council) to experiment with **contributive economy** and **contributive income** (revenu contributif) as alternative to universal basic income. This included a *Chaire de Recherche Contributive* (Contributive Research Chair).

## For Amber

- **Why interesting:** Ars Industrialis is the most important example of philosophers building a practical political-cultural institution around philosophy of technology. Stiegler's concepts — pharmacology, hypomnémata, neganthropy, exorganology, contributive economy — are deeply relevant to Amber's work on trustworthy AI, agent behavior, and more-than-human design. The project of thinking technics as constitutive of human existence (not just instrumental) maps directly onto concerns about AI agents and machine behavior.

- **Intellectual relevance:** Stiegler's critique of attention economy and digital proletarization is a powerful framework for Amber's work on AI agents that could either proletarize or liberate human knowledge/care. The contributive economy model (as alternative to attention economy) is directly relevant to thinking about agent-mediated commons.

## Why Interesting

Ars Industrialis represents the most sustained attempt to build a political platform out of philosophy of technology. It's not just Stiegler's theoretical output — it's a model for how philosophers can create institutions, partner with territorial governments (Plaine Commune), design alternative economic experiments (contributive income), and engage publics through annual conferences at major cultural institutions (Centre Pompidou).

For Amber: the pharmacological framework is one of the strongest conceptual tools for thinking about AI — not as neutral tool but as *pharmakon* requiring careful attention to both its poisonous and remedying potentials. The critique of attention economy and digital proletarization maps directly onto concerns about what AI agents do to human knowledge and care. Ars Industrialis is now historical, but its successor Épokhè keeps the flame alive.
