# Center for Curatorial Studies (CCS), Bard College

One of the world's most prestigious graduate programs in curatorial studies — academically rigorous, globally networked, and unusually positioned at the intersection of theory and practice. Founded 1990 in Annandale-on-Hudson, New York (two hours from NYC). CCS combines a two-year MA program with an on-site museum — the Hessel Museum of Art — which holds the Marieluise Hessel Collection of 1,700+ works of post-1960s contemporary art. Its graduates include the curators of Venice Biennale (Cecilia Alemani, 59th edition 2022), Berlin Biennale, Documenta, and major museum positions globally. The current director of the Graduate Program, **Lauren Cornell**, came from Rhizome / New Museum with deep roots in art and the internet — making CCS unusually well-positioned at the art × technology intersection for a traditional curatorial institution.

## Mission & Objectives

CCS Bard is "an exhibition and research center dedicated to the study of art and exhibition practices from the 1960s to the present." Its Graduate Program (begun 1994) offers a two-year MA in Curatorial Studies — one of the oldest curatorial pedagogy programs in the world. The program operates on a core belief that curatorial practice is a form of intellectual and creative production, not just administration. Students work directly with a live museum collection and have real exhibition responsibilities from year one.

Three interlocking components define CCS:
1. **The MA Graduate Program** — rigorous academic + practical training
2. **The Hessel Museum of Art** — a 17,000 sq ft museum housing the Marieluise Hessel Collection
3. **The Research Center** — library (30,000+ volumes), archives, and research initiatives

The institution self-consciously embraces its "off-center" location (not in a major city) as an advantage: "We try things that might not be able to be done in other places" — Tom Eccles, Executive Director.

## Key Publications & Output

- **Exhibition catalogues** — every major Hessel exhibition produces a scholarly catalogue, often co-published with other institutions (MIT Press, ISLAA, Dancing Foxes Press, Wendy's Subway, etc.)
- **[*Mass Effect: Art and the Internet in the 21st Century*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/mass-effect)** (Lauren Cornell & Ed Halter, eds.; New Museum / MIT Press, 2016) — essential anthology on art and the internet; written while Cornell was at New Museum but reflects CCS's direction under her
- **Student theses** — collected in the CCS Bard Library & Archives. Notable thesis: Laurel Ptak on feminist art from the 1990s
- **Artists' books and limited-edition publications** via Research Center and Library Special Collections
- Library holds 30,000+ volumes focused on post-1960s contemporary art and curatorial practice, plus international exhibition catalogues, artists' monographs, art journals

## Key Contributions

- Produced 300+ curators since 1994, including curators of Venice Biennale, Berlin Biennale, Documenta, and senior curatorial positions at MoMA PS1, High Line, Forge Project, and institutions globally

- **Indian Theater: Native Performance, Art, and Self-Determination since 1969** (2023, Hessel Museum) — curated by Candice Hopkins (CCS alumna), bringing Indigenous contemporary art to one of the world's most visible curatorial stages
- Ongoing commitment to artists working with video, digital media, and networked technologies

## Events & Programmes

### MA in Curatorial Studies
- **Duration:** 2 years full-time
- **Application deadline:** February 1 annually
- **Enrollment:** Small cohort (intimate program)
- **Structure:** Combines theoretical seminars, curatorial practicums, and direct work with Hessel Museum collection
- **First Year:** "Curatorial Practice" (year-long course) — first-year students curate works from the Hessel Collection in semester one
- **Requirement:** Students curate two types of exhibitions over the two-year program
- **Faculty:** International curators, scholars, writers, theorists, art historians, artists rotating through
- **Application URL:** <https://ccs.bard.edu/school/application-information>
- **Contact:** ccsadmissions@bard.edu

**CCS Bard Fellowships** — merit-based; for U.S. and international students
<https://ccs.bard.edu/school/application-information>

**Lowery Stokes Sims Scholarships** — for students from underrepresented backgrounds in the curatorial and museum field; no separate application required
<https://ccs.bard.edu/school/application-information>

### Hessel Museum of Art

The physical heart of CCS Bard. 17,000 sq ft galleries opened November 12, 2006.
- **Collection:** Marieluise Hessel Collection — 1,700+ works of contemporary art (post-1960s to present)
- **Eisenberg Gift (2014):** Martin and Rebecca Eisenberg donated ~200 artworks including works by Trisha Baga, Uta Barth, Phil Collins, Anne Collier, Martin Creed, Moyra Davey, Marlene Dumas, Rachel Harrison, Rirkrit Tiravanija, and more
- **Architecture:** Jim Goettsch / Goettsch Partners (original 1991; expansion 2006)
- **Hours:** Currently open Tues–Sun 11AM (check site for current hours)
- **Website:** <https://ccs.bard.edu/museum>

## Why Interesting

CCS Bard is interesting to Amber for several overlapping reasons:

1. **The Cornell factor:** Lauren Cornell is one of the most sophisticated thinkers about art and technology operating within a major curatorial institution. Her background at Rhizome, her co-editing of *Mass Effect*, her co-curation of *Phantom Plane* — this is someone who has built an entire career at the art × internet × technology intersection and is now running one of the world's most prestigious curatorial programs. The combination is rare.

2. **The alumni network effect:** CCS graduates run the world's most important contemporary art institutions. Connecting to CCS — as student, lecturer, symposium participant, or collaborator — means connecting to this network. The Venice Biennale artistic director, the Berlin Biennale curator, the Documenta curators — all passed through CCS Bard.

3. **Theory + practice integration:** CCS is one of the few institutions that takes both rigorous theoretical work AND hands-on exhibition-making seriously. For Amber, who bridges practice-based research with critical theory, this is the right institutional model.

4. **Scale of institution:** CCS is small (intimate cohort), academically serious, physically embedded in a museum — not a mega-university program but a focused, elite environment where ideas actually get made into exhibitions. This matches Amber's aesthetic preference for small over big.

5. **The Hessel collection as research resource:** 1,700+ works of post-1960s contemporary art — an enormous archive for studying how technology, politics, and culture have intersected in contemporary practice.
