## What I Found

### Origins
MOCO was founded in 2014 at IRCAM-Centre Pompidou, Paris. The "International Workshop on Movement and Computing" — a workshop, not yet a conference. Became a full conference (MOCO'17 in London). Co-founded by **Frédéric Bevilacqua** (IRCAM) and **Thecla Schiphorst** (SFU). The founding duo is itself fascinating: one is a biomedical physicist turned music/gesture researcher at the world's most prestigious music research lab; the other is a choreographer turned computing professor. The conference's DNA comes from this tension.

### Conference History (2014-2026)
Full 10-year history documented. Most notable: **SloMoCo 2021** — when COVID hit, instead of just moving to Zoom, the community spent a year experimenting with a distributed, durational, telematic "anti-conference" using Manifold (e-publishing). This self-reflexivity is a signal of the community's intellectual seriousness.

**MOCO'24 in Utrecht** was the 10-year anniversary edition, hosted at the Faculty of Humanities (unusual for MOCO — most hosts are science/engineering). The theme "Beyond Control" drew heavily on critical theory and posthumanist thought (Franklin, Barad, Haraway, Bennett). This humanities turn is significant.

**MOCO'26 in Montpellier** is happening April 23-25, 2026. Theme: "In the Pink of Health" — health applications, but critically framed. **Submissions are closed** (deadline was November 10, 2025).

## Reflections

MOCO is a rare thing: a technically rigorous conference that has never lost its artistic soul. The rotating host model means each edition has a different flavor — ASU brought the desert + movement imaginaries + political openness; Utrecht brought the humanities lens; Montpellier brings health science. This structural flexibility prevents ossification.

The SloMoCo experiment is the clearest signal of community quality: when a community responds to crisis by *experimenting with knowledge production forms* rather than just replicating them on Zoom, that's a sign of genuine intellectual vitality.

The critical turn (race, surveillance, glitch feminism, posthumanism) happening at MOCO'22-24 is significant. This isn't tokenism — the MOCO'24 call for papers cites Safiya Umoja Noble and Virginia Eubanks alongside Barad and Haraway. The community is becoming more critical as it matures.
