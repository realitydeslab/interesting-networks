## What I Found

Brian Massumi is considerably richer than the stub suggested. Key upgrades:

### Personal History Is Politically Significant
The Wikipedia entry is unusually detailed about his pre-academic life: environmental activism as a teenager (Wilderness Society intern testifying to Congress on shale oil at 17!), then anti-nuclear direct action with the Clamshell Alliance offshoot CDAS, organizing occupation attempts of Seabrook Nuclear Power Plant. The **Seabrook Freestate** — a prefigurative community squatted on public land — directly informs his theory of micropolitics and prefigurative politics. This is not academic background; this is biographical. It's rare that a philosopher's activist history is this well documented and this continuous with their theoretical work.

### He Has Been Retired Since 2018
The stub implied he's an active professor. He retired from Université de Montréal in 2018, at around age 62. But he's still publishing prolifically — two books in 2025. His institutional independence means he's operating more freely now.

### The 2025 Dual Publication Is Strategically Smart
*The Personality of Power* (Duke UP, April 2025) and *Toward a Theory of Fascism for Anti-fascist Life* (Minor Compositions, April 2025) published simultaneously — one for the academy, one free download for activists. The Personality of Power is a full-length theoretical treatise; the Minor Compositions book is a glossary/vocabulary of key concepts. He's deliberately targeting two publics at once. Given the Trump context, this is urgent political work.

### Theory Out of Bounds Is Underappreciated
The stub didn't mention his 15-year co-editorship of Theory Out of Bounds (Minnesota UP, 1993–2007) with Michael Hardt and Sandra Buckley. This series was arguably as important as his own writing in shaping what "theory" meant in English. Publishing Agamben, Guattari, Virilio, Lyotard — he was functioning as institutional infrastructure for an entire intellectual milieu. This is how Hardt and Massumi are connected (not just philosophically but practically, as series co-editors).

### Three Books in One Year (2015)
Massumi published *Ontopower*, *The Power at the End of the Economy*, and *Politics of Affect* all in 2015. That's a remarkable output that signals he had been building up major arguments for years and released them all together.

### The Libet Connection
Massumi's use of Benjamin Libet's "missing half-second" neuroscience experiment as support for affect's autonomy is both his most famous and most controversial theoretical move. It's what Ruth Leys attacks directly. Worth noting: Libet's experiments are themselves contested in the neuroscience literature. But Massumi's use is not strictly empirical — he's using Libet as philosophical provocation, not neurological proof.

### Research-Creation + Neurodiversity
The connection to neurodiversity through Tito Rajarshi Mukhopadhyay (*Plankton Dreams*, Immediations series) and Adam Wolfond is philosophically significant. Manning and Massumi argue that autism isn't a deficit but a *different* mode of experience and knowledge — and that research-creation practices attuned to minor modes of knowing are structurally aligned with neurodiversity. This is politically radical and epistemically interesting.

### Inflexions Is Technically Broken
Most issues were built in Flash. Flash is dead. This is a significant cultural loss. The journal is a cautionary tale about experimental digital publishing — the medium can outlive the format it depends on. PDFs of issue contents exist for most issues, but the full interactive experience is gone.

## Reflections

Massumi is a rare case where the biographical, the intellectual, and the institutional are all equally interesting. Most theorists I've researched have an interesting intellectual project but a fairly conventional institutional life. Massumi has both — plus a genuinely unusual pre-academic history that isn't just color but structurally informs the theory.

The SenseLab / Manning collaboration is also unusual: 20 years of genuine co-creation, not just occasional co-authorship. They've built an institution together. That kind of sustained collaborative practice is worth attending to as a model.

His project is increasingly urgent: the affective theory of fascism is exactly what's needed in 2025 and he's been building toward it since at least 2015 (Ontopower) and arguably since First and Last Emperors (1993). He's been thinking about fascism longer than most people currently writing about it.

**Confidence in note:** High. Wikipedia entry is unusually well-cited with primary sources. Duke UP pages, OHP pages, punctum books page all confirmed. The LA Review of Books interview is long and substantive and I read it fully. The only gaps are empirical data (citations, specific events) that would require different tools.
