## Key Discoveries

### Intellectual Position
Vogl is the **bridge figure** between German media theory (Kittler tradition, Bauhaus Weimar, Kulturtechnik) and Foucauldian political economy applied to financial capitalism. This is an unusual combination — most media theorists don't do finance; most finance critics don't do media theory.

His core concept **"oikodicy"** is the key original contribution: financial markets as a secular theodicy that absorbs crises and legitimizes market rationality as rational order. This is more useful than ideology critique because it explains *how* finance constitutes an epistemic regime (like religion does) rather than merely disguising exploitation.

### The Finance Trilogy
The three books trace an arc:
1. *Specter of Capital* (2010/2015) — the epistemological/media regime of finance (oikodicy)
2. *Sovereignty Effect* (2015/2021) — the political/governmental dimension (state-finance entanglement)
3. *Kapital und Ressentiment* (2021) — the affective/psychological dimension (what financialization does to subjects)

This is a complete theory of contemporary capitalism across three registers. Remarkable scope.

### IKKM Connection
His Spring 2011 fellowship at IKKM is documented in the IKKM Weimar file I worked from earlier. This was during IKKM's Programme 1, "Semiosis — the Transformation of Objects into Signs" period (2010/11 theme). Appropriate: Vogl's finance work is exactly about how financial instruments produce signs/representations/values.

### Princeton Connection
The "permanent visiting professor" arrangement at Princeton German Department is unusual — he's not a Princeton faculty member but teaches there regularly. This gives him ongoing access to American graduate students and the Anglo-American theory discourse. His key English translators (William Callison, Joachim Redner/Robert Savage) appear to be connected to this Princeton network.

### Kluge Collaboration
The *Soll und Haben* TV conversation book (2009) with Alexander Kluge represents a significant collaborative thread. Kluge (born 1932) is a major figure in his own right — his DCTP television production company produces short essay films for late-night German TV; his literary/essayistic theory of *Zusammenhang* (coherence, attachment) is a distinctive contribution. Vogl and Kluge share an interest in money, narrative, and time that makes the collaboration organic.
