### Phase 3: Key Findings

**The format problem:** Inflexions is one of the most ambitious examples of web-as-medium publishing in the humanities — and one of the biggest digital preservation failures. Issues 1-7 required Flash, now defunct. Most content inaccessible. Important for Amber's thinking about archiving experimental digital work.

**The intellectual core:** Deleuze-Guattari + Whitehead + Simondon + Bergson. Micropolitics, transversality, process philosophy, affect. The journal effectively created an English-language infrastructure for Deleuzian research-creation methodology.

**The neurodiversity turn:** Issues in 2018-2019 (Pop Up 1, No. 11) explicitly center non-speaking autistic writers (Tito Mukhopadhyay, Adam Wolfond). This is ahead of its time and connects to disability studies, somaesthetics, and questions of what counts as legitimate knowledge.

**The Brasil network:** The final issue is heavily Brazilian (Christine Greiner, Fabiane M. Borges, Jorge Menna Barreto). The SenseLab's Brazilian connections are significant and underexplored.

**3 Ecologies as successor:** The SenseLab's extra-university activities now continue as 3 Ecologies Project (3ecologies.org), a non-profit with 800+ participants worldwide and groups in Montreal, Australia, Brazil, and Europe. This is essentially Inflexions' community continuing without the journal format.

## Reflections

Inflexions is a graveyard of ambition. It tried to be the most radical humanities journal in terms of form, and it succeeded so thoroughly that it's now mostly inaccessible. The irony is that the textual content (especially Massumi's essays) circulates widely in PDFs and citations, while the designed environments — the actual innovation — are gone.

The research-creation concept it developed is now mainstream in practice-based PhD programs. Manning and Massumi got what they wanted; the medium just didn't survive.

For Amber: the SenseLab/3 Ecologies network is worth knowing as a community, not just a bibliography. It has active groups in multiple countries, operates as a gift economy, and explicitly includes neurodiversity and non-normative practice. The research-creation methodology is directly applicable to Amber's practice-based Oxford PhD.
