## What Was Found

Erin Manning is substantially more theoretically sophisticated than her stub suggested. The key dimensions:

### 1. The Full Intellectual Trajectory

The stub mentioned her current position (affect theory, movement, neurodiversity). What I found: a three-phase trajectory that makes the current position make sense —
- Phase 1: Québec nationalism + political theory of sovereignty (first book 2003, PhD work)
- Phase 2: Touch → movement → process philosophy (2007–2013), co-building SenseLab infrastructure
- Phase 3: Neurodiversity → blackness → ecology → land/settler-colonialism (2016–2026)

The pivot from nationalism to sensation (phase 1 → 2) is the structurally interesting move. She's essentially asking: what is sovereignty in the body? That question led her all the way to autistic perception and ProTactile.

### 2. The "All Black Life Is Neurodiverse Life" Formulation

This is her most provocative theoretical claim, in *For a Pragmatics of the Useless* (2020). It's not an equation — she calls it "an approximation of proximity." The argument: neurotypicality and whiteness co-constitute a normative baseline, such that blackness and neurodiversity both operate as the minor/excessive against that baseline. This creates a resonance between disability studies and Black radical theory that hadn't been systematically mapped. Very controversial, very generative.

### 3. The Infrastructure Strategy

The sequence SenseLab (2004) → Inflexions journal (2008) → Immediations series at OHP (2015) → 3Ecologies at punctum books (2018) → 3 Ecologies Institute as land-based community (ongoing) is a deliberate multi-decade platform-building project. Each layer adds a different form of institutional permanence. This is one of the most sustained infrastructure-building projects in the humanities by a single person.

### 4. The Blurber Circuit

Following who blurbs her books: William Forsythe (choreographer, 2013) → Fred Moten (black studies, 2016) → Stefano Harney + Ashon T. Crawley (black studies, 2020) → Kim TallBear + Peter Kulchyski (Indigenous studies, 2026). This is not just marketing; it traces her expanding intellectual coalitions. Each new blurber signals a disciplinary constituency she's built alliances with.

### 5. The 3 Ecologies Institute (3ecologies.org)

This was not in the stub and deserves its own note. It's an attempt at building a post-capitalist research infrastructure — extra-university, gift-economy-based, land-based, 800+ participants globally. One of the few genuine implementations of the "what if we built institutions differently" argument that many theorists make but few actually attempt.

### 6. Unsettled (Minor Compositions, March 2026)

Just released — a philosophical memoir about settler-colonial ancestry in Québec. The choice to publish with Minor Compositions (free download available) rather than Duke UP is significant. She's moving from academic argumentation toward a more personal, land-engaged mode. The free digital download is itself a political gesture.

## Reflections

Manning is a model for what it means to build a research practice rather than just a publication record. The 20-year sequence of SenseLab → Inflexions → Immediations → 3Ecologies is extraordinary. Most researchers produce; she has produced while simultaneously building the infrastructure for others to produce.

The neurodiversity thread is genuinely interesting for Amber's work. The move from "what is autistic perception?" to "this is actually how all perception works (pre-chunked, relational)" is a classic "minor reveals the general" move. Applied to HCI: what do edge-case users reveal about the underlying architecture of standard interaction? Manning's method says: start there.

The *Unsettled* move (personal memoir, free download, Minor Compositions) is worth watching as a model for how a theorist's late career can go: from argument to witness, from system to story.

Fred Moten is a separate ⭐⭐⭐ worth noting for Amber. He's not just a blurber; he's doing something philosophically adjacent that Amber might find generative — the undercommons as a model for what happens around and below institutions.
