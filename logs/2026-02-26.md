### Key Findings
- **Canonical expanded cinema artist** — *Reel Time* (1973) is the paradigmatic example of structural-materialist film as feminist practice: a sewing machine sews through a 16mm film loop while it is projected, making the production trace identical to the image. One of the most celebrated works in British avant-garde history.
- **Circles co-founder** — One of four co-founders (with Lis Rhodes, Felicity Sparrow, Jo Davis) of the UK's first feminist film distribution network (1979). Circles ran initially from Felicity Sparrow's flat; early catalogue included Nicolson's *Women and Creativity* recordings.
- **Filmaktion membership confirmed** — Tate (both Filmaktion art term and Expanded Cinema art term) explicitly identifies Nicolson as a Filmaktion member alongside Gill Eatherley, Malcolm Le Grice, and William Raban. All LFMC members; all working in the participatory expanded cinema tradition.
- **Three-period LFMC involvement** — Cinema programmer in 1974, 1976–77, and 1992/93 (bridging early structural-materialist era, post-Circles feminist rupture, and late LFMC period).
- **New Arts Lab connection** — Gallery director at the New Arts Lab (Robert Street Arts Lab, Camden Town) 1969–70, before completing her postgraduate study at St Martins. This placed her at the centre of London's experimental film scene from its most formative moment.
- **Key publications** — "Women and the Formal Film" (manifesto, 1979, with 7 co-signatories); editorial contributor *Musics* magazine 1976–79; co-editor *Readings* magazine 1977.
- **Collections** — Belgian National Film Archive; British Film Institute; Canterbury University; Women Artists' Slide Library; V&A (artist's book *Escaping Notice*).
- **Award** — John Brinkley Fellowship, Norwich School of Art, 1980–81.
- **Quote** — "It is what happens to things when they are not being looked at that puzzles me." — works across formal and feminist registers simultaneously.
