## What I Found

### The Core Insight
Kristina Andersen's work is centered on a single powerful question: *who gets to imagine technological futures?* Her answer — give everyone the methods and materials to do it themselves — is enacted through the "Magic Machine Workshops" methodology, which she published with Ron Wakkary at CHI 2019 (now 103 citations). This is a rare case where method is theory.

### The STEIM Connection
14 years at STEIM (Studio for Electro-Instrumental Music, Amsterdam) is the most distinctive thing about her trajectory. STEIM closed in 2019 after losing Dutch funding, but it was one of Europe's great experimental music technology institutions for 50 years. This practitioner network — electronic instrument designers, experimental composers, sonic artists — is unusual in academic design research. It gives Kristina credibility and community connections that purely academic researchers don't have.

### The Wakkary Relationship
The Ron Wakkary co-authorship (Magic Machine Workshops + Digital Crafts-machine-ship) is the intellectually deepest collaboration. Wakkary is the author of *Things We Could Design* (MIT Press 2021) — the canonical more-than-human design text — and they share a genuine intellectual project about design methodology in the post-human moment.

### Arts Track Strategy
Her committee service reveals a deliberate strategy: she chairs arts tracks (TEI Arts Chair 2024, CHI Art Chair 2018) rather than competing in technical paper tracks. This gives her influence in the more creative, practice-based corner of HCI with less competition and more methodological freedom.

### Dutch Design Ecosystem
The Stimuleringsfonds Creatieve Industrie advisory role means she has significant gatekeeping power in Dutch creative industry funding. Combined with Gerrit Rietveld Academie and STEIM, she's a senior figure in the Amsterdam/Dutch design-art research scene.

## Reflections
The "arts track as power base" insight is interesting from a career architecture perspective. In crowded academic fields, the arts and practice tracks at major conferences are structurally undervalued but offer more freedom and differentiation. This is a generalizable lesson.

The STEIM connection is a reminder: the most interesting people often have deep practitioner roots in unusual places. Kristina's 14 years in experimental music technology is what makes her different from other design researchers who theorize about "making with machines" without having actually done it for a decade.

Her work connects directly to three of Amber's key threads: more-than-human design (via Wakkary), participatory futures (via Magic Machine Workshops), and practice-based research methodology. She's a genuine ⭐⭐⭐ node.
