## What I Found

### The Scale of Impact
*In the Wake* (2016) has achieved something rare for academic scholarship: it has migrated beyond the academy to become a reference point in visual art, community organizing, prisons, high-school debate, documentary film, and mainstream literary culture. Harper's Bazaar ran "Everything Comes Back to Christina Sharpe's *In the Wake*" in 2023 — a feature in a fashion magazine about an academic text. That's the measure. Her *Ordinary Notes* (2023) confirmed this status with three simultaneous major-press editions (FSG, Knopf Canada, Daunt Books UK), the Windham-Campbell Prize ($165K, Yale), and the Guggenheim Fellowship.

### The Practicing Refusal Collective Is a Model Worth Studying
The PR Collective (formed 2015 by Tina Campt and Saidiya Hartman, 16 members across US and Canada, no headquarters) and its Sojourner Project (mobile Black Studies academy, first convening Paris 2018 at Columbia Global Center/Reid Hall, 300+ participants) is a remarkable organizational structure. It's explicitly built to exceed the university — transnational gatherings in sites with existing Black communities (Paris, South Africa), not just academic venues. The structure: a small curated collective of aligned scholars + artists who host public events and working groups, pulling in local artists, scholars, and community workers as collaborators. This is community-as-research-infrastructure (one of Amber's emerging interests) operating at a sophisticated level.

### The Artist-Scholar Position
Sharpe has unusual institutional hybridity: she's embedded in both the academy (Canada Research Chair, York University) and the art world (catalogue essays for Simone Leigh, Jennifer Packer, MoMA Reconstructions, New Museum Grief and Grievance). Visual artists like Simone Leigh directly commission her work — "Beauty as a Method" was written for Leigh's Hugo Boss Prize Exhibition at the Guggenheim. This is not the typical academic-writes-about-art relationship; it's genuine collaboration. Her position is worth studying for how to construct it.

### Form as Epistemology
Both *In the Wake* and *Ordinary Notes* make formal choices that are also epistemological arguments. The fragments and white space in *Ordinary Notes* are not stylistic preferences — they argue that some aspects of Black life cannot be said through argumentation. The "Dictionary of Untranslatable Blackness" (entries contributed by Phoebe Boswell, Belinda Kazeem-Kamiński, Alexandra Bell, and others) makes the claim that some Black experiences have no adequate term in existing lexicons. This is Wittgensteinian philosophy done through prose poetry.
