### Hugh J. Silverman Prize (2024)
*Who Would You Kill to Save the World?* (Nebraska, 2023) won the **inaugural** Hugh J. Silverman Book Prize from the Association for Philosophy and Literature — "cutting-edge, single-authored books that productively explore the space where philosophy and literary study intersect." This is her most recent major recognition and signals that her work has broken out of the continental philosophy niche into philosophy/literature crossover.

### Book Description of *Who Would You Kill to Save the World?*
"Colebrook redefines 'the world' as affluent Western society and 'saving the world' as preventing us from becoming the othered them who are viewed in their suffering... combines postapocalyptic fiction, concern over the global climate crisis, colonialism, and anti-Blackness to explain how contemporary postapocalypse blockbusters circulate ideas of whiteness and the right of the privileged to rebuild the world." This is her most accessible book (cinema/popular culture) and her most explicitly political.

### The De Manian Thread
The Cohen-Colebrook-Miller collaboration is built on **Paul de Man's late deconstruction** — specifically de Man's argument that all meaning-making has a structural blindness, that performative and constative dimensions of language never align. Their application: climate change is the collapse of the meaning-making frameworks (anthropocentrism, futurity, political agency) that sustained modern thought. This is a genuinely distinctive intellectual lineage — very different from the Latour/Haraway branch of posthumanism or the OOO branch.

## Reflections

Colebrook is intellectually important for Amber precisely because she represents the **uncomfortable** position in posthumanism. The more-than-human design world tends toward the affirmative: designing with ecosystems, making kin, expanding the circle of moral consideration. Colebrook asks: what is the logical endpoint of this move? If we genuinely take the nonhuman seriously, doesn't that evacuate the human subject who was doing the "including" in the first place? This is not a nihilist position — it's a philosophical demand for rigor.

Her career also models **platform building as primary intellectual contribution**: the CCC series has done more to shape the field of climate humanities than any single book she's written. For Amber thinking about how to build intellectual infrastructure around critical computing / more-than-human design, this is a model worth studying.
