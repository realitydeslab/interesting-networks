# Michael Hardt

American political philosopher and literary theorist at Duke University. Co-author of the *Empire* trilogy with Antonio Negri — the closest thing the 21st century has to a *Communist Manifesto*. Long-form theorist of Empire (global networked sovereignty), the Multitude (the distributed political subject that opposes it), immaterial/affective labor, and the commons. Editor of the *South Atlantic Quarterly*. Also the person who demonstrated what 40 years of radical intellectual friendship can produce.

## Background

Hardt began as an **engineer**, not a philosopher. BS in Engineering from Swarthmore College (1978–1983), during the 1970s energy crisis; he was drawn to solar energy for developing countries as a form of politics. "I thought that doing alternative energy engineering for third world countries would be a way of doing politics that would get out of all this campus political posing that I hated." He was quickly disabused.

After college he joined the **Sanctuary Movement** — the political-religious campaign providing safe haven in the US to Central American refugees fleeing civil wars backed by the US government. He also helped establish a project to bring donated computer hardware and software to the **University of El Salvador**. He has said this political activity did more for him than for the Salvadorans.

In 1983 he moved to Seattle to study comparative literature at the University of Washington. **MA 1986**, then **PhD 1990** — dissertation on Gilles Deleuze. While working on his PhD, Hardt began translating Antonio Negri's book on Spinoza (*The Savage Anomaly*) in order to come into contact with him. He first met Negri in Paris in summer 1986 to discuss translation difficulties. That meeting changed everything. After completing his PhD, he briefly taught at the University of Southern California, then joined the **Literature Program at Duke University in 1994** — where he has remained.

He is also a professor of philosophy at the **European Graduate School (EGS)** in Switzerland, where he regularly teaches and lectures. Since 2010 he has been **editor of the *South Atlantic Quarterly***, one of Duke's flagship interdisciplinary journals.

The intellectual trajectory: engineering → Central American solidarity → Deleuze → Spinoza → Negri → autonomism → global theory. The unusual starting point (engineering → comparative literature → political philosophy) explains the unusual quality of his work — he thinks about structure, about forces and flows, not just texts.

## Key Works

### Books (solo)
- [*Gilles Deleuze: An Apprenticeship in Philosophy*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/gilles-deleuze) (University of Minnesota Press, 1993, ISBN 0-8166-2161-6) — his published dissertation. Reads Deleuze through Spinoza and Bergson; constructs an account of Deleuze as political ontologist. The philosophical foundation for everything that follows. Focuses on three lines of development: ethics (Spinoza), ontology (Bergson), politics (Nietzsche). Accessible gateway to Deleuze.
- [*The Subversive Seventies*](https://academic.oup.com/book/55726) (Oxford University Press, 2023, ISBN 978-0197674659) — solo book. Analyzes liberation movements of the 1970s worldwide (anticolonial, feminist, indigenous, environmental, LGBTQ+), arguing their experiments in democratic organization have direct relevance for political struggles today. Written after Negri's death; a kind of political testament.

### Books (with Antonio Negri)
- [*Labor of Dionysus: A Critique of the State-Form*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/labor-of-dionysus) (University of Minnesota Press, 1994, ISBN 0-8166-2086-5) — first full Hardt-Negri collaboration. Critical engagement with Marxist political theory, the state, and law. Less read than the trilogy but philosophically foundational.
- [*Empire*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674006713) (Harvard University Press, 2000, ISBN 0-674-00671-2) — the landmark text. Argues that global capitalism has produced a new form of sovereignty — "Empire" — that is diffuse, networklike, without a center (unlike traditional imperialism). Nation-states unravel but regulation proliferates. Empire is a society of the spectacle. The new revolutionary subject is the "multitude." Often called the *Communist Manifesto* of the 21st century. Influenced globalization debates, alter-globalization movement, World Social Forum.
- [*Multitude: War and Democracy in the Age of Empire*](https://www.penguin.com/ajax/books/preview/9780143035596) (Penguin, 2004, ISBN 1-59420-024-6) — sequel to Empire. Foregrounds the "multitude" as the political subject to oppose Empire: transversal, not a class in the traditional sense, including immaterial laborers, migrants, queer people, peasants. War in the age of Empire as permanent global police action. Democracy as possibility emerging from the multitude.
- [*Commonwealth*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674035119) (Harvard University Press, 2009, ISBN 0-674-03511-9) — trilogy conclusion. Centers the concept of the **commons**: not just natural resources but "languages we create, the social practices we establish, the modes of sociality that define our relationships." Argues for **communism** (not socialism/statism) defined by the commons vs. capitalism defined by private property. Introduces "altermodernity" — beyond both modernity and anti-modernity.
- [*Declaration*](https://books.google.com/books?isbn=0786752904) (Argo-Navis, 2012, ISBN 0-786-75290-4) — self-published ebook/pamphlet in response to the occupation movements of 2011–12. Short, direct. Argues these movements explored new forms of democracy; critiques leadership cults.
- [*Assembly*](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/assembly-9780190677961) (Oxford University Press, 2017, ISBN 978-0190677961) — the "fourth volume." Responds to the previous decade's social movements (Arab Spring, Occupy, Podemos, Syriza). Key argument: horizontal movements suffer when they refuse leadership, but need new forms of it. How to keep the radical democratic energy of the multitude while developing organizational power.

### Edited volumes
- [*Radical Thought in Italy: A Potential Politics*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/radical-thought-in-italy) (ed., University of Minnesota Press, 1996, ISBN 0-8166-2553-0) — key anthology introducing Italian autonomism to English-language readers. Includes essays by Negri, Virno, Lazzarato, Agamben, and others. Landmark translation/editorial work.

### Key Articles
- ["The Withering of Civil Society"](https://doi.org/10.2307/466673) (1995) — *Social Text* 45: 27–44. Argues civil society in Gramscian sense has eroded; new forms of biopower have replaced it. Foundational paper.
- ["Prison Time"](https://doi.org/10.2307/2930374) (1997) — *Yale French Studies* 91: 64–79. On carceral time and political prisoners (Gramsci, Negri's own imprisonment). Connects embodied experience of incarceration to political philosophy.
- "The Global Society of Control" (1998) — *Discourse* 20(3): 139–152. Develops Deleuze's "control societies" concept in global capitalist context. Surveillance, modulation, dividuals.
- ["Affective Labor"](https://doi.org/10.1215/00382876-2010-020) (1999) — *boundary 2* 26(2): 89–100. The paper that put "affective labor" in wide circulation. Labor that produces affects, relationships, communications, social life — not just goods. Key for understanding service economy and reproductive labor.
- ["Porto Alegre: Today's Bandung?"](http://newleftreview.org/II/14/michael-hardt-porto-alegre-today-s-bandung) (2002) — *New Left Review* II/14. On the World Social Forum as a new internationalist project.
- ["Jefferson and Democracy"](https://doi.org/10.1353/aq.2007.0026) (2007) — *American Quarterly* 59(1): 41–78. A surprising engagement with Jefferson: what can radical democratic theory take from early American republicanism?
- ["The Common in Communism"](https://doi.org/10.1080/08935696.2010.490365) (2010) — *Rethinking Marxism* 22(3): 346–356. Key short statement: why communism must be grounded in the common, not the state.
- ["The Militancy of Theory"](https://doi.org/10.1215/00382876-2010-020) (2011) — *South Atlantic Quarterly* 110(1): 19–35. On theory as a form of political practice.
- ["The Fight for 'Real Democracy' at the Heart of Occupy Wall Street"](https://www.foreignaffairs.com/articles/americas/2011-10-11/fight-real-democracy-heart-occupy-wall-street) (2011, with Negri) — *Foreign Affairs*, October 11, 2011. Their intervention in the mainstream policy debate during Occupy.
- "Reclaim the Common in Communism" (2011) — *The Guardian*, February 3, 2011. Public intellectual intervention.
- ["How to Write with Four Hands"](https://doi.org/10.1215/00166928-2088007) (2013) — *Genre* 46(2): 175–182. Unique: an essay about the process of intellectual collaboration with Negri. How do two people write as one? What does friendship do to thought?
- ["The Power to Be Affected"](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10767-014-9191-x) (2015) — *International Journal of Politics, Culture, and Society* 28(3): 215–222. Develops the concept of being affected as a form of political capacity — active receptivity, not passive suffering.
- ["Empire, Twenty Years On"](https://newleftreview.org/issues/ii120/articles/empire-twenty-years-on) (2019, with Negri) — *New Left Review* 120. A landmark retrospective: how do *Empire*'s theses hold up in an era of rising nationalism, Brexit, Trump, China? Their answer: the twin spheres of power and (re)production have spun out of sync — symptoms of a system working by breaking down.
- ["Standpoint theory and double abolition"](https://dx.doi.org/10.1177/09213740231206103) (2023) — *Cultural Dynamics*, November 2023. Reads Denise Ferreira da Silva's *Unpayable Debt* as an epistemological standpoint theory from the "wounded captive body." Connects Black radical tradition to political ontology.
- ["A Global War Regime"](https://newleftreview.org/sidecar/posts/a-global-war-regime) (2024, with Sandro Mezzadra) — *New Left Review* Sidecar, May 9, 2024. Their analysis of the current period: warfare as governance, the militarization of economic and social life. Proposes "desertion" as the appropriate political tactic — collective withdrawal from the war regime. Connects Ukraine, Gaza, Indo-Pacific tensions into a unified framework.

## Key Relationships & Collaborators

- **[[Antonio Negri]]** ⭐⭐⭐ (1933–2023) — the defining intellectual partnership. Hardt met Negri in Paris in 1986 while translating Negri's book on Spinoza. Six books together over 40 years. Negri spent years in prison in Italy (1979–1983 for "moral" leadership of Red Brigades, widely disputed) then in exile in Paris and France. Their collaboration crossed continents and decades. Negri died December 2023. The partnership produced *Empire*, *Multitude*, *Commonwealth*, *Declaration*, *Assembly*, and *Labor of Dionysus* — arguably the most sustained Marxist theoretical project of the late 20th/early 21st century.

Massumi wrote a blurb for Hardt's work; Hardt blurbed Massumi's *The Personality of Power* (2025): *"By mobilizing and building creatively on Deleuze and Guattari's conceptual arsenal, Brian Massumi provides a new and convincing analysis of the political and affective logics of Trump and his followers."*

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Hardt moves in three primary circuits:

**1. Critical theory / academic left (global)**
Duke University, European Graduate School, New Left Review, *boundary 2*, *Social Text*, *South Atlantic Quarterly* — the flagship institutions of the Anglophone critical theory world. He is not just a participant but an **editor and series editor** — an infrastructure-builder. This circuit runs through Harvard UP, Oxford UP, Minnesota UP, Verso.

**2. European political left (activist-intellectual)**
World Social Forum (Porto Alegre, Mumbai, etc.), Subversive Festival Zagreb, and the circuit of European radical politics: ZDF/Arte documentaries, *Le Monde Diplomatique*, *Der Spiegel*, *Eurozine*. His analysis of Occupy Wall Street appeared in *Foreign Affairs* (mainstream policy) but the deeper circuit is this European activist-intellectual one. Connected to the autonomist tradition in Italy and its European extensions.

**3. North American public intellectualism**
Appearances on Charlie Rose, *Adbusters*, *The Nation*, *The Guardian* — not just academic venues but an attempt to reach broader publics. The *Empire* trilogy achieved this at scale: it was reviewed everywhere from the *New York Times* to activist zines.

**What's distinctive:** Unlike most critical theorists, Hardt genuinely straddles activist spaces and elite academic ones. He was on the ground at Porto Alegre; he also publishes with Harvard UP. This is not a contradiction but a strategy: use the academy as a megaphone for movement ideas, use movements as raw material for theoretical development. The Subversive Festival circuit — Zagreb, Belgrade, Sarajevo — reveals an Eastern European radical left connection that most Anglophone theorists lack.

**Small venues as signal:** His EGS lectures, his Croatian radio interview, his appearance at the Subversive Festival before it was well-known — these are the signs of genuine intellectual commitment, not career-strategic platform-building.

## Why Interesting

**The *Empire* diagnosis is aging extremely well.** In 2000, Hardt and Negri said: forget about nation-states as the primary unit of sovereignty; global capitalism has produced a diffuse, networked Empire without a center, and resistance must emerge from the transversal Multitude (not the nation, not the working class in the old sense). In 2024, looking at AI platforms, cloud infrastructure, logistics networks, financial circuits, the war regime — this framing looks prescient. The question of whether there is a center (US? China? Silicon Valley?) or only diffuse Empire remains live.

**"Immaterial labor" and "affective labor"** — concepts Hardt developed in the late 1990s — are now the primary category for understanding the digital economy, care work, platform labor, and the political economy of AI training data. When we talk about "data labor" or "ghost work" or "AI workers," we are working in terrain Hardt helped map.

**The commons** argument anticipates open-source, Creative Commons, and the current debates about AI and knowledge enclosure. His critique of the "republic of property" is the philosophical ground for arguments about why AI training data should be a commons, not a private asset.

**The Subversive Seventies** (2023) is a late-career argument that the 1970s liberation movements (feminist, anticolonial, LGBTQ+, environmental) experimented with exactly the decentralized, prefigurative, horizontalist organizational forms that contemporary movements (Occupy, Black Lives Matter, climate strikes) need to learn from.

**The human angle:** Hardt cares about **joy** in political life. He has written about love and friendship as political categories. The 40-year partnership with Negri is itself an argument: that sustained intellectual friendship, crossing national borders, prison bars, and generational difference, can produce something neither person could alone. That's a compelling model for how knowledge is made.

**Amber's angle:** Connects to Critical Computing (critique of platforms as enclosure of the commons), More-than-Human (the Multitude as non-unified political subject), and Participatory Design (constituent power as bottom-up political design). The commons framework is directly applicable to debates about AI governance and data ownership.

## Research Taste Analysis

**This is not about Hardt's *work* — it's about the *meta-strategies* Amber can learn from.**

### Conceptual Strategy: The Two-Concept Engine
Hardt's most successful work always runs on two complementary concepts in productive tension: **Empire / Multitude**, **immaterial labor / affective labor**, **the public / the common**, **modernity / altermodernity**. The strategy: coin paired concepts that map a terrain (one describing domination, one describing resistance) and let the tension between them generate analysis. This is more generative than single-concept frameworks because it forces the question of *how the two relate* — which is where the real intellectual action is.

**Lesson for Amber:** When developing a theoretical contribution, try to coin concept *pairs*, not single concepts. "Machine behavior / machine ecology." "AI agency / human agency." The pairing is what makes it productive.

### Career Architecture: The Long-Form Collaboration Model
Hardt's entire career is built around one extraordinary collaboration. Rather than building a solo system, he found a genuine intellectual partner (Negri) with complementary skills (philosophical depth + political economy + militant experience) and committed to the partnership for 40 years. The result exceeds what either could do alone. The risk: the partnership defines you (Hardt is always "Hardt *and Negri*"). The payoff: compound intellectual returns.

**Lesson for Amber:** Is there a partner whose thinking is genuinely complementary? Long-form intellectual collaboration is not common in CS/design research but has precedents (Bell & Kuhn, Haraway & Goodeve). When you find the right partner, go deep.

### Infrastructure Building: Series Editing as Field-Shaping
The Theory Out of Bounds series (1993–2007) was not just publishing books — it was **constructing a field**. By curating which texts got translated and introduced to English readers, Hardt (with Massumi and Buckley) shaped what "critical theory" meant for a generation. The SAQ editorship does the same: Hardt gets to set the agenda for one of the oldest interdisciplinary journals.

**Lesson for Amber:** The most durable forms of intellectual influence are often infrastructural — editing, organizing, curating — not just publishing. What would it mean to be an editor of the critical AI studies space?

### The Activist-Academic Hybridization
Hardt's unusual quality is that he is genuinely both. He was at Porto Alegre and he publishes with Harvard UP. He wrote for *Foreign Affairs* and attended Subversive Festival Zagreb. This creates a dual circulation: his ideas reach both academic and activist audiences, and each legitimizes him in the other's eyes. Very few people manage this; most drift to one side.

**Lesson for Amber:** The dual circulation strategy is high-risk, high-reward. If you can write a piece that appears in a top academic venue *and* in a venue that reaches practitioners/activists, it circulates in two different networks simultaneously. Aim for that whenever possible.

### The Engineering Inheritance
Hardt started as an engineer, and it shows — in the structural quality of his thinking, the diagrams-in-prose style, the focus on systems and flows rather than just texts. He never abandoned the engineering sensibility; he redirected it. *Empire* is written like an engineer analyzing a system.

**Lesson for Amber:** Technical training is not a liability in humanities/social science research — it's an asset, especially now. Lean into it explicitly. The "engineer turned theorist" framing is distinctive.
