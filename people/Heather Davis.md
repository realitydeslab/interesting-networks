# Heather Davis

Art theorist, cultural studies scholar, and curator working at the intersection of art, ecology, and Anthropocene politics. Associate Professor at The New School for Social Research (NYC). Key figure in thinking the relationship between **contemporary art and the Anthropocene** — not art "about" climate change but art that formally restructures ecological thought.

Co-edited *Art in the Anthropocene: Encounters Among Aesthetics, Politics, Environments and Epistemologies* (Open Humanities Press, 2015) with Etienne Turpin — one of the defining anthologies of ecological art theory, published FREE in the [[Open Humanities Press]] Critical Climate Change series.

Her current work focuses on **plastic** — not as environmental bad guy but as a material that carries the inscription of petrocapitalism into deep time. *Plastic Matter* (Duke University Press, 2022) argues that plastic is a stratigraphic layer of the Anthropocene, a material index of capitalist extraction.

## Key Works

- [*Art in the Anthropocene: Encounters Among Aesthetics, Politics, Environments and Epistemologies*](https://openhumanitiespress.org/books/titles/art-in-the-anthropocene/) (Open Humanities Press, 2015) — **FREE OA** — co-edited with Etienne Turpin; essays by Anna Tsing, Ursula Biemann, Nato Thompson, Joanna Zylinska, Timothy Morton, many others; the key anthology for art × Anthropocene
- [*Plastic Matter*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/plastic-matter) (Duke University Press, 2022) — plastic as material history of petrocapitalism; plastics as stratigraphic layer; **highly recommended**
- Articles on plastic, queer ecology, and Anthropocene aesthetics in *e-flux*, *Afterall*, *Environmental Humanities*

## Why Interesting

*Plastic Matter* is genuinely important for Amber: it offers a model for how to think about a specific material (plastic) as carrier of deep political and temporal structures — an approach directly applicable to thinking about digital materials, silicon, data centers as Anthropocene stratigraphic layers. The *Art in the Anthropocene* anthology is probably the best single volume for understanding how contemporary art has engaged with ecological crisis as formal/aesthetic/epistemological problem (not just "climate art" as messaging).

Davis represents the **art-theory → ecology** track that parallels Amber's potential **design-theory → more-than-human** track. Model to study.
