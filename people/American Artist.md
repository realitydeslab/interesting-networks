# American Artist

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-27:** Full deep research completed. Updated from stub to comprehensive profile.
- **2025-01-24:** *Shaper of God* solo exhibition opened at Pioneer Works, Brooklyn (runs Jan 24–Apr 13, 2025), accompanied by monograph published by Pioneer Works Press (Mar 14, 2025).

- **2024-04:** Artadia New York Award.

Interdisciplinary conceptual artist (b. 1989, Altadena, CA) whose multidisciplinary practice mines the history of technology, race, and knowledge production — beginning with their legal name change to "American Artist" in 2013. Works in sculpture, software, video, and installation to expose anti-Black racism embedded in technological systems: algorithmic policing, digital labor, surveillance infrastructure, and the Silicon Valley mythologies that naturalize racial hierarchy. Faculty at Yale University School of Art; former co-director of the School for Poetic Computation (SFPC). Conversant with Black Studies scholars — Fred Moten, Simone Browne, Ruha Benjamin — and situates art as a form of critical theory and speculative practice.

## Background

- **Born 1989, Altadena, CA** — grew up in and around Altadena and Pasadena, the same communities that shaped Octavia E. Butler (born Pasadena, 1947). American Artist's deep engagement with Butler's work is autobiographical as well as theoretical.
- **MFA Fine Art, [Parsons, The New School for Design](https://www.newschool.edu/parsons/)** — MFA training in fine art provided the craft base for their conceptual practice.
- **[Whitney Museum Independent Study Program](https://whitney.org/research/isp/) (2016-17)** — the most prestigious artist-theorist programme in the US. Alongside art-making, ISP has a seminar track grounded in critical theory. Alumni include many of the most significant US-based critical artists.
- **Legal name change (2013)** — conceptual foundation of the entire practice. "American Artist" as a name is: (a) an assertion that Blackness is descriptive of an American artist; and (b) a disappearing act — an anonymous name that computers and algorithms cannot validate as a real person. The name performs its own critique.
- Co-founded **[unbag](http://unbag.net/)** — arts and politics publication.
- Based in Brooklyn, NY. Works across the US and internationally.

## Domains

- **Anti-Black racism in technology** — how algorithmic systems encode and amplify racial bias (predictive policing, facial recognition, digital redlining)
- **Surveillance and carcerality** — technology as an extension of policing and mass incarceration
- **Digital labor and platform capitalism** — invisible labor powering tech systems, especially by Black and brown workers
- **Historical memory and computational infrastructure** — how history is stored, retrieved, and suppressed by technical systems
- **Speculative artifacts and fiction** — works that resemble devices or evidence of real events but are subtle fictions, asking "is this real?"
- **Afrofuturism and Octavia Butler** — deep engagement with Butler's work as a frame for understanding race, technology, and possible futures
- **Net art and browser-based work** — online interventions as political art form

## Key Works

- **[Sandy Speaks](http://sandyspeaks.americanartist.us/)** (2016) — Chatbot modeled on Sandra Bland, the Black woman who died in police custody in 2015 after a routine traffic stop. The bot uses archival materials (Bland's own YouTube videos, transcripts) to simulate conversations that continue her advocacy and activism. Featured in [Huffington Post](http://www.huffingtonpost.com/entry/sandy-speaks-chatbot-american-artist_us_57e96fc0e4b0e28b2b557a12) (Sep 2016). One of the earliest works to confront AI voice synthesis as a site of racial politics.
- **[Black Gooey Universe](http://housing-art.info/black-gooey-universe)** (2018) — Solo exhibition at HOUSING, NY. Title refers to the Black interface underlying the white desktop GUI: the dark void that precedes the Apple Macintosh's white aesthetic. Proposes that whiteness in computing is not neutral but a specific design choice that encodes racial hierarchy. Reviewed in [Hyperallergic](https://hyperallergic.com/425881/american-artist-black-gooey-universe-housing/) and [Filthy Dreams](https://filthydreams.org/2018/02/09/blackness-as-the-original-technology-american-artists-black-gooey-universe/).
- **[I'm Blue (If I Was █████ I Would Die)](https://commonwealthandcouncil.com/exhibitions/im-blue-if-i-was-i-would-die)** (2019, 2021) — Solo exhibitions at Koenig & Clinton, NY (Mar–Apr 2019) and Commonwealth & Council, LA (Jun–Jul 2021). Title from a Eiffel 65 lyric. Investigates Blue Lives Matter ideology through the surveillance technologies police deploy, the history of police "blue wall of silence," and the legal mechanisms protecting officers from accountability. Connects technology to legal structures of racial violence.
- **[My Blue Window](https://queensmuseum.org/2019/02/american-artist)** (2019) — Solo exhibition at Queens Museum, Corona, NY (Oct 2019–Feb 2020). Immersive multimedia installation examining predictive policing algorithms: AI tools designed to dispatch officers to "high-risk" areas before incidents are reported. Reveals how collective racial bias is encoded into ostensibly neutral/scientific tools. Uses the Blue/Black Lives divide as the conceptual frame. Funded through the [Queens Museum/Jerome Foundation Fellowship for Emerging Artists](https://queensmuseum.org/program/qm-jerome-foundation-artist-fellowship/) (2018-19). Reviewed in [NYT](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/10/23/arts/design/american-artist-my-blue-window.html) and [Art in America](https://www.artnews.com/art-in-america/features/american-artist-queens-museum-predictive-policing-60221/).
- **[Looted](https://whitney.org/exhibitions/american-artist)** (2020) — Online commission for Whitney Museum website. Temporarily replaced all images on whitney.org with plywood textures; turned the background black and faded text — symbolically boarding up the museum and its website. Made during the 2020 protests following George Floyd's murder. Intervenes in debates about looting/protest, colonial museum collections, and who controls access to art during crisis. Reviewed in [Artforum](https://www.artforum.com/print/reviews/202008/american-artist-83987) and [Frieze](https://www.frieze.com/article/american-artist-boards-whitney).
- **[Shaper of God](https://www.redcat.org/events/shaper-god)** (2022–2025) — Major ongoing project. Started as solo exhibition at [REDCAT](https://www.redcat.org/), Los Angeles (May–Oct 2022). Inspired by Octavia E. Butler's Pasadena/Altadena life and her novels (especially *Parable of the Sower*, 1993). Draws title from Butler's fictional religious text. Maps the overlapping histories of Altadena: Butler, NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory (JPL), African-diasporic families migrating from South to West, and American Artist's own childhood. KADIST commissioned [a research machine version](https://kadist.org/program/shaper-of-god/) (online) — designed to trace connections between Butler's life, rocket science, and the Black Altadena diaspora. Expanded into major solo at [Pioneer Works](https://pioneerworks.org/exhibitions/american-artist-shaper-of-god) (Brooklyn, Jan–Apr 2025), with monograph published by Pioneer Works Press (Mar 2025), edited by Zainab Aliyu, with essays from Taylor Renee Aldridge, Lou Cornum, Tananarive Due, Alexis Pauline Gumbs, Ayana A. Jamieson, and Fred Moten.
- **[The Monophobic Response](https://www.lacma.org/art/exhibition/american-artist-monophobic-response)** (2024) — Two-channel film and sculptural installation at LACMA (Nov 1–4, 2024). Documents a re-creation of a pivotal 1936 static rocket engine test that initiated US space travel. Filmed in the Mojave Desert, summer 2024. Inspired by Butler's *Parable of the Sower* (set in 2024). Weaves together Earthseed (Butler's fictional religion), rocket history, and questions about who gets to enact "destiny." Part of Getty PST ART: Art & Science Collide initiative; supported by Hyundai Motor (The Hyundai Project: Art + Technology at LACMA).
- **[Dignity Images](https://www.moadsf.org/exhibition/dignity-images-bayview-hunters-point/)** (2019) — Solo at Museum of African Diaspora, San Francisco; also Lightbox Series at University of Toronto Mississauga (2021). Community photography project.
- **[Blue Life Seminar](https://www.moma.org/magazine/articles/755)** (2022) — Written for MoMA Magazine. Online essay/curriculum.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

American Artist moves in **three distinct circuits**:

1. **US institutional museums** — Guggenheim, Whitney, MoMA PS1, LACMA, Kunsthalle Basel, Nam June Paik Art Center (Seoul), Museum of Contemporary Art Chicago, Studio Museum in Harlem, Museum of African Diaspora. These are the white-cube, blue-chip venues that validate. Artist chooses them strategically and critiques them simultaneously (see: *Looted*, which boards up the Whitney).

2. **Critical art/theory spaces** — REDCAT (CalArts), Pioneer Works, Eyebeam, HOUSING, Miguel Abreu Gallery, Performance Space New York, Smack Mellon, Abrons Art Center, Recess, The Luminary (St. Louis), SAVVY Contemporary (Berlin), LC Queisser (Tbilisi). These are the experimental margins where the actual discourse happens.

3. **Academic and para-academic** — Vera List Center (New School), Goldsmiths London, UCSC, USC, SAIC, Yale (as faculty), Whitney ISP. The theory-discourse circuit. Artist operates as a scholar-practitioner who can speak at both the museum and the university.

The key signal: Artist chose **small, mission-driven** venues (HOUSING, Recess, Abrons) long before the major museums. They built credibility in the critical art community first. The major institution shows (Guggenheim, LACMA, MoMA PS1) came after 5+ years of sustained critical work. This is an inverse of the typical art-career ladder — politics first, prestige second.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy

**Single-claim amplification**: The entire practice runs on one claim — anti-Black racism is not incidental to technology but foundational to it. Every project is a new angle on the same argument: Sandy Speaks (voice AI and policing), My Blue Window (predictive algorithms), Black Gooey Universe (GUI design), Looted (colonial museum logic), Shaper of God (space race and Black diaspora). This is coherence through depth, not breadth.

**Speculative artifact method**: Works "resemble devices, tools, or video evidence of real world events but are clever fictions." The formal strategy is deliberately ambiguous — the viewer must decide if the thing is real. This *forces epistemic work* that the artist wants the viewer to do. It's not illustration of an argument; it's an epistemological trap.

**Name as foundation**: The legal name change is the most radical conceptual move — it makes the person the artwork. Everything that follows is commentary on a single gesture. This is a once-in-a-career move that defines a practice entirely.

### Career Architecture

**Theory-first, institution-second**: American Artist built credibility in critical art spaces (HOUSING, Recess, Eyebeam, Performance Space New York) and academic venues (Whitney ISP, Vera List, Goldsmiths) before appearing at Guggenheim and LACMA. The intellectual legitimacy preceded the institutional validation.

**Scholar-practitioner identity**: Multiple public conversations with Fred Moten, Simone Browne, Ruha Benjamin, Robin D.G. Kelley — the leading Black Studies scholars. This signals to the academic world that Artist operates at their level. Works in both registers (gallery and seminar room) without apologizing to either.

**Institutional bridging as critique**: *Looted* appears *on* the Whitney website while critiquing the Whitney. American Artist shows how to critique institutions from inside them — not naively, but as a structural move that uses the institution's platform to undermine its logic.

**SFPC as organizational infrastructure**: Co-directing SFPC allowed American Artist to shape a whole generation of artist-coders toward critical politics. Teaching is world-building; faculty positions are infrastructure. Yale School of Art is the amplification of this strategy.

### Methodology Signature

**Deep archival research**: *Shaper of God* involved research into Octavia Butler's papers (the Huntington Library archives), NASA's JPL history, Altadena community history, the 1936 rocket tests. The art is grounded in serious historical scholarship. This is what allows the speculative to feel rigorous.

**Community accountability**: Projects like *Dignity Images* (community photography in BayView-Hunters Point, San Francisco) involve the communities whose lives are at stake in the critique. Art as research-with, not research-on.

**Platform-specific intervention**: *Looted* only works as a Whitney website intervention. *Sandy Speaks* only works as a chatbot. American Artist chooses the medium based on which platform they want to critique — the medium IS the critique.

### Key Moves

1. **Name change (2013)**: Established a practice foundation that is: legally committed (can't be undone without cost), conceptually rich (two contradictory readings), practically productive (generates a body of work). Made everything that followed coherent.
2. **Whitney ISP (2016-17)**: Access to the US critical art theory canon and the ISP alumni network. This is where Artist became fluent in the theoretical language that connects to Moten, Browne, etc.
3. **Sandy Speaks (2016)**: First major work to receive mainstream attention (Huffington Post, 2016). Showed that chatbot AI could be a form of political art. Established the "speculative artifact" method before it had a name.
4. **Black Gooey Universe (2018)**: The title became a concept. "The black gooey universe" is now a recognized critical framework for analyzing race in computing. Naming something is world-building.
5. **My Blue Window / Queens Museum Jerome Fellowship (2018-19)**: First major institutional solo. The NYT review broke into broader public consciousness. The predictive policing focus arrived exactly as this technology was becoming a public controversy.
6. **Looted / Whitney Commission (2020)**: Major institutional legitimacy arrived at the exact political moment (post-Floyd, 2020) when the work became impossible to ignore. Artist had been building toward this for seven years.
7. **LACMA Art+Tech Grant (2021) + Shaper of God REDCAT (2022) + Monophobic Response LACMA (2024)**: The West Coast pivot toward LA's Black Altadena history and Octavia Butler. The *Shaper of God* series is a long-term project that deepens over time — not a new idea but a sustained excavation.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **The single-claim discipline**: Run every project on one master claim. Don't diversify ideas — deepen one. American Artist has done more interesting things with "anti-Black racism in technology" than most artists do with ten separate topics. Amber's machine behavior + cyborg sociology work needs its equivalent core claim.

2. **Platform-as-critique methodology**: Don't just make art *about* technology — make art that *acts through* the technology being critiqued. *Looted* works because it's a Whitney website intervention. What would Amber's equivalent be? Code that runs on the platforms it critiques?

3. **The speculative artifact**: Make things that make people ask "is this real?" Ambiguity about the ontological status of the artifact is a feature, not a bug. Amber's work on machine death could produce "artifacts" of machine death that resist easy categorization.

4. **Scholar-network as career infrastructure**: American Artist's visibility at Simone Browne, Ruha Benjamin, Fred Moten, Robin D.G. Kelley conversations is strategic. Being seen conversing with the scholars signals intellectual equal footing. Amber should seek similar conversations with key figures in machine behavior, ALife, and critical AI.

5. **Long-form projects (not one-shots)**: *Shaper of God* began in 2021 (online Kadist), became a solo in 2022 (REDCAT), then a film in 2024 (LACMA), then a major solo + monograph in 2025 (Pioneer Works). Seven years on one project, deepening rather than moving on. Amber's work on machine death should take this long-form approach.
