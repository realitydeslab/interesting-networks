# Kimchi and Chips 김치앤칩스

Duo of **Mimi Son** (Korean) and **Elliot Woods** (British), founded Seoul 2009. Create large-scale public installations that make images float in air without screens — using millions of calibrated light beams merged in haze, sunlight reflected through robotics, and laser projectors powered by solar panels. Their unifying concept is *"Drawing in the Air"*: materializing images from immateriality. Pioneers in volumetric light field projection. Prolific open-source contributors (200+ code libraries on GitHub).

## Background

**Mimi Son** (KR): Born Seoul. Studied Digital Media Art at [Middlesex University London](https://www.mdx.ac.uk/) and Interaction Design + Design Research at [CIID Copenhagen](https://ciid.dk/) (Copenhagen Institute of Interaction Design). An obsession with geometry and Buddhist philosophy drives her practice — exploring existence, substance, and relationship. Has worked as designer, professor, storyteller, curator, and artistic director across multiple countries.

**Elliot Woods** (UK): From Manchester. MSc from [School of Physics and Astronomy, University of Manchester](https://www.physics.manchester.ac.uk/), with thesis on neural networks. Entered the arts through co-founding Kimchi and Chips. Brings computer vision, robotics, optics, structural engineering, machine learning, electronics, and real-time graphics to the practice. Active contributor to [openFrameworks](https://openframeworks.cc/) and [VVVV](https://vvvv.org/) creative coding platforms; has released [100+ open source libraries on GitHub](https://github.com/elliotwoods).

**The studio** was founded 2009 in Seoul and operates from there. Their collaborative model is rooted in recognizing that art, science, and philosophy are "not distinct disciplines, but alternative ways of mapping the same terrain." They personally design all technical aspects: code, electromechanics, structural engineering, optical systems, electronics, AI, etc.

**Core Philosophy:** The *"Drawing in the Air"* series (their entire output since 2009) combines relativistic physics (the Light Barrier — Einstein's law separating light from matter) with Buddhist philosophy of duration and impermanence, to investigate the problematics of images in contemporary society. Key motifs: the **circle** (birth/death/rebirth), the **moon** (reflection of another reality), **material vs. immaterial**, **presence vs. absence**. Technology is never autonomous but always *collaborating* with nature — weather, sunlight, wind.

## Key Works

### "Drawing in the Air" series (chronological)

**[Link](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/link/)** (2010) — First studio work.

**[Lit Tree](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/littree/)** (2011) — Early work.

**[Assembly](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/assembly/)** (2012) — Early installation.

**[Line Segments Space](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/linesegmentsspace/)** (2013) — Architectural web of nylon threads spanning a gallery; projections map onto the threads to create 2D imagery in 3D space. Inspired by Picasso's light painting and Gego's *Reticuláreas*. Shown: Seoul Art Space Geumcheon (2013), Whanki Museum Seoul (2014), Scopitone Festival Nantes (2014), QUAD Gallery Derby UK (2017).

**[483 Lines](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/483linesfirstedition/)** (2014, First Edition) — 483 Kevlar strings (one per NTSC TV scan line) stretched 16 meters; video imagery mapped onto the physical strings. Shown: Jeju Museum of Art. *Second Edition* (2016): [Asia Culture Center](https://www.acc.go.kr/en/) Gwangju.

**[Light Barrier](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/lightbarrier/)** (2014) — **World's first volumetric image created via light field projection within a scattering medium** (haze). Floating graphic objects animate through space. Co-commissioned by [FutureEverything](https://futureeverything.org/) (Manchester) and the British Council. Premiered at New Media Night Festival, Nikola-Lenivets, Russia, June 2014.

*Light Barrier Second Edition* (2015) — Further development.

**[Light Barrier Third Edition](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/lightbarrierthirdedition/)** (2016) — Asia Culture Center, Gwangju. 8 architectural projectors → 630 sub-projectors via computationally-designed concave mirrors → 16,000,000 individually calibrated pixels drawn into haze. 42-channel sound field. 6-minute sequence on birth, death, rebirth through the circle motif. Enabled by *Rulr* (their own open source calibration toolkit).

**[Lunar Surface](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/lunarsurface/)** (2014–2015) — B39 art space, Bucheon (decommissioned city incinerator). A 20-meter silk flag blown by wind in the cavernous space; 3D camera tracks the flag; projector renders cross-sections of a virtual sphere onto the fabric in real time. Long-exposure photography captures the moon being "born" from wind and light. Collaboration with photographer **Eunyoung Kim**. Begins the moon motif (from Haruki Murakami's *1Q84*).

**[Obscurities](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/obscurities/)** (2017) — Short film (1m 41s) animated at sub-millimeter scale, filmed with a microscope. No digital effects. Music contributed by **Murcof**. Explores *dépaysement* — uncanny images of things outside ordinary usage.

**[Halo](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/halo/)** (2018) — **Landmark work.** 99 robotic mirrors track the sun across the sky; beams of reflected sunlight merge in a cloud of water mist to draw a glowing circle in the air. Dimensions: 18.5m × 4.8m × 8.1m. Navigation system uses Bayesian inference ML developed with [Improbable Worlds Limited](https://improbable.io/) (London). First use of natural light as primary material — the work is *completed by nature* (only visible when sun, wind, water, and technology align). Explores "technology to capture what is out of reach." Shown: **Somerset House**, Edmond J. Safra Fountain Courtyard, London (June 2018, Summer Solstice); **MMCA** (National Museum of Modern and Contemporary Art), Seoul (2021).

**[Difference and Repetition](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/differenceandrepetition/)** (2019) — Permanent kinetic installation, Korea.

**[Collective Behaviour](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/collectivebehaviour/)** (2019) — Contemporary dance performance, 40 min. Mirrors, half-mirrors, mylar, motorised winches, haze intervene light to manipulate space, time, and dancer identity. Explores individual vs. collective identity; mirrors duplicate, isolate, and stretch the dancers. Collaboration with **Danish choreographer Simone Wierød**. Premiered [KPH Volume](https://kph.dk/), Copenhagen, Dec 18–19, 2019. Later: Sogang Mary Hall Main Theater, Seoul (2021); Sejong Art Center S-Theatre (2022). Supported by ARKO (Arts Council Korea), STATENS KUNSTFOND (Danish), BIKUBENFONDEN, Københavnermærket, Seoul Dance Center.

**[Every Colour Is Made of Every Other](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/everycolourismadeofeveryother/)** (2020) — Permanent kinetic installation, Lee Seok Young New Media Library, Korea.

**[Another Moon](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/anothermoon/)** (2021) — **Pinnacle of the "Drawing in the Air" series, 6 years in development.** 40 custom laser projector modules, solar-powered. During the day, panels charge batteries. At night, lasers shoot upward; overlapping cones form a 3D sphere (a moon) visible from up to 1km. As solar energy is depleted, lasers extinguish one by one through the night — *the duration of the moon is determined by the solar irradiance of that day*. An energy ethics artwork: proposes living with "fast and slow energy" like internet bandwidth rather than on-demand fossil fuels. Developed with [LaserAnimation Sollinger](https://www.laseranimation.com/) (DE). Commissioned by RAG Stiftung (German coal industry foundation) and Arts Council Korea. Premiered at **NEWNOW Festival**, Zeche Zollverein (historically Europe's most productive coal mine), Essen, Germany (2021). Shown: **PLASMATA Festival**, Pedion tou Areos, Athens, Greece (2022).

**[Optical Rail](https://kimchiandchips.com/works/opticalrail/)** (2021) — Permanent kinetic installation, U Planet Gwangmyeong, Korea.

**[Cyclops Retina](https://beyondresolution.info/Cyclops-Retina)** (2023) — Collaboration with **[[Rosa Menkman]]** at **The Beams**, London, commissioned for *Thin Air* festival. Menkman's narrative about a Cyclops-god and sacrificed vision; Kimchi and Chips provide the light installation infrastructure. Millions of beams of light merged in haze to create floating graphics in the air.

## Open Source Contributions

Elliot Woods has released [100+ open source libraries on GitHub](https://github.com/elliotwoods), with the studio collectively releasing **200+ code libraries** adopted by practitioners worldwide and integrated into popular creative tools.

**[Rulr](https://github.com/elliotwoods/ofxRulr)** — Their flagship open source tool. "An application and high-level framework for calibrating spaces with devices. Built on openFrameworks." Used in production for *Light Barrier Third Edition*, *Another Moon*, and other major works. It solves the core technical problem of their practice: how to align multiple projectors/lasers/mirrors so their beams converge at a precise point in space.

This open-source commitment is a form of **artistic ethics** — they share the technical knowledge that makes their world-first installations possible.

Kimchi and Chips operate across **three primary circuits**:

1. **Korean institutional circuit** — MMCA Seoul, Asia Culture Center Gwangju, Jeju Museum of Art, Lee Seok Young New Media Library, Sejong Center, permanent commissions. They are genuinely embedded in Korea's contemporary art infrastructure, not tourists. This is their home base.

2. **European media art circuit** — Ars Electronica (Linz), ZKM (Karlsruhe), Somerset House (London), NEWNOW/Zeche Zollverein (Essen), PLASMATA (Athens), Resonate (Belgrade), Scopitone (Nantes), QUAD (Derby). 3. **Anglo-American art-tech** — SXSW, British Council commissions, FutureEverything (Manchester). Less prominent than the Korea/Europe circuits.

**Pattern:** They systematically choose venues where architecture or environment creates the conditions for their work — Zeche Zollverein (coal mine = energy artwork's conceptual home), Somerset House courtyard (enclosed square = natural wind + light + public gathering). The site is always a collaborator.

**Notable small venues:** QUAD Gallery Derby (UK), Nikola-Lenivets (rural Russia), Scopitone Nantes — early choices that signal willingness to work outside prestige institutions.

## Why Interesting

Kimchi and Chips sit at a fascinating intersection for Amber:

**More-than-human design, materialized.** Their practice literally enacts what more-than-human design theorists describe abstractly: the work is unfinished without nature (sun, wind, humidity). The installation cannot exist without these non-human collaborators. *Another Moon* is specifically structured so that nature determines its duration — no human operator switches it off. This is one of the most rigorous artistic implementations of more-than-human philosophy in contemporary art.

**Light as the medium of perceptual research.** Their "Drawing in the Air" concept asks: what is an image? Where does it exist? When 16 million pixels are individually calibrated into a haze cloud, where is the "screen"? This is closely related to Rosa Menkman's resolution studies — the questioning of what infrastructure mediates perception. Both practices reveal the normally-invisible systems of image production.

**Physics → Philosophy pipeline.** Elliot's physics background (Manchester MSc, neural networks thesis) combined with Mimi's Buddhist philosophy creates a rare synthesis. The "Light Barrier" is Einstein's physical law repurposed as artistic concept. This model — taking a scientific concept literally and building an artwork from it — is exemplary.

**Open source as politics.** 200+ libraries released freely; Rulr enables other practitioners globally. This is a form of infrastructure commons that aligns with Amber's interest in participatory design and knowledge sharing.

**Connection to [[Rosa Menkman]]:** The *Cyclops Retina* collaboration is significant. Both practitioners are working on the "impossible image" — images that shouldn't exist by conventional standards of mediation. This is a live collaboration circuit worth watching.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy
**Literalizing physics as philosophy.** Their signature move: take a physics concept (the Light Barrier — Einstein's law that matter cannot move at the speed of light) and build an artwork that *embodies* that concept materially. This is not "art inspired by science" but art that *is* the scientific concept made visible. The circle motif (no beginning, no end) parallels Buddhist impermanence while also describing the topology of the light field. Two frameworks, one geometry.

**Duration as material.** Influenced by Buddhist philosophy and Bergsonist duration-thinking: time is not a container but a material. *Another Moon* makes this physical — the moon's duration is literally the energy stored that day. *Lunar Surface* uses long-exposure photography to extrude time into space. Every work is about how long something exists and what determines that.

**The "world's first" claim as conceptual anchor.** The Light Barrier (2014) was documented as the world's first volumetric image via light field projection in a scattering medium. This claim is both technical and philosophical — it marks a new category of image-making. Building their entire practice around this conceptual breakthrough (and deepening it through Another Moon) is exemplary field-declaration strategy.

### Career Architecture
**Korea-embedded, internationally circuiting.** Unlike many Korean artists who move to New York or Berlin for legitimacy, Kimchi and Chips are genuinely Seoul-based with deep Korean institutional relationships (MMCA, ACC, ARKO, permanent commissions). They use European circuits (Ars Electronica, ZKM, Somerset House) as amplifiers, not primary stages. This is a strategic choice that preserves identity and funding relationships.

**Permanent installations as infrastructure.** Multiple permanent commissions in Korea (*Optical Rail*, *Every Colour Is Made of Every Other*, *Difference and Repetition*) create a durable presence in institutional space. Unlike temporary exhibitions, these generate ongoing visibility and relationship with institutions.

**Open source as reputation capital.** Releasing Rulr and 200+ libraries makes their technical achievements legible and replicable to peers worldwide. This generates community belonging (openFrameworks, VVVV) and positions them as infrastructure-builders, not just artists.

### Methodology Signature
**Technology designed for one work, released as commons.** For each major work, they develop novel technical systems (custom laser projectors with LaserAnimation Sollinger; Bayesian inference with Improbable Worlds; Rulr calibration framework). Then they release the tools publicly. The artwork *generates* open-source infrastructure.

**Nature as co-author, not obstacle.** Radical methodological commitment: the unpredictability of weather, sun, and wind is baked into the work's ontology. *Halo* only appears when all conditions align. This is the opposite of engineering's usual approach (eliminate variance). It's a practice philosophy.

**Cross-disciplinary technical fluency.** Physics, ML, robotics, optics, structural engineering, electronics — all in-house. This breadth allows them to make things that specialists would say are impossible, because no single specialist domain can authorize the whole system.

### Key Moves (Chronological)
1. **2009 — Found studio in Seoul** (not London/Berlin/NYC): Anchors in Korean art infrastructure while maintaining European connectivity through Elliot's British origin. Dual-circuit access from the start.
2. **2014 — Light Barrier "world's first"**: Establishes a technical + conceptual claim that legitimizes the studio internationally. Co-commissioned with FutureEverything + British Council — two institutional validators.
3. **2014–16 — Three editions of Light Barrier**: Iterates the concept publicly across three major institutional contexts. Editions signal R&D practice, not one-off spectacle.
4. **2016 — Rulr released open source**: Converts technical knowledge into community capital. Places them at the center of spatial calibration practice globally.
5. **2018 — Halo at Somerset House**: Major UK public stage; natural light as material is a conceptual leap from artificial light. ML collaboration with Improbable Worlds brings new institutional relationship.
6. **2021 — Another Moon at Zeche Zollverein**: Energy ethics artwork premiered at a former coal mine. The most conceptually complete statement of technology-nature collaboration. 6-year development timeline shows sustained commitment.
7. **2023 — Cyclops Retina with Rosa Menkman**: Cross-practice collaboration with one of the most intellectually rigorous artists working on image infrastructure. Signals entry into a critical-theoretical discourse that their previous work engaged aesthetically but not discursively.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **Claim the "world's first."** Kimchi and Chips built their entire international reputation on one documented technical first (2014). Amber has potential "world's first" claims in MR spatial computing (HoloKit). Document them aggressively with institutional validation (co-commissioning bodies, press, conference papers).
2. **Build tools through artworks, release as commons.** Rulr was built to make art, then released publicly. This is a more sustainable model than building tools for tools' sake — the art justifies the engineering, and the engineering outlives the art. Amber could apply this to HoloKit's software stack.
3. **Choose institutional partners for their conceptual resonance.** RAG Stiftung (coal heritage) commissioning *Another Moon* (renewable energy artwork) is perfect conceptual fit. Amber should seek commissioners who *are* the subject of the work — institutions whose identity the work addresses.
4. **Korea-embedded + internationally circuiting = sustainable.** Strong home base + selective international appearances is more durable than chasing global nomadism. Amber has Oxford + London + Shanghai + Cambridge — use these as the base, treat international venues as amplifiers.
5. **Duration-awareness.** Every work asks: how long does this exist, and what determines that? This is both a design question and a philosophical one. Amber's interest in machine mortality and agent death connects directly.
