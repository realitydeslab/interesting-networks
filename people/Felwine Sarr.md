# Felwine Sarr

Senegalese economist, philosopher, musician, and writer. Anne-Marie Bryan Distinguished Professor of French and Francophone Studies at [Duke University](https://duke.edu). Co-founder with [[Achille Mbembe]] of [Les Ateliers de la Pensée](https://www.lesateliersdelapensee.com/) — the premier intellectual forum for African critical thought. Co-author of the [Sarr-Savoy Report](https://web.archive.org/web/20210815041636/http://restitutionreport2018.com/) (2018), which triggered Europe-wide debates on restitution of African cultural heritage. Author of *Afrotopia* (2016), now one of the canonical texts of African political philosophy. Also a musician (three albums) and poet. Embodies the polymathic African intellectual who thinks from within the continent's epistemic resources rather than catching up to Western frameworks.

## Background
Born 11 September 1972 in Sine Saloum (Niodior Arrondissement), Senegal. Grew up in a musical family — eight siblings, six of whom are musicians (the family is one of Senegal's most culturally prolific: see siblings Sahad Sarr [Sahad and the Nataal Patchwork], Saliou/Alibeta, Djibril/Majnun, Ngnima/Tie, Youssoupha/Rhapsod). Music and philosophy are not separate projects for Sarr — they are the same impulse.

Completed primary and secondary education in Senegal, then moved to France for his **agrégation in economics**. This dual formation — West African intellectual tradition + French academic rigor — is the source of his ability to engage European frameworks while refusing their universalist claims.

Returned to Senegal and rose to become:
- **Dean, Faculty of Economics & Management**, [Gaston Berger University](https://www.ugb.edu.sn/), Saint-Louis (2011)
- **Head, Faculty of Civilizations, Religions, Arts and Communication (CRAC)**, Gaston Berger — new interdisciplinary faculty he built from scratch
- **Co-founder, LASPAD** (Laboratory for the Analysis of Societies and Powers/Africa-Diasporas), Saint-Louis — research infrastructure for African social science

In 2020, left Senegal for Duke University as Anne-Marie Bryan Distinguished Professor in the Department of Romance Studies — a strategic move to the global academic stage without abandoning African intellectual commitments.

## Key Works

### Philosophy & Essay
- [*Afrotopia*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517905491/afrotopia/) (Philippe Rey, 2016; English: University of Minnesota Press, 2019, tr. Drew S. Burk) — **the major work**. Argues that Africa must not catch up with Western modernity but invent its own future by activating its own epistemic resources. "Africa is not behind; it is elsewhere." Grand Prix of Literary Associations (Research Category) 2016. Now canonical in African political philosophy.
- [*Habiter le monde*](https://www.memoiredencrier.com/habiter-le-monde/) (Mémoire d'Encrier, 2018) — meditative essay on dwelling, belonging, and being-in-the-world. Quieter than Afrotopia; more phenomenological.

### Policy & Report

Proposes full restitution of African cultural objects held in French state museums (estimated 90,000+ objects). Became globally influential, triggering policy debates in France, Germany, Belgium, Netherlands, UK. Translated into multiple languages. This is theory → policy in one document.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Framework inversion** — rather than applying existing frameworks to Africa, Sarr *inverts the direction of universalism*. In *Afrotopia*, he doesn't argue that African modernity failed. He argues that modernity itself is the wrong frame. This is a higher-order move: critique the meta-level, don't play the object-level game. Applicable to Amber: don't do "AI ethics for African contexts" — ask what a non-Western AI epistemology looks like.

**Concept coining with embedded politics** — "Afrotopia" is memorable because it fuses utopia (European tradition) with Afro (positioning). It's not "African utopia" (descriptive) but "Afrotopia" (constitutive). The word does the work. Similarly, the Sarr-Savoy Report's subtitle "Toward a New Relational Ethics" reframes restitution from a legal/property question to an ontological one. Amber: how do your project names do philosophical work?

**The meditative essay as genre** — *Dahij*, *Habiter le monde*, *Lieux qu'habitent mes rêves*, *La Saveur des derniers mètres* are all a specific genre: not philosophical argument, not fiction, but lyrical philosophical meditation. This genre is underused in CS/AI research but is increasingly influential (cf. Robin Wall Kimmerer, Édouard Glissant). Sarr uses it to think at the edge of what systematic argument can reach.

### Career Architecture

**Build infrastructure BEFORE going global.** Sarr spent 15+ years in Senegal (Gaston Berger University 1999-2020), building LASPAD, Jimsaan, the CRAC faculty, and launching Ateliers de la Pensée, before moving to Duke in 2020. When he arrived in the US, he arrived as the builder of Africa's premier intellectual forum — not as an individual scholar seeking a platform. **The institution gave him power the individual paper never could.**

**Strategic co-authorship for impact.** The Sarr-Savoy Report worked because it paired an African philosopher (Sarr, providing legitimacy vis-à-vis African stakeholders + philosophical framework) with a French art historian (Savoy, providing insider credibility + institutional European art-world knowledge). Neither could have produced a document with the same political traction. Sarr chose Savoy specifically because they were different kinds of experts.

**The dual career as multiplication.** Music + philosophy + policy + institution building is not dilution — it's cross-pollination. Each domain feeds the others: the music gives him access to cultural spaces that philosophy doesn't reach; the philosophy gives depth to the music; the policy work gives him political relationships that amplify both. Amber's HoloKit + research + art connections work the same way.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **1999-2011:** Built an academic base in Senegal. Didn't immediately seek European or American tenure. Established himself as credible in Senegal first — dean at 39. *Why it worked:* gave him authority over his own intellectual infrastructure rather than being a guest in someone else's.

2. **2009-2012:** Published first literary and philosophical works (*Dahij*, *105 Rue Carnot*, *Méditations africaines*) in Mémoire d'Encrier (Montreal publisher for Francophone African/diaspora literature). *Why it worked:* positioned him in the African literary field, not just economics — broadened his audience and identity.

3. **2012-2014: Co-founded Jimsaan**, the first Senegalese-owned literary publisher with international distribution. *Why it worked:* gave Senegalese writers a platform without going through Parisian gatekeepers. Infrastructure as intellectual sovereignty.

4. **2016: Published *Afrotopia* with Philippe Rey** (Paris), launched in same year as Les Ateliers de la Pensée. The book and the forum amplified each other: the book gave the forum a philosophical frame; the forum gave the book a community. *Why it worked:* the simultaneous launch was strategic, not coincidental.

5. **2018: Delivered the Sarr-Savoy Report.** Not a conventional academic publication — a commissioned government report. *Why it worked:* he used the Macron commission to do something no academic paper could: change policy. The choice to make the report academically rigorous (252 pages, full philosophical framework) rather than a brief gave it intellectual permanence beyond its immediate political context.

6. **2020: TIME 100 + ArtReview Power 100 + Move to Duke.** Three simultaneous global-stage arrivals. *Why it worked:* the restitution debate had made him famous in the art world (hence ArtReview #3); the policy impact had made him famous more broadly (TIME 100). 7. **2020-present: Duke base + continued African engagement.** Didn't abandon Senegal for the American academy. Maintains OSIWA vice-chairmanship, Ateliers de la Pensée co-direction, Jimsaan connection. *Why it works:* keeps African intellectual authority intact even while using Duke as a global platform.

### What Amber Can Learn

- **Build the forum before you need it.** Les Ateliers de la Pensée ran for years before the restitution report; when Sarr needed a platform for restitution debates, it was already there. Amber should build intellectual forums *now* that she'll use when her research matures.
- **Commission strategy.** Sarr showed that a commissioned report can be a philosophical text. Amber should think about who could commission a "report" on machine behavior / AI ethics that would let her do rigorous research with policy impact.
- **Co-author across disciplines for maximum impact.** The Sarr-Savoy model — philosopher + art historian → policy document — is replicable. Amber + legal scholar? Amber + anthropologist? The cross-disciplinary co-authorship opens new doors.
- **Name the project, not just the research.** "Afrotopia" is a concept that travels. "Sarr-Savoy Report" is a document that travels. Neither is a paper title. Amber's most impactful work may need project-level names, not paper-level names.
