# Cuauhtémoc Medina

Mexican curator, art historian, and critic. Senior researcher (Investigador Titular B) at the [Instituto de Investigaciones Estéticas (IIE)](https://www.esteticas.unam.mx/), UNAM, where he has been based since 1993. Founding Chief Curator of [[MUAC]] (Museo Universitario Arte Contemporáneo), UNAM — Mexico's preeminent contemporary art institution, opened 2008. Chief Curator of the **12th Shanghai Biennale "Proregress"** (2018–2019) and the **9th Manifesta "The Deep of the Modern"** (Genk, Belgium, 2012). One of the most intellectually rigorous voices in global curating — his practice sits at the intersection of Marxist-inflected art criticism, postcolonial theory, and the political economy of the contemporary art world.

## Background

**Education:** Trained in history at UNAM's Facultad de Filosofía y Letras (licenciatura), then moved to the UK for a doctorate in art history and theory at the **[University of Essex](https://www.essex.ac.uk/)** — the Essex MA/PhD programme is one of the key transmission belts for critical theory into art history (Marxist aesthetics, Screen theory, institutional critique, feminist art history). This dual training — deep in Mexican national history, then sharpened by British critical theory — is foundational to his method.

**Academic trajectory:** Joined IIE-UNAM in 1993, quickly becoming its primary voice on contemporary art. The IIE is the most prestigious institute for art historical research in Mexico, traditionally focused on colonial and pre-Columbian art — Medina's appointment represented a deliberate move to integrate contemporary practice into serious historical scholarship.

**MUAC:** When UNAM inaugurated MUAC on November 26, 2008, Medina was installed as its founding Chief Curator — a position that gave him a permanent institutional base from which to develop an ambitious program linking Mexican, Latin American, and global contemporary art. Unlike commercial galleries or private foundations, MUAC's university mandate allowed Medina to take on ambitious, politically charged projects.

**Intellectual formation:** His conceptual toolkit draws from:
- British cultural studies and Marxist art history (Essex tradition: T.J. Clark, John Tagg)
- Latin American dependency theory and postcolonialism
- The Mexican muralist tradition as a historical problem (not a model)
- The "post-Mexico City" art scene of the 1990s–2000s: [[Francis Alÿs]], [[Teresa Margolles]], [[Carlos Amorales]], [[Abraham Cruzvillegas]], [[Damián Ortega]], Minerva Cuevas, Yoshua Okón, Melanie Smith
- The international biennial circuit and its structural inequalities

## Curated Exhibitions & Projects

### Biennials & International Projects

**[12th Shanghai Biennale: "Proregress — Art in an Age of Historical Ambivalence"](https://www.powerstationofart.com/en/)** ([[Power Station of Art]], Shanghai, 2018–2019)
- Chief Curator. PSA, Shanghai's flagship contemporary art museum.
- Title from e.e. cummings' portmanteau "proregress" (progress + regress) to name the dialectical contradiction of modernity: neither pure advancement nor simple decline.
- One of the most intellectually ambitious Shanghai Biennale editions; navigated the constraints of a Chinese state institution while maintaining critical edge.
- Artists included internationally-known figures from Latin America, Europe, Asia, and the USA.
- See [[Power Station of Art]] for full context.

**[Manifesta 9: European Biennial of Contemporary Art — "The Deep of the Modern"](https://manifesta.org/)** (Genk, Limburg, Belgium, June 2–September 30, 2012)
- Chief Curator.
- Site: former Waterschei coal mine in Genk — a region in post-industrial transition after coal mining collapsed.
- Theme: the mine as both literal and metaphorical "underground" of modernity — capitalism's material unconscious, the deep infrastructure that industrial society was built upon but tries to forget.
- One of the most theoretically sophisticated Manifesta editions; Medina's use of an industrial heritage site transformed the biennial format into an archaeological excavation of capitalism.
- Established his international reputation as a biennale curator of the first order.

**Teresa Margolles: "¿De qué otra cosa podríamos hablar?" ("What Else Could We Speak About?")** (Mexican Pavilion, 53rd Venice Biennale, Venice, Italy, 2009)
- Curator.
- [[Teresa Margolles]]' presentation using materials soaked in water used to wash corpses of drug-war victims in Mexico.
- One of the most critically celebrated national pavilion presentations in Venice Biennale history — widely discussed as a model for politically engaged art.
- The work turned the pavilion into a space saturated with forensic residue of narco-violence; visitors walked on water-soaked cloths that had cleaned murder victims' bodies.
- Medina's curatorial framing positioned the work as a political statement on behalf of Mexican civil society during the intensifying Drug War.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Medina operates in **three interlocking circuits**:

### 1. Mexico City Academic-Institutional Circuit
**MUAC/UNAM → IIE-UNAM → El Colegio Nacional → UANL**
This is his primary base. Mexico City is where he does his most sustained work — building MUAC's collection, programming multi-year exhibition series, conducting academic research at IIE. Teaching at Ceiida/UANL extends his reach to Monterrey's growing art scene.

### 2. European Biennial Circuit
**Manifesta 9 (Genk, 2012) → Venice Biennale (2009) → Tate Latin America advisory (London) → Royal College of Art (London) → Bard CCS (New York)**
His European circuit runs through the biennial system (Manifesta, Venice) and curatorial education (RCA, Bard). This gives him standing in the international curatorial field while maintaining Mexico City as his home base. He moves between these circuits but doesn't relocate — a strategic choice that preserves his distinctiveness as a non-Western curator while engaging the Western institution.

### 3. China-Latin America Axis
**MUAC Mexico City → Power Station of Art Shanghai (2018–19)**
The 12th Shanghai Biennale created a specific circuit connecting his Mexico City base to Chinese contemporary art institutions — a South-South axis that he explicitly theorized through the "Proregress" concept. This positions him at a moment when the art world's geopolitical center of gravity was shifting.

## Theoretical Concepts & Ideas

Medina has developed several key concepts that have become widely cited:

### "Contemp(t)orary"
His most influential intervention in art theory: the neologism combining "contemporary" and "contempt." Published in e-flux Journal #12 (2010). The argument: the art world's use of "contemporary" as a global descriptor hides a temporal hierarchy — the "West and the rest" remains operative under the guise of simultaneity. To be "contemporary" in art world terms means to be legible to a metropolitan critical framework, which requires a form of structural contempt for other temporalities, other modernities. This critique of the "contemporary" as a hegemonic category resonates with debates in postcolonial theory (Dipesh Chakrabarty's "provincializing Europe").

### "Proregress"
From e.e. cummings' portmanteau: the 12th Shanghai Biennale's title named the dialectical logic of historical time under capitalism — neither pure progress nor pure regression, but both simultaneously. In an era of climate crisis, technological acceleration, and rising authoritarianism, the 20th century's confidence in historical progress seems naive, but simple declension is equally false. "Proregress" names the productive contradiction.

### "Arte Postmexicano" (Post-Mexican Art)
His term for the generation of Mexican artists (Alÿs, Orozco, Margolles, Amorales, Cruzvillegas, Ortega, Cuevas) who emerged in the 1990s and circulate globally while maintaining an ambivalent relationship to Mexican national identity. Not "Mexican art" in the nationalist sense, but not simply "international" either — the "post" marks a complex relationship to place, nation, and the global art market. The collection *Abuso mutuo* (2017) gathers his writings on this formation.

### "The Deep of the Modern"
Manifesta 9's subtitle: the mine as underground capitalism, the hidden infrastructure of industrialization. Modernity's self-image is surface (cities, technology, culture); Medina turned a former coal mine into a space that made visible the material foundations that modernity buries. The move from "contemporary" to "modern" in this framing is significant — he wanted to connect present post-industrial conditions back to the longer history of capitalist extraction.

### The Age of Discrepancies
*La era de la discrepancia* title: discrepancy as the operative logic of Mexican modernism, which never produced the coherent avant-garde narrative that French or US art history does — instead, it produced competing claims, contradictory impulses, simultaneous traditional and experimental tendencies. Discrepancy (rather than synthesis) as the generative principle.

## Why Interesting

Medina is interesting to Amber for several reasons:

**The Non-Western Theory Problem**: His "Contemp(t)orary" essay is one of the clearest articulations of how global contemporary art encodes Western temporal authority. This is structurally analogous to how "AI" as a category encodes Western technological authority — a critique that Amber's work on machine behavior, critical computing, and cosmotechnics is positioned to make.

**Institutional Model**: MUAC as a public university museum doing intellectually rigorous political art is a rare institutional form. As a model for "art + ideas" institutions, it's relevant to Amber's thinking about what kinds of institutions can sustain research-based art practice.

**Political Art Curating**: He's developed the most theoretically sophisticated framework for thinking about art that directly engages political violence (Teresa Margolles), labor (Manifesta 9), and historical time (Proregress). Amber's interest in AI and power structures connects here.

**Mexico City Scene**: The post-Mexican art scene he theorized (Alÿs, Margolles, Orozco, etc.) is one of the few non-Western contemporary art formations that has achieved genuine international theoretical influence — not just market success. Understanding how that happened is relevant to thinking about non-Western AI art.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Two-word concept coining**: "Contemp(t)orary" — the neologism as conceptual claim. He takes a word everyone uses (contemporary) and cracks it open by revealing the latent word inside (contempt). This is a classic move of immanent critique — the problem is already hidden in the language we use uncritically. "Proregress" (from cummings) is another instance: finding in an obscure poetic portmanteau a concept more adequate to historical reality than the available vocabulary.

**Provocation-first framing**: The Venice Biennale 2009 curation of Teresa Margolles is an example of his provocation-first approach — putting work that includes human corpse-washing fluids in a diplomatic/national context forces everyone to confront what the art world is for. The formal institutional setting (Venice Biennale Mexican Pavilion) amplifies the political charge.

**Framework multiplication**: He combines Marxist art history (the Essex tradition) × postcolonial critique × specific site histories (coal mines, drug war Mexico) to produce interpretations that are simultaneously local and structural.

### Career Architecture

**Home-base internationalism**: He didn't migrate to New York or London. He built international significance from Mexico City, using the IIE-UNAM as his scholarly anchor and MUAC as his curatorial platform. This is a different strategy from most internationally prominent curators — it gives him a stable position from which to be selectively international rather than continuously itinerant.

**Field declaration**: By coining "arte postmexicano" and editing *La era de la discrepancia*, he effectively named and defined the field he was already operating in. The anthology (Abuso mutuo) collected 20 years of essays retroactively constituting a coherent critical project.

**Coalition writing**: The *Francis Alÿs* Phaidon monograph, co-written with Ferguson, Fisher, and Taussig, performs institutional coalition — a Latin American curator working alongside a major London publisher, with a New York critic (Ferguson), a British art theorist (Fisher), and an American anthropologist (Taussig). The choice of Taussig is significant: anthropological theory (rather than art history alone) frames the work.

**Biennial positioning**: His two major biennial projects (Manifesta 9, Shanghai Biennale) are carefully chosen: Manifesta 9 (European, site-specific, industrial heritage) demonstrated his ability to work with Western institutional structures on his own terms; Shanghai 2018 (Chinese state museum) demonstrated his willingness to engage with the geopolitical Other of the Western art world. Together they position him as genuinely international rather than Western-adjacent.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **1993** — Joins IIE-UNAM as contemporary art researcher. A young Mexicanist at the most prestigious art history institute in Mexico, claiming contemporary practice as a legitimate research object. Smart institutional bet at the beginning.
2. **Late 1990s–2000s** — Becomes primary critical voice for the "Mexico City scene" (Alÿs, Margolles, Orozco, etc.) — writes the essays that make this generation legible internationally. This makes him the gatekeeper and interpreter of what becomes the most internationally significant Latin American contemporary art formation.
3. **2007** — *Francis Alÿs* Phaidon monograph. London publisher, English primary language, international distribution. This book puts him in dialogue with the major art historical infrastructure of the English-language world.
4. **2007** — *La era de la discrepancia* (with Debroise) establishes the historical archive for post-1968 Mexican art. He controls the canonical account of the period.
5. **2008** — Founding Chief Curator of MUAC. UNAM builds a major contemporary art museum; Medina is its first curatorial director. This gives him both platform and resources.
6. **2009** — Venice Biennale Mexican Pavilion (Teresa Margolles). One of the most talked-about Venice presentations of that decade. International reputation cemented.
7. **2010** — "Contemp(t)orary: Eleven Theses" in e-flux Journal. Theoretical intervention that names the problem of contemporaneity from a non-Western position. Published alongside Hal Foster, HUO — he's at the table.
8. **2012** — Chief Curator, Manifesta 9. First time a Latin American curator leads the European Biennial of Contemporary Art. "The Deep of the Modern" uses mining as theory.
9. **2012** — Walter Hopps Award. Menil Collection validates him as a global curator.
10. **2018–2019** — Chief Curator, 12th Shanghai Biennale. South-South connection: Mexico City → Shanghai.
11. **2024–25** — Beatriz González show travels to De Pont Museum (Netherlands). New European institutional relationship.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **The anchor strategy**: Medina shows that you don't need to leave your home institution to have international influence. Build credibility at home first; let the international projects come to you. Amber's Oxford/Cambridge base could work similarly.

2. **Name the field you're already in**: "Arte postmexicano" didn't exist as a concept until Medina named it. Amber could similarly name an emerging formation in critical AI art that she's already observing. The concept does work — it becomes a node around which others organize.

3. **Use the anthology strategically**: *Abuso mutuo* collected 20 years of dispersed essays into a coherent book. This retroactive coherence-making is a powerful move. When Amber has 5-7 years of writing, an edited collection could do the same.

4. **Coalition monographs**: The multi-author approach to the Alÿs book (curator + critic + theorist + anthropologist) is more than a publishing convenience — it's a demonstration that a work of art requires multiple disciplinary approaches simultaneously. Amber could apply this format to collaborations with scientists, engineers, and artists.

5. **Provocation within constraints**: Manifesta 9 (European state funding, heritage sites) and Shanghai Biennale (Chinese state museum) both involve significant institutional constraints. Medina demonstrates that you can do radical curatorial work within institutional structures by using the constraints as the medium — the former coal mine *is* the concept.
