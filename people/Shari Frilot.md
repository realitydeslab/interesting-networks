# Shari Frilot

**Chief Curator and Senior Programmer at [[Sundance New Frontier]]** (2007–present). The single most influential curator of immersive and experimental media art in North America. Over 16+ years, she has shaped what "serious" VR/AR/installation art looks like institutionally — centering feminist, Afrofuturist, and non-Western perspectives at the world's most prestigious immersive media showcase. Born June 18, 1965. Harvard graduate. Creole and Puerto Rican heritage. Grew up in Denver, Colorado.

## Background

Frilot's career is a story of building institutions for art forms that didn't yet have them. Three phases:

**Phase 1: Queer Experimental Cinema (1989–2001)**
Her films began with *Fly Boy* (short, 1989) and culminated in *Black Nations/Queer Nations?* (documentary feature, 1995) — one of the first films to explicitly address the intersection of Black and queer identity. Part of a generation of African-American directors alongside Marlon Riggs (*Tongues Untied*), Cheryl Dunye (*The Watermelon Woman*), and Isaac Julien (*Looking for Langston*). She served as Director of MIX NYC (1992–1996), the gay and lesbian experimental film festival in New York — one of the oldest such festivals. During this time she co-founded **MIX Brasil** and **MIX México**, the first gay Latin American subsidiary film festivals. Her third film, *Strange & Charmed* (short, 2003), came later.

**Phase 2: Outfest and Los Angeles (1998–2006)**
Co-Director of Programming for OUTFEST (1998–2001), where she founded "Platinum Oasis" — the festival's first cinematic performance installation series. She also exhibited "Cyberqueer" at MIX in 1994, showing an early instinct for digital/interactive work. This positioned her perfectly for what came next.

**Phase 3: Sundance New Frontier (2007–present)**
Joined Sundance in 1998 as a senior programmer. Founded and became Chief Curator of New Frontier in 2007, turning a small experimental sidebar into the world's most prestigious immersive media exhibition. Her 16+ year tenure produced: the first institutional platforming of VR as art (2012, Nonny de la Peña), the first VR residency at a film festival, the first seven-figure VR acquisition at a film festival (Eliza McNitt's *SPHERES*, 2018), and an open international call that diversified VR from a US-dominated field.

**Personal philosophy:** Rooted in quantum physics — "a photon is wholly a wave and wholly a particle simultaneously, which contradicts common sense but is the basic fact of our physical world." She uses this as a framework for identity (feeling wholly Puerto Rican *and* wholly Creole) and for curatorial practice: putting different worlds together in one room generates something larger than the sum of its parts.

## Key Works (Curatorial)

### New Frontier Exhibition Highlights

- **[[Sundance New Frontier]]** (2007–present) — Founded and runs the program annually at Sundance Film Festival, Park City, Utah. The flagship project. URL: <https://www.sundance.org/initiatives/new-frontier/>

- **"Hunger in Los Angeles"** by [[Nonny de la Peña]] (2012) — VR journalism about a diabetic man collapsing at a food bank in LA. First serious VR artwork at Sundance. Palmer Luckey attended this show and it influenced the creation of Oculus Rift. A directly causative link between one New Frontier exhibit and the birth of commercial VR.

- **"Kahlil Joseph + Kendrick Lamar: Double Conscience"** (2016) — Double-channel video installation; a community portrait of life in Compton set to Lamar's music. Part of the 10th anniversary edition.

- **"NeuroSpeculative AfroFeminism"** by [[Hyphen-Labs]] (2017) — Speculative salon/beauty parlor + Afrofuturist wearable tech. Breakthrough moment for the studio.

- **"SPHERES: Songs of Spacetime"** by Eliza McNitt (2018) — Exec. produced by Darren Aronofsky; narrated by Jessica Chastain. First VR work to sell for seven figures at Sundance. URL: <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=oGBEHaXEq7s>

- **"Life of Us"** by Chris Milk & Aaron Koblin (2017, shown in New Frontier) — Social VR experience; "a huge quantum leap of what it's like to create with other people inside of a virtual environment" (Frilot).

- **"Breathe"** by Diego Galafassi (2020) — Body sensors + mixed reality + breath visualization; climate as embodied experience.

- **"Infinitely Yours"** by [[Miwa Matreyek]] (2020) — Dance + shadow puppetry + animation; climate grief.

- **"7 Sounds" / "32 Sounds"** by Sam Green (2021, 2022) — Live cinema with binaural audio; Green's ongoing exploration of the performance-documentary hybrid pioneered at New Frontier.

- **"Beyond the Breakdown"** by Tony Patrick, Grace Lee, [[Lauren Lee McCarthy]] (2021) — AI companion "Serenity" + collective browser gathering; emerged from 2017 New Frontier World Building Residency.

- **"Frankenstein AI: A Monster Made by Many"** (2018) — Participants interact with an AI trained on Mary Shelley's text; documentary-AI hybrid.

- **"Collisions"** by [[Lynette Wallworth]] — Frilot gave Wallworth the first Sundance VR residency (~2016); led to *Collisions*, which then traveled internationally.

- **"HaptX: Experience Realistic Touch"** (2018) — Haptic tech showcase, not a narrative piece but surfaced to show storytellers the tool.

- **10th Anniversary: "Slithering Screens" at MoMA** (2016) — New Frontier expanded to MoMA, New York, for the 10th anniversary.

### Curator Statements / Concepts

- **"Superbody Reflection"** (2018 curator's statement) — Argues technology is not separate from us; we are made of the same material as our tools.
- **"The Power of the Erotic: Curatorial Strategies at Sundance's New Frontier"** — Talk at UC Berkeley Art, Technology, and Culture Colloquium, 2010. URL: <https://atc.berkeley.edu/>
- **"Physical Cinema"** — Her theoretical framework: cinema that activates the body as a generative agent, not a passive receptor. Developed in collaboration with Roya Rastegar (Scholar & Feminist Online, 2012). URL: <https://sfonline.barnard.edu/curating-physical-cinema-at-sundances-new-frontier/>

## For Amber

1. **She is the gatekeeper** — If Amber submits immersive work to Sundance New Frontier, understanding Frilot's lens is essential. The question she asks: Does this work have something to say? Is it centered on real human experience, not just technical novelty? Does it come from a non-dominant perspective?

2. **The HoloKit angle** — HoloKit as open-source, democratizing AR is exactly the kind of anti-monopoly technology story Frilot values. Contrast this with Facebook/Meta's headsets, which she has implicitly critiqued through her curation.

3. **More-than-human + embodiment** — Her 2020 program was deeply about non-human beings and ecological embodiment (mushrooms, breath, insects). Amber's more-than-human design work sits squarely in this current.

4. **Quantum physics as identity/method** — Frilot uses quantum physics (superposition, complementarity) as a philosophical tool for thinking about mixed identities and mixed practices. This resonates deeply with Amber's interest in paradox, machine death, and post-binary thinking.

5. **The Oculus Rift story** — Nonny de la Peña + Palmer Luckey = an experimental artwork accidentally seeding a multi-billion-dollar industry. This is the most important case study Amber should know for arguing that speculative art has material consequences. Directly relevant to machine behavior and unintended agency.

6. **Residency opportunity** — The New Frontier World Building Residency is an application target. Amber's work on HoloKit, more-than-human AR, and agentive design would be compelling.

7. **Queer physics** — Frilot's use of quantum superposition as a framework for queerness and mixed identity is philosophically rich. Connects to Karen Barad's "agential realism" — Frilot is doing a curatorial version of that thinking.

8. **Access as politics** — Her use of Google Cardboard (at $20) to democratize VR distribution at Sundance 2015 is a direct parallel to HoloKit's open-source ethos. Worth citing/referencing.

9. **"Physical cinema"** — Her theoretical framework for immersive media is the best existing curatorial theory for what immersive XR art is and why it matters. Amber should read the Barnard Scholar & Feminist article and the Leonardo paper.

10. **Potential mentor** — Frilot is a senior figure who has built exactly the kind of curatorial infrastructure for emerging tech art that Amber might want to build for AR/XR in an academic/research context. She's a model for the curator-researcher role.

## Why Interesting

Frilot represents the possibility of **institutional infrastructure with critical rigor** — a rare combination. She didn't just ride the VR hype wave; she was there before it, shaped it, and has maintained a consistent critical lens throughout. Her curation has:

- **Launched the commercial VR industry** (Nonny de la Peña → Palmer Luckey → Oculus Rift)
- **Defined the aesthetic and ethical standards** for VR as art (not just VR as entertainment)
- **Centered marginalized voices** consistently when every market incentive pushed the opposite direction
- **Survived the VR winter** and institutional pressure better than most

Her philosophical framework — quantum superposition as a model for mixed identity and hybrid practices — is intellectually serious and genuinely interesting as a curatorial epistemology.

For Amber's knowledge graph: she's the bridge between queer cinema history, immersive media art, social justice tech, and the critical discourse around XR as a medium.

## Research Taste Analysis

Frilot aligns with Amber's research taste in several key dimensions:

- **Non-Western and feminist technology** — She consistently platforms Afrofuturist, Indigenous, queer, and women artists working in technology. Not tokenistically — structurally.
- **Embodied, non-screen experience** — Her "physical cinema" framework resists the dominant screen paradigm. Her interest is in what happens to bodies in space.
- **Documentary impulse + speculative form** — The Nonny de la Peña model (journalism + immersive art) is exactly the kind of genre-collapse Amber values.
- **Scale matters** — She has both intimate performance works and epic exhibitions; she's not afraid of scale or of intimacy.
- **Technology as humanistic practice** — Her 2018 curator's statement explicitly argues technology is not separate from us; it's material continuity with our bodies.

Where she diverges: She is primarily an institutional curator, not a researcher or artist-technologist. Her output is programs and exhibitions, not papers or code. But her intellectual framework maps cleanly onto research-adjacent thinking.
