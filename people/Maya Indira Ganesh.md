## Key Works

### Monograph
- [*Auto-Correct: The Fantasies and Failures of AI, Ethics, and the Driverless Car*](https://artezpress.artez.nl/books/auto-correct/) (ArtEZ Press, March 2025) — published monograph from her doctoral work. Argues that autonomous vehicles are Foucauldian "ethical apparatuses" — epistemic devices that generate and circulate notions of autonomy and ethical decision-making as speculative/probabilistic fantasy. Examines how the Trolley Problem and Moral Machine game functioned as epistemic infrastructure for a discourse of "ethical AI." Central claim: autonomy in AI is a fantasy masking embodied human labour and computational dependencies.

Inspired by Baradian and Foucauldian notions of apparatuses. Two main case studies: (1) the Trolley Problem / Moral Machine as epistemic infrastructure for AI ethics; (2) driverless car crashes as sites where human micro-labour is hidden.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Maya's circuit bridges **three distinct worlds** that rarely overlap:

**1. European Critical Media Arts (German-speaking stronghold)**
Transmediale (Berlin), ZKM (Karlsruhe), Akademie der Künste (Berlin), Ludwig Forum Aachen, Gropius Bau Journal, Het Nieuwe Instituut (Rotterdam), Haus der Kulturen der Welt (Berlin). These are the premier European venues for critical theory + art + technology. ZKM and Akademie der Künste specifically represent the prestige European media art world — not neutral, but politically and aesthetically committed to critical-cultural practice.

**2. Global Feminist and Critical AI Governance Spaces**
FAccT (ACM's premier AI fairness conference), NeurIPS (equity workshops), ACM Interactions, APC (Association for Progressive Communications), Making All Voices Count, Council of Europe, Konrad Adenauer Stiftung, Rockefeller Foundation. This circuit connects academic computer science fairness with NGO and policy worlds — a genuinely unusual crossing.

**3. Global Cultural/Curatorial Contemporary Art**
13th Gwangju Biennale, Singapore Art Museum (Proof of Personhood), LAS Art Foundation, Library Stack, HOLO magazine/press. These are high-prestige contemporary art spaces taking AI seriously as cultural question.

**The pattern:** Maya chooses venues that *integrate* critical theory with cultural practice — she does not publish only in STS journals or only in art catalogs, but insists on both. This is a deliberate move to refuse disciplinary capture. The small-press / artist-book circuit (Lenz Press, Library Stack, HOLO, ArtEZ Press) is also a signal — these are high-taste, low-circulation publications that accrue cultural capital precisely through selectivity.

**What's notable by absence:** She is *not* in the Silicon Valley / startup conference circuit, not on the AI safety conference circuit (EA adjacent), not at TED or Davos. These are deliberate non-choices. Her publication in *Nature*-affiliated journals (Humanities & Social Sciences Communications) while simultaneously publishing in artist books is a calculated dual strategy.

## Key Concepts & Ideas

1. **"The ironies of autonomy"** — autonomy in AI is a fantasy. Machines presented as autonomous in fact rely on enormous hidden human labour (micro-work, training data, crash reporting). The irony: AI "autonomy" requires *more* human dependency, not less.

2. **The ethical apparatus** — (after Foucault) the driverless car as an epistemic device that actively generates notions of "autonomy" and "ethical decision-making." Ethics is not discovered by the car; it is produced through the car as a site of regulatory and speculative discourse.

3. **"Epistemic infrastructures of moral decision-making"** — specific tools (Trolley Problem, Moral Machine game's 40M+ responses) become the infrastructure of AI ethics. They shape what questions can be asked and who gets to answer them, long before any actual ethical decision is needed.

4. **"Reciprocity deficits"** — persistent gap between AI innovation discourse (government/industry promises) and everyday public experience of AI in urban space. Communities that live with AI infrastructure are rarely consulted in its governance.

5. **"Varieties of knowledge projects"** — communities deploy different forms of resistance and refusal to algorithmic harms: counter-data, advocacy, refusal to be surveilled, legal challenges. No single resistance but a diverse ecology of knowledge-making.

6. **A Is for Another** — alternative AI dictionary showing how AI concepts are shaped by and through human constructs. The dictionary form as feminist pedagogy: de-center the technical definition, re-enter from humanistic and non-Western angles.

7. **"Minor feelings of AI"** — adapts Cathy Park Hong's concept of "minor feelings" (unvalidated, racialized affect experienced by Asian Americans) to the experience of AI ethics failures. The "minor" is not small but oppositional — the feelings that mainstream discourse cannot accommodate.

8. **"Body Machine"** — the body under AI/LLM age. As LLMs generate language and images from human data, what happens to the body? The essay asks: if AI processes human language into more language, and human images into more images, are we becoming data-feedstock? Bodily existence as the thing that exceeds AI's grasp — and also the site of AI's most extractive operations.

9. **Sutradhar complex** — the Sutradhar (Sanskrit: "one who holds the strings") as a figure for interdisciplinary curation and connection. Can break the fourth wall, move between performance and reality, audience and actor — a model for what the critical researcher-curator does.

10. **AI Anarchies** — repositioning ethics and accountability not as managerial fixes to AI problems, but as human acts of resistance and imagination within the conditions AI creates.

## Research Taste Analysis ⭐⭐⭐

### Conceptual Strategy

**Framework multiplication**: Ganesh's most consistent move is to apply a non-technical framework to a technical object — Foucault's apparatus to autonomous vehicles, Baradian entanglement to driverless crashes, Cathy Park Hong's "minor feelings" to AI ethics. This is not mere metaphor-borrowing; she goes deep into the source framework before applying it. The "ethical apparatus" concept is genuinely Foucauldian in its attention to power-knowledge production, not just a gesture.

**Provocation-first**: Her titles announce an argument: *"Auto-Correct"* (machines fix us, not themselves), *"The ironies of autonomy"* (autonomy isn't), *"Complete machine autonomy? It's a fantasy!"* She doesn't hedge. This makes her work citable and quotable — essential for influence.

**The counterdictionary move**: *A Is for Another* is a classic feminist/postcolonial move: meet the dominant taxonomy on its own terms (a dictionary), then systematically rewrite it from inside. This makes it simultaneously accessible to lay audiences and theoretically precise.

**Two-case architecture**: Her dissertation uses two cases (Trolley Problem/Moral Machine; driverless crashes) not to prove a general claim but to show the same apparatus operating at different scales. This is a sophisticated Foucauldian genealogical method: not sampling, but tracking.
