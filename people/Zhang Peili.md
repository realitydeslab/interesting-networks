# Zhang Peili 张培力

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Full deep research completed. Comprehensive profile with all works, exhibitions, Pond Society history, institutional lineage to Amber, Research Taste Analysis.

"Father of Chinese video art." Born 1957 in Hangzhou, China. Created China's first video artwork *30x30* (1988), co-founded the **Pond Society** (池社, 1986) — one of the earliest Chinese avant-garde artist collectives — and built the **New Media Art Department** at [[China Academy of Art]] from scratch, creating the institutional infrastructure that made Chinese new media art education possible. His students include [[Lu Yang]], one of China's most internationally prominent media artists. **Amber teaches at the very institution Zhang Peili built.** Works in permanent collections of [MoMA](https://www.moma.org/artists/29370), [Guggenheim](https://www.guggenheim.org/artwork/artist/zhang-peili), and [Centre Pompidou](https://www.centrepompidou.fr/).

## Background

Born November 1957 in Hangzhou, Zhejiang Province. Attended the **Zhejiang Academy of Fine Arts** (浙江美术学院, now [[China Academy of Art]]), graduating from the Department of Oil Painting in 1984. This is significant: he trained as a painter, then deliberately abandoned painting for video — a radical move in 1980s China where oil painting carried enormous cultural prestige.

### The Pond Society (池社, 1986)

In **1986**, Zhang Peili co-founded the **Pond Society** (池社) with fellow artists **Geng Jianyi** (耿建翌) and **Song Ling** (宋陵), along with other members including **Wang Qiang** (王强) and **Bao Jianfei** (包剑斐). The group was based in Hangzhou and became known for its existentialist bent — deliberately separating art from emotion, organizing public happenings and interventions in the city. They were part of the broader **'85 New Wave** (八五新潮) movement that swept Chinese contemporary art in the mid-1980s.

The Pond Society was notable for its conceptual rigor compared to other '85 New Wave groups. Where many Chinese avant-garde collectives of the era were politically confrontational or expressionistic, the Pond Society pursued a cooler, more analytical approach — influenced by Western conceptual art but grounded in Chinese experience. Their public interventions in Hangzhou streets prefigured Zhang's later interest in the relationship between art, public space, and media.

### The Huangshan Conference (1988)

In 1988, Zhang screened his first video work *30x30* at the **Huangshan Conference** — a pivotal gathering of Chinese avant-garde artists and critics in the Yellow Mountains that helped organize the landmark **China/Avant-Garde** exhibition (中国现代艺术展). This conference was a watershed moment: it brought together disparate experimental art movements from across China and laid the groundwork for the most important exhibition of Chinese contemporary art in the 1980s.

### China/Avant-Garde Exhibition (1989)

The **China/Avant-Garde** exhibition opened on February 5, 1989 at the **National Art Museum of China** (中国美术馆) in Beijing — famously under a "No U-Turn" sign. It was the first comprehensive survey of Chinese experimental art, organized by critics **Gao Minglu** (高名潞), **Li Xianting** (栗宪庭), and others. Zhang Peili participated, though reportedly had to fast-forward *30x30* because audience members complained about its slow pace — an early sign that his aesthetic of deliberate boredom was intentionally confrontational. The exhibition was shut down twice by authorities, once after artist Xiao Lu fired gunshots at her own installation.

### Transition to Video (late 1980s–1990s)

After experimenting with oil painting (the *X?* series, 1986–87, which used medical/surgical imagery with a clinical detachment that prefigured his video work), public installations, and street art with the Pond Society, Zhang turned almost completely to video art. His work directly engaged with the parallel developments of video art in the West (Nam June Paik, Bruce Nauman, Bill Viola) but from a distinctly Chinese position — responding to state television, Cultural Revolution cinema, and the rise of mass entertainment in reform-era China.

Writing in 1989, on the cusp of his transition to video, Zhang observed: *"People never ask what in this world is not subject to constraints in some way (no one doubts the legitimacy of such constraints). Why is art an exception? Is art doomed to provide only entertainment?"*

### Institution Building (2000s–present)

In the early 2000s, Zhang Peili founded the **New Media Art Department** (新媒体艺术系) at [[China Academy of Art]] — the first such department at a major Chinese art academy. This was a genuinely radical institutional act: convincing a traditional fine arts academy to legitimize video, digital art, and new media as academic disciplines on par with painting and sculpture. The department grew into the **School of Intermedia Art** (跨媒体艺术学院), which Zhang served as dean. This school became the training ground for an entire generation of Chinese media artists, including [[Lu Yang]].

He currently lives and works in Hangzhou, continuing to make art while maintaining his institutional role at CAA.

## Key Works

### Video Works
- **[*30x30*](https://www.artic.edu/exhibitions/2595/zhang-peili-record-repeat)** (1988) — China's first video artwork. 180 minutes of Zhang repeatedly smashing a 12-inch mirror into pieces and gluing it back together, then smashing it again. Shot with a single static camera. The repetitive destruction and reconstruction is both absurdist ritual and political metaphor — an allegory for cycles of Chinese history, or perhaps the futility of reform. Screened at the Huangshan Conference 1988. Collection of the artist, Hangzhou.
- ***Document on Hygiene No. 3*** (1991) — Zhang continuously washes a live chicken with a bar of soap for an extended period. The camera transforms a mundane, slightly absurd act into something deeply uncomfortable — evoking themes of political control, hygiene as ideology, and the violence of repetition. Acquired by the [Art Institute of Chicago](https://www.artic.edu/exhibitions/2595/zhang-peili-record-repeat). A foundational work of Chinese video art.
- ***Water: Standard Version from Cihai Dictionary*** (1991) — A CCTV news anchor (Xing Zhibin, one of China's most recognizable television faces) reads the dictionary definition of "water" from the *Cihai* (辞海) encyclopedia with complete professional affect and authority. The contrast between the anchor's gravitas and the meaningless content is devastating institutional critique — exposing how state media's authority derives from form, not content. [MoMA collection](https://www.moma.org/artists/29370).
- ***Uncertain Pleasure I*** (1996) — Multi-channel video installation simultaneously showing 10 different camera angles of a man scratching himself. The multiplied perspectives create a vague eroticism and sense of voyeurism/surveillance. Forces the viewer to question social uses of video communication and mediated pleasure. [Guggenheim collection](https://www.guggenheim.org/artwork/artist/zhang-peili).
- ***Continuous Reproduction*** (1993) — Installation shown at [Hirshhorn Museum and Sculpture Garden](https://hirshhorn.si.edu/) in 2022.
- ***Actor's Lines*** (2002) — Remixed footage from a 1964 state-sanctioned film. Zhang edits the dialogue so that patriotic exchanges between an older comrade and a young soldier suggest romantic attraction, transforming hierarchical military relationships into reciprocal, horizontal ones. Subverts the apparatus of military power through the slightest shift in editing.
- ***Go Ahead, Go Ahead*** (2004) — Dual-channel parallel projection showing clips from American and Chinese war films simultaneously. Rather than searching for common ground, Zhang exposes the shared absurdity of cinematic violence across ideological lines.
- ***The Lowest Resolution*** (2005) — Footage from a sexual instruction video for newlyweds. Resolution decreases the closer the viewer approaches the screen. Questions how technical mediation alters reality and the interplay between affect and understanding.
- ***Happiness*** (2006) — Edited Cultural Revolution-era film (originally released 1970). Focuses on a single scene: an overzealous crowd irrationally applauding a speaker. Zhang edits audio and video to make the crowd appear not to be responding to the speaker at all, but overcome by mindless collective frenzy. Explores reception theory, mass media, and crowd action.
- ***A Scene in Black and White Unfolded Four Times*** (2007) — Video installation responsive to the viewer's presence, exploring the juxtaposition of images and their relationship to the space occupied by the viewer. Shown at [Art Institute of Chicago retrospective](https://www.artic.edu/exhibitions/2595/zhang-peili-record-repeat).
- ***A Gust of Wind*** (2008) — Large-scale video installation.
- ***Last Words*** (2003) — Video work.

### Painting / Early Work
- ***X?* series** (1986–87) — Oil paintings using medical/surgical imagery with clinical detachment. Painted gloves, surgical procedures, and clinical objects with a flat, affectless style that prefigured the emotional distance of his video work. These paintings were part of the Rationalist Painting (理性绘画) tendency within the '85 New Wave.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Zhang Peili operates across three distinct circuits, reflecting his dual role as artist and institution-builder:

### 1. Chinese Institutional Circuit
CAA (Hangzhou), National Art Museum of China (Beijing), Shanghai Biennale, TANK Shanghai, Power Station of Art (Shanghai), Rén Space (Shanghai). This is his home base — the institutional ecosystem of Chinese contemporary art.

### 2. Global Biennial Circuit
Venice Biennale (3x), Lyon Biennale, Sydney Biennale, Gwangju Biennale, Taipei Biennale. His international visibility came through the biennial system, particularly as Chinese contemporary art gained global attention in the 1990s–2000s.

### 3. Western Museum Circuit
MoMA (New York), Guggenheim (New York), Centre Pompidou (Paris), Art Institute of Chicago, Hirshhorn (Washington DC), Tate Liverpool. Permanent collections in three of the world's most important museums. The AIC retrospective (2017) was the definitive Western institutional recognition.

**Key pattern:** Zhang's career tracks the broader arc of Chinese contemporary art — from underground experimentation in the 1980s → biennial circuit visibility in the 1990s → Western museum canonization in the 2000s–2010s. But uniquely, he simultaneously built the *institutional infrastructure* for the next generation rather than just pursuing his own international career.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
- **Aesthetic of boredom as weapon** — Zhang's signature move: making viewers sit through extreme repetition (180 min mirror-smashing, endless chicken-washing). This isn't laziness — it's a deliberate attack on entertainment expectations, forcing attention to the mechanics of watching itself. The boredom IS the content.
- **Institutional voice theft** — Taking the forms of state authority (CCTV news anchors, war films, Cultural Revolution cinema) and hollowing them out. The form remains official; the content becomes absurd. This is more devastating than direct critique because it exposes the mechanism.
- **Found footage as political archaeology** — His later works remix Mao-era films not to mock them but to reveal what was always latent in them (homoeroticism in military hierarchy, mindless crowd psychology).

### Career Architecture
- **The artist-as-institution-builder** — Zhang's most important "work" may not be any single video but the New Media Art Department at CAA. He converted personal practice into institutional infrastructure, ensuring his aesthetic and conceptual values would propagate through generations of students. This is **field declaration** at its most concrete.
- **Loyalty to place** — He stayed in Hangzhou his entire career. While peers like Huang Yong Ping moved to Paris and Ai Weiwei moved to New York/Berlin, Zhang built his power locally. This is a deliberate strategy: deep institutional embeddedness over international mobility.
- **Alma mater return** — He graduated from CAA (then Zhejiang Academy), then built a new department there. This is the "come home and transform the institution" move — leveraging insider status and institutional memory.

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **Pond Society founding (1986)** — Created a collective identity during the '85 New Wave. Gave his conceptual approach an institutional form before he had institutional power. This built the network and reputation needed for everything after.
2. **30x30 at the Huangshan Conference (1988)** — Strategic timing. Showed China's first video artwork at the gathering that organized the China/Avant-Garde exhibition. Maximum visibility, minimum infrastructure (one camera, one mirror). WHY it worked: it was the right gesture at the right historical moment.
3. **Abandoning painting for video (late 1980s)** — A radical career move. Oil painting was prestigious; video was nothing in China. By committing fully, he claimed the entire medium as his territory. No hedging.
4. **Founding New Media Art Department at CAA (early 2000s)** — Converted personal authority into institutional power. Instead of just being "the video art guy," he became the person who decided what video art education looks like in China. This is the move from practitioner to field-builder.
5. **Training Lu Yang and others (2000s–2010s)** — The proof that the institutional strategy worked. His students now represent China at Venice Biennale. The lineage validates the institution.
6. **Art Institute of Chicago retrospective (2017)** — Western museum canonization, with the first bilingual English-Mandarin catalogue from AIC. This cemented his art historical status in the West.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Institution-building as creative practice** — Zhang's most important lesson: building a department IS an artwork. It's a system that produces culture, shapes aesthetics, and creates lineages. Amber, working within CAA, can think about her own institutional role not as separate from her art but as part of it.

2. **The power of staying** — In an art world that rewards mobility, Zhang stayed in Hangzhou and made Hangzhou come to him. For Amber, who has connections in both China and the UK, this raises the question: where do you plant your institutional flag?

3. **Claim the medium, not the message** — Zhang didn't argue for a specific style of video art. He argued that video art WAS a legitimate medium for Chinese art. By claiming the medium itself, he made all future practitioners his successors. Amber could think about what medium or field she could claim at this level.

4. **Aesthetic commitment as strategy** — Zhang's boredom aesthetic wasn't audience-friendly. But its uncompromising nature is exactly what made it historically significant. The lesson: don't optimize for accessibility; optimize for distinctiveness.

5. **The collective → individual → institution pipeline** — Pond Society (collective) → solo practice (individual) → CAA department (institution). This is a reproducible career architecture: build community first, establish personal authority, then institutionalize.
