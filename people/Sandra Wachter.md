# Sandra Wachter

Professor of Technology and Regulation at the Oxford Internet Institute (OII), University of Oxford, and Humboldt Professor at the Hasso Plattner Institute. One of the most cited AI law/ethics researchers in the world. Inventor of "counterfactual explanations," architect of the EU AI ethics regulatory framework, and a persistent critic of the gap between what AI law *says* and what it can actually *do*. Bridges CS, law, and policy in ways that produce real regulatory change — her tools are literally running inside Google, IBM, Microsoft, Amazon, and Accenture.

## Background

Sandra Wachter trained as a lawyer at the **University of Vienna** (Law Faculty), then moved to Oxford for her DPhil, where she never left. She became one of the founders of the **Governance of Emerging Technologies (GET) Research Programme** at OII, which she now leads.

Her trajectory: Austrian lawyer → Oxford DPhil → OII Research Fellow → Senior Research Fellow → Associate Professor → full Professor. She also worked at the **Alan Turing Institute**, the **Royal Academy of Engineering**, and the **Austrian Ministry of Health** before fully settling at Oxford. 

She is simultaneously a **Visiting Professor at Harvard Law School** (previously) and an affiliate at the **Berkman Klein Center for Internet & Society at Harvard**.

The unusual thing: she *combines* legal training, philosophy, and enough computer science to publish real empirical work. She's not just a commentator on AI — she develops actual tools (counterfactual explanations, CDD bias test) that get shipped in enterprise software. This is rare.

**Origin story of key idea:** In 2017, the debate about GDPR's "right to explanation" was intensely contested. Wachter argued (controversially, provocatively) that no such right exists in GDPR as written — but that we *should* build one. Rather than just making a legal argument, she proposed a technical solution: counterfactual explanations. "You were denied a loan. If your income had been €5,000 higher, you would have been approved." This reframed the entire XAI (Explainable AI) field away from post-hoc mechanistic explanation toward actionable recourse.

## Key Works

**Foundational:**
- [**Counterfactual Explanations without Opening the Black Box: Automated Decisions and the GDPR**](https://jolt.law.harvard.edu/assets/articlePDFs/v31/Counterfactual-Explanations-without-Opening-the-Black-Box-Sandra-Wachter-et-al.pdf) (Wachter, Mittelstadt, Russell, *Harvard Journal of Law & Technology*, 2018) — 4,600+ citations. Invented the counterfactual explanation approach to XAI. This paper changed how the entire field thinks about algorithmic transparency. Implemented by Google, IBM, Microsoft, Accenture, Amazon, Vodafone.
- [**Why a right to explanation of automated decision-making does not exist in the general data protection regulation**](https://academic.oup.com/idpl/article/7/2/76/3860948) (Wachter, Mittelstadt, Floridi, *International Data Privacy Law*, 2017) — 2,200+ citations. Provocative argument that GDPR's Article 22 does NOT create a general right to explanation — setting up the need for counterfactual approach.
- [**The ethics of algorithms: Mapping the debate**](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2053951716679679) (Mittelstadt, Allo, Taddeo, Wachter, Floridi, *Big Data & Society*, 2016) — 4,900+ citations. The field-mapping paper that defined what "ethics of algorithms" even means.

**Bias and Fairness:**
- [**Why fairness cannot be automated: Bridging the gap between EU non-discrimination law and AI**](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0267364921000406) (Wachter, Mittelstadt, Russell, *Computer Law & Security Review*, 2021) — 675+ citations. Showed that 13/20 popular AI fairness tests fail EU legal standards.
- [**Bias preservation in machine learning: the legality of fairness metrics under EU non-discrimination law**](https://researchrepository.wvu.edu/wvlr/vol123/iss3/4/) (Wachter, Mittelstadt, Russell, *West Virginia Law Review*, 2021) — 311+ citations. Developed "Conditional Demographic Disparity" (CDD) test that meets EU/UK standards. Implemented by Amazon and IBM.
- [**The Theory of Artificial Immutability: Protecting Algorithmic Groups Under Anti-Discrimination Law**](https://journals.library.columbia.edu/index.php/CBLR/article/view/3424) (*Tulane Law Review*, 2022) — proposes protecting algorithmically-defined groups (e.g., "people the model thinks are high-risk") under anti-discrimination frameworks.

**Data and Inference:**
- [**A Right to Reasonable Inferences: Re-thinking Data Protection Law in the Age of Big Data and AI**](https://journals.library.columbia.edu/index.php/CBLR/article/view/3424) (Wachter, Mittelstadt, *Columbia Business Law Review*, 2019) — 1,146+ citations. Won the **Privacy Law Scholars (PLSC) Award 2019**. Argues for a new data right: protection against *unreasonable inferences* drawn from your data.
- [**Affinity Profiling and Discrimination by Association in Online Behavioural Advertising**](https://lawcat.berkeley.edu/record/1175984) (*Berkeley Technology Law Journal*, 2020) — 319+ citations. Shows how ad targeting creates group-level discrimination even when targeting individuals.
- **Normative Challenges of Identification in the Internet of Things** (*Computer Law & Security Review*, 2017) — early work on privacy in IoT/sensor environments.

**AI Governance and Regulation:**
- [**Trustworthy artificial intelligence and the European Union AI act: On the conflation of trustworthiness and acceptability of risk**](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/rego.12512) (Laux, Wachter, Mittelstadt, *Regulation & Governance*, 2024) — 502+ citations. Critiques how the EU AI Act conflates "trustworthy" with "acceptable risk."
- [**Three pathways for standardisation and ethical disclosure by default under the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act**](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0267364924000031) (Laux, Wachter, Mittelstadt, *Computer Law & Security Review*, 2024) — proposes concrete implementation paths for AI Act compliance.
- [**Limitations and loopholes in the EU AI Act and AI Liability Directives**](https://openyls.law.yale.edu/handle/20.500.13051/18671) (*Yale Journal of Law & Technology*, 2024) — 132+ citations. Identifies what the EU AI Act still can't do.
- [**Artificial intelligence and the 'good society': the US, EU, and UK approach**](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11948-018-0031-5) (Cath, Wachter, Mittelstadt, Taddeo, Floridi, *Science & Engineering Ethics*, 2018) — 1,475+ citations. Comparative governance analysis.

**XAI:**
- [**Explaining explanations in AI**](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3287560.3287574) (Mittelstadt, Russell, Wachter, *FAccT*, 2019) — 1,213+ citations. Presented at ACM FAccT.
- [**Transparent, explainable, and accountable AI for robotics**](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/scirobotics.aan6080) (Wachter, Mittelstadt, Floridi, *Science Robotics*, 2017) — 475+ citations.

**Generative AI / LLMs:**
- [**Science in the age of large language models**](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-023-06158-4) (Birhane, Kasirzadeh, Leslie, Wachter, *Nature Reviews Physics*, 2023) — 427+ citations. On how LLMs are changing scientific practice — risks and opportunities.

## Why Interesting

**Critical Computing in the most literal sense:** Wachter's work is about the gap between what AI governance *claims* and what it can actually *deliver*. She's the person who said "GDPR says you have a right to explanation — but actually you don't, and here's why, and here's what we should build instead." That's not criticism for its own sake; it's rigorous structural critique that produces actionable alternatives.

From Amber's research-taste.md: Wachter embodies **"critical computing"** — computing as political act, structural critique that goes beyond ethics checklists. Her "right to reasonable inferences" work is essentially arguing that current data protection law is inadequate for the inference era — you can't protect just the data; you have to protect what's *deduced* from data. This is the philosophical kernel that connects to how AI agents make inferences about human behavior, which is exactly what Amber's sovereign/dissociative agents work is about.

The FAccT connection is direct: Wachter is a central figure at FAccT, the same venue where Amber's machine behavior / agent accountability papers will be evaluated. She may literally be an area chair or reviewer for Amber's submissions.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy

**Provocation-first + solution-bundled:** Wachter's signature move is the *provocative legal claim that creates demand for a technical solution she already has*. The 2017 paper says "no right to explanation exists in GDPR" — this is intentionally controversial, designed to force debate. The 2018 paper provides the alternative: counterfactual explanations. The provocation and the solution arrive almost simultaneously, but the provocation runs ahead to create the vacuum that the solution fills.

**Framework multiplication:** Law × CS × Ethics → new governance concepts. "Counterfactual explanation" is not a legal concept and not quite a CS concept — it's a new thing that only exists at their intersection. "Right to reasonable inferences" is another: takes a legal concept (rights) and a CS concept (inference) and creates a new legal-technical hybrid.

**Two-word concept coining:** "Counterfactual explanations," "conditional demographic disparity," "artificial immutability," "reasonable inferences." She is excellent at naming things. Named concepts become citable, searchable, teachable. This is deliberate.

### Career Architecture

**Field declaration:** She didn't just work in AI ethics — she helped *define* what AI ethics law even is. The 2016 "ethics of algorithms" paper with Floridi et al. was a field-mapping exercise: here are all the concerns, here's how they connect. Planting a flag.

**Coalition papers:** Her most cited work is always multi-authored: law + ethics + CS. The GET triumvirate (Wachter = law, Mittelstadt = ethics, Russell = CS) is a structural coalition. No single paper represents just one discipline.

**Platform building:** The GET Research Programme at OII is infrastructure she built and leads. A research programme is a budget, a brand, a hiring pipeline, a grant attractor. This is institutional power, not just publication power.

**US law review strategy:** European researchers don't normally publish in Harvard Law Review, Columbia Business Law Review, Yale JL&Tech. Wachter does this systematically. It's a deliberate market expansion — building credibility in the US legal system, which shapes tech company behavior more directly than European academic journals.

### Key Moves (chronological)

1. **2016-2017 — The Ethics of Algorithms + GDPR critique:** Join Floridi/Mittelstadt coalition → publish the field-mapping paper → immediately pivot to GDPR right-to-explanation argument. Two papers in 2017 that define an agenda.

5. **2023-2025 — EU AI Act critique + Humboldt Professorship:** As the AI Act passes, positions herself as the leading expert on its limitations. Wins €3.5M Humboldt award — highest validation possible.
