# Ambelin Kwaymullina

Palyku writer, artist, and academic. Practitioner of [Indigenous Futurisms](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/walking-the-clouds) (term coined by Anishinaabe academic Grace Dillon). Author of Young Adult speculative fiction (Catching Teller Crow series, co-authored with nephew Jay Boochani). 2023 Cybernetic Imagination Resident at [[ANU School of Cybernetics]], where she applied Indigenous futurist thinking to AI through acrylic painting. Core philosophical argument: in Indigenous knowledge systems, there is no "artificial" intelligence — intelligence is relational, embedded in Country, not produced by machines. Academic at University of Western Australia.

## Why Interesting
Her AI critique from Indigenous epistemology is one of the most radical yet grounded challenges to the AI field's foundational assumptions. Connects to Amber's interest in non-Western tech philosophies ([[cosmotechnics]] tradition), more-than-human design, and machine behavior. The argument that "intelligence is relational and embedded in Country" maps interestingly onto situated cognition, active inference, and ecological framings of AI.
