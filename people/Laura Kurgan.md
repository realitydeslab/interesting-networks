# Laura Kurgan

Architecture professor and spatial data visualization researcher at [Columbia GSAPP](https://www.arch.columbia.edu/). Director of the [Center for Spatial Research](https://c4sr.columbia.edu/) and Visual Studies curriculum at Columbia. Key bridge between critical theory and spatial data practice. Author of *[Close Up at a Distance: Mapping, Technology, and Politics](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780942299687/close-up-at-a-distance/)* (Zone Books, 2013). Long-term collaborator with [[Wendy Chun]] on homophily and algorithmic segregation: co-authored "[Homophily: The Urban History of an Algorithm](https://www.e-flux.com/architecture/are-friends-electric/289193/homophily-the-urban-history-of-an-algorithm/)" (e-flux Architecture, 2019) and "Weak Ties" (e-flux Architecture, 2020).

## Background
Works at the intersection of architecture, data visualization, and political critique. Founded the Spatial Information Design Lab at Columbia GSAPP (2004–2015), which pioneered visualization of mass incarceration data ("Million Dollar Blocks," now in MoMA's permanent collection). 

## Key Works
- *[Close Up at a Distance: Mapping, Technology, and Politics](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780942299687/close-up-at-a-distance/)* (Zone Books, 2013) — ethics and politics of digital mapping
- "Million Dollar Blocks" — incarceration data visualization, in MoMA permanent collection
- "[Homophily: The Urban History of an Algorithm](https://www.e-flux.com/architecture/are-friends-electric/289193/homophily-the-urban-history-of-an-algorithm/)" (e-flux Architecture, 2019) — with [[Wendy Chun]] et al.
- "[Weak Ties: The Urban History of an Algorithm](https://www.e-flux.com/architecture/are-friends-electric/348398/weak-ties-the-urban-history-of-an-algorithm/)" (e-flux Architecture, 2020) — with [[Wendy Chun]], Dare Brawley, Jia Zhang
- "Living In/difference; or, How to Imagine Ambivalent Networks" (Qui Parle, 2021) — with [[Wendy Chun]], Carina Albrecht

## Why Interesting
Exemplar of the architect-as-data-critic. Makes the political stakes of data visualization concrete and actionable. The "Million Dollar Blocks" project is one of the most powerful examples of critical spatial data practice — it visualized where incarceration money comes from neighborhood-by-neighborhood, making abstract policy tangible. Her work with Chun on homophily connects to Amber's interest in what AI systems do in urban/social space.
