# Greg Tate

Writer, cultural critic, musician, and Afro-Futurist theorist. Died December 7, 2021. Staff writer at the *Village Voice* from the mid-1980s through the early 2000s, where he developed a sui generis form of criticism that fused journalism, philosophy, Black radical theory, and hip-hop aesthetics. Leader of the band Burnt Sugar the Arkestra Chamber. Author of *Flyboy in the Buttermilk: Essays on Contemporary America* (1992), *Midnight Lightning: Jimi Hendrix and the Black Experience* (2003), and *Flyboy 2: The Greg Tate Reader* (2016).

Lifelong closest friend of [[Arthur Jafa]]. "Have you seen the books?" — Tate was always Jafa's first audience for the photobooks and the first to affirm the BVI project. Jafa says Tate "always heard me," meaning understood what he was doing before anyone else did.

## Why Interesting
Tate's criticism is worth reading as a model of what engaged arts writing can be: theoretically sophisticated but never academic, obsessed with the formal properties of Black culture (hip-hop, free jazz, science fiction), and deeply committed to the idea that Black cultural production is philosophically serious. He was one of the first critics to take hip-hop seriously as art. His concept of "Afro-Futurism" (coined in parallel with Mark Dery) was central to defining a generation's thinking about Black innovation and futurity.
