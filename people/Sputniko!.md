# Sputniko! (Hiromi Marissa Ozaki) スプツニ子!

Japanese-British artist, designer, and entrepreneur. Creates music-video-based speculative design works that use emerging biotechnology, robotics, and digital technology as entry points for feminist critique and interspecies imagination. Graduated from [[Design Interactions (RCA)]] under [[Anthony Dunne]] & Fiona Raby; former Assistant Professor at MIT Media Lab (Design Fiction group); now at Tokyo University of the Arts. Co-founded art duo **AnotherFarm** (2018) exploring genetic modification and human-nature relations. Founded **Cradle**, a women's healthcare company in Japan. One of the most widely-exhibited speculative designers in Asia; her work is in permanent collections at V&A London and 21st Century Museum of Contemporary Art Kanazawa.

## Background

Born in Tokyo 1985, Japanese-British. Father a scientist; grew up between Japan and UK. Won math competitions internationally (Mathematical Olympiad level) throughout middle/high school. Studied **BSc Mathematics & Computer Science at [Imperial College London](https://www.imperial.ac.uk/)** — went hardcore into programming (Java, C++, ActionScript). Pivoted to art after discovering that technology alone wasn't asking the right questions.

**MA Design Interactions, [Royal College of Art](https://www.rca.ac.uk/)** (2010) — the legendary programme run by [[Anthony Dunne]] & Fiona Raby that invented Critical Design as a practice. Her graduation works (Menstruation Machine, Crowbot Jenny, Sushiborg Yukari) were shown at Museum of Contemporary Art Tokyo *before she even graduated*. Then MoMA six months later.

**MIT Media Lab (2013-2017):** Founded and directed the **Design Fiction** research group. Made permanent art pavilion at Teshima Island (Setouchi Art Triennale 2016). Mentored [[Ai Hasegawa]] — was production adviser on *(Im)possible Baby* (2015).

**University of Tokyo RCA-IIS Design Lab (2017-2019):** Appointed Associate Professor at IIS.

**Tokyo University of the Arts (2019-present):** Associate Professor, Department of Design. Geidai is Japan's national arts university — the most prestigious art school in the country.

**SDS Chiba Institute of Technology (2025-present):** Founding faculty as "Designer of Speculative Futures" at Joi Ito's new antidisciplinary graduate school. Launches 2026.

**Cradle (founded ~2021):** Women's healthcare/employee benefits service for Japanese corporations. Real institutional venture, not just an art project — "designing a system enabling everyone to flourish in their own way, regardless of gender."

**Nickname origin:** High school friends called her "Sputnik" because of her science obsession + pale skin. She added "Ko" (子 — a common Japanese girls' name suffix) after learning Sputnik was the first satellite. The exclamation mark (!) — characteristically hers.

## Key Works

- **[Menstruation Machine—Takashi's Take](https://www.media.mit.edu/people/sputniko/overview/)** (2010) — A device strapped to a body that simulates bleeding and cramp pain to allow people to experience menstruation. Video follows a male character (Takashi) wearing it. First work to break internationally. Shown at: MOT Tokyo (2010), MoMA "Talk To Me" (2011), Ars Electronica 2012 (Honorary Mention), V&A permanent collection. *The canonical speculative design work on bodily empathy across gender.*

- **[Crowbot Jenny](https://www.media.mit.edu/people/sputniko/overview/)** (2011) — Device for communicating with crows. Video + mechanical bird. Shown at MoMA "Talk to Me" (2011). Japan Media Arts Festival jury-recommended 2010.

- **Sushiborg Yukari** (2010) — Graduation work at RCA. A cyborg/sushi hybrid persona.

- **Nanohana Heels / Healing Fukushima** (2012) — High heels that mechanically plant rapeseed into soil with each step, to remediate radioactive land around Fukushima. Ars Electronica 2013 Honorary Mention (Hybrid Art). Poetic + political: fashion meets bioremediation.

- **[Tranceflora—Amy's Glowing Silk](https://www.media.mit.edu/people/sputniko/overview/)** (2015) — Dress made from fluorescent silk produced by silkworms genetically modified with jellyfish + coral genes (GFP). Shown at Gucci Gallery Shinjuku (solo), 21st Century Museum Kanazawa. *The moment bioart becomes haute couture.*

- **[Red Silk of Fate—Tamaki's Crush / Teshima 8 Million Lab](https://www.media.mit.edu/projects/teshima-8-million-lab/overview/)** (2016, permanent 2016-2020) — First Shinto shrine worshipping a genetically engineered organism: a silkworm. Located on Teshima Island, Setouchi Art Triennale, Benesse Art Site Naoshima. Merges Shinto animism (Yaoyorozu — "8 million gods in all things") with synthetic biology. *The most philosophically ambitious work: a religion for the GMO era.*

- **Bionic** (2017)

- **[Drone in Search for a Four-Leaf Clover](https://lumenprize.org/futures-award-finalists-2024/sputniko)** (2018) — Drone equipped with AI image recognition that instantly locates four-leaf clovers at speed. Raises question: if efficiency eliminates the search, do we still enjoy the happiness? A critique of AI optimization culture. **Lumen Prize 2024 nominee**, Nam June Paik Prize 2024 nominee.

- **[Modified Paradise: Dress](https://ars.electronica.art/outofthebox/en/paradise/)** (as **AnotherFarm**, 2019) — Sculptural dress made from "luminescent silk" from GMO silkworms. Floats within a frame with no body. Shown at: Cooper Hewitt "Nature: Collaborations in Design" (2019), Ars Electronica Center (2019), Triennale di Milano "Broken Nature" (2019). *Three major museum shows in one year for the same work.*

- **Tokyo Medical University for Rejected Women** (2019, TED Fellows context) — multimedia art exploring systematic discrimination of female applicants to medical school in Japan (real scandal: TMU secretly downgraded women's scores for years).

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Empathy machine as provocation-first.** Sputniko!'s primary strategy is: take a social asymmetry (men don't experience menstruation; we don't experience Fukushima soil; we optimize away the search for luck) → build a device that lets you *feel* it → make a pop music video that performs the experience publicly. This is provocation-first, but the provocation works because the *device is technically real* (or could be). She never bluffs — the silk really glows, the rapeseed really plants, the GMO silkworms actually exist.

**Doradical design**: her self-coined term for Critical Design done via Japanese popular entertainment aesthetics (Doraemon has "future gadgets" that explore ethics; manga has done this since the 60s). This reframes Critical Design as native to Japan, not imported from UK — a powerful positioning move.

**The music video as format.** Most speculative designers use objects + text. Sputniko! adds *herself as performer* in a pop music context. This makes the work accessible to mainstream Japanese audiences AND gives her celebrity status (Vogue Japan, Nikkei Business) that most artists never achieve. The format is the message: technology critique + pop culture = broad reach.

### Career Architecture

**Field declaration through format invention.** She didn't just make art — she invented *how* to make a particular kind of art (music video speculative design) that couldn't be easily replicated. By making herself the performer, she personified the work in a way that creates a unique brand.

**Strategic migration at key moments:**
1. Imperial College → RCA: moved from pure math/programming into art but kept the technical rigor
2. RCA graduation → MOT exhibition before graduation: the work was so strong it demanded immediate institutional recognition
3. RCA → MIT Media Lab: moved into the most prestigious tech-art institution in the world at the right moment (2013, peak speculative design)
4. MIT → University of Tokyo → Geidai: returned to Japan with international credentials that made her untouchable domestically
5. Geidai → SDS Chibatech: now building with Joi Ito on a new institution — moving from institution-joining to institution-building

**Pop personality as strategy.** Being on Vogue Japan, being "famous" in mainstream Japanese media, is not incidental — it's how she reaches audiences that speculative design never reaches. This is intentional. Her work asks questions about gender and technology at a population level, not just in gallery contexts.

### Methodology Signature

**Art-science bridging with actual science.** The silkworms are real. The GMO works. The fluorescent silk actually glows. This is not design fiction using fake science — it's design fiction using *frontier science as material*. This gives her a credibility that most speculative designers lack.

**Permanent installation as lifetime commitment.** The Teshima 8 Million Lab (2016-2020) ran for 4 years as a permanent shrine. This is a different scale of commitment than exhibition work. It says: I believe in this enough to build a place of worship.

**Entrepreneurship as extension of practice.** Cradle is not a side project — it's the non-art instantiation of the same critique. If art asks "what if women's healthcare was taken seriously in Japanese corporations?" then Cradle *is* the answer. This is rare: most speculative designers don't build the thing they're critiquing into.

### Key Moves (chronological)

1. **2010: Menstruation Machine at MOT before RCA graduation** — unprecedented; the institution came to her, not the other way around. Established that her work demanded institutional response immediately.

2. **2011: MoMA's Talk to Me** — the international market signal. From Tokyo design student to MoMA in one year. This made her globally legible.

3. **2013: MIT Media Lab appointment** — moved into the most globally influential tech-culture institution. Founded Design Fiction group from scratch, which gave her the infrastructure to mentor the next generation (Ai Hasegawa etc.).

4. **2016: Teshima 8 Million Lab** — permanent installation at Setouchi Art Triennale. Moved from international gallery circuit into deep Japanese site-specificity. This is when she became embedded in Japan's art landscape, not just passing through.

5. **2017: WEF Young Global Leaders** — entry into a completely different network: global governance, business leaders, policy makers. This is how Cradle became possible. Speculative designers don't usually join WEF.

6. **2018: AnotherFarm formation** — moved from solo practice into collective working with craftspeople (Masaya Kushino, kimono/fashion tradition) and scientists. This signals a shift toward collaborative, material-first practice.

7. **2025: SDS Chibatech founding faculty** — institution-building alongside Joi Ito. This is the most interesting recent move. If SDS succeeds, she'll have helped create a new intellectual node.

### What Amber Can Learn

**The empathy device as research format.** Instead of writing a paper about more-than-human design, build a thing that makes someone *feel* the more-than-human perspective. The Menstruation Machine is a research intervention that no paper could match. Amber's interest in machine death, machine behavior — what would the equivalent "empathy machine" be?

**Pop format as reach strategy.** Amber works in academic contexts (CHI, AAAI, FAccT). Sputniko! uses pop music videos to reach millions. Is there a way to embed research critique in forms that reach beyond academic circuits? She deliberately chose the most popular format available in Japan (music video + idol culture).

**Japan as a different cosmotechnical context.** Teshima 8 Million Lab puts GMO silkworms inside Shinto animism. This is exactly the kind of East Asian cosmotechnics connection that Amber cares about (Yuk Hui, cosmotechnics). Sputniko! is doing it in practice, not just theory.

**Entrepreneur + artist combination.** Cradle shows that the art critique and the commercial product can coexist. Amber has HoloKit — the same move from art/research to product. The models rhyme.

**Mentorship as network building.** Sputniko! → Ai Hasegawa is a 1:1 relationship that produced a second globally significant artist. Building the person, not just the programme.
