# Lauren Cornell

Chief Curator of the Hessel Museum of Art and Director of the Graduate Program at the [Center for Curatorial Studies, Bard College (CCS Bard)](https://ccs.bard.edu/). Former Executive Director of [Rhizome](https://rhizome.org/) (2005–2012) and curator at the [New Museum](https://www.newmuseum.org/) (2007–2017). Co-editor of *[Mass Effect: Art and the Internet in the Twenty-First Century](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262029261/mass-effect/)* (MIT Press, 2015) and co-founder of the [Seven on Seven](https://rhizome.org/seven-on-seven/) conference. One of the most important architect-curators of the digital turn in contemporary art.

## Background

Cornell was born and raised in New York City. Before entering institutional curatorial work, she ran an avant-garde cinema/screening space in Brooklyn focused on experimental film and video — an origin story that explains both her deep commitment to moving-image practice and her preference for artists working between media and contexts.

**Rhizome (2005–2012):** Cornell joined Rhizome in 2005 as Executive Director, where she built one of the most rigorous platforms for internet-based and technologically-engaged art. At Rhizome she held together art history and digital culture at a moment when that bridge was contested from both sides: fine art institutions were skeptical of net art's institutional legitimacy, while the tech world didn't take art seriously. By 2007 she also held an Adjunct Curator position at the New Museum (Rhizome's institutional partner), letting her bring net art into that building's programming. She stepped down from Rhizome in July 2012 to focus on curatorial work at the New Museum full-time, including the 2015 Triennial.

**New Museum (2007–2017):** Alongside the Triennial, Cornell organized a series of exhibitions exploring the internet's transformation of public life, circulation, and art practice. The 2009 Generational (*Younger Than Jesus*) was a landmark international survey of artists born after 1976 — co-curated with Massimiliano Gioni and Laura Hoptman, it introduced the "millennial generation" as an art-world subject. *Free* (2010) and *Walking Drifting Dragging* (2013) followed her signature thread of art engaging with digital infrastructure and public space. The *Surround Audience* triennial (2015, co-curated with Ryan Trecartin) crystallized her thinking about the post-internet condition.

**CCS Bard (2017–present):** Cornell was appointed Chief Curator of the Hessel Museum and Director of the CCS Graduate Program simultaneously — the first person to hold both roles at once. At Bard, her curatorial program has deepened to include major retrospectives of video art pioneers (Dara Birnbaum, Nil Yalter), socially engaged work (Sky Hopinka), and surveys of painters working at the boundary of cultural memory and visual identity (Leidy Churchman, Martine Syms, Erika Verzutti). The international footprint is evident in co-organizing the Hong Kong cyberpunk exhibition *Phantom Plane* (2019, Tai Kwun Contemporary) with curators Dawn Chan, Xue Tan, Tobias Berger, and Jeppe Ugelvig.

Her intellectual formation bridges critical theory, media studies, and the history of video and performance art — she writes with fluency about technology's social effects while staying rooted in specific artistic practices and their material and historical contexts.

## Key Works / Curatorial Projects

### Exhibitions (chronological)

**At the New Museum:**
- **Young-Hae Chang Heavy Industries: *Black on White, Gray Ascending*** (Dec 2007–Mar 2008, New Museum) — co-curated with Laura Hoptman. Early showcase of Korean net art collective's typography-based web art.
- **The Generational: *Younger Than Jesus*** (Apr–Jul 2009, New Museum) — co-curated with [[Massimiliano Gioni]] and Laura Hoptman. 50 artists from 25 countries, all born after 1976. The first major international museum exhibition devoted to the "millennial generation" in art. Artists selected through a participatory process of 150+ international correspondents. Catalogue co-published with Steidl.
- **[*Free*](https://archive.newmuseum.org/exhibitions/1092)** (Oct 2010–Jan 2011, New Museum) — solo curatorial. Explored how the internet transformed the notion of public space and information commons. Inspired by Seth Price's essay "Dispersion." Catalogue took the form of an active website.
- ***Walking Drifting Dragging*** (Jan–Feb 2013, New Museum) — solo curatorial. Explored the figure of the urban wanderer in contemporary art.
- **New Museum Triennial: *[Surround Audience](https://www.newmuseum.org/exhibitions/view/surround-audience)*** (Feb–May 2015, New Museum) — co-curated with Ryan Trecartin. The third edition of The Generational; named for the totalizing mediated environments of contemporary life. Catalogue co-published with Rizzoli Skira, edited with Helga Christoffersen.
- **Beatriz Santiago Muñoz: *Song, Strategy, Sign*** (New Museum) — co-curated with Johanna Burton and Sara O'Keeffe. Puerto Rican artist working in experimental documentary and anti-colonial counter-histories.

**Bridging New Museum and CCS Bard:**
- **[*Invisible Adversaries*](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/234-invisible-adversaries)** (Jun–Sep 2016, Hessel Museum/CCS Bard) — co-curated with [[Tom Eccles]]. Group exhibition marking CCS Bard's 25th anniversary; title taken from Valie Export's 1977 feminist sci-fi film. Addressed politics of visibility, feminist art, and technology.

**At CCS Bard / Hessel Museum:**
- **Daniel Steegmann Mangrané: *[A Transparent Leaf Instead of The Mouth](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/249-daniel-steegmann-mangrane-a-transparent-leaf-instead-of-the-mouth)*** (2018, CCS Bard) — first US institutional solo show of the Barcelona/Rio-based artist. Commissioned large-scale glass pavilion containing a live ecosystem combining local foliage and foreign insects. Work explores permutation, interdependency, and entanglement across life forms.
- **Nil Yalter: *[Exile Is a Hard Job](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/529-nil-yalter-exile-is-a-hard-job)*** (2019, CCS Bard) — co-organized with Museum Ludwig, Cologne. First solo US museum exhibition of Yalter (b. 1938, Cairo/Istanbul/Paris). Focused on 1960s–80s work: video, photography, and socially-engaged installations addressing migrant workers, women's prisons, and displacement. Cornell wrote the catalogue essay alongside Fabienne Dumont and Övül Durmusoglu.
- **[*Phantom Plane: Cyberpunk in the Year of the Future*](https://www.taikwun.hk/en/programme/detail/phantom-plane-cyberpunk-in-the-year-of-the-future/467)** (2019, Tai Kwun Contemporary, Hong Kong) — co-curated with Dawn Chan, Xue Tan, Jeppe Ugelvig, and Tobias Berger. Group exhibition examining how cyberpunk's visual grammar (set in 2019, the "year of the future" in *Blade Runner* and *Akira*) had become a permanent present. Artists included: Nadim Abbas, Aria Dean, Tishan Hsu, JODI, Lee Bul, Seth Price, Jon Rafman, Sondra Perry, Zheng Mahler, and others. Co-presented by CCS Bard.
- **Leidy Churchman: *[Crocodile](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/528-leidy-churchman-crocodile)*** (2019, CCS Bard) — most extensive US survey of Churchman's painting, with 60+ works including newly commissioned floor painting. Catalogue published by Dancing Foxes Press, with essays by Ruba Katrib and Arnisa Zeqo. Cornell contributed a conversation with the artist.
- **Sky Hopinka: *[Centers of Somewhere](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/560-sky-hopinka-centers-of-somewhere)*** (2020, CCS Bard) — survey of Ho-Chunk Nation artist and filmmaker's work; included newly commissioned multi-channel video *Here you are before the trees* (2020) exploring Indigenous histories of the Hudson Valley. CCS Bard co-published *Perfidia*, a book of Hopinka's writings, with Wendy's Subway.
- **Dara Birnbaum: *[Reaction](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/695-dara-birnbaum-reaction)*** (2022, CCS Bard) — the first US retrospective of the video art pioneer; works 1975–2011. Title echoes Birnbaum's career-long "talking back to the media." Catalogue contributors: Alex Kitnick, Jordan Carter (Dia), Erika Balsom, Giampaolo Bianconi, Legacy Russell, Elizabeth Chodos. Partnered with a complementary thematic survey at Miller ICA, Carnegie Mellon.
- **Martine Syms: *[Grio College](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/696-martine-syms-grio-college)*** (2022, CCS Bard) — staging of Syms's work 2017–2022, taking its name from the fictional school in Syms's feature film *The African Desperate* (2022). Newly commissioned sweatshirt sculptures embedded with videos from her work *Lessons*. Script published by Nightboat Books.
- **Erika Verzutti: *[New Moons](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/718-erika-verzutti-new-moons)*** (2023, CCS Bard) — first US survey of the São Paulo artist; 60+ wall works and sculptures in bronze, clay, aluminum, papier maché. Catalogue co-published with Dancing Foxes, ISLAA, and CCS Bard; Cornell contributed an essay.
- **Liliana Porter: *[The Gardener and Other Situations](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/728-liliana-porter-the-gardener-and-other-situations)*** (2023/24, CCS Bard) — intimate presentation of the Argentine/New York artist's work; precarity, narrative, and the contingency of plot.

## Seven on Seven Conference

Co-founded in 2010 with **Fred Benenson**, **John Borthwick**, and **[Peter Rojas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Rojas)** (the GDGT/Engadget journalist). Annual one-day conference at the New Museum pairing seven visual artists with seven technology innovators, challenging each pair to create something new together in 24 hours. Deliberately modelled on Billy Klüver and Robert Rauschenberg's [Experiments in Art and Technology (E.A.T.)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experiments_in_Art_and_Technology) at Bell Labs (1967).

Notable pairings have included: **Taryn Simon × Aaron Swartz** (produced *Image Atlas*, a search engine comparing results across national contexts); artists including Evan Roth, Monica Narula, Ryan Trecartin, Tauba Auerbach, Liz Magic Laser, Rashaad Newsome, LaToya Ruby Frazier, Jon Rafman; technologists including Jeff Hammerbacher, Joshua Schachter, Matt Mullenweg, Hilary Mason, Andrew Kortina, David Karp.

The conference was a deliberate institutional claim: that artists and technologists need to be in direct conversation, not mediated by press cycles or product launches.

## *Mass Effect* Anthology

[*Mass Effect: Art and the Internet in the Twenty-First Century*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262029261/mass-effect/) (MIT Press, 2015; co-edited with [[Ed Halter]]) is the canonical reference text for understanding the shift from net art (niche, 1990s) to the internet as a mass medium shaping all of contemporary art practice. Published in the MIT Press "Critical Anthologies in Art and Culture" series. The title plays on both media "mass effect" and the video game — art about technology that also uses technology's own cultural syntax.

## Venue / Circuit Analysis

Cornell operates across **three distinct but connected circuits**:

1. **New York institutional art world:** New Museum / CCS Bard. These are the two most intellectually serious contexts in NY for the art-technology intersection. She has built her entire career here, which is a deliberate choice to work at depth rather than spread thin.

2. **International contemporary art circuit:** Tai Kwun Contemporary (Hong Kong), Museum Ludwig (Cologne). Selective but pointed international collaborations — she doesn't spray presence across biennials but chooses specific co-organizational partnerships that build lasting institutional relationships.

3. **Art-technology publishing circuit:** MIT Press, Rizzoli Skira, Dancing Foxes Press, Steidl. Her catalogue work is unusually strong for a curator — she treats the publication as a second argument alongside the exhibition, not a souvenir.

Her writing circuit: *Frieze*, *Mousse*, *Art in America*, *Aperture* — all the major critical venues, but always essays with genuine argument, not puff pieces. She has remained a writer while ascending to administrative leadership, which is unusual.

The artists she champions fall into a few overlapping clusters: (1) artists working in video/digital and claiming that lineage from feminist '70s video art (Birnbaum, Yalter); (2) artists from Latin America using ecological, bodily, or vernacular knowledge systems (Steegmann Mangrané, Verzutti, Porter); (3) artists for whom digital media is the site where race, gender, and representation are contested (Syms, Hopinka, Churchman). These aren't separate interests — they converge on the question of how media shapes identity and whose identity gets to be mediated.

## Why Interesting for Amber

Cornell is the proof of concept that a rigorous art-technology practice leads *into* institutional power rather than away from it. She navigated from running a small screening space in Brooklyn → net art organisation → co-curating a major triennial → leading a top-flight curatorial graduate program — without ever softening her intellectual commitments.

The *Mass Effect* anthology is the key text for the intellectual genealogy Amber is operating within. Cornell and Halter essentially wrote the canonical history of the art-internet intersection, which is the immediate history that AI art is now extending.

**Overlap with Amber:** The Seven on Seven model (artists + technologists, one day, produce something) is structurally very similar to Amber's interest in collaborative art-science practice. The *Phantom Plane* cyberpunk exhibition is directly relevant to Amber's cosmotechnics/sinofuturism work. Cornell is among the most likely people to be interested in work that bridges technological agency, identity, and art history — which is Amber's exact territory.

**Strategic value:** Cornell runs the graduate program that trains the next generation of curators who will be deciding what technology art gets shown in the next decade. Being on her radar, or having students engage with Amber's practice, would be a force multiplier.
