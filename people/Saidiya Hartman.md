# Saidiya Hartman

University Professor of English and Comparative Literature at **Columbia University**. One of the most consequential theorists of the Black radical tradition. Hartman practices what she calls **"critical fabulation"** — a writing methodology that combines archival research with speculative narrative to give voice to lives the historical record has omitted, silenced, or distorted. Coined the phrase "the **afterlife of slavery**" — now perhaps the most widely cited concept in Black studies. Her three books each represent a distinct methodological advance; her influence extends deep into contemporary art, literature, and social movements.

Born Valarie, she later took the name Saidiya — Swahili for "to help."

## Background

Hartman grew up in Brooklyn in a mixed immigrant neighborhood — Panamanian and Haitian neighbors, a Jewish lesbian couple who were close family friends. Her father, Virgilio Hartman, came from Curaçao; her mother, Beryle, from Montgomery, Alabama (Tuskegee/Tennessee State lineage). The collision of diasporic identities was formative. She attended Stuyvesant High School, then Wesleyan University (BA 1984), then briefly NYU's film program (left unhappy), then Yale for her doctorate (PhD 1992).

Her dissertation committee at Yale included **Hazel Carby** (Birmingham School of Cultural Studies) and she was deeply shaped by **Gayatri Spivak**'s post-structuralism. As an undergraduate at Wesleyan she had **Judith Butler** as a professor — Butler now teaches Hartman's books in her own courses.

She was a professor in the Department of English and African American Studies at **UC Berkeley from 1992 to 2006** — a period that shaped a generation of scholars, including former student **Frank B. Wilderson III** (who would later coin "Afropessimism"). She joined Columbia in 2007, was former director of the Institute for Research on Gender and Sexuality at Columbia, and was named **University Professor** in 2020 — the highest faculty distinction Columbia bestows, reserved for scholars whose work transcends disciplinary limits.

She lives in Brooklyn. Her partner is Samuel Miller, a civil-rights attorney; they have a daughter.

## Key Works

### Books
- **[*Scenes of Subjection: Terror, Slavery, and Self-Making in Nineteenth-Century America*](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/scenes-of-subjection-9780195089844)** (Oxford University Press, 1997; revised 25th anniversary edition [W.W. Norton, 2022](https://wwnorton.com/books/9781324091660)) — foundational text examining how slavery structured not only terror but the forms of resistance available to the enslaved; challenges the commonsense idea that recognition of humanity ends violence (it often enables new forms); argues slavery is foundational to America's concept of freedom itself. The 2022 edition includes: new foreword by **Keeanga-Yamahtta Taylor**, afterword by Marisa Fuentes and Sarah Haley, notations by **Torkwase Dyson** and **Cameron Rowland**. Had a "chilly reception" initially; graduate students kept it alive; became retroactively canonical.

- **[*Lose Your Mother: A Journey Along the Atlantic Slave Route*](https://us.macmillan.com/books/9780374531157/loseyourmother)** (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2007) — combines memoir and historiography; follows Hartman's year-long journey tracing the slave trade routes in Ghana. First major deployment of critical fabulation: reimagines the case of an African girl murdered by a ship captain, cited in an abolitionist speech by Wilberforce in 1792. Excerpt won the **2007 Narrative Prize** from Narrative Magazine.

- **[*Wayward Lives, Beautiful Experiments: Intimate Histories of Riotous Black Girls, Troublesome Women, and Queer Radicals*](https://wwnorton.com/books/9780393356403)** (W.W. Norton, 2019) — critical fabulation at full scale: recovers the radical lives of young Black women and queer people in turn-of-the-century Philadelphia and New York (1890s–1920s). Draws on sociological surveys, tenement photographs, reformatory case files, and photographs like Thomas Eakins' *Photograph 308* (the same image Fred Moten analyzes in *Black and Blur*). Shows the archive's violence — "these are the lives the official record recorded only to condemn." Won the **2019 National Book Critics Circle Award for Criticism**. Listed by NYT as **#96 in the Top 100 Books of the 21st Century** (2024).

### Key Essays
- **["Venus in Two Acts"](https://www.jstor.org/stable/27802430)** — *Small Axe* 12, no. 2 (2008): 1–14. The canonical statement of "critical fabulation": attempts to recover the story of a girl named Venus on the slave ship Recovery, confronts the limits of what can be known, introduces "narrative restraint" (the ethical refusal to fill all gaps). Probably the most cited methodological essay in Black studies of the last two decades.

- **"The Belly of the World: A Note on Black Women's Labors"** — *Souls: A Critical Journal of Black Politics, Culture, and Society* 18, no. 1 (2016): 166–173. On Black women's reproductive, domestic, and affective labor as the foundation of the world's wealth — and on the archive's failure to count this labor as labor.

- **"The Description of a Slave Ship"** — essay engaging the famous Brooks slave ship diagram as image, archive, and aesthetic object. Published in collaboration with various artistic contexts.

- Essays in *South Atlantic Quarterly*, *Brick*, *Small Axe*, *Callaloo*, *The New Yorker*, *The Paris Review*, and exhibition catalogues.

- Essay in **Glenn Ligon: *America*** (Whitney Museum of American Art) — on the afterlife of slavery in contemporary Black aesthetics.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Hartman operates in a very specific and distinctive circuit:

**1. Elite literary / intellectual institutions** (her primary home): Columbia, Yale, UC Berkeley, Princeton (Whitney Oates), NYPL Cullman Center, University of Chicago. These are the credentialing spaces — but she moves through them with evident unease with their conventions.

**2. Contemporary art world** (her most interesting circuit for Amber): MoMA PS1, Serpentine Gallery (London), Hammer Museum (LA), Whitney Museum of American Art. This is unusual for a literary scholar — she's not just cited by artists, she's *in the room* with them, at book celebrations, at museum openings. Arthur Jafa, Glenn Ligon, Lorraine O'Grady, Okwui Okpokwasili, Cameron Rowland, Torkwase Dyson — these are the people she moves with. Her works inspire art installations, performances, films. The 2022 *Scenes of Subjection* anniversary edition was published more like an artist's book than an academic text.

**3. Popular/mass cultural crossover** (unprecedented for a humanities scholar): The Jay-Z "4:44" appearance is extraordinary — a leading scholar of Black life appearing in one of the most-anticipated hip-hop albums of 2017, directed by Arthur Jafa. This is not the standard "scholar on a podcast" crossover; it's a full artistic collaboration.

**4. Literary / activist circles**: Mariame Kaba, Nikole Hannah-Jones (MacArthur cohort), The Nation, London Review of Books, The New Yorker, The Paris Review. Her writing in these venues demonstrates that she refuses the academic enclosure.

**Signal from small venues:** The Serpentine event (organized by Rizvana Bradley) was intimate and intellectually driven — not a public lecture but a "day of study." This is a strong signal: she doesn't just do prestige appearances; she values the sustained, slow, generative encounter.

## Why Interesting

For Amber, Hartman matters on several axes:

**1. Methodology as design problem.** "Critical fabulation" is not just a literary technique — it's a principled response to working with incomplete, biased, or hostile archives. The historical archive of slavery is the ultimate case study in how power determines what gets recorded and what disappears. Hartman asks: how do you recover what was never meant to be preserved? This is directly analogous to the problem of AI training data: the archive of the internet is also a site of systematic exclusion, misrepresentation, and violence. Her methods are transferable.

**2. "Afterlife" as a temporal concept.** The "afterlife of slavery" is a way of thinking about how past structures reproduce themselves in present systems without being acknowledged. "Afterlife of colonialism" in HCI/CSCW research is an underdeveloped but growing area. Hartman gives it its richest theoretical grounding.

**3. Art-world / humanities intersection.** Hartman is evidence that humanities scholarship can be genuinely artistically generative — her work directly inspires artists (Arthur Jafa, Kara Walker, Cameron Rowland, Torkwase Dyson, Jeremy O. Harris). For Amber's interdisciplinary practice, this circuit (scholar ↔ artist ↔ institution) is exactly the kind of cross-pollination that produces new forms.

**4. Speculative method in practice.** Critical fabulation is essentially "speculative design applied to the past" — recovering not what happened but what might plausibly have happened, and in doing so revealing the mechanisms that suppressed the real. This connects to Amber's interest in design fiction and speculative methods.

**5. The archive as design object.** Hartman's sustained engagement with archives — their structure, their silences, their violence — is productive for thinking about data, databases, and information systems as designed artifacts with embedded politics.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

Hartman is a **concept-coiner of extraordinary precision**. Her two most cited concepts — "critical fabulation" and "afterlife of slavery" — share a structure: they name phenomena that were already intuited but unnamed, and by naming them, they make them thinkable and citable across disciplines. This is a deliberate strategy. She doesn't just describe; she **mints vocabulary** that others can use as tools.

Her conceptual work also has a **negative dimension** — "narrative restraint" is a concept that tells you what *not* to do, an unusual move in academic writing. She theorizes the ethical limit of the method itself, which gives it credibility precisely by admitting its failure conditions.

### Career Architecture

**Three-phase career of escalating methodological risk:**

1. **Phase 1 (Scenes of Subjection, 1997):** Critical theory in conventional academic form. Rigorous, dense, grounded in historical sources. Had a "chilly reception" — but graduate students kept it alive for 25 years. **Key lesson:** write for the cohort that doesn't exist yet. The book found its moment when the moment caught up to it.

2. **Phase 2 (Lose Your Mother, 2007):** First major departure from academic form. Combines memoir with historiography; breaks genre conventions. This is the risk moment — stepping out of the academy-approved container. The critical fabulation method gets its first full deployment. **Key lesson:** once you've established credibility in the conventional form, use it to buy freedom to experiment.

3. **Phase 3 (Wayward Lives, 2019):** Full methodological maturity. The book is a critical text, a historical study, a literary work, and a visual essay simultaneously. Gets a major commercial publication (W.W. Norton), wins mainstream awards (National Book Critics Circle), makes the NYT Top 100. The experiment worked. **Key lesson:** the long game — the methodology that seemed marginal in 1997 became the dominant mode for an entire field by 2019.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **Writing toward slavery when it was "boring" (late 1980s–1991):** She was writing a dissertation on the blues when she discovered she couldn't understand it without understanding slavery. Most peers at Yale thought this was retrograde. She persisted against the tide. **Why it worked:** the "post-racial moment" of the 1990s was already cracking; she was early.

2. **Sitting with the archive's violence rather than overcoming it (1997):** *Scenes of Subjection* doesn't rescue enslaved people from the archive; it analyzes how the archive is itself an instrument of their domination. This refusal to be a redemptive historian was unusual and uncomfortable. **What it enabled:** a generation of scholars who learned to read archives against their grain.

3. **Coining "afterlife of slavery" (2007):** A two-word concept that changed how historians, artists, policy scholars, and activists understood the relationship between past and present. It wasn't just a metaphor — it was a structural claim about how power reproduces itself. **What it enabled:** the entire critical infrastructure of contemporary anti-racist scholarship, from the 1619 Project to prison abolition theory.

4. **The Jafa collaboration (2017):** Appearing in the Jay-Z "4:44" short film signaled that Hartman understood her ideas had moved beyond the academy. Choosing to appear in a hip-hop film directed by a visual artist — not a PBS documentary — was a deliberate aesthetic choice about where Black intellectual life lives. **What it enabled:** legitimized the art-scholarship boundary crossing that she had already been doing quietly.

5. **The 2022 *Scenes* re-edition as artist book:** Bringing in Cameron Rowland and Torkwase Dyson — artists, not scholars — to add visual commentary to her foundational theoretical text was an institutional declaration: these ideas belong to art as much as to academia. **What it enabled:** a new pathway for academic texts to enter the art world without becoming pop-academic.

### Methodology Signature

- **Negative capability as method.** Keats' concept — the ability to remain in uncertainty without irritably reaching after facts. Hartman's narrative restraint is the methodological formalization of this. She builds her credibility on what she *refuses* to claim, as much as what she asserts.
- **Multi-genre refusal.** She consistently refuses to be only a literary scholar, only a historian, only a memoirist. Her books cannot be shelved in a single section — they go in American History, in African American Literature, in Cultural Theory, in Narrative Nonfiction. This is not sloppiness; it's strategic unclassifiability.
- **Working at the limit.** Every major project is about an impossible task: recovering the voices of those who were systematically prevented from leaving records. The impossibility is the content, not the problem. She writes *about* the impossibility as much as she writes through it.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Coin the concept before you fully know what it is.** "Critical fabulation" and "afterlife of slavery" were named before they were fully theorized. The name creates the pressure to theorize. Amber might try applying this to her own work: name the thing, then figure it out.

2. **Write for the cohort that doesn't exist yet.** *Scenes of Subjection* found its moment 20 years after publication. The most important work is often out of phase with its audience. Publish it anyway; the moment may catch up.

3. **Refusal as method.** Hartman's career is built on a series of refusals: to fill in the gaps, to rescue the victims, to stay within genre, to stay within the academy. Refusal is not passivity — it's a form of rigor. Amber might ask: what should she refuse to do in her own research that everyone expects her to do?

4. **The art world as theoretical laboratory.** Hartman's relationships with Arthur Jafa, Cameron Rowland, Torkwase Dyson are not peripheral to her intellectual project — they are part of it. Art makes ideas visible and tangible that academic prose cannot. Amber is already positioned at this intersection; Hartman's circuit is a model for how to work it.

5. **The slow accumulation of the indefensible position.** Hartman spent years making claims that were uncomfortable, unpopular, and hard to refute. Over time, the world changed in a direction that made her indefensible positions defensible. This is a high-variance, high-conviction strategy — it requires willingness to be wrong about the timing even while being right about the substance.
