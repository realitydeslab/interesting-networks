# Saidiya Hartman

Professor of English and Comparative Literature at **Columbia University**. One of the most important scholars of the Black radical tradition. Her work sits at the intersection of history, literary criticism, and narrative nonfiction — she practices what she calls **"critical fabulation"**: archival research that fills in the gaps of the historical record through speculation grounded in documentary evidence.

Closely connected to [[Fred Moten]] through overlapping interests in Black aesthetics, slavery, and the history of vision/sound.

## Background
- BA and PhD from Yale University
- Currently professor at Columbia University
- MacArthur Fellow (2019)
- Previously taught at UC Berkeley, Ohio State University

## Key Works
- *[Scenes of Subjection: Terror, Slavery, and Self-Making in Nineteenth-Century America](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/scenes-of-subjection-9780195089844)* (Oxford University Press, 1997) — foundational text; examines the structure of racial slavery through performance (the "scenes" of degradation, punishment, and coerced performance)
- *[Lose Your Mother: A Journey Along the Atlantic Slave Route](https://us.macmillan.com/books/9780374531157/loseyourmother)* (Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2007) — memoir and history; follows the slave trade routes in Ghana
- *[Wayward Lives, Beautiful Experiments: Intimate Histories of Social Upheaval](https://wwnorton.com/books/9780393356403)* (W.W. Norton, 2019) — narrative history of Black women's radical lives in early 20th century Philadelphia and New York; practices "critical fabulation" to recover lives the archive barely records. Discusses the Thomas Eakins photograph #308 — the same image Moten analyzes in *Black and Blur*.
- *Venus in Two Acts* (essay, 2008) — key statement of the "critical fabulation" method

## Key Connections
- **[[Fred Moten]]** — overlapping engagement with the Thomas Eakins photograph #308; both working on the intersection of Black life, visuality, and archive
- **[[Stefano Harney]]** — overlapping Black radical tradition orbit
- **Christina Sharpe** — Black studies theorist, author of *In the Wake*; closely connected

## Why Interesting (for Amber)
- **Critical fabulation** is a methodological concept with potential applications in speculative design — how do you design for lives the archive doesn't record? What would a design practice that fills historical gaps through grounded speculation look like?
- Her work on **performance as a site of subjection and resistance** parallels Moten's "phonic substance" — both are thinking about sound, embodiment, and the limits of the visible
- The Hartman-Moten-Manning triangle (Black Studies ↔ Performance Studies ↔ Neurodiversity) is one of the most generative theoretical intersections in current humanities
