# Gary Hall

Professor of Media at **Coventry University**, co-founder and Director of **[[Open Humanities Press]]**, founding figure of the **[[Radical Open Access Collective]]**, and co-founder of the **Centre for Postdigital Cultures**. Experimental media philosopher working at the intersections of digital theory, culture, and politics — treating publishing itself as philosophical practice. His career is a sustained, 30-year attempt to demonstrate that critical theory and open access are not compatible positions but a single, indivisible commitment.

## Background
Hall's trajectory is unusual: he began as a continental philosopher trained in Derrida and cultural theory (his earliest papers are on Bhabha, deconstruction, and cultural studies' relationship to critical theory), and from the mid-1990s began to ask: what would it mean to *practice* the theories we profess to believe, rather than just publish them in commercial journals? This question — at once philosophical and institutional — has driven everything since.

His earliest academic post was at Teesside University; he moved to Coventry University where he has spent most of his career, rising to Professor of Media. At Coventry he founded and ran the **Disruptive Media Learning Lab** (later **Open Media Group**), a collaborative research-practice lab, and co-founded the **Centre for Postdigital Cultures** (CPC) in 2017.

The CPC was a genuinely unusual institution: not a department or institute but a centre explicitly committed to exploring what scholarly practice looks like *after* the digital — not celebrating technology but asking what political and ethical choices it forces. Members included [[Janneke Adema]], Mel Jordan, Andrew Hewitt (Partisan Social Club), and others. By 2025 Hall had stepped back from formally directing it.

In parallel, his **Media Gifts** blog/website and the range of projects he calls "media gifts" constitute an experimental philosophy practice: projects like Culture Machine journal, Open Humanities Press, Liquid Books, Living Books About Life, the Open Scholarship Full Disclosure Initiative — each designed to *enact* rather than merely argue for open, commons-based scholarly practice.

His Latin American circuit (especially the **17, Institute for Critical Studies** in Mexico City, with whom he has collaborated since 2009) reflects a serious transnational intellectual commitment — not just keynoting but developing long-term publishing and educational projects with Mexican and Colombian scholars.

## Why Interesting
Hall is the most principled and persistent radical open access advocate in UK academia, and that's only the surface. Beneath the OA politics is a sustained philosophical argument: that the forms through which knowledge circulates are not neutral containers but are themselves doing ideological work. A book published by MIT Press by a single named human author enacts a specific set of assumptions about authorship, originality, intellectual property, the liberal humanist subject — and all of these, Hall argues, are contestable.

*Masked Media* (2025) is the culmination of 30 years of this argument: a book co-written with AI, collective human collaborators, and published under a non-standard licence that explicitly refuses the "human-authored work" category. This is not a stunt. It is a philosophical position enacted through the form of the book itself — and for that reason it demands engagement with Amber's interests in AI, posthumanism, and what creative intelligence means.

His Latin American intellectual circuit (17 Institute since 2009) is a genuine rarity: a UK academic who has built serious long-term intellectual relationships in Mexico and Colombia, not just given one-off keynotes. His connections to Gabriela Méndez Cota, Felix Stalder, Cornelia Sollfrank, and the Creating Commons project also place him squarely in the European critical commons community.

Finally: *Defund Culture* (forthcoming) will be a major intervention on British arts and class. Its argument — that the arts are structurally white, male, and middle-class — extends the class critique of *A Stubborn Fury* to institutional critique. This is relevant terrain for anyone thinking about who gets to participate in cultural and intellectual life.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy
Hall's core move is **provocation-through-form**: he doesn't just argue that existing forms of scholarly production are politically problematic — he *instantiates* the alternatives he describes. This is what he calls a "media gift": a project that enacts the theory rather than representing it.

**Framework multiplication:** He takes existing theoretical frameworks (Derrida, Stiegler, Foucault, posthumanism) and applies them to the sociology of scholarly publishing — a domain those frameworks have never been turned on. The result is conceptually productive: Derrida on authorship + the OA debate = a genuinely new question about whether "open access" can be more than a reformist tweak to liberal humanism.

**Two-word concept coining:** "Pirate Philosophy", "Liquid Books", "Living Books", "Postdigital Cultures" — Hall has an unusual capacity to name positions in ways that travel. "Uberfication" became a meme in UK higher education circles precisely because it named something real in a memorable way.

**Provocation-first:** Starting with the outrageous claim ("We need to pirate academic publishing", "There is no such thing as the human") and then doing the philosophical work. This is high risk but high reward: it ensures the argument gets a hearing.

### Career Architecture
**Platform-building as primary strategy**: Hall's career is not built around publications in the standard sense. It's built around *institutions* — Culture Machine (1999), CSeARCH (2006), OHP (2006/2008), Liquid Books (2008), Living Books About Life (2011), Radical Open Access Collective (2015), Centre for Postdigital Cultures (2017), COPIM (2019), mediastudies.press (2025). Each institution is itself a publication, argument, and community-forming act.

**Coalition papers as territory claims**: The collaboratively authored books (*Open Education*, *Públicos Fantasma*, *Experimental Publishing Compendium*) function as manifestos — they signal a community, not just an argument.

**Strategic positioning against the mainstream**: Hall doesn't just argue against commercial academic publishing from within it (by publishing with MIT, Minnesota, etc.) — he simultaneously establishes the alternative infrastructure. The combination of "I can do prestige venues when necessary" + "I'm building the alternative" is a specific power move.

### Methodology Signature
**The experimental book as method**: Each book is also an experiment in form. *Pirate Philosophy* is itself written "as a pirate" — it appropriates, remixes, reuses. *Masked Media* is co-written with AI. *A Stubborn Fury* steals Tom McCarthy's prose style. The form enacts the argument.

**Longitudinal institutional projects**: Unlike most academics who write papers about things, Hall runs multi-year institutional projects (Liquid Books has been running since 2008, OHP since 2006). This is a research methodology of a different order.

**Cross-linguistic, transnational circulation**: His work has been translated and published in Japanese (*Gendai-Shiso*, 2011), Spanish (multiple translations), Turkish, Slovenian. And he has actively cultivated Latin American networks (17 Institute, UDLAP, Iberoamericana). This is a form of methodological commitment: the work should travel on its own terms.

### Network Strategy
**Hub role**: Hall doesn't just collaborate — he creates the institutions through which others collaborate. OHP is a hub that connects Haraway, Spivak, Latour, Harman, Morton, and dozens of others without them necessarily being in direct relation to each other. Hall is the infrastructure.

**Cross-generational**: His networks span from Samuel Weber (his early philosophical influence), to Clare Birchall (career-long colleague), to Janneke Adema (younger colleague at COPIM), to the students at 17 Institute, Mexico City who produce Liquid Books volumes.

**South-North**: Unlike most UK academics whose international networks run East-West (UK-US-Europe), Hall has a sustained North-South axis (UK-Mexico-Colombia-Australia). This is deliberate and unusual.

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **Founded *Culture Machine* (1999)** — Before he had major books. WHY IT WORKED: The humanities had no serious OA journal; there was a gap. Being first and building community before the prestige came was the move. It enabled: establishing legitimacy as an OA advocate with credentials.

2. **Published *Digitize This Book!* with Minnesota UP (2008)** — A major press book arguing for radical OA. WHY IT WORKED: The contradiction was productive — a book published by a prestigious press arguing that prestige presses are the problem. It got the argument to the right audience. It enabled: being taken seriously by the people he was critiquing.

3. **Co-founded Open Humanities Press (2006/2008)** — Simultaneously to building his traditional publication record. WHY IT WORKED: OHP's board (Haraway, Spivak, Latour, etc.) gave it instant prestige. It enabled: demonstrating that OA was compatible with serious intellectual standards.

4. **MIT Press + radical move (2016)** — *Pirate Philosophy* with MIT, *Uberfication* with Minnesota (Forerunners series). WHY IT WORKED: Using the exact prestige venues he critiques to distribute the critique. Both books are OA (or have OA versions). Enabled: reaching audiences who would not have engaged with OHP directly.

5. **Centre for Postdigital Cultures (2017)** — Creating an institutional home for his mode of practice. WHY IT WORKED: Having a named centre legitimizes the activity as research. It enabled: attracting colleagues (Adema, Jordan, Hewitt) and getting UKRI/COPIM funding.

6. **COPIM / Experimental Publishing Compendium (2019-2023)** — Moving from advocacy to infrastructure funding. WHY IT WORKED: UKRI money + community infrastructure = sustainable model that doesn't depend on his individual advocacy. Enabled: the Compendium (2023) as a practical tool for others.

7. **Co-writing *Masked Media* with AI (2025)** — The most radical step yet. WHY IT WORKED: Timing (peak AI panic), philosophical coherence (30 years of argument about authorship leads here), and the form enacting the argument. Enabled: becoming central to the AI-and-authorship debate from a critical theory perspective.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **Build the institution before you fully know the argument**: Hall's career shows that institutions clarify what you're arguing — you learn by building. Don't wait until the theory is complete.

2. **Form is argument**: The most powerful thing about Hall's work is that his books ARE his arguments — not just containers for them. Amber should ask: what would it mean for the form of her work to embody its argument?

3. **Refuse the false binary**: Hall publishes with MIT and Minnesota AND with OHP. He keynotes at Cambridge AND at the 17 Institute in Mexico City. The binary between "prestige" and "alternative" is a trap. Use both strategically.

4. **Name things**: "Uberfication", "Pirate Philosophy", "Liquid Books", "Postdigital Cultures" — each became a traveling concept precisely because it was named. Amber should think about what in her work needs a name.

5. **Build South-North networks deliberately**: Hall's Latin American circuit is unusual and valuable. Amber's interest in non-Western tech theory (sinofuturism, cosmotechnics) could develop similar networks with China, Taiwan, or Southeast Asia — these are currently undersupplied in the European critical theory circuit.

6. **The interview as a philosophical tool**: Hall has done major interviews with Samuel Weber, Homi K. Bhabha, Stelarc, Gayatri Spivak, and Alexandra Elbakyan. Each interview is itself a philosophical positioning — being in the same text as Elbakyan says something. Amber could think about who she wants to be seen in intellectual proximity to.
