# Lynette Wallworth

Australian artist and filmmaker pioneering VR/XR as a medium for empathy, indigenous sovereignty, and climate urgency. Inventor of the synchronized large-scale VR screening format. Two-time Emmy winner. The only Australian ever to sit on the Sundance Institute Board of Trustees. Works at the intersection of immersive technology, indigenous storytelling, and ecological witness.

## Background
Wallworth trained at [UNSW Art & Design](https://www.artdesign.unsw.edu.au/), the premier Australian art school embedded within a research university. Her early practice emerged through interactive video installations that invited intimate one-to-one encounters with images of the natural world and human resilience. The key to her trajectory: she was never content to make art for gallery walls — she wanted to put audiences *inside* the experience.

In 2012, the [Martu people](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martu_people) of Western Australia invited her to develop work for their exhibition *We Don't Need a Map*, beginning a long-term engagement with indigenous storytelling that would define her career. This collaboration seeded *Collisions* (2016), the landmark VR work made with Martu elder Nyarri Morgan about his first contact with Western culture — witnessing British nuclear testing at Maralinga.

In 2016, she became the first Australian on the [Sundance Institute](https://www.sundance.org/) Board of Trustees — a position that reflects her unique position bridging art, tech, and film institutions across hemispheres. Her platform grew: she directed the WEF's New Narratives Lab, joined the Global Future Council on Virtual and Augmented Reality, and became a recurring presence at Davos as a climate advocate.

Based in Sydney. Originally from Australia, internationally mobile.

## Key Works

### VR / XR
- **[Collisions](http://www.collisionsvr.com/)** (2016) — VR narrative. Story of Nyarri Morgan (Martu elder) witnessing British nuclear tests at Maralinga in the 1950s. First theatrical VR screening at Sundance Egyptian Theatre; first VR screening at MoMA. Emmy 2017. Produced by Infinite Field / [Jaunt VR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaunt_(company)), funded by WEF, Sundance Institute, MacArthur Foundation, Australia Council for the Arts. Sound design by Skywalker Sound's Tom Myers (first-ever Dolby Atmos mix at Skywalker Ranch).
- **[Awavena](https://viveport.com/6792ef3d-0775-4ab4-b3d3-3d9c15b64d47)** (2018) — XR/mixed reality. Story of Hushahu, first female shaman of the Yawanawá people, Amazon, Brazil. Met Amazonian Chief Tashka Yawanawá at WEF; he compared VR to visioning medicine and invited Wallworth to the Amazon. Premiered Sundance 2018 New Frontier; Venice Film Festival 2018 competition. Emmy 2020.

### Fulldome
- **[CORAL: Rekindling Venus](http://coralrekindlingvenus.com/)** (2012) — digital fulldome feature for planetariums. Underwater footage of coral reefs; launched on Transit of Venus 2012; screened in 23 cities worldwide. World Science Festival NYC, London 2012 Cultural Olympiad, Sundance 2013 New Frontier. Music by [Antony Hegarty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antony_Hegarty), Max Richter, Tanya Tagaq Gillis, Geoffrey Gurrumul Yunupingu, Fennesz, Sakamoto. DomeFest Award 2012.

## Venue Circuit Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

Wallworth operates on **five distinct circuits simultaneously**, which is unusual for an Australian artist:

**1. US Independent Film Power (Sundance axis)**
Sundance is the linchpin — she's not just a filmmaker who shows there, she's a board member and lab advisor. This gives her access to emerging talent 10 years before they're famous (Charlotte Wells connection). The Sundance network opens: MacArthur Foundation funding, WEF partnerships, Skoll Stories of Change Award.

**2. Institutional Global Policy (Davos/UN axis)**
Most artists never reach this circuit. Wallworth achieved it by making *Collisions* specifically demonstrable — VR headsets for Hans Blix at the UN, 75 synchronized headsets for world leaders at Davos. She weaponized the technology's unique capability for policy impact. The WEF Crystal Award and Director of New Narratives Lab formalized this.

**3. Australian Cultural Infrastructure**
Deep roots: ACCA, ACMI, Adelaide Film Festival, Samstag Museum, Fremantle Arts Centre, Sydney Opera House. She hasn't abandoned the home circuit despite global reach — smart, as it keeps institutional support and government arts funding flowing.

**4. Academic/Research Residency (ANU + UNSW)**
The cybernetics residency at ANU is strategic — positions her work within systems theory and technology ethics discourse, not just art. The UNSW Human Rights Institute residency positions VR as a human rights tool. Both extend the intellectual legitimacy of immersive media.

**5. European Media Art (Venice/festival axis)**
Venice competition for *Awavena* (2018) is the highest European legitimacy signal. Festival circuit (IDFA, CPHDOX, Sheffield DocFest, Brighton) keeps European distribution and critical discourse active.

**What's notably absent:** Commercial gallery circuit (no Gagosian, Hauser & Wirth), academic publishing, crypto/web3 (unlike younger generation). Her power comes through *demonstrated impact* — policy change, Emmy wins, viral Davos moments — not market value.

## Why Interesting

Wallworth sits at the intersection of several threads from research-taste.md:

**More-than-human**: Her work is literally about decentering the Western human — both through indigenous partnership (the Martu and Yawanawá as autonomous knowledge systems, not "subjects") and through climate work (*CORAL*). She embeds the viewer in non-human-centered perspectives.

**Embodied interaction**: Her entire career from *Hold: Vessel* (2001) onward is about computing that's felt in the body — you hold the bowl, you put your hand on the video, you wear the headset. "When something goes from concept to sensation... it's held in the body." She articulates the somaesthetic theory without the academic framing.

**Participatory & democratic design**: The indigenous co-creation model (invited by Martu, invited by Yawanawá — not a white artist extracting stories) is a radical participatory practice. The synchronized headset format achieves "collective dreaming" — a form of participatory immersive experience.

**Edge of chaos**: She's 25 years into immersive media and still advancing — the *Edge of Life* documentary, the cybernetics residency, the metaverse governance work. Not coasting.

**Non-Western + Western bridging**: Australian-indigenous × Western technology, Amazonian shamanism × XR tech, European culture × antipodean perspective. She doesn't resolve these tensions — she makes them the medium.

The Amber resonance: Wallworth asked "what does VR make possible that nothing else can?" and built her career on that. Amber is asking the same question about HoloKit, about MR, about collective AR. They are working on the same fundamental problem from different continents and generations.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

**Most important insight:** Wallworth's strategy is *impact-first technological invention* — she doesn't pick technologies to explore; she identifies the experience she needs to create and then figures out what technological invention is required.

### Conceptual Strategy

**Experience-as-argument**: She makes the work do the rhetorical work that papers and speeches cannot. *Collisions* didn't argue that nuclear testing was traumatic — it put world leaders in the body of Nyarri Morgan. Hans Blix didn't *hear* an argument; he *had* an experience. This is a research strategy: use the artwork as the evidence.

**Empathy at scale through synchronization**: The synchronized VR headset format (first ever at Davos, 75 headsets) is a conceptual invention before it's a technical one. The concept: "collective dreaming" — how do you make VR communal rather than isolating? Once you have the concept, you solve the engineering. The Two Bit Circus sync tech was found because she needed it.

**Indigenous cosmology as technology**: Tashka Yawanawá comparing VR to visioning medicine is not just a nice quote — it's a theoretical framework. Wallworth understood that indigenous peoples had developed sophisticated technologies for collective perception-alteration, and that VR was entering that same territory. She made this connection explicit in her work, which gave *Awavena* a depth that purely technology-focused VR work lacks.

### Career Architecture

**Institutional ladder, not gallery ladder**: Most media artists build gallery careers (Gagosian → white cube → museum). Wallworth built an *institutional* career: Sundance → WEF → UN → ANU/UNSW. Each institution gives access to the next. The Sundance board position unlocks the WEF invitation; the WEF crystal award unlocks the UN platform.

**Advisory positions as research residencies**: The Sundance Advisor role (10+ years) is not just service — it's access to every emerging filmmaker's most vulnerable creative problem. You see Charlotte Wells before *Aftersun* exists. You understand what the next generation is struggling with. This is field intelligence that no literature review provides.

**Southern hemisphere perspective as differentiation**: Being Australian is genuinely differentiating on the global circuit. She's the only Australian on the Sundance board. She brings indigenous Australian and Pacific perspectives that no US or European artist can claim. Geographic identity as intellectual positioning.

### Methodology Signature

**The long indigenous collaboration**: Both major works (*Collisions*, *Awavena*) required years-long trust-building with indigenous communities before any technology appeared. The Martu collaboration started in 2012; *Collisions* premiered in 2016. This patience is rare in tech-art — and the depth it produces is unmistakable.

**Technology demonstration as advocacy**: The UN Vienna moment is a methodology, not just an anecdote. She *brought the technology to the decision-makers* rather than waiting for decision-makers to find the technology. This required understanding which decision-making bodies mattered (CTBT signatories), which formats would be appropriate (closed meeting of Eminent Persons Group), and what the specific argument needed to be.
