# Jenny Odell

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-01:** Full deep research completed. Upgraded to ⭐⭐⭐. Found all exhibitions, residencies, collaborators, venue circuit, writing corpus. Third book (on repair) in progress.
- **2026-02-26:** Created stub. Discovered via [[Eyeo Festival]] deep research.

Multi-disciplinary artist and writer based in Oakland, California. Best known for *[How to Do Nothing: Resisting the Attention Economy](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/600671/how-to-do-nothing-by-jenny-odell/)* (Melville House, 2019) — a NYT bestseller reframing attention, withdrawal, and slowness as political acts. Former Stanford digital art lecturer. Her method: **acts of close observation** — birdwatching, satellite imagery, trash archaeology, e-commerce deep dives — to reveal the strangeness embedded in everyday reality. Currently working on a third book about **repair**.

## Background
Born 1986 in San Francisco, grew up in Cupertino — deep inside Silicon Valley's geography. This proximity to tech culture is not incidental; her entire practice is a meditation on living *inside* the machine while refusing its terms.

- **BA English Literature, [UC Berkeley](https://www.berkeley.edu/)** (2008) — gave her close reading practices that she applies to objects, landscapes, infrastructure, and the internet
- **MFA Design + Technology, [San Francisco Art Institute](https://sfai.edu/)** (2010) — hybrid art/tech training; early web-native practice
- Taught **internet art and digital/physical design at [Stanford University](https://stanford.edu/)** from 2013 to 2021 — embedded in Silicon Valley academia while critiquing tech culture. Left in 2021, now fully independent.
- Based in Oakland — which she treats as a bioregion, community, and source of ecological observation (the night herons at KFC; Calabazas Creek through Cupertino)

Her trajectory: digital artist → internet researcher → writer → public intellectual. The books grew from talks; the talks grew from artistic projects; the projects grew from residencies. Everything she does starts with **close looking**.

## Key Works

### Books
- **[How to Do Nothing: Resisting the Attention Economy](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/600671/how-to-do-nothing-by-jenny-odell/)** (Melville House, 2019) — NYT bestseller. Started as an [Eyeo 2017 talk](https://medium.com/@the_jennitaur/how-to-do-nothing-57e100f59bbb). - **[Saving Time: Discovering a Life Beyond the Clock](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/672377/saving-time-by-jenny-odell/)** (Random House, 2023) — On the political economy of time; distinguishes chronos (capitalist clock-time) from kairos (crisis-time, transformation-time). Named "Clocking Out" by Sarah Jaffe in The Nation.
- **[Inhabiting the Negative Space](https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/inhabiting-negative-space)** (MIT Press, 2021) — her 2020 Harvard GSD commencement address as a small book. On emptiness, pause, the importance of not-doing in design.

### Art Projects (selected)
- **[Satellite Collections](https://jennyodell.com/satellite.html)** — manmade elements (swimming pools, baseball diamonds, golf courses) extracted from Google Maps satellite view and arranged as typologies. Her most-reproduced work.
- **[Satellite Landscapes](https://jennyodell.com/satellite-landscapes.html)** — infrastructural elements (highways, power plants, airstrips) cut from satellite screenshots; made visible through decontextualization
- **[The Bureau of Suspended Objects](https://www.suspended-objects.org/)** (2015) — created during Recology SF residency at the dump. Online archive of 200+ salvaged objects with deep research into each item's manufacturing, distribution, and corporate history. Exhibited at Contemporary Jewish Museum.
- **[Neo Surreal](https://jennyodell.com/neosurreal.html)** (2017) — BYTE magazine images from 1980s, found during Internet Archive residency. Culled, retitled, and reframed to reveal technology's latent surrealism (a computer in a policeman's hat, a pill revealing a chip inside). Exhibited at Photographers' Gallery London.
- **[Power Trip](https://jennyodell.com/power-trip.html)** — traces the hydroelectric power used by San Francisco municipal buildings to its source in Yosemite. Infrastructure made visible.
- **[Pipe Dream](https://jennyodell.com/pipe-dream.html)** — traces the Portland-Montreal underground crude oil pipeline via Google Street View
- **[All the People on Google Earth](https://jennyodell.com/allthepeople.html)** — crowds extracted from Google Earth screenshots; the uncanny humans embedded in mapping infrastructure
- **[The Memory of All Traces](https://jennyodell.com/ybc.html)** — mobile walking tour of SOMA produced while in residence at Yerba Buena Center for the Arts
- **[There's No Such Thing as a Free Watch](https://jennyodell.com/free-watch.html)** — deep research into fake/free Instagram watch ads; museum catalogue for the Museum of Capitalism
- **Merce-bau** — video piece inspired by Schwitters and Cunningham; ODC Dance Center residency

## Why Interesting
Odell is one of the clearest articulators of **why slowing down is political**. This is directly relevant to Amber's more-than-human research: the accelerationism of AI computation vs. the slow attention required to notice non-human agency. Her argument isn't anti-technology — it's against the *terms* technology imposes on attention and time. Amber's work on agent ethology implicitly asks: what does it look like to *observe* AI systems rather than just deploy them? Odell operationalizes exactly this for birdwatching and trash archaeology.

Her method of **using Google Maps/satellite imagery to reveal infrastructural strangeness** is also directly relevant: using computational tools to defamiliarize computational infrastructure. This is what Amber does philosophically with machine behavior — using AI to study AI.

The birdwatching practice is a model: **close observation of non-human agents as a critical act**. Crows that recognize faces, night herons that adapt to urban environments — these are animal agents navigating human infrastructure. Exactly what interests Amber at the animal-machine behavioral crossover.

Her forthcoming book on **repair** intersects with Amber's interest in machine death, maintenance, and care ethics. The ethics of keeping things running (vs. disposability) has implications for how we think about machine obsolescence and care.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
Odell works through **accumulation-and-zoom**: she begins with small, hyper-specific close observations (a night heron at KFC, a free watch on Instagram, a creek buried under a data center) and then — precisely because the observation is so grounded — zooms to structural critique (capitalism, attention economics, the political economy of time). The small detail *earns* the big claim. This is the opposite of most critical theory, which begins with the framework.

Her other key move: **the residency as epistemic access**. She doesn't theorize the dump — she lives in it. She doesn't critique Silicon Valley from outside — she teaches at Stanford and uses the access to research. The residency gives her empirical grounding that prevents abstraction.

**Concept coining:** "How to Do Nothing" as a phrase is a near-perfect conceptual move — it appropriates the productivity-hack genre title ("How to X") and subverts it immediately. This naming strategy is worth studying.

### Career Architecture
- **Eyeo as launch pad**: The tech-culture conference circuit (Eyeo, KIKK, Re:publica) was her publishing platform before she had a traditional publisher. She tested ideas in talks, got community response, and turned validated concepts into books.
- **Cross-medium coherence**: Every medium she uses — satellite art, essay, book, newsletter, birdwatching — says the same thing differently. Her brand is not a brand; it's a sustained philosophical position that happens to be legible across media.
- **Independence over institutionalization**: Leaving Stanford in 2021 was a crucial move. She is more influential as an independent writer than she was as a lecturer. The academy was a launchpad, not a destination.
- **Platform ethics as argument**: Being on Mastodon (social.coop) and using a newsletter rather than Twitter/Instagram is itself part of her argument. Her presence IS her praxis.

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **Satellite Collections (2011–2013)** — established visual language of defamiliarizing computational infrastructure through typological sorting. Created a body of work before she had an argument.
2. **Recology residency → Bureau of Suspended Objects (2015)** — figured out the residency-as-research-method. Embedded in waste infrastructure, produced an archive. Showed that art could do the work of journalism and history simultaneously.
3. **Internet Archive residency → Neo Surreal (2017)** — same method applied to digital archives. Revealed: the same approach works anywhere there's a hidden archive.
4. **Eyeo 2017 talk** — "How to Do Nothing" talk crystallized the political argument that had been implicit in the work. Became the most-watched Eyeo talk. Let community response validate concept.
5. **How to Do Nothing book (2019)** — expanded talk to book; NYT bestseller. The book made her a public intellectual, not just an artist.
6. **Left Stanford (2021)** — chose independence over tenure track. MIT Press book (Harvard GSD commencement) proved she had academic standing without needing a permanent position.
7. **Saving Time (2023)** — extended the argument to temporal politics. Showed the concept could expand. Newsletter + Mastodon = embodied platform ethics.
8. **Repair book (in progress)** — the trilogy will be: attention → time → repair. Each book is a different register of what's lost when productivity rules.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **The talk-to-book pipeline**: Give one definitive talk at a high-quality venue (Eyeo for tech, ALIFE for ALife, Ars Electronica for media art). Let the community response tell you if the idea has legs. Then expand to paper/book. Don't start with the book.
2. **The residency-as-access strategy**: Amber's ERA Fellowship in Cambridge is not just a credential — it's epistemic access. What can you see from inside ERA that you can't see from outside? That's the research.
3. **Small observations earn big claims**: Amber's work on machine behavior risks staying abstract. Odell's model: start with one specific agent (a crow, a night heron, a free watch algorithm) and let it reveal the structural. What is the night heron for Amber's machine behavior work?
4. **Platform ethics as praxis**: If Amber cares about attention economics and more-than-human computation, what platforms does that imply? Odell's Mastodon use is not incidental.
5. **Accumulation first, argument second**: Odell spent 5 years making satellite art before she had the theoretical frame. The artwork IS the research. Amber can learn to let the making precede the thesis.
