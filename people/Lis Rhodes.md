# Lis Rhodes

Artist, filmmaker, author. One of the foundational figures of British experimental and feminist film. Made her first films at the **[London Film-Makers' Co-operative (LFMC)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London_Film-Makers%27_Co-op)** in the 1970s — *Dresden Dynamo* (1971), *Light Music* (1975), *Light Reading* (1978). In 1979 co-founded **[[Circles]]**, the UK's first feminist film and video distribution network (now [Cinenova](https://www.cinenova.org/)), with Annabel Nicolson, Felicity Sparrow, and Jo Davis. Has taught at the **[[Slade School of Fine Art]]** (UCL) since 1978. Writing collected in ***[Telling Invents Told](https://lux.org.uk/artist/lis-rhodes/)*** (Visible Press, 2019). Fifty-year career, still active: *Disquiet* (2022). Works distributed by [LUX](https://lux.org.uk/artist/lis-rhodes/).

> *"It is dangerous to step out of line — and lethal not to."* — Lis Rhodes

## Background
Lis Rhodes was born in 1942 in Cornwall, England. She studied at the **[North East London Polytechnic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_East_London_Polytechnic)** and then the **[Royal College of Art](https://www.rca.ac.uk/)** (Film and Television). Her trajectory takes her directly into the heart of the British avant-garde film scene of the 1970s — at a moment when it was politically charged, materially experimental, and explicitly contested by feminist artists demanding recognition.

Her first major film, *Dresden Dynamo* (1971), was made **without a camera** — she affixed patterned Letratone stickers directly to the film, using the optical soundtrack area to generate both image and sound. This established her lifelong concern with the materiality of film itself and the synthesis of image and sound as a single substrate. *Light Music* (1975) extended this: she drew patterns directly onto the film's sound track, making the drawn sound waveform itself the image — a literal inscription of sound as vision.

In 1975–76, she served as **Cinema Curator at the [[LFMC]]** — the most important avant-garde film institution in Britain. From 1978 onward she taught part-time at the **[[Slade School of Fine Art]]** (UCL), a position she has held continuously. In 1982–85 she served as **Arts Adviser to the Greater London Council** under Ken Livingstone's GLC.

The 1979 co-founding of **[[Circles]]** was a defining institutional act: growing out of feminist artists' frustration at being excluded from the Arts Council's *[Film as Film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Film_as_Film)* exhibition, Rhodes and her collaborators withdrew their research from the show, issued the statement **"Women and the Formal Film"** (published in the exhibition catalogue), and channelled that energy into building their own distribution infrastructure. By the time Circles closed in 1991 (merging with Cinema of Women to form [[Cinenova]]), its catalogue contained over 200 titles spanning 90 years of women's film production.

Since 2008, her work has circulated internationally through retrospectives at the Film Museum Vienna, Internationale Kurzfilmtage Oberhausen, and the ICA London's landmark **Dissonance and Disturbance** (2012), which positioned her as a canonical figure. The 2017 Freelands Award gave her a significant platform, resulting in *Ambiguous Journeys* (2019, commissioned by Nottingham Contemporary). Her 2022 essay film *Disquiet* continues the political analysis of neoliberalism, now extending to pandemic-era globalisation and the violence of profit.

## Domains
- **Sound-image synthesis** — the drawn/inscribed relationship between optical sound and image in film
- **Language and power** — how language constructs (not merely reflects) gender inequality; how sentences "imprison" meaning
- **Feminist film infrastructure** — distribution, exhibition, and the institutional conditions that make women's film visible or invisible
- **Political essay film** — long-form materialist analysis of capitalism, protest, surveillance, statelessness, debt
- **Structural/Materialist film** — the film-as-object tradition of the LFMC
- **Teaching and transmission** — decades of pedagogy at the Slade as part of her practice

## Key Works

### Early Films (1971–1978): Materialism and Language
- **[Dresden Dynamo](https://lux.org.uk/work/dresden-dynamo/)** (UK, 1971, 5 min, 16mm) — Made without a camera. Letratone stickers affixed directly to the filmstrip; optical sound area generates both image and sound. "The film is not a sequential piece. It does not develop crescendos. It creates the illusion of spatial depth from essentially flat, graphic, raw material." — Tim Bruce. Screened: ICA 2012, Ann Arbor 2011, Film Museum Vienna 2009.
- **[Notes From Light Music](https://lux.org.uk/work/notes-from-light-music/)** (UK, 1975, 12 min) — preparatory notation films alongside *Light Music*.
- **[Light Music](https://lux.org.uk/work/light-music/)** (UK, 1975, 25 min, 16mm) — Canonical work. Patterns drawn directly on the film's optical soundtrack become the image; the waveform IS the image. Sound as inscription. In the [Tate Collection](https://www.tate.org.uk/).
- **[Light Reading](https://lux.org.uk/work/light-reading/)** (UK, 1978, 20 min, 16mm) — Language broken apart on screen. Words are "already sentenced — imprisoned in meaning." An unmade bed: "the scene of a crime." Feminist analysis of language as ideology. Toured in Circles' first programme, "Her Image Fades as Her Voice Rises" (1980, with films by Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, Joanna Davis).

### Political Feminist Films (1982–1993): Language, Economy, Resistance
- **[Pictures On Pink Paper](https://lux.org.uk/work/pictures-on-pink-paper/)** (UK, 1982, 35 min, 16mm) — *"she thinks she imagines — really she sees / it's all a question of / who makes real — whose ideals / whose values are valued"* — Analyses language as a cause (not symptom) of gender inequalities. Women associated with "nature" through pronouns; men with "culture." Music by **Lindsay Cooper**, vocals by **Dagmar Krause**. Screened: Berlin Film Festival 1984, Channel 4 1985, WACK! MOCA LA 2007, NMWA Washington 2008, Vancouver Art Gallery 2009.
- **[Hang on a Minute](https://lux.org.uk/work/hang-on-a-minute/)** (UK, 1983, 13 min, 16mm) — Series of 13 one-minute films co-made with **Jo Davis**, commissioned by Channel 4 to air between programmes (in the manner of adverts). Topics: Greenham Common, domestic violence, uranium from Namibia, RTZ profits. Music by Lindsay Cooper, vocals by Dagmar Krause. Full series available from [Cinenova](https://www.cinenova.org/).
- **[A Cold Draft](https://lux.org.uk/work/a-cold-draft/)** (UK, 1988, 30 min, 16mm) — "Drawn from (a drawing of) the conditions produced by 'liberal' economics in the UK in the 1980s." Drawn images and urban landscapes dissolving together. "Truth is reckless, certainty a sham, but such is faith in repetition that line by line certainty is drawn." Reviewed by Sandra Lahire (*Undercut*, 1990). Screened: ICA 2012, Film Museum Vienna 2009, Oberhausen 2008, ICA London 1991.
- **[Just About Now](https://lux.org.uk/work/just-about-now/)** (UK, 1993, 23 min)

### Documentary / Research Films (1996–2004): Statelessness and Justice
- **[Running Light](https://lux.org.uk/work/running-light/)** (UK, 1996, 15 min, SD video) — Co-researched with **Mary Pat Leece** (American artist, UK-based) during a 1985 visit to West Virginia, where they documented the conditions of migrant farmworkers. Pope Barford: *"Without the illegals… without the migrants in general their system — it really does collapse… they've got to have that cheap labour."* Minimal photographs taken to avoid further exploiting the migrants.
- **[ORIFSO](https://lux.org.uk/work/orifso/)** (UK, 1999, 12 min)
- **[Journal of Disbelief](https://lux.org.uk/work/journal-of-disbelief/)** (UK, 2000, 98 min) — "Tears pages out of the last 20 years to remind and to warn." Corporate law, investor-state dispute settlement, secret trials, the Iraq war legal advice. A 98-minute political essay.
- **[Riff](https://lux.org.uk/work/riff/)** (UK, 2004, 18 min, SD video) — Identity, borders, surveillance. "Recognition places one in many, many in one place, removed and removed again, categorically policed." Screened: 47th New York Film Festival 2009, Seoul Experimental Film Festival 2005, European Media Arts Festival Osnabrück 2005.

### Late Career (2010–2022): Surveillance, Protest, Globalisation
- **[In the Kettle](https://lux.org.uk/work/in-the-kettle/)** (UK, 2010, 21 min, HD) — Protest photography from UK demonstrations; kettling by police; CCTV. *"Since 2009, photographing public demonstrations has been prosecutable under the Counter-Terrorism Act."* Part of the *Dissonance and Disturbance* installation at ICA 2012. Screened: no.w.here London 2011.
- **[Dissonance and Disturbance](https://lux.org.uk/work/dissonance-and-disturbance/)** (UK, 2012, 26 min) — Two-screen installation: *In the Kettle*, *Whitehall* (2012), and *A Cold Draft* (1988), with a shared soundtrack. "Geography of disturbance": Carlton Gardens, Pall Mall, Trafalgar Square, Whitehall, Mansion House — near the ICA itself.
- **[Ambiguous Journeys](https://lux.org.uk/work/ambiguous-journeys/)** (UK, 2018, 40 min, HD) — On statelessness, debt, migrant labour, deportation. *"There is no ambiguity in the reasonable reasons for the journeys made by many — to escape conditions that are organised, imposed and untenable."* Commissioned by [Nottingham Contemporary](https://www.nottinghamcontemporary.org/), funded by the Freelands Foundation. B&W, 16:9.
- **[Disquiet](https://lux.org.uk/work/disquiet/)** (UK, 2022, 78 min, HD) — Made during the pandemic. Continues the analysis of neoliberal capitalism: from Hiroshima to the conquest of the skies, drawing connections between local, transnational, and extra-terrestrial violence and profit. *"Can warnings warn, when violence is a profitable industry?"* — Maria Palacios Cruz on the film.

## Key Communities
- **[[LFMC|London Film-Makers' Co-operative]]** ([lux.org.uk history](https://luxonline.org.uk/history/1960-1969/london_film-makers_co-op.html)) — Cinema Curator 1975–76. The LFMC was founded in 1966 by Stephen Dwoskin and Malcolm Le Grice, inspired by Jonas Mekas's Film-Makers' Cooperative. Worksharing model: production + distribution + exhibition. The crucible of British structural-materialist film.
- **[[Circles]] / [[Cinenova]]** ([cinenova.org](https://www.cinenova.org/)) — Co-founder, November 1979, with Annabel Nicolson, Felicity Sparrow, and Jo Davis (Joanna Davis). First catalogue (1980) printed by Only Women Press. First touring programme: "Her Image Fades as Her Voice Rises" (films by Joanna Davis, Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, Light Reading). 30 → 200+ titles by closure in 1991. Merged with Cinema of Women to form Cinenova.
- **[[Slade School of Fine Art]]** ([ucl.ac.uk](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/slade/)) — Part-time lecturer since 1978. One of the UK's premier fine art schools, part of UCL. Her continuous 40+ year presence there is itself a form of feminist infrastructure.
- **[[Four Corners Film Workshop]]** ([fourcornersfilm.co.uk](https://www.fourcornersfilm.co.uk/)) — Member. East London radical film/photography collective with roots in the 1970s. Now Four Corners, a charity focused on creative media access.
- **[[LUX]]** ([lux.org.uk](https://lux.org.uk/)) — Primary distributor. The successor organisation to the LFMC/London Video Arts merger.
- **Greater London Council (GLC)** — Arts Adviser 1982–85, under Ken Livingstone's GLC; a moment of sustained public funding for radical arts in London.

## Venue Circuit Analysis
Rhodes's circuits span **five decades** and reveal a remarkably consistent political geography:

**1. Feminist avant-garde infrastructure (1970s–1991):** LFMC, Circles, Channel 4. She's building the circuits, not just using them. The key move is building distribution infrastructure when it doesn't exist.

**2. British experimental film (1980s–2000s):** ICA London, National Film Theatre, no.w.here. These are the home venues for British structural and feminist film. She's a canonical figure at all of them.

**3. International short/experimental film festivals (2000s–2010s):** Internationale Kurzfilmtage Oberhausen (profile retrospective 2008), Ann Arbor Film Festival, Berlin Film Festival, Seoul Experimental Film Festival, European Media Arts Festival. The global circuit for experimental and essay film.

**4. Museum retrospectives (late 2000s–2012):** Film Museum Vienna (2009), MOCA LA (2007), NMWA Washington (2008), Vancouver Art Gallery (2009), ICA London (2012), Tramway Glasgow (2012). Belated institutional recognition after 30+ years of practice.

**5. Commission-driven (2017–present):** Nottingham Contemporary + Freelands Foundation commission for *Ambiguous Journeys* (2019). The Freelands Award enables her to continue making work with institutional backing.

The circuit is **weighted toward the institutional** (museums, galleries, retrospectives) rather than commercial. She deliberately refused a Hayward Gallery exhibition in 1979 because she was the only woman — a pattern of institutional critique through refusal that later became the founding act of Circles.

## Why Interesting
Rhodes sits at the precise intersection of several threads Amber cares about:

**Feminist infrastructure building:** The founding of Circles is a perfect case study in how artists build the missing institutions — not advocacy but *making the thing exist*. The 1979 statement "Women and the Formal Film" is a manifesto-as-act: research withdrawn, infrastructure created.

**Language and power:** Her work on language as cause (not symptom) of gender inequality (especially *Light Reading* and *Pictures on Pink Paper*) is a materialist feminist linguistics that has aged well. The sentence as prison. The pronoun as political structure. This connects to contemporary critical computing debates about language models.

**Sound-image synthesis:** *Dresden Dynamo* and *Light Music* are early examples of what we'd now call "data sonification" — but inverted: she's drawing the sound directly, making the medium's physical substrate visible. The question "what if the sound IS the image?" is still a generative research question.

**Political essay film:** Her late work (*Journal of Disbelief*, *Ambiguous Journeys*, *Disquiet*) is a 20-year-long investigation of neoliberal capitalism through the essay film form. The essay film as political instrument: not documentary, not fiction, but a genre that makes arguments through juxtaposition, voice, and image.

**Transmission and pedagogy:** 40+ years at the Slade as part of the practice, not separate from it. How does she think about what she teaches?

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
**Infrastructure-as-manifesto:** Rhodes's most important conceptual move is recognising that distribution IS politics. The founding of Circles is not a secondary activity — it IS the argument about what feminist film is and can be. Compare: founding LUX would later demonstrate that the same logic applies to the next generation. This "build what's missing" approach is more powerful than critique alone.

**The medium IS the politics:** In *Dresden Dynamo* and *Light Music*, the critique of representation is enacted at the material level — no camera, no conventional image/sound split. This is not illustration of a political point but practice of it. The argument is inseparable from the form.

**Accumulation over breakthrough:** Rhodes has no single canonical breakthrough work that "made" her reputation. Instead: 50 years of consistent practice, each work building on the previous, the late retrospectives (2008–2012) being the institutional moment when the accumulation became visible. This is a long-game strategy.

### Career Architecture
**Teaching as infrastructure:** The Slade appointment (1978, never ended) is not a day job — it's a permanent node in the feminist film network. Every student she taught carried the tradition forward. The Slade connection also gives her access to the UCL/London institutional world.

**Refusal as founding act:** The 1979 withdrawal from the Hayward/Arts Council exhibition is a classic move: the refusal creates the space for something new. Rhodes and her collaborators didn't just protest — they channelled the energy into Circles.

**Archival patience:** She continued making work during the 1980s and 1990s (GLC period, Channel 4 commissions, *Running Light*, *Journal of Disbelief*) without major international retrospectives. The 2007 WACK! inclusion was the first major international recognition. By then she had 35 years of practice behind her.

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **1971: *Dresden Dynamo*** — establishes her as a filmmaker who treats film as material substrate, not transparent window. The camera-less film as radical statement.
2. **1975: *Light Music*** — the optical sound track as visual drawing. A single conceptual insight that becomes a signature. Gets her into the LFMC cinema curator role immediately after.
3. **1978: *Light Reading*** — pivot from pure formalism to feminist language politics. Keeps the formal rigour but adds the political content. Crucial: she doesn't abandon form for content, she finds form that IS the content.
4. **1979: Founding Circles** — the most important infrastructure move. Builds the missing distribution network. The statement "Women and the Formal Film" gives it a theoretical foundation.
5. **1983: *Hang on a Minute* for Channel 4** — takes the feminist experimental film into broadcast TV. 13 one-minute films between programmes: radical form in a mass medium.
6. **2007: WACK! at MOCA LA** — first major international institutional recognition. After 35 years of practice, her work is now canonical.
7. **2012: ICA *Dissonance and Disturbance*** — the definitive UK retrospective. Positions her as the founding figure of British feminist experimental film.
8. **2019: *Telling Invents Told*** — the collected writings make her theoretical framework available. Confirms she is not just a filmmaker but a thinker whose writing practice matters.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **Build the missing thing** — When the institution doesn't include you, don't wait. Circles was founded in 3 months after the Hayward exclusion. The question is: what's the Circles equivalent in Amber's field?
2. **Form IS argument** — In Rhodes's best work, the political claim is inseparable from the formal choices. The feminist politics of *Light Reading* are not illustrated by the film — they ARE enacted by the fragmenting text, the broken words, the "imprisoned sentences." Amber's critical computing work can aim for this level of integration.
3. **Infrastructure as practice** — Rhodes's career shows that building institutions (Circles), editing catalogues, programming cinemas (LFMC), and teaching (Slade) are forms of practice, not distractions from it. Infrastructure IS the work.
4. **The long-game retrospective** — Patience. Rhodes's major retrospectives came 30–40 years in. The work was being done the whole time. This is not a strategy for everyone, but for work that has formal integrity, the retrospective moment eventually arrives.
5. **Teaching as node-building** — 40 years at the Slade means 40 years of students. Each one is a node in the network. Teaching is a form of network-building that doesn't look like networking.
