# Donna Haraway

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Full deep research completed. Expanded stub into comprehensive profile. Awarded Erasmus Prize 2025 (€150,000) — major recognition confirming her canonical status.
- **2025:** Erasmus Prize 2025 awarded by Praemium Erasmianum Foundation, Netherlands.
- **2021:** Nuevo León Alfonso Reyes Prize (Mexico).
- **2026-02-26:** Initial stub created during Anna Tsing deep research.

Emeritus Professor of History of Consciousness and Feminist Studies at UC Santa Cruz. The foundational thinker at the intersection of feminist theory, science studies, and multispecies thinking. Author of *A Cyborg Manifesto* (1985), *Situated Knowledges* (1988), *Primate Visions* (1989), *Staying with the Trouble* (2016). ArtReview named her the **3rd most influential person in the contemporary art world** (2017). Colleague of [[Anna Tsing]], co-participant with [[Bruno Latour]] in key debates. The intellectual mother of posthumanism as a field. 2025 Erasmus Prize laureate.

---

## Background

Born September 6, 1944, Denver, Colorado. Father Frank O. Haraway was a sportswriter for The Denver Post. Catholic upbringing (mother died of heart attack when Donna was 16) — the eucharist's linkage of the figurative and material influenced her entire intellectual style.

**Education:**
- **Colorado College** — BA Zoology, minors Philosophy & English. Boettcher full-tuition scholarship.
- **Fulbright Scholarship** — One year in Paris, studied evolutionary philosophy and theology at the [Fondation Teilhard de Chardin](https://www.fondationteilharddechardin.com/). The Teilhard connection is crucial: his vision of an "Omega Point" of cosmic convergence — and her subsequent rejection of teleological thinking — shapes everything she writes.
- **Yale University, PhD Biology (1972)** — Dissertation: *The Search for Organizing Relations: An Organismic Paradigm in Twentieth-Century Developmental Biology*. Later published as *Crystals, Fabrics, and Fields* (1976). A science historian by training before she was a theorist.

**Career trajectory:**
- 1971–1974: Women's Studies & History of Science, **University of Hawaiʻi**
- 1974–1980: Women's Studies & Philosophy of Science, **Johns Hopkins University**
- 1980–retirement: Professor of **History of Consciousness & Feminist Studies**, **UC Santa Cruz** — became the **first tenured professor in feminist theory in the United States**.
- Retired as Emeritus. Lives north of San Francisco with partner Rusten Hogness (married 1975), with dogs, roosters, and a menagerie.

The trajectory: scientist → philosopher of science → feminist theorist → multispecies thinker. Each move deepens rather than abandons the previous.

## Domains
- **Feminist science studies** — How gender, race, and class shape scientific knowledge production
- **Science & Technology Studies (STS)** — Actor-network theory adjacent, but more biologically grounded

- **Cyborg theory** — The collapse of human/machine/animal distinctions as political possibility
- **Posthumanism** — Against anthropocentrism, toward "making kin" with non-humans
- **Speculative fabulation** — SF as method: Science Fiction, Science Fact, Speculative Feminism, Speculative Fabulation

## Key Works

### Books (chronological)
- **[*Crystals, Fabrics, and Fields: Metaphors of Organicism in Twentieth-Century Developmental Biology*](https://www.amazon.com/Crystals-Fabrics-Fields-Metaphors-Organicism/dp/0300018649)** (Yale University Press, 1976) — Revised dissertation. Analyzes how metaphors shape experimental biology. First proof that metaphor is epistemic, not just rhetorical.

- **[*Primate Visions: Gender, Race, and Nature in the World of Modern Science*](https://www.routledge.com/Primate-Visions-Gender-Race-and-Nature-in-the-World-of-Modern-Science/Haraway/p/book/9780415902946)** (Routledge, 1989) — Feminist deconstruction of primatology. Shows how heterosexual ideology and racial assumptions are embedded in how Western scientists study other primates. Won the **Robert K. Merton Award (ASA, 1992)**.

- **[*Simians, Cyborgs and Women: The Reinvention of Nature*](https://archive.org/details/simianscyborgswo0000hara)** (Routledge, 1991) — Collection including the updated "A Cyborg Manifesto." The text that made her a cult figure. Argues the cyborg — a fusion of machine and organism — offers a way past the paralyzing binaries of feminist theory (nature/culture, male/female, human/machine).

- **[*Modest_Witness@Second_Millennium.FemaleMan©Meets_OncoMouse™: Feminism and Technoscience*](https://www.routledge.com/ModestWitnessSecondMillenniumFemaleMancMeetsOncoMousetm-Feminism-and-Technoscience/Haraway/p/book/9780415912457)** (Routledge, 1997) — Won the **4S Ludwik Fleck Prize (1999)**. Collaborative work with painter Lynn Randolph. Examines the OncoMouse (first patented mammal) as a figure for thinking about feminism, capitalism, and biotechnology.

- **[*How Like a Leaf: A Conversation with Donna J. Haraway*](https://www.routledge.com/How-Like-a-Leaf-An-Interview-with-Thyrza-Nichols-Goodeve/Haraway-Goodeve/p/book/9780415924023)** (Routledge, 2000) — Book-length interview with Thyrza Nichols Goodeve. Best entry point for understanding her life and intellectual development.

- **[*The Companion Species Manifesto: Dogs, People, and Significant Otherness*](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/distributed/C/bo3618612.html)** (Prickly Paradigm Press, 2003) — Her shift from cyborgs to dogs. The companion species relationship (human-dog co-evolution) as a model for understanding how to live with radical difference. "Companion species" not "companion animals" — because what we learn is about relation, not ownership.

- **[*The Haraway Reader*](https://www.routledge.com/The-Haraway-Reader/Haraway/p/book/9780415966894)** (Routledge, 2004) — Anthology of key essays.

- **[*When Species Meet*](https://upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/when-species-meet)** (University of Minnesota Press, 2008) — Deepens companion species thinking. Explores biosociality, the laboratory, and what happens when different kinds of species encounter each other. Examines agility training with dogs as cross-species worlding.

- **[*Staying with the Trouble: Making Kin in the Chthulucene*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/staying-with-the-trouble)** (Duke University Press, 2016) — Her masterwork. Proposes the **Chthulucene** as alternative to Anthropocene: an epoch of multispecies entanglement rather than human dominance. "Make kin not babies" — the political slogan. Theorizes **sympoiesis** (making-together) vs. autopoiesis (self-making). The SF method: Science Fact + Science Fiction + Speculative Feminism + Speculative Fabulation + So Far.

- **[*Manifestly Haraway*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/manifestly-haraway)** (University of Minnesota Press, 2016) — Compiles both the Cyborg Manifesto and Companion Species Manifesto with a new interview.

- **[*Making Kin Not Population: Reconceiving Generations*](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/distributed/M/bo29052227.html)** (Prickly Paradigm Press, 2018) — Co-edited with **Adele E. Clarke**. Grows out of the 2015 "Make Kin not Babies" panel. Feminist proposals for addressing population, environment, race, and class.

### Key Essays
- **["A Manifesto for Cyborgs: Science, Technology, and Socialist-Feminism in the 1980s"](https://warwick.ac.uk/fac/arts/english/currentstudents/undergraduate/modules/fictionnownarrativemediaandtheoryinthe21stcentury/manifestly_haraway_----_a_cyborg_manifesto_science_technology_and_socialist-feminism_in_the_....pdf)** (Socialist Review, 1985) — Originally commissioned as a piece on feminist strategy for the left after Reagan's election. Grew into an oracular meditation on cybernetics, gender, and politics. Became one of the most cited texts in 20th century feminist theory.
- **["Situated Knowledges: The Science Question in Feminism and the Privilege of Partial Perspective"](https://philpapers.org/rec/HARSKT)** (Feminist Studies, 14(3), 1988) — Argues all knowledge is situated, embodied, and partial. Against the "God trick" of scientific objectivity. Developed in conversation with **Nancy Hartsock** and feminist philosophers. The epistemological foundation for everything else she does.
- **["The Promises of Monsters: A Regenerative Politics for Inappropriate/d Others"](https://doi.org/10.4324/9780203699850-15)** (1992) — Examines how monsters (inappropriate/d others) can serve as political figures for multispecies solidarity.

## Venue / Circuit Analysis

Haraway operates in **three overlapping circuits**:

1. **US Academic/Feminist Theory** — Feminist Studies journals (*Feminist Studies*, *Signs*, *differences*), 4S conferences, MLA, AHA. The disciplinary home base. She publishes in both science and humanities journals, which is rare.

2. **European Critical Theory/Art Circuit** — ZKM Karlsruhe (with Latour), Mudam Luxembourg, Barbican London, MUCEM Marseille, CIVA Brussels, Centrum Sztuki Warsaw, Busan Museum of Art. The documentary has screened at 20+ major contemporary art institutions. ArtReview Power 100 #3 (2017). This is the art-philosophy circuit.

3. **Multispecies/STS/Environmental Humanities** — UC Santa Cruz, 4S, Multispecies Salon. International environmental humanities networks (Nordic countries, Germany, Australia). *Staying with the Trouble* used in multispecies studies, environmental humanities, animal studies worldwide.

**What's unusual:** She moved into the contemporary art world without trying. Her ideas — cyborg, situated knowledge, Chthulucene — became "part of the art world's DNA" (ArtReview). Artists cite her. Residencies reference her. This happened because her concepts are aesthetically generative, not just analytically useful.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
**Two-word concept coining, perfected.** Haraway's career is built on generating *unforgettable conceptual labels* that force encounters:
- "Cyborg" — not a new word, but a new political metaphor (1985)
- "Situated knowledges" — not a new concept, but a precise label for the feminist epistemology already being developed (1988)
- "Companion species" — not "companion animals," because the shift matters (2003)
- "Chthulucene" — not "Anthropocene," for specific political reasons (2016)
- "Sympoiesis" — not "autopoiesis," to emphasize togetherness (2016)
- "Making kin not babies" — the slogan as concept (2015)
- "SF" — Science Fiction / Science Fact / Speculative Feminism / Speculative Fabulation / So Far — one acronym doing multiple things

**Pattern**: she takes an existing situation (cybernetics, dogs, climate crisis) and invents a *label* that reframes what's already there. The label isn't decoration — it's the epistemic intervention.

**Framework multiplication**: Biology × feminism → cyborg theory (field-breaking). Primatology × race theory × feminist theory → *Primate Visions*. Animal studies × environmental humanities × speculative fiction → *Staying with the Trouble*.

### Career Architecture
**Field declaration through manifesto.** The Cyborg Manifesto (1985) didn't just theorize cyborgs — it *declared* that feminist theory must engage with information technology. Before the manifesto, feminism and technoscience were largely separate. After: they had to talk.

**Institutional positioning**: Becoming the **first tenured feminist theory professor in the US** (1980, UCSC) was not accidental. UCSC's History of Consciousness program was the only department in the country that could house someone who wanted to do feminist biology × philosophy × history of science. She found the one institutional home that fit.

**Staying put.** Unlike many academics who collect prestigious chairs, Haraway stayed at UCSC for her entire career. The counterexample to the "strategic migration" pattern: **deep embeddedness in a single eccentric institution** created the intellectual community (with Anna Tsing, Angela Davis, etc.) that made her later work possible.

**Coalition papers as territorial claims**: *Making Kin Not Population* (2018) with Adele Clarke is a co-authored manifesto — claiming territory collaboratively rather than solo.

### Methodology Signature
**Companion figures as thinking tools.** This is Haraway's most distinctive methodological move: she doesn't illustrate arguments with examples, she *thinks with figures*. The cyborg, the OncoMouse, the dog, the string figure, the spider — these are *conceptual apparatus*, not examples. The move allows her to do philosophy while appearing to do natural history. It's simultaneously rigorous and wildly generative.

**Making trouble (staying with it)**: Her method is deliberately non-resolving. She doesn't provide answers. She complicates. This is philosophically serious (following Whitehead's process philosophy) but also strategically useful: a text that doesn't resolve stays relevant longer.

**Collective knowledge-making**: Every book acknowledges enormous debts to students, conversations, and co-thinkers. This isn't false modesty — it's a methodological claim that knowledge is produced collectively and in response to particular conditions. The method matches the argument.

### Network Strategy
**Coloring across disciplines.** Her collaborators span: feminist philosophers (Hartsock, Harding), biologists (Hutchinson), artists (Randolph), anthropologists (Tsing), philosophers (Stengers, Latour), filmmakers (Terranova). She doesn't network within one field — she's the person that multiple fields reference but none fully claims.

**The art world as amplifier**: Not seeking it — it came to her. Once ArtReview named her #3 (2017), a new generation of artists discovered her texts through art institutions rather than universities. This created a secondary network of practitioners who operationalize her concepts in artistic practice.

**Teaching as network**: UCSC HistCon produced an extraordinary cohort of scholars who carry Haraway's methods forward. Karen Barad (quantum feminist physics, now at UCSC), Chandra Mohanty (feminist postcolonialism), many others. Her students are her extended network.

### Key Moves (Chronological)
1. **1972: PhD in biology, not feminism** — When feminist theory was only beginning to exist in academia, Haraway got her credentials in biology first. This gave her the scientific authority to critique science from within. She couldn't be dismissed as ignorant of what she was critiquing.

2. **1980: First tenured feminist theory professor, UCSC** — By taking this position, she simultaneously *legitimized feminist theory as a discipline* and *anchored herself at the most intellectually wild institution in the US*. The provocation was the content; the institution gave her cover.

3. **1985: The Cyborg Manifesto** — Originally a political essay for the left after Reagan's election. Became one of the most cited texts in 20th century feminist theory by framing technological transformation as a feminist opportunity rather than a threat. Timing: before the internet, but precisely when cybernetics was reshaping the world.

4. **1988: "Situated Knowledges"** — The epistemological complement to the Manifesto. Where the Manifesto is political, this is philosophical. Together they establish: feminism must engage technoscience, and technoscience must acknowledge its situated, gendered nature. Two essays doing the work of a whole field.

5. **1989: *Primate Visions*** — Applied the feminist STS method to a whole scientific field. Controversial and important. Demonstrated that this wasn't just abstract theory — it could do real analytical work on real science.

6. **2003: *The Companion Species Manifesto*** — The shift from cyborgs (human-machine) to dogs (human-animal) surprised people. But it was consistent: she'd always been interested in the non-human. Dogs are a more literal version of the cyborg argument: they emerged through co-evolution with humans, they are "companion species," neither fully natural nor fully cultural.

7. **2016: *Staying with the Trouble*** — Synthesized 30 years of work into a single framework. The Chthulucene, sympoiesis, "make kin not babies" — all the concepts she'd been developing. The timing (Anthropocene discourse peaking) was perfect: she had the best alternative concept.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **The concept is the contribution**: Amber's most impactful moves will probably be conceptual labels that reframe fields. "Situated knowledges," "companion species," "Chthulucene" — each of these *reorganized* existing discussions. Amber could think about: what's the concept that reorganizes the conversation around machine behavior / more-than-human design?

2. **Science training as credential for critique**: Haraway got her PhD in biology so she could critique biology with authority. For Amber, having deep CS knowledge while doing critical computing is similarly powerful. The credential enables the critique.

3. **The manifesto as field-declaring genre**: Haraway wrote manifestos at key moments (1985, 2003). They work because they're provocative but specific, grounded but speculative. Amber might think about when manifesto-writing would serve her intellectual project.

4. **Dogs over abstractions**: Haraway grounds her most abstract arguments in specific, material, embodied things (dogs, primates, string figures, spiders). This makes philosophy accessible and gives it traction in practice. Amber's own work might find its "dog" — the specific non-human entity or system that makes her argument concrete.

5. **Don't resolve**: Haraway's refusal to provide neat answers is philosophically honest and strategically smart. Papers that stay with trouble rather than resolving it generate more conversation. The field continues talking about *Cyborg Manifesto* 40 years later precisely because it didn't close down debate.

6. **Coalition thinking > solo thinking**: Every major Haraway text names its debts extensively. This isn't weakness — it's a model of how ideas actually develop, and it builds networks at the same time. Amber's citations and acknowledgments could be more generous and visible as a network-building strategy.
