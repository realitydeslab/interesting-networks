# Mercedes Bunz

Professor of Digital Culture and Society at **[King's College London](https://www.kcl.ac.uk/people/mercedes-bunz)** (promoted 2022). Co-director of the **[[Creative AI Lab]]** — a research partnership between KCL's Department of Digital Humanities and Serpentine Galleries. Came to academia through electronic music journalism, internet history, and Berlin digital culture; brings an unusually grounded political economy perspective to questions of how computational systems produce knowledge, transform creative practice, and reshape power.

The rare figure who co-founded a Berlin electronic music magazine in the 1990s, ran Der Tagesspiegel's online operation, reported for The Guardian, gave evidence to the House of Lords on AI, and now publishes at NeurIPS. Her career traces the full arc of how "the internet" became "AI" — and she documented it at every stage.

## Background

Born in **Magdeburg, East Germany** (November 16, 1971). Attended Celtis-Gymnasium secondary school in Schweinfurt (graduated 1991). Studied **philosophy and art history at Freie Universität Berlin** — the politically radical West Berlin university with strong continental philosophy and 1970s student-movement DNA. Received a **Heinrich-Böll-Stiftung scholarship** (the political foundation of the German Green Party) to complete her degree at **Bauhaus-Universität Weimar**, writing her dissertation on the history of the internet from the 1950s to the 1980s — a genealogical project before internet genealogy was fashionable.

Her dissertation was published as *Vom Speicher zum Verteiler – Die Geschichte des Internet* (Kulturverlag Kadmos, 2008), and subsequently used by Melih Bilgil in his 2009 YouTube animation ["History of the Internet"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9hIQjrMHTv4) which went viral.

**The Berlin years**: In 1997, while still a student, she co-founded **[De:Bug](https://de-bug.de/)** — a Berlin monthly that was Germany's definitive electronic music / digital culture magazine, fusing techno, house, net culture, and media theory. She became co-editor and editor-in-chief (1999–2001). This immersion in Berlin club culture and early internet culture gives her a different relationship to digital technology than most media theorists — she was present at the scene as journalist, not just as analyst.

She then worked as **editor-in-chief of zitty** (Berlin city magazine), then ran the **online operation of Der Tagesspiegel** (major Berlin daily). In 2009 she joined **The Guardian** as media and technology reporter, covering online journalism and social networking (until early 2011).

**2010**: Won the **Fachjournalisten-Preis** — the award given by the German Association of Specialist Editors (Deutscher Fachjournalisten-Verband).

**2011**: **Impakt Fellowship**, Centre for the Humanities, Utrecht University — first academic fellowship.

**2012–2014**: Directed the **Hybrid Publishing Lab at Leuphana University of Lüneburg**, researching the effects of digitalization on academic publishing. Co-founded **meson press** (with Marcus Burkhardt and Andreas Kirchner) as a direct outcome — an open-access press for digital cultures.

**2014**: Became **Senior Lecturer at the University of Westminster**, London. Her Westminster colleague **Graham Meikle** co-authored *The Internet of Things* (Polity, 2018) with her.

**2018**: Started at **KCL Department of Digital Humanities** (September).

**2022**: Promoted to **Professor of Digital Culture and Society** — the first time this specific title appeared at KCL.

### German Medientheorie Lineage

Her formation at FU Berlin and Bauhaus-Weimar places her adjacent to but distinct from the strict Kittlerian tradition (Kittler was at Humboldt, Berlin, and his approach prioritizes technical media as such). Bunz's orientation is more political-economic: she's concerned with what algorithms *do to* knowledge, work, and public life rather than with the technical inscriptions of media channels. Her intellectual sympathies are closer to the Frankfurt School tradition filtered through digital culture — algorithmic governance, the transformation of labor, and public epistemology — than to Kittler's materialist media archaeology. Her Heinrich-Böll-Stiftung affiliation signals the Green/progressive-left formation explicitly. The Bauhaus-Weimar connection is also significant: the Bauhaus site carries a history of integrating craft, art, and technology that maps onto her interest in creative AI practice.

- [*Communication*](https://meson.press/books/communication/) (with Paula Bialski and Finn Brunton; University of Minnesota Press & meson press, Minneapolis, 2019, **open access**) — On how machine communication — interacting not just *via* but *with* machines — has transformed contemporary communication. Close readings of interfaces and field study of software development. Co-published in hybrid format (print + open-access digital).

## The C5 Model

The **C5 model** (2023) is the Creative AI Lab's main intellectual framework — a position paper co-authored by all five investigators (Bunz, Chávez Heras, Jäger, Milne, Zylinska). The "C5" stands for five criteria for evaluating AI art practice:

1. **Creative** — does it generate genuinely new aesthetic work?
2. **Critical** — does it interrogate the conditions of its own production?
3. **Cultural** — is it embedded in specific cultural contexts, not generic?
4. **Contextual** — is it responsive to the specific social/historical/institutional context?
5. **Collaborative** — does it involve genuine collaboration across humans, machines, institutions?

This is a direct counter-framework to the dominant "wow, it made an image" mode of AI art discourse. It insists that creative AI must be simultaneously *critical* of AI — that the aesthetics and the critique must coexist. The framework reflects Bunz's deep skepticism about AI-as-culture (see her 2019 article) while providing a practical evaluative model for collaborative creative work.

Project title: **"AI art beyond the gallery: exploring the capacity of cultural institutions to impact tech policy."** Partner: **Kay Watson, Head of Arts Technologies, Serpentine Galleries**.

Core research question: How can cultural institutions' AI experimentation inform public policy? Can artistic AI research shape government understanding of AI in more humane directions? The project initiates dialogue with policymakers and civic initiatives, sharing knowledge gained during the commissioning process. Builds on Creative AI Lab's work. Will produce a joint KCL/Serpentine report, plus workshops and a conference.

Quote from Bunz: *"A recent report by the House of Lords stressed the narrow pool of private sector expertise to inform policy and standards when it comes to AI. This BRAID Fellowship allows me to research and highlight alternative expertise in the cultural sector bridging AI divides by integrating Arts and Humanities research more fully."*

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Bunz moves between **four overlapping circuits**:

1. **UK digital policy / responsible AI**: BRAID, AHRC, House of Lords (2017), Ada Lovelace Institute, Alan Turing Institute — the UK AI governance circuit. The BRAID fellowship solidifies this.

2. **UK/European AI art + gallery**: Serpentine (Creative AI Lab), CCCB (Barcelona), Congreso Futuro (Santiago) — the culturally-ambitious international art/tech institutions that present technology as a humanistic question.

3. **UK critical humanities / academic**: Radical Philosophy, KCL colleagues (Aradau in War Studies, Gray in critical data studies), *Culture Theory and Critique*, meson press / Open Humanities Press — the left-critical humanities academic publishing circuit.

4. **Berlin digital culture (historical)**: De:Bug, Tagesspiegel, taz, Telepolis — her formative circuit; gives her a relationship to "digital culture" as a lived urban phenomenon, not just an academic object.

She is notably *absent* from the pure AI industry circuit (no Google I/O, no OpenAI events) and largely absent from the aesthetic-philosophy circuit (no e-flux, no documenta). Her positioning is at the intersection of policy, cultural institutions, and critical academia — neither pure art world nor pure tech world.

## Research Taste Analysis

**The problem she's working on**: How do digital systems quietly transform the conditions of knowledge production, democratic participation, and creative practice — without triggering the political resistance that loud revolutionary change would provoke?

The title *The Silent Revolution* encodes this perfectly: the revolution is real, structural, and profound, but it happens without noise. This epistemological-political framing is different from the more visible AI-risk discourse (which is noisy) and from the art world's AI-excitement discourse (also noisy in a different way). She's tracking the *quietness* of the transformation as itself a political feature.

**Her distinctive position in the UK AI discourse**:
- She reads the Frankfurt School but came through club culture, not pure academia
- She cares about policy outcomes (BRAID, House of Lords) but from a humanities position, not a tech-policy position
- She produces open-access scholarship (meson press, Minnesota Press) as a *political* commitment, not just a distribution strategy
- Her journalism background means she writes clearly, avoids jargon, and cares about public audiences

**The C5 model as crystallization**: The C5 framework (Creative/Critical/Cultural/Contextual/Collaborative) is her attempt to give practical form to the core tension of her work: how can AI be simultaneously creative and self-critical? The framework demands that good AI art be *both* generative and critical — it refuses the split between "making" and "interrogating." This is a programmatic statement about what humanities research should contribute to AI discourse.

**The data governance turn**: The NeurIPS 2024 paper on choral AI datasets, the "Reciprocity Deficits" paper, and the BRAID project all signal a recent turn toward practical governance frameworks — how do communities retain agency over AI systems that use their data or operate in their environments? This is a move from critique to infrastructure, consistent with the broader trajectory of critical AI studies.

## Why Interesting for Amber

1. **The C5 model is immediately usable**: For anyone thinking about how to situate AI art practice, C5 provides a vocabulary that balances creative ambition with critical responsibility. It's the most practical academic framework for evaluating AI art that exists.

2. **The BRAID project as model**: "AI art beyond the gallery" is a template for how art institutions can contribute to AI governance. If Amber is thinking about her work's relationship to policy, this shows the specific institutional pathway.

3. **The journalism-to-academia trajectory**: Bunz's career demonstrates that being embedded in a scene (Berlin club culture, early internet) and writing *about* it can produce genuinely rigorous academic scholarship. Her books on internet history and algorithmic culture are informed by direct participation, not just external observation.

4. **German media theory, anglicized**: She brings the Freie Universität / Bauhaus tradition — materialist, politically suspicious of technology — into an English-language academic context. This is a translation project that maps onto Amber's own position at the intersection of cultural traditions.

5. **meson press as infrastructure**: The model of academics building their own open-access press for their own field is worth knowing. meson press has 91 titles, is distributed worldwide, and manages to be taken seriously without APC fees — it's a viable infrastructure model.

6. **The quiet-revolution thesis**: "Digitalization transforms without making noise" — this framing is useful for anyone thinking about how AI is changing creative practice. The change doesn't have to be spectacular to be structural.
