# Anthony Dunne

The intellectual founder of speculative design and critical design as taught disciplines. Co-director (with Fiona Raby) of [[Dunne & Raby]] since 1994. Former Professor and Head of Design Interactions at the [[Royal College of Art]] (2005–2015) — the ten-year golden era that trained a generation of critical designers. University Professor of Design and Social Inquiry at Parsons / [[The New School]], New York (2016–2025). Now Distinguished Visiting Professor at [[China Academy of Art]] (CAA), Hangzhou (2025–). Author of the canonical texts of the field: *[Hertzian Tales](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262541992/)* (1999), *[Design Noir](https://www.bloomsbury.com/uk/design-noir-9781350116375/)* (2001), *[Speculative Everything](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262019842/)* (2013), *[Not Here, Not Now](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262049665/)* (2025). Works in MoMA, V&A, MAK Vienna.

## Background

Anthony Dunne studied **Industrial Design** at the [Royal College of Art (RCA)](https://www.rca.ac.uk), then worked at **Sony Design in Tokyo** — a critical formative period that shaped his understanding of consumer electronics culture as a site of ideological inscription. Returning to London, he completed a **PhD in Computer Related Design (CRD)** at the RCA, producing what would become *Hertzian Tales* (published internally 1999, MIT Press 2005). He coined the term "critical design" there.

He was a founding member (with Fiona Raby) of the **CRD Research Studio** at RCA, where they began their long-term partnership. The studio fused critical theory, design practice, and electronic culture. Dunne began teaching at RCA in 1991.

In **2005**, Dunne became **Professor and Head of Design Interactions (DI)** at RCA — a role he held for a decade. Under his direction, DI became the world's most influential critical/speculative design programme. When they left in 2015, MoMA senior curator [Paola Antonelli](https://twitter.com/curiousoctopus/status/572444136885915648) stated their tenure had "changed the course of design."

The **RCA suspended Design Interactions** for a year after their departure triggered a mass exodus of teaching staff — testament to how much the programme was built around their intellectual leadership. The programme was eventually reformulated.

In **2016**, Dunne and Raby moved to New York as **University Professors of Design and Social Inquiry at Parsons / The New School**, directing the **[Designed Realities Studio](https://www.newschool.edu/parsons/)**. They worked across Parsons' School of Design Strategies (SDS) and the [Graduate Institute for Design, Ethnography and Social Thought (GIDEST)](https://www.newschool.edu/) — bringing design into conversation with social science, humanities, and political theory. They also developed the Many Worlds Working Group and the GIDEST Collective, producing collaborative research on posthumanism and interdisciplinary imagining.

After **9 years at Parsons** (2016–2025), Dunne and Raby moved on, now holding positions as **Distinguished Visiting Professors at the [China Academy of Art (CAA)](http://www.caa.edu.cn/)**, Hangzhou — the premier Chinese art academy, a pivotal turn toward East Asia.

## Domains

- **Speculative design** — using design to explore possible futures and provoke debate (vs. affirmative, market-driven design)
- **Critical design** — design as critical theory made material; objects that challenge assumptions about technology, culture, ethics
- **Design fiction** — narrative-embedded designed objects that make ideas experiential
- **Electronic products and technology culture** — the hertzian, the invisible, the electromagnetic landscape we inhabit
- **Posthumanism and more-than-human design** — moving beyond human-centered design to pluriversal, ontological alternatives
- **Ontological design** — design that shapes possible worlds, not just products

## Key Works (Dunne & Raby, chronological)

### Books
- [*Hertzian Tales: Electronic Products, Aesthetic Experience, and Critical Design*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262541992/) (MIT Press, 1999/2005) — The founding document of critical design. Argues that industrial design has ignored the rich experiential terrain of electromagnetic space. Coined "critical design." PhD thesis turned book. Korean translation 2002.
- [*Design Noir: The Secret Life of Electronic Objects*](https://www.bloomsbury.com/uk/design-noir-9781350116375/) (Birkhäuser/August, 2001; republished Bloomsbury 2021 in Designing in Dark Times series) — Critical design practice applied to electronic objects and their "secret lives." Chinese translation 2023.
- [*Speculative Everything: Design, Fiction and Social Dreaming*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262019842/) (MIT Press, 2013) — **The canonical text.** Defines speculative design as a practice, maps its intellectual genealogy (Italian radical design, critical theory, SF, art), and argues for design as a tool for social dreaming. Translated into Russian (Strelka Institute, 2017), Chinese (Jiangsu Fine Art, 2017; new edition 2024), Korean (agbook, 2024). New paperback edition MIT Press 2024 with new foreword.
- [*Not Here, Not Now: Speculative Thought, Impossibility, and the Design Imagination*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262049665/) (MIT Press, 2025) — Their most recent book. "What it means to design at a time when, for many people, the future seems to have become an impossibility." A conceptual travelogue through the edges of science, philosophy, and literature. Imagined archives, glossaries, taxonomies, design responses to impossible objects. A design experiment itself.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Dunne operates across **four circuits simultaneously** — a deliberate multi-institutional strategy:

1. **UK / European museum circuit**: MoMA, V&A, MAK Vienna, Centre Pompidou, Design Museum London, Vitra Design Museum, MUDAC Lausanne, Whitechapel Gallery. These are legitimacy-conferring institutions; his work in their permanent collections guarantees citation and influence.

2. **Academic/design research circuit**: RCA, Parsons, CAA, ELISAVA Barcelona, Design Academy Eindhoven, Aalto, Konstfack Stockholm, IASPIS. Lectures at 30+ schools globally — primarily via invitation. He doesn't do conferences; he does *lectures*. This is an individual influence circuit, not a committee-membership one.

3. **East Asian circuit** (growing, now dominant): China Academy of Art Hangzhou, Central Academy of Fine Art (CAFA) Beijing, MMCA Seoul, KYOTO Design Lab, Tokyo University of the Arts. The move to CAA as Distinguished Visiting Professor in 2025 is the culmination of a decade-long China-Japan engagement. The Chinese translations of both *Design Noir* and *Speculative Everything* (2017, 2023) are strategic publishing moves that preceded this.

4. **Small/emerging/critical circuit**: Strelka Institute Moscow, Serpentine Podcast, e-flux event, Near Future Laboratory podcast, Domaine de Boisbuchet. These are the ideological edge — where D&R speak to the avant-garde. Strelka in particular (Russian design school with a strong speculative design culture) ran workshops and published the Russian translation.

**Key pattern**: Dunne is almost never at large commercial design fairs (no Milan, no SXSW mainstage, no Consumer Electronics Show). His circuit is art-museums × art schools × critical institutes × East Asia. Every venue choice signals allegiance to design-as-inquiry, not design-as-product.

## Why Interesting

Anthony Dunne sits at the **origin point of the entire intellectual lineage** of what Amber does. *Speculative Everything* is not just a book — it's the infrastructure of a field: it gave speculative designers a shared vocabulary, a historical genealogy (Italian radical design → critical theory → SF → design fiction), and a legitimating institutional framework.

More importantly for **Amber's current work**: Dunne's trajectory from electromagnetic culture (*Hertzian Tales*) → biotechnology futures (Foragers, BioLand) → governance/political fiction (UMK) → philosophical impossibility (*Not Here, Not Now*) shows how critical design can evolve from technology critique to ontological inquiry. This is the direction Amber's machine behavior + speculative design work could move.

The **East Asian pivot** is a strong signal: Dunne is betting that the most intellectually interesting space for speculative design is now in China/Asia. This aligns with Amber's sinofuturism interests and institutional base.

He also models an unusual career architecture: no Twitter, no self-promotion, no large research group. Influence flows through **teaching (students), books (readers), exhibitions (museum visitors), and invitations (peers)**. Quiet, deep, lasting.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Philosophical object-making**: Dunne's core strategy is taking an abstract philosophical or theoretical problem and materializing it as a designed object or system. *Hertzian Tales*: electromagnetism as aesthetic material. *Placebo Project*: invisible radiation made tangible through furniture. *Meinong's Jungle*: impossible objects from philosophy of object theory. *Not Here, Not Now*: impossibility itself as design brief. The pattern is consistent: **take a concept from philosophy/theory, build a designed artifact around it, let the artifact provoke**.

**Two-step move** (theory → design): Unlike most designers who start with problem statements, D&R start with conceptual problems from philosophy, sociology, science fiction. They read widely (Borges, Meinong, de Quincey, quantum mechanics, synthetic biology) and then ask: what designed object could this idea inhabit? This is *inverse engineering* — philosophy to artifact, not problem to solution.

**Taxonomy of worlds**: Their most recent move (*Not Here, Not Now*, UMK, Many Worlds) is toward designing *multiple possible worlds* simultaneously — not one speculative scenario but a pluriverse of scenarios. This is deeply connected to multiverse physics (Everett) and pluriversal anthropology (de la Cadena). A systematic world-building methodology.

### Career Architecture

**Field declaration move** (1999): *Hertzian Tales* didn't just present work — it *named a field* ("critical design"), provided its theory, and located it within design history. This is the field-declaration move: zoom out from your own projects, articulate the larger space, claim it. One book became the founding document of a discipline.

**Programme as infrastructure** (2005–2015): Running Design Interactions wasn't just teaching — it was building a field-generating machine. 150+ students over 10 years, each going out into the world as a carrier of critical design thinking. The programme WAS the infrastructure. Dunne didn't write papers about speculative design — he built the institution that produced speculative designers.

**Strategic timing of departure** (2015): Leaving RCA when Design Interactions was at its peak — rather than waiting until decline — preserved D&R's reputation and intellectual authority. They left while they were still defining the field. The decision to move to Parsons (social science × design × humanities) rather than another pure design school signals intentional intellectual expansion.

**Book + exhibition as unit** (consistent throughout): Each major project generates both an exhibition AND a book chapter or catalogue essay. The exhibition reaches gallery visitors; the book reaches students and researchers globally. This doubled output model means every project has two audiences and two time horizons (exhibition = now; book = 10-year citation window).

**East Asia move** (2019–2025): Before becoming Distinguished Visiting Professor at CAA, Dunne was visiting CAA and CAFA repeatedly (lectures Dec 2019, Oct 2018). The Chinese translations of their books (2017, 2023, 2024) preceded the appointment. This is a *multi-year strategic positioning* before committing to an institution.

### Key Career Moves (chronological)

1. **Sony Design Tokyo stint** (~late 1980s) → Understanding consumer electronics culture from inside the industry, not as outsider critique. This gave D&R's early work a *technical specificity* that distinguished it from pure art theory.

2. **PhD in Computer Related Design at RCA (early 1990s)** → Legitimating speculative practice as research, not just art. The academic framing was crucial for institutional leverage later.

3. **CRD Research Studio founding** (~mid 1990s) → Building institutional infrastructure *early*, before personal reputation was established. The research studio became the springboard for the later teaching programme.

4. **Hertzian Tales published (1999)** → Field-declaration move. Named "critical design." One book → founding document. Enabled invitation to head Design Interactions.

5. **Heading Design Interactions (2005–2015)** → Transformed a programme into a field-generating machine. 10 years of students. The phrase "Design Interactions alumni" became a signal in job applications worldwide.

6. **Receiving inaugural MIT Media Lab Award (2015)** → Validation from MIT/Paola Antonelli circuit just as they left RCA. Perfect timing: high-prestige recognition at the moment of institutional transition.

7. **Moving to Parsons/New School (2016)** → Expanding beyond "design" into social science, humanities. The Many Worlds Working Group and GIDEST collaboration are the outcomes. Intellectual growth, not just institutional lateral move.

8. **Relocating to CAA (2025)** → Betting on East Asia as the next center of speculative design. Following the Chinese translations, student visits, lecture tours. A 10-year positioning strategy paying off.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Name the field, don't just occupy it.** D&R wrote *Speculative Everything* not just to document their work but to *define a discipline*. Amber could consider: what is the name for what she does at the intersection of machine behavior, speculative design, and critical computing? Writing the field-declaring paper/book is a high-leverage move.

2. **Build students, not papers.** The long-term influence of Design Interactions exceeds any paper D&R could have written. Teaching → students → propagation. For Amber: building a pedagogical practice that generates more practitioners is more influential than accumulating citations.

3. **The double output.** Exhibition + book. Performance + paper. Show + tell. Every project should reach two audiences on two time horizons. This is a simple but powerful productivity pattern.

4. **Philosophical raw material.** D&R use philosophy (Meinong, possible worlds, ontology) as design raw material, not as decoration. They read Borges and ask "what does this mean for designed objects?" Amber's machine behavior work can draw on this — what philosophical tradition (philosophy of action? philosophy of mind? speculative realism?) gives her projects their theoretical spine?

5. **Geographic repositioning as intellectual move.** The RCA → Parsons → CAA arc is not just career management — each move was driven by intellectual appetite (social science, then East Asian theory). When Amber considers institutional moves, think of them as intellectual bets, not just career choices.

6. **Quiet influence.** No Twitter. No self-promotion. Influence through exhibitions, teaching, books, and invitations. In an attention economy, this is counterintuitive but durable. Dunne is cited by everyone; he's rarely seen in the feeds.
