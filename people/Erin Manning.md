# Erin Manning

Professor in the Faculty of Fine Arts at **Concordia University**, Montreal, and founding director of the **SenseLab** — a research-creation collective working at the intersection of movement, sensation, art, and philosophy. One of the most important contemporary theorists of **affect, movement, and neurodiversity** in the art-academic field.

SenseLab is organized around the premise that bodies think, and that thinking happens through movement and sensation, not just cognition. The lab has produced the **Immediations** book series at [[Open Humanities Press]] (now moved to punctum books under 3Ecologies), edited with Anna Munster and Bodil Marie Stavning Thomsen. Their events (called "events of radical pedagogy") are collectively designed experiences, not lectures.

Manning's theoretical commitment: **Whitehead's process philosophy** as the basis for thinking movement, relation, and becoming. This intersects with her practice in movement research and her advocacy for **neurodiversity** — particularly the rights and capacities of autistic people, who she argues have a different but not lesser mode of sensation and knowledge.

## Key Works
- *Politics of Touch: Sense, Movement, Sovereignty* (Minnesota UP, 2007)
- *Relationscapes: Movement, Art, Philosophy* (MIT Press, 2009)
- *Always More Than One: Individuation's Dance* (Duke UP, 2013)
- *The Minor Gesture* (Duke UP, 2016) — theory of neurodiversity, minor practice
- *Immediation I & II* (OHP, 2019) — co-authored; with Anna Munster and Bodil Marie Stavning Thomsen
- *For a Pragmatics of the Useless* (Duke UP, 2020)

## SenseLab
Co-founded with Brian Massumi. SSHRC-funded. Runs events that are choreographic + intellectual simultaneously. The *Immediations* project was funded by Canada's SSHRC as a major research-creation grant. Series moved from OHP to punctum books / 3Ecologies imprint.

## Why Interesting
Manning is doing something rare: genuine research-creation that integrates choreographic practice with rigorous process philosophy. The SenseLab events produce knowledge *through* artistic experience, not just *about* it. Her work on neurodiversity is politically significant — arguing for neurodiversity as epistemic diversity, not just identity difference. *The Minor Gesture* is one of the most important recent books on disability, knowledge, and practice. Highly relevant for any thinking about sensing, embodiment, and what "intelligent" bodies are.
