# Erin Manning

**Research Chair in Speculative Pragmatism, Art and Pedagogy** in the Faculty of Fine Arts at Concordia University (Montréal). Founder of the **[[SenseLab]]** — one of the most sustained and generative research-creation collectives in the humanities worldwide, running since 2004. Co-directed with [[Brian Massumi]].

Manning works at the intersection of movement, sensation, neurodiversity, ecology, and process philosophy. Her signature move: take a marginal practice (autistic perception, blackness, the "useless") and show that it reveals something structurally true about how all experience works. Her books progressively map an ontology of relation: from sovereignty and touch → movement and individuation → the minor gesture and neurodiversity → the useless surplus of black life → settler land and ancestry.

As a practitioner she makes large textile works — woven, tufted, hooked, knotted — that enact process philosophy through color, material encounter, and relational making. Her art practice and her theory are not separate tracks: both are research-creation in her specific sense.

## Background

Manning's trajectory moves across at least three major intellectual domains:

**Phase 1 — Political theory of nationalism and space** (late 1990s–early 2000s): Her PhD work at Concordia engaged Québec nationalism, race, and visual culture — work that produced her first book, *Ephemeral Territories* (2003). This gave her a grounding in political theory, sovereignty, and the politics of representation that never entirely disappears from her later work (it resurfaces in *The Minor Gesture*'s politics of disability rights and in *Unsettled*'s settler-colonialism critique).

**Phase 2 — Touch, movement, process philosophy** (2004–2014): The founding of SenseLab with [[Brian Massumi]] in 2004 marks the key pivot. She draws on Whitehead's process philosophy and Simondon's theory of individuation to theorize movement as the primary ontological category. Movement is not what a body does — it is what reality is. *Politics of Touch* (2007) bridges her earlier political theory work to the new sensory framework. *Relationscapes* (2009) and *Always More Than One* (2013) develop "choreographic thinking" as a philosophical method.

**Phase 3 — Neurodiversity, blackness, ecology** (2016–present): *The Minor Gesture* (2016) introduces neurodiversity as epistemological difference — autistic perception as a mode of sensing that reveals the relational field prior to neurotypical "chunking." *For a Pragmatics of the Useless* (2020) extends this to blackness, proposing that neurotypicality and whiteness co-produce a normative baseline ("all black life is neurodiverse life"). The 3Ecologies project runs parallel: a land-based research community north of Montréal. *Unsettled* (2026) moves into memoir and settler-colonial critique. The 3 ecologies (social, environmental, conceptual) are now explicitly in play.

Her institutional affiliations have tracked these phases: from Associate Professor with a Concordia University Research Chair in Relational Art and Philosophy → to full Professor with a Tier 1 Canada Research Chair in Speculative Pragmatism, Art and Pedagogy (among the top tier of Canada's national research chair program, ~$200K/year for 7 years, renewable).

## Key Works

### Single-Authored Books

- [*Ephemeral Territories: Representing Nation, Space and Culture*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/ephemeral-territories) (University of Minnesota Press, 2003) — her first book, from PhD work; Québec nationalism, race, visual culture, sovereignty. Establishes political theory foundations that feed into all later work on the body politic and the minor.

- [*Politics of Touch: Sense, Movement, Sovereignty*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/politics-of-touch) (University of Minnesota Press, 2007) — pivotal transitional book. From sovereignty to sensation. Touch as a political act; the sovereign body retheorized through Derrida on touch, Levinas on ethics, and her own movement practice. Bridges political theory and process philosophy.

- [*Relationscapes: Movement, Art, Philosophy*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262513302/relationscapes/) (MIT Press, 2009) — in the **Technologies of Lived Abstraction** series (co-edited with [[Brian Massumi]]). Uses dance, painting, and new media works to theorize movement as philosophically productive. Introduces "preacceleration" (movement begins before a body moves), "incipient action," and the relational field. Foundation text for her movement-philosophy.

- [*Always More Than One: Individuation's Dance*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/always-more-than-one) (Duke University Press, 2013) — **327 pp., 33 illus.** Works from Whitehead's process philosophy and Simondon's theory of individuation toward "choreographic thinking" — a mode of perception prior to settled categories. Introduces "autistic perception" as a lens for ecological politics: autistics experience the relational field *before* chunking it into discrete objects. Manning argues this underlies all perception. Prelude by [[Brian Massumi]]. Blurbed by William Forsythe (choreographer), Steven Shaviro, Ralph James Savarese.

- [*Thought in the Act: Passages in the Ecology of Experience*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/thought-in-the-act) (University of Minnesota Press, 2014) — **co-authored with [[Brian Massumi]]**. Ten "passage" essays on research-creation as philosophical practice; ecology of experience; the event as unit of analysis. Their collaborative theoretical voice at its most developed. Core text for understanding research-creation as they define it.

- [*The Minor Gesture*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/the-minor-gesture) (Duke University Press, 2016) — her most cited and widely assigned book. The "minor gesture" (from Deleuze/Guattari's minor literature) is the barely perceptible movement that opens new fields of potential. Applied to disability rights, neurodiversity, autism, race. Chapters on "autistic perception," "dancing with autism," and the politics of the minor. Engages Fred Moten and Stefano Harney's undercommons. Blurbed by Fred Moten.

- [*For a Pragmatics of the Useless*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/for-a-pragmatics-of-the-useless) (Duke University Press, 2020) — **384 pp., 30 illus.** Her most ambitious book. Examines what falls outside already-known functions and established standards of value "not for want of potential but for carrying an excess of it." Central figures: the infrathin, the artful, proprioceptive tactility, neurodiversity, black life. Key claim: "All black life is neurodiverse life" (not an equation, but "an approximation of proximity"). Dialogue with Guattari (schizoanalysis) and the Black radical tradition (Moten, Harney). Proposes alter-pedagogy and alter-economy as practices that generate surplus value of life outside capitalist logics of usefulness. Blurbed by Stefano Harney and Ashon T. Crawley.

- [*Unsettled*](https://www.minorcompositions.info/?p=1618) (Minor Compositions, forthcoming March 20, 2026) — **178 pp.** Her most personal book. A philosophical memoir exploring settler-colonial ancestry in Québec, Indigenous land, and what it means to belong through relation rather than bloodline. Centers on her friend R. and the question of Métis ancestry claims in Québec. Critiques the phenomenon of usurping Indigeneity for social/political gain while reinforcing whiteness. Blurbed by Kim TallBear (Indigenous Peoples, Technoscience, and Society, University of Alberta) and Peter Kulchyski (Native Studies, University of Manitoba). Available as [free digital download](https://archive.org/details/unsettled-erinmanning).

## Art Practice

Manning's artistic practice runs parallel to and entangled with her philosophy — it IS her philosophy, materially realized. Not illustration; research-creation.

**Medium**: Predominantly textile — woven, tufted, hooked, knotted, stitched works. Large-scale hangings that use color, texture, and material process to embody the philosophical concepts she writes about. Color is central: not as representation but as relational event (Whitehead: "the smile spreads over the face as the face fits itself onto the smile").

**ProTactile**: She is exploring ProTactile — the language developed by DeafBlind people that uses touch on the body to communicate — as an artistic practice and a philosophical lens. ProTactile extends her neurodiversity work into different sensoriality: what does art become when its medium is touch on skin rather than vision?

**Collaborative and participatory**: Her art works are often made in collective processes — workshop settings, participatory installations. The making is the event.

## Why Interesting

Manning is doing something genuinely rare: sustaining a 20+ year research-creation collective that has produced a sustained body of philosophical work, a journal, a book series, and a successor institution — all while maintaining an art practice that shows internationally. Most academics produce solo books; most artists build studio practices. Manning builds *infrastructure*.

Her most provocative theoretical move — "all black life is neurodiverse life" — creates a resonance field between disability studies and Black radical theory that hadn't been systematically mapped. This is not a comfort zone maneuver; it's intellectually risky and has generated real engagement across multiple fields.

For anyone thinking about **embodied cognition, non-standard sensing, and collective knowledge production**, Manning's work is essential. Her account of autistic perception as "pre-chunked" relational awareness has direct implications for how we think about AI perception systems (which also operate prior to semantic categorization, in some sense) — though she would likely resist this reading.

The **research-creation methodology** she has developed with Massumi is the most articulated alternative to standard academic epistemology in the humanities today. It's directly relevant to practice-based design research.

Her turn to **settler-colonialism and Indigenous land** in *Unsettled* suggests the 3 ecologies framework is expanding: it was always about more than art and philosophy — now it's explicitly about land sovereignty and belonging. This is where process philosophy meets political urgency.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**The "minor" as a lever**: Manning consistently works from the margins toward the center. She takes a practice considered minor (autistic perception, useless art, black sociality) and shows it reveals the structure of all experience. This is not romanticization of the margin — it's epistemological judo. The "minor" undoes the normate's claim to universality by demonstrating that the norm is itself a special case.

**Framework multiplication**: Whitehead × Simondon × Deleuze/Guattari → a processual ontology of movement. She doesn't synthesize these; she uses them as lenses on the same problem (how does relation emerge?). Each lens reveals something the others miss. Adding Guattari's three ecologies in the later work expands the spatial/ecological dimension.

**Concept circulation across registers**: "Choreographic thinking" starts in dance philosophy; arrives in neurodiversity theory (autistic perception as choreographic). "Minor gesture" starts in aesthetic theory; arrives in disability rights and Black radical tradition. "Research-creation" starts as a funding category in Canadian research councils; becomes a full epistemological claim. She takes concepts out of their original register and demonstrates their transversal range.

**Two-word concept coining**: "Minor gesture," "choreographic thinking," "immediation," "autistic perception," "infrathin," "proprioceptive tactility" — each is a portable capsule that travels beyond the original argument. Massumi's "autonomy of affect" is the model; Manning produces a cascade of similar concepts.

### Career Architecture

**Infrastructure-first**: The founding move is not a book but an institution: SenseLab (2004). The books come after, emerging from the collective practice. This is the opposite of the standard academic model (book → reputation → possible lab). Manning builds the lab first, then the books are its output. This creates a feedback loop: the lab generates the research, the research funds the lab, the books document the process.

**Series editing as intellectual shaping**: Technologies of Lived Abstraction (MIT Press), Thought in the Act (Duke UP), Immediations (OHP/punctum) — three book series spanning three major publishers, each positioning research-creation as a distinct field. This is territory-claiming through editorial labor, not just authorship.

**Migration from institutional to post-institutional**: SenseLab → 3Ecologies traces a deliberate move from university-affiliated collective practice to an autonomous, land-based, gift-economy community. This is a career strategy: build institutional credibility through university affiliation, then use that to build something more autonomous. The Canada Research Chair funds the platform from which she operates outside the university.

**Coalition-building through cross-disciplinary blurbs**: Her blurbers trace an expanding arc — from choreography (Forsythe) → Black studies (Moten, Harney, Crawley) → Indigenous studies (TallBear, Kulchyski). Each new blurber signals a new disciplinary coalition she's building. Not marketing; intellectual relationship-building that makes the books events.

### Key Moves (chronological)

1. **(2003–2004) From nationalism to SenseLab**: Her first book on Québec nationalism quickly recedes; the SenseLab founding with Massumi is the pivot. *Why it worked*: she identified a gap — no one had built institutional infrastructure for research-creation in Canada. She and Massumi filled it. *What it enabled*: 20+ years of SSHRC funding, a journal, a book series, a global community.

2. **(2007–2009) Two books in two years**: *Politics of Touch* and *Relationscapes* establish her as a movement theorist with rigorous philosophical foundation. *Why it worked*: dance/performance studies lacked a philosopher-practitioner willing to engage process philosophy at this level. She became the go-to theorist for choreographers. *What it enabled*: Forsythe blurb, standing in the dance world that academic theorists usually lack.

3. **(2013) Always More Than One + autistic perception**: Introduces "autistic perception" as a philosophical concept (not a clinical category). *Why it worked*: it gave disability studies a process-philosophy vocabulary and gave process philosophy a concrete empirical anchor. The move is intellectually generous: she's saying autism isn't exceptional — it's actually the clearest view of how all perception works. *What it enabled*: collaborations with autistic practitioners (Mukhopadhyay, Wolfond) as co-theorists.

4. **(2016) The Minor Gesture**: The book that travels. Neurodiversity × blackness × minor practice, routed through Fred Moten. *Why it worked*: timing (disability studies and Black studies were both having methodological crises around 2015–2016), and the unusual coalition she assembled. *What it enabled*: her work being assigned in Black studies courses, not just disability/performance studies.

5. **(2018) Immediations series migrates to punctum books / 3Ecologies**: A deliberate move outside the university press system for the collective intellectual work. *Why it worked*: punctum books' open-access model + 3Ecologies imprint identity gave the series a clearer extra-academic identity. *What it enabled*: the 3Ecologies community infrastructure.

6. **(2020) For a Pragmatics of the Useless**: Her most ambitious theoretical synthesis. "All black life is neurodiverse life." *Why it works*: it's a claim that generates necessary controversy and forces careful reading. The subtitle-quality formulation demands engagement. *What it enables*: positions her at the center of a conversation between process philosophy, disability studies, and Black radical theory.

7. **(2026) Unsettled (Minor Compositions)**: A personal/philosophical memoir that goes fully outside academic publishing. *Why it matters*: the choice of Minor Compositions (and the free digital download) signals she's willing to give this book to the world rather than monetize it. The settler-colonial turn completes the 3 ecologies arc: social + environmental + conceptual, now fully earthed in land and political responsibility.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Build infrastructure first**: The SenseLab model shows that creating the container (lab, journal, book series, community events) generates the content. Amber's most powerful move would be to create infrastructure for her interests, not just to produce solo work. What would Amber's equivalent of SenseLab look like?

2. **Use the minor strategically**: Taking non-standard cases and users seriously as revealing the structure of the standard is a research strategy, not just an ethical position. What "minor" computation practices, failure modes, or edge-case users might reveal the architecture of interaction that normate use hides?

3. **Collective authorship as intellectual practice**: Manning's series editing, workshop events, and collective publications build genuine intellectual community. The SenseLab model of events that are simultaneously choreographic and philosophical — where participants move and make together, not just talk — is directly applicable to how Amber might run workshops or research events.

4. **Non-linear theory migration**: Manning moved from nationalism → touch → movement → neurodiversity → blackness → ecology, following the internal logic of her concepts. Each pivot was driven by what the previous framework couldn't account for. Amber can learn to let conceptual honesty (following what the concept actually implies) override disciplinary loyalty.

5. **The free download as community move**: *Unsettled* is available as a free digital download. This, like Massumi's free fascism primer at Minor Compositions, is a deliberate community gesture — getting the work to the people who need it. There are times when giving work away is the more powerful move than publishing through institutional channels.
