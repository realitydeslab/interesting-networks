# Liam Young

Australian-born speculative architect, filmmaker, and director who operates in the spaces between design, fiction, and futures. Cofounder of the think tank [Tomorrow's Thoughts Today](http://www.tomorrowsthoughtstoday.com/) and the nomadic research studio [Unknown Fields Division](https://unknownfieldsdivision.com/). Described by the BBC as "the man designing our futures." Now runs the groundbreaking [MS in Fiction and Entertainment](https://www.sciarc.edu/academics/postgraduate/fiction-and-entertainment) at [SCI-Arc](https://www.sciarc.edu/) in Los Angeles. BAFTA-nominated producer whose work uses film, animation, VR, and installation to make speculative futures viscerally real.

## Background
- Born in Australia. Trained as an architect.
- Taught at the [Architectural Association (AA)](https://www.aaschool.ac.uk/) in London, and held guest professorships at [Princeton University](https://www.princeton.edu/), [MIT](https://mit.edu/), and [University of Cambridge](https://www.cam.ac.uk/).
- Co-founded **Tomorrow's Thoughts Today** (c. 2008), an urban futures think tank exploring implications of new technologies on cities.
- Co-founded **Unknown Fields Division** (c. 2011) with [[Kate Davies]], a nomadic research studio that conducts expeditions to remote and extreme locations worldwide — from rare earth mines in Mongolia to container ship routes across the Pacific — to document the hidden supply chains and emerging conditions of the present as raw material for speculative futures.
- Relocated to Los Angeles to run the **MS Fiction and Entertainment** programme at SCI-Arc, which he designed as a fundamentally new kind of architecture degree — one that produces films, games, and VR experiences rather than buildings.
- His career trajectory represents a deliberate evolution: from architecture → urban speculation → filmmaking → entertainment industry worldbuilding. He identified that architecture's most powerful medium is not the built environment but media — and restructured his entire practice and pedagogy around that insight.

## Key Works

### Films
- **[Planet City](https://planetcity.world/)** (2020) — Speculative film and book proposing that all 10 billion humans retreat into a single hyper-dense sustainable megacity, freeing the rest of Earth for rewilding and the return of stolen Indigenous lands. Costumes by [[Ane Crabtree]] (The Handmaid's Tale). Premiered at NGV Triennial 2020. [TED Talk](https://www.ted.com/talks/liam_young_planet_city_a_sci_fi_vision_of_an_astonishing_regenerative_future) at TEDMonterey 2021. One of the most provocative climate-architecture proposals of the decade.
- **[The Great Endeavor](https://dac.dk/en/exhibitions/age-of-nature)** (2023) — Film depicting a multigenerational collective project to extract greenhouse gases from the atmosphere at planetary scale. Featured at NGV *Planetary Redesign* exhibition and DAC "Age of Nature" exhibition (2025–2026).
- **[Where the City Can't See](https://www.dezeen.com/tag/liam-young/)** (2016) — World's first film shot entirely with laser scanners (LIDAR). Set in the autonomous machine zone of a driverless city, the film explores the spaces invisible to the camera systems of self-driving vehicles. Premiered at [SXSW](https://www.sxsw.com/).
- **[In the Robot Skies](https://www.dezeen.com/tag/liam-young/)** (2016) — World's first film shot entirely by autonomous drones. A love story set in the surveillance state. Commissioned by the [BFI](https://www.bfi.org.uk/) London Film Festival.
- **Renderlands** (2020) — Documentary exploring the invisible labour of CGI rendering farms, where workers in developing countries create photorealistic images of luxury spaces they'll never inhabit.

### Books
- [*Planet City*](https://www.amazon.com/Planet-City-Liam-Young/dp/0648685829) (Uro Publications, 2020) — The companion book to the film, featuring contributions from scientists, cultural theorists, and Indigenous leaders.
- [*Machine Landscapes: Architectures of the Post-Anthropocene*](https://www.amazon.com/Machine-Landscapes-Architectures-Post-Anthropocene-Architectural/dp/1119453011) (AD/Wiley, 2019) — Guest-edited issue of Architectural Design examining data centres, automated warehouses, server farms, and other environments designed for machines rather than humans.
- *Neo-Machine* — anthology exploring automated landscapes

### Installations & Objects
- **[Radioactive Ming Vases](https://www.dezeen.com/2015/04/28/radioactive-ming-vases-toxic-smartphone-waste-mud-unknown-fields-division-kevin-callaghan/)** (2015, with Unknown Fields & Kevin Callaghan) — Three Ming dynasty–style vases made from toxic radioactive mud collected from a rare earth mineral refining lake in Inner Mongolia. The objects trace the supply chain from smartphone to toxic waste. Collected by the [V&A](https://www.vam.ac.uk/).

## Venue Circuit Analysis
Liam Young moves across **four distinct circuits**:

1. **Architecture biennale circuit**: Venice Architecture Biennale (multiple editions), DAC Copenhagen, NGV Melbourne. These give him legitimacy within architecture discourse while he subverts it.
2. **Film festival circuit**: SXSW, Tribeca, BFI London Film Festival, Channel 4, BBC. This is unusual for an architect — he's crossed fully into film as a distribution medium.
3. **Major museum/institution circuit**: MoMA, V&A, Royal Academy, MET, NGV. These collect and exhibit his work as both art and architecture.
4. **Design media circuit**: Dezeen (head judge, keynote, regular features), The World Around, NeueHouse. He's embedded in the design media ecosystem.

**Strategic insight**: Young deliberately avoids the traditional gallery system and instead uses film festivals, architecture biennales, and museums as his distribution network. He treats each project as simultaneously a film, an exhibition, a book, and a provocation — maximizing surface area across all four circuits simultaneously. *Planet City* exemplifies this: it's a film (SXSW/Tribeca), a book (Uro Publications), a museum exhibition (NGV Triennial), a TED talk, and a Dezeen feature — all from one core concept.

## Why Interesting
Young is the most cinematic speculative designer working today. He makes the invisible visible — supply chains, machine landscapes, climate futures — through film and installation. His work sits at the exact intersection of architecture, film, climate, and technology that defines edge-of-chaos territory.

Three things make him essential:
1. **Medium is the message**: He doesn't just speculate about futures, he builds the *media infrastructure* to distribute those speculations (films, TED talks, a degree programme, a think tank).
2. **Provocation over solution**: *Planet City* is not a proposal — it's a provocation that forces you to think about density, land rights, rewilding, and collective action. This is the "two-word concept" strategy at its most effective.
3. **Architecture without buildings**: He's proven that an architect can have more influence through a film seen by millions than through a building used by hundreds. This challenges the entire discipline.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
- **Provocation-first**: Every project begins with an extreme premise (*all humans in one city*, *film shot entirely by drones*, *vases made from toxic waste*) that forces engagement. The premise IS the work.
- **Two-word concept coining**: "Planet City," "Machine Landscapes," "Unknown Fields" — each is a memorable, citeable label that becomes a discourse object.
- **Framework multiplication**: Architecture × Film = speculative worldbuilding. Supply chain research × ceramics = Radioactive Ming Vases. Climate science × costume design = Planet City wardrobes.

### Career Architecture
- **Field declaration**: Didn't just practice speculative architecture — declared "fiction and entertainment" as a legitimate architectural discipline and created a degree programme for it.
- **Platform building**: Three platforms built: Tomorrow's Thoughts Today (think tank, ~2008), Unknown Fields Division (research studio, ~2011), SCI-Arc MS Fiction & Entertainment (degree programme, ~2016). Each gave him a different kind of institutional power.
- **Strategic migration**: London (AA, Tomorrow's Thoughts Today) → Los Angeles (SCI-Arc, entertainment industry). Moved to where the film/entertainment industry actually is, recognizing that proximity to Hollywood was essential for his vision.

### Methodology Signature
- **Art-science bridging**: Uses LIDAR scanners, autonomous drones, and satellite data as filmmaking tools. The technology IS the aesthetic.
- **Expedition-as-research**: Unknown Fields Division travels to rare earth mines, container ship routes, and radioactive lakes. This physical, embodied research generates both raw material and moral authority for the speculative work.
- **Public engagement**: TED, BBC, Dezeen, Channel 4 — treats media platforms as distribution infrastructure for architectural ideas.

### Network Strategy
- **Industry embedding**: SCI-Arc Fiction and Entertainment brings in production designers from The Batman, Blade Runner 2049, Dune, Westworld, Rick and Morty — building a bridge between speculative architecture and Hollywood.
- **Dual legitimacy**: Maintains credibility in both architecture (Venice Biennale, MoMA, V&A) and entertainment (BAFTA nomination, SXSW, Tribeca).

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **~2008: Founded Tomorrow's Thoughts Today** — Framed urban speculation as a *think tank* rather than an architecture practice. This gave him permission to speculate without needing to build.
2. **~2011: Co-founded Unknown Fields Division** — Created a research methodology (expeditions to supply chain endpoints) that generates both content and credibility. The expeditions produce objects (Radioactive Ming Vases → V&A collection), films, and stories.
3. **2016: *Where the City Can't See* + *In the Robot Skies*** — Two "world's first" films in one year (first LIDAR film, first drone film). Established him as a filmmaker, not just an architect who makes videos.
4. **~2016: Moved to LA, launched SCI-Arc MS Fiction & Entertainment** — Institutional capture: created a degree programme that embeds his philosophy into the next generation. Drew in Hollywood industry partners (Framestore, Disney, Netflix). This is the move that turned a practice into a movement.
5. **2020: *Planet City*** — The masterwork provocation. Film + book + exhibition + TED talk + Dezeen coverage. Demonstrated the full multi-platform distribution strategy.
6. **2021: TED Talk at TEDMonterey** — Reached mainstream audience. "Planet City" becomes a reference point in climate/design discourse.
7. **2023: NGV *Planetary Redesign*** — First major solo show in his home country of Australia. Full institutional legitimacy.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **Build platforms, not just projects**: Young doesn't just make work — he creates *institutions* (think tanks, degree programmes, research studios) that give his ideas structural power. Amber could think about what institutional form her research practice could take beyond a lab.
2. **The provocation strategy**: Planet City works because it's impossible (you can't put 10 billion people in one city) but productive (it forces you to think about density, land rights, rewilding). The best speculative work doesn't propose solutions — it creates *productive impossibilities*.
3. **Medium migration**: Young recognized that architecture's most powerful medium is film, not buildings, and rebuilt his entire career around that insight. For Amber: what is the most powerful medium for *her* research ideas? Is it papers? Games? Installations? Films? The answer should determine the career architecture.
4. **Expedition-as-research**: Unknown Fields Division's methodology — physically traveling to the sites of your speculation — generates both authenticity and content. Amber could adopt this for her own research areas.
5. **Multi-platform distribution**: Every Young project exists simultaneously as film, book, exhibition, talk, and media feature. This isn't self-promotion — it's treating each format as a different *interface* to the same idea, reaching different audiences.
