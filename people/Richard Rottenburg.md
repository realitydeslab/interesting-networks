# Richard Rottenburg

Anthropologist of Science and Technology at [[WISER]], building continent-wide STS infrastructure in Africa. Foundational theorist of how facts, models, indicators, and computational knowledge systems travel across cultures, get translated, and govern life — especially on the African continent. Author of the landmark *Far-Fetched Facts* (MIT Press, 2009). Co-initiator of the [[STS-Africa Network]] (2011). Currently at the bleeding edge of social study of machine learning and AI in African governance contexts.

## Background

Richard Walter Rottenburg (born February 6, 1953) studied social anthropology, sociology, and Arab studies (1973–1978) at the [Free University of Berlin](https://www.fu-berlin.de/en/). For his PhD in anthropology he conducted **39 months of fieldwork** in [South Kordofan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Kordofan) (Sudan) — an extraordinary investment in ethnographic depth, studying the Moro Nuba (Lemwareng) people and their strategies for cultural syncretization and economic survival.

From **1984–1987** he lectured at the University of Transkei, South Africa — this early African engagement shaped his later trajectory. In the 1990s he worked as a **consultant for development cooperation**, conducting research in Africa and Europe; this ethnographic material became the basis for his habilitation (completed at [Viadrina European University](https://www.europa-uni.de/en/), Frankfurt/Oder, 1999) and eventually the core of *Far-Fetched Facts*.

In **2002** he became full professor and **founding director of the Institute for Social and Cultural Anthropology at the [University of Halle-Wittenberg (MLU)](https://www.uni-halle.de/)** — one of the leading German anthropology positions. There he established the [[LOST Research Group]] (Law, Organisation, Science and Technology) in 2006, which became a center for studying how juridical, organizational, technological, and scientific models translate in the Global South.

From **2006–2012** he was a **Max Planck Fellow**, heading the "Biomedicine in Africa" project at the [Max Planck Institute for Social Anthropology](https://www.eth.mpg.de/), Halle. In **2011** he co-initiated the **[[STS-Africa Network]]** — a platform advancing STS across the African continent. In **2014/15** he was appointed **Theodor Heuss Professor** at [The New School for Social Research](https://www.newschool.edu/nssr/), New York.

He now holds a professorship at **WISER, University of the Witwatersrand**, Johannesburg, where he is building continental-scale African STS infrastructure and, crucially, extending his work to **machine learning and AI governance in Africa**.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Translation as master concept:** Rottenburg's entire intellectual architecture rests on *translation* — how models, facts, procedures, and technologies travel from one context to another and necessarily get transformed in the process. This is not one paper; it's the organizing concept of his entire career. It appears in: the Moro Nuba syncretization work (Akkreszenz), *Far-Fetched Facts* (how development facts travel), the LOST group (Law/Organization/Science/Technology models translate across systems), *Travelling Models* (conflict management), *World of Indicators* (metrics as governing devices), biomedicine in Africa (therapeutic protocols as traveling models). The concept has infinite generative capacity.

**Framework stacking:** He consistently layers STS (Latour, Callon, Law) + anthropology of science + postcolonial theory + philosophy of social science. None alone is sufficient; the stack creates distinctive analytical capacity.

**Post-foundationalism as provocation:** His engagement with "post-foundationalism" — the philosophical position that there is no ultimate foundation for knowledge claims — gives him license to ask uncomfortable questions about how objectivity and rationality get *made* in specific contexts, without collapsing into relativism. This is a careful philosophical position that most anthropologists avoid.

### Career Architecture

**Field declaration:** Did not just do STS anthropology — declared it as a field in Germany (building the MLU Halle institute from scratch), then declared it as a continental field in Africa (co-initiating STS-Africa). This multiplied his influence beyond what any individual research project could achieve.

**Platform building:** The LOST group (2006) was not just an organizational convenience but a territorial claim — it brought together Law, Organisation, Science and Technology in a single institutional space and became a reference point for the field. Similarly, STS-Africa is infrastructure, not just networking.

**Strategic institutional migration:** Transkei (SA) → Berlin/Halle (Germany) → Viadrina (European crossroads) → Halle (full professorship) → Max Planck (prestige) → New School (US bridge) → WISER (African institutional commitment). Each move deepened his geographic and institutional range.

**Coalition edited volumes:** The multi-edited volumes (Brill, Cambridge, Berghahn) are territorial claims in adjacent fields — conflict management, genetics/identity, biomedicine, indicators, Sudan studies. Each volume opens a new area while extending his core framework.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **1977–1983 — 39-month South Kordofan fieldwork:** The empirical foundation. Gave him irreplaceable authority on Sudanese ethnography that sustained decades of writing. Most scholars don't invest at this depth. This is the root of all credibility.

2. **1984–1987 — Lecturing in Transkei, apartheid-era South Africa:** Early African institutional presence; gave him the lived sense of African institutions that later informed his critical development work. Few European anthropologists have this kind of early career African institutional embedding.

3. **1990s — Development consultancy:** Counterintuitive move — academics rarely do consultancy with such intellectual seriousness. He mined it ethnographically, turning the contradictions of development work into the material for *Far-Fetched Facts*. Lesson: engage with the systems you're critiquing from the inside.

4. **2002 — Full professorship + founding MLU Halle institute:** Institution-founding is a career multiplier. He didn't just occupy a chair; he built a department and established its intellectual direction.

5. **2006 — LOST group + Volkswagen grant:** Naming strategy ("LOST" for Law/Organization/Science/Technology) creates memorable institutional identity. The interdisciplinary grouping was ahead of STS's broader institutionalization in Germany.

6. **2006–2012 — Max Planck Fellowship, Biomedicine in Africa:** Entering biomedical territory extended STS credibility into one of Africa's most pressing empirical domains (HIV, malaria, clinical trials). Health = high-stakes empirical test case for his theory of traveling models.

7. **2011 — Co-initiating STS-Africa:** The single most influential structural move. Creating continental infrastructure for a field is permanently influential even after individual papers are forgotten.

8. **2015 — *World of Indicators* (Cambridge UP):** The pivot from development critique to the study of quantification and governance. Sally Engle Merry as co-editor brought feminist and legal anthropology perspectives, widening the audience and deepening the theoretical toolkit. This book anticipated the AI governance conversation by a decade.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **The "traveling models" frame is underused in AI research.** Amber could explicitly adopt and cite Rottenburg's framework to analyze how ML architectures, training pipelines, and deployment practices are "traveling models" that transform (and are transformed) in new contexts. This is not just a metaphor — it's a rigorous analytical framework.

2. **39-month fieldwork ≠ obsessive data collection; it's authority accumulation.** Rottenburg's depth gives him permanent credibility to speak about Sudan. Amber's years building HoloKit and understanding its deployment in different cultural contexts are an equivalent reserve. Don't minimize the value of that embodied knowledge.

3. **Consultancy as ethnography.** Amber's industry work (thatgamecompany, DJI, Twitter) can be mined the same way Rottenburg mined his development consultancy — as ethnographic field material that becomes foundational theoretical work.

4. **Build infrastructure, not just papers.** The LOST group, STS-Africa, the Halle institute — these are durable contributions. For Amber: labs, workshops, networks, working groups around machine behavior or agent ethology would have similar long-term leverage.

5. **Post-foundationalism as philosophical stance.** Rottenburg's post-foundationalist position allows him to ask "how is objectivity *made*?" rather than "is this objective?" — applicable to Amber's work on AI systems whose claims to neutrality, universality, and rationality need to be interrogated the same way.
