# Candice Hopkins

Carcross/Tagish First Nation independent curator, writer, and researcher. Executive Director and Chief Curator at [[Forge Project]], a Native-led nonprofit with one of the largest lending collections of contemporary Indigenous art in the United States. CCS Bard alumna. Co-curator of Documenta 14; Senior Curator, Toronto Biennial 2019 and 2021; co-curator Canadian Pavilion, 58th Venice Biennale (2019, featuring Isuma). Organized *Indian Theater: Native Performance, Art, and Self-Determination since 1969* (2023, Hessel Museum, CCS Bard). Married to [[Raven Chacon]] — Diné composer, 2022 Pulitzer Prize winner, 2023 MacArthur Fellow.

## Background
Born 1977 in Whitehorse, Yukon. Raised in Whitehorse and Fort St. John, British Columbia. Citizen of Carcross/Tagish First Nation, a people of the northern interior of the Yukon/B.C. borderlands.

Funded her BFA and MA by working as **a horse trainer on her family's ranch** in Baldonnel, BC, and as **a lab technician at a pulp and paper mill** in Taylor, BC — an unusual origin story that she carries consciously as part of her practice and identity.

After her BFA at the [Alberta University of the Arts](https://www.auarts.ca/) (1999) and an exchange year at [Margaret Street, University of Birmingham](https://www.bcu.ac.uk/arts/schools-and-departments/birmingham-school-of-art), she worked as a **contract archivist with Treaty 8 Tribal Association** in Fort St. John, and an **assistant cultural programmer at the [Native Friendship Centre](https://nafc.ca/)**. 

A pivotal shift: an 8-month internship for Indigenous youth with **Wainimate in Suva, Fiji**, working with local medicine practitioners on recovering Indigenous knowledge of traditional medicine, sponsored by the South Pacific Peoples Foundation. She helped organize Wainimate's representation at **Expo 2000 in Hannover, Germany** — her first experience on the international stage. This experience redirected her from artistic practice to curatorial practice.

She attended the masters programme at the [[Center for Curatorial Studies Bard]] (MA, 2003).

**Early career (2000–2005):** Aboriginal Curatorial Fellow at the [Walter Phillips Gallery](https://www.banffcentre.ca/walter-phillips-gallery), [[Banff Centre for Arts and Creativity]]. Organized early exhibitions including projects with [Cheryl L'Hirondelle](https://cheryllhirondelle.com/) and [Minerva Cuevas](http://www.minerva-cuevas.com/). Mentored by the influential Mohawk curator **Lee-Ann Martin** and museum director **Anthony Kiendl** — two key institutional figures in Canadian Indigenous art.

**Mid-career:** Positions at Western Front Society (Vancouver), National Gallery of Canada, SITE Santa Fe, IAIA Museum of Contemporary Native Arts. Built a portfolio of major biennials: documenta 14, Toronto Biennial, Venice Biennale.

**Forge Project (2021–present):** Transitioned from itinerant international curator to institution builder. Spent many years in New Mexico, Canada, and Europe before moving to the unceded Moh-He-Con-Nuck homelands in upstate New York to build Forge Project into what it is today.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Hopkins operates across **four circuits** that she has built deliberately over 25 years:

### 1. Global Biennial Circuit (institutional power)
**documenta 14 → Venice Biennale → Toronto Biennial → Sydney Biennale adjacent**
documenta 14 (2017) was her pinnacle of European institutional legitimacy; she curated the most globally Indigenous-inclusive documenta ever. Venice 2019 added another continental flag. Toronto Biennial gave her founding-curatorial status in a new institution. This circuit established her as a globally recognized figure — not just a "Canadian Indigenous curator" but a major international voice.

### 2. North American Indigenous Art Circuit
**National Gallery of Canada → SITE Santa Fe → Crystal Bridges → IAIA MoCNA**
These are the major institutional homes for Indigenous art in North America. She has worked with all of them, positioning Forge Project as a peer institution and lending collection partner.

### 3. Experimental/Critical Venues
**Western Front (Vancouver) → Plug In ICA (Winnipeg) → Agnes Etherington → Performance Space New York**
Smaller, artist-run, experimental. Western Front is where she did her first major experimental exhibition (*Before the Internet*, 2007). This circuit signals her critical/theoretical credibility beyond the biennial mainstream.

### 4. Forge Project as Destination Venue (new)
By building Forge Project as a site-specific institution on Indigenous land, she has created **a venue that doesn't exist anywhere else**: Indigenous governance + Ai Weiwei buildings + Indigenous lending collection + residential fellowship. Artists travel to her now. This inversion (building your own institution vs. fitting into others) is her most strategic move.

**Pattern:** She consistently chooses venues with strong collections, institutional permanence, and national/international audiences. She avoids commercial galleries. Her circuit is public institutions + independent/artist-run. No blue-chip gallery relationships — though she noted in 2024 that she's still waiting for a Native artist to be represented by a blue-chip US gallery.

## Why Interesting

Hopkins is interesting to Amber for several reasons that connect to research-taste.md:

**1. Sovereignty as design problem.** Her life's work is building sovereign Indigenous institutions — institutions that are governed, funded, and run by Indigenous people, on Indigenous land or in relationship to it. This is a structural/design challenge, not just a political one. The Forge Project is a *designed system* for sovereignty. This is deeply resonant with Amber's interest in agent sovereignty, decentralized governance, and participatory design.

**2. Non-Western technology epistemology.** The potlatch as economic protocol; ceremony as a form of computation; land as memory infrastructure — these are all genuine alternative epistemologies for how knowledge, exchange, and participation work. Hopkins's curatorial research is implicitly a body of alternative tech theory. Connects directly to Amber's interest in cosmotechnics and non-Western tech-philosophical traditions.

**3. *Before the Internet* (2007).** This early exhibition — organized when "network art" was barely a category — shows that Hopkins was thinking about networks, sovereignty, and Indigenous art together before it was fashionable. The title is provocative: what did Indigenous network art look like *before* the internet? What would it look like now?

**4. Institutional critique from inside.** She is not anti-institutional; she works *within* documenta, Venice, and the Toronto Biennial. But she uses those platforms to build something beyond them (Forge Project). This "complicit critique" strategy — using the master's tools to build your own house — is the most politically sophisticated institutional move in contemporary art.

**5. [[Raven Chacon]] connection.** Being married to the first Native American Pulitzer Prize winner in music (2022) and a MacArthur Fellow (2023) means she operates at the center of the most intellectually exciting Indigenous artistic practice in North America. The Chacon-Hopkins nexus is a major node in the network.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy

**Sovereignty as Master Concept** — Hopkins has developed "sovereignty" as a multi-scalar concept that works simultaneously at the level of land, body, performance, institution, and knowledge. Her "Golden Potlatch" essay (2011) applied it to economic exchange; her documenta 14 work applied it geopolitically; her Forge Project applies it institutionally. This is *concept multiplication*: one strong framework recursively applied across domains.

**Archive as Activation** — She consistently returns to suppressed histories (potlatch ban, 1969 Alcatraz occupation) to activate them in the present. *Indian Theater* is literally titled after a 1969 document from the IAIA. *Native Visual Sovereignty* recovers performance as an "origin point." This is not nostalgia — it is genealogical recuperation as a strategic curatorial move.

**Oral History as Primary Source** — Unlike most art theorists who rely on textual archives, Hopkins builds from oral history (interviews with Spiderwoman Theater, G. Peter Jemison, Rebecca Belmore). This is both epistemologically Indigenous (oral traditions as knowledge systems) and practically powerful (exclusive access to living knowledge keepers).

### Career Architecture

**Key Move 1: Horse Trainer → CCS Bard → Banff (1999–2005)**
Used an unusual origin story — not an art world insider — as intellectual capital. The horse ranch and pulp mill are not embarrassing background to hide; they are constitutive of her perspective. Banff gave her the Indigenous curatorial mentorship (Lee-Ann Martin) that no MFA program would. The CCS Bard MA gave her the theoretical language. Combined: a unique formation.

*Why it worked:* She arrived at institutions already knowing what she wanted to say. The fellowships trained her craft; they didn't need to train her politics.

**Key Move 2: Western Front + Fillip Writing (2007–2011)**
Built a theoretical voice through writing before having institutional power. The "Golden Potlatch" essay (2011) staked a distinctive intellectual claim that could not be dismissed as "identity politics" — it was rigorous economic theory applied to Indigenous exchange.

*Why it worked:* Writing travels further and faster than exhibitions. Her essays circulated internationally before her curatorial name was known outside Canada.

**Key Move 3: Sakahàn + National Gallery (2013)**
The first truly global survey of Indigenous contemporary art at a major national institution. This was an agenda-setting move: instead of curating a thematic show, she declared the entire scope of Indigenous contemporary art as a legitimate survey category.
