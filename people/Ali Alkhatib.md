# Ali Alkhatib

Critical computing researcher. Wrote some of the most politically sharp papers in HCI alongside [[Michael Bernstein]]. Known for applying social theory and political philosophy to algorithmic systems. Writes and speaks with unusual directness about the politics of computing.

## Background
- PhD candidate / postdoc in HCI, connected to Stanford
- Now at UCSF or independent
- Trained with Michael Bernstein at Stanford HCI Group

## Key Works
- **[Street-Level Algorithms: A Theory at the Gaps Between Policy and Decisions](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2019/streetlevelalgorithms/streetlevelalgorithms-chi2019.pdf)** (CHI 2019, **Best Paper**) — With [[Michael Bernstein]]. Applied Lipsky's "street-level bureaucracy" theory to AI/algorithms. One of the most theoretically sophisticated papers in recent HCI.
- **[Piecework](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2017/piecework/pn4226.pdf)** (CHI 2017, Best Paper Honorable Mention) — With Bernstein and Margaret Levi. Labor rights analysis of crowd work.
- **[Dynamo](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2015/dynamo/DynamoCHI2015.pdf)** (CHI 2015, Best Paper HM) — Platform enabling Mechanical Turk workers to collectively organize.

## Why Interesting
Street-Level Algorithms is one of the smartest papers to come out of HCI — it applies bureaucracy theory from public administration to understand where and why algorithmic systems fail. This is exactly the kind of "critical computing" thinking that appears in Amber's research taste. Worth tracking his independent writing and future work.
