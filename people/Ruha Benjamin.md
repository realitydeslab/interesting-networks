# Ruha Benjamin

Sociologist and public intellectual who coined the term **"New Jim Code"** — the idea that algorithmic systems embed and amplify racial discrimination under the guise of neutrality. Author of five books including the defining *Race After Technology* (2019) and *Imagination: A Manifesto* (2024). MacArthur Fellow (2024). Founding Director of the [Ida B. Wells Just Data Lab](https://www.thejustdatalab.com/). One of the two or three most cited voices in critical AI discourse globally.

## Background
Born 1978 in Wai, Maharashtra, India — to an African-American father and Persian-Indian mother. Grew up traversing multiple "Souths": South Central Los Angeles, Conway SC, Majuro in the South Pacific, Eswatini in Southern Africa. This transnational underbelly perspective shapes everything: she writes from the view of those who bear the costs of progress designed by others.

- **BA Sociology & Anthropology**, [Spelman College](https://www.spelman.edu/) — HBCU in Atlanta; politically formative
- **PhD Sociology**, [UC Berkeley](https://sociology.berkeley.edu/) (2008) — trained in Science and Technology Studies (STS)
- **Postdoctoral Fellowship**, UCLA Institute for Society and Genetics (2008–2010) — bioethics, genomics, race
- **Faculty Fellowship**, Harvard Kennedy School STS Program
- **Assistant Professor** of AAS & Sociology, Boston University (2010–2014)
- **Professor**, then **Alexander Stewart 1886 Professor** of African American Studies, Princeton University (2014–present) — named chair, one of Princeton's most prestigious
- **Founding Director**, [Ida B. Wells Just Data Lab](https://www.thejustdatalab.com/) (2018–) — named after pioneering Black journalist Ida B. Wells; brings together students, educators, activists, and artists to "rethink and retool data for justice"

Also serves on Executive Committees for Princeton's Program in Global Health and Health Policy, and Center for Digital Humanities.

## Key Works

### Books
- [*People's Science: Bodies and Rights on the Stem Cell Frontier*](https://www.sup.org/books/title/?id=20585) (Stanford University Press, 2013) — how innovation in genomics reinforces racial/gender categories; critiques the allure of "objectivity" in life sciences
- [*Race After Technology: Abolitionist Tools for the New Jim Code*](https://politybooks.com/bookdetail/?isbn=9781509526407) (Polity, 2019) — coined "New Jim Code"; argues racist algorithms are not bugs but features; uses Michelle Alexander's "New Jim Crow" as launchpad; winner of Oliver Cox Cromwell Prize
- [*Captivating Technology: Race, Carceral Technoscience, and Liberatory Imagination in Everyday Life*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/captivating-technology) (Duke University Press, 2019, editor) — edited volume; brings together essays on surveillance, carceral tech, and liberation imagination
- [*Viral Justice: How We Grow the World We Want*](https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691222882/viral-justice) (Princeton University Press, 2022) — argues small everyday acts of resistance spread virally and can transform systems; sibling-focused, personal, hopeful
- [*Imagination: A Manifesto*](https://wwnorton.com/books/9781324020974) (W.W. Norton, 2024) — most ambitious book; argues imagination itself is contested political terrain; calls for "abolitionist imagination" to envision what has not yet been built

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Benjamin moves in **four circuits simultaneously**, very different from art-tech figures:

1. **Prestige academic circuit**: Harvard Tanner Lectures, ISA Keynote, AAAS Plenary, STS Italia, LSE Festival, Edinburgh — she is fully embedded in the highest tier of academic recognition. The Tanner Lectures especially signal she's been canonized: previous Tanner lecturers include figures like Amartya Sen, Jürgen Habermas, Martha Nussbaum.

2. **Critical tech/policy circuit**: ICLR (ML conference), Mozilla Festival, Decidim Fest, AI Now, FAccT — she crosses from social science into tech spaces that usually only take engineers seriously. Her ICLR 2020 keynote was remarkable — a sociologist given a keynote at a core ML conference signals how much the ML community needed external critique.

3. **Black institutions / liberation circuit**: Spelman College honorary degree, Juneteenth events, Black Tech Policy Week, Black in AI, Haymarket Books, Cite Black Women podcast — she is deeply embedded in Black intellectual and activist communities. This is not tokenism; it's her primary institutional home.

4. **Literary/public intellectual circuit**: Brooklyn Public Library award, Charleston Literary Festival, The Internet Days, Boston Review, WIRED, Atlantic, Nature — she writes for general audiences and is profiled in flagship publications. *Imagination: A Manifesto* is her most mainstream/trade book yet.

**Key observation:** She does NOT heavily appear in the art-tech festival circuit (Ars Electronica, ISEA, transmediale, ZKM) — her public is primarily academic, policy, and activist audiences. This makes her different from many people in this knowledge graph who straddle art and technology. She IS cited by artists (Stephanie Dinkins), but operates in the scholarly infrastructure that those artists draw from.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Two-word concept coinage** — "New Jim Code" is the defining example. It works because:
- Piggybacks on an already-famous concept (Michelle Alexander's "New Jim Crow") — borrows its political charge
- Adds a new word ("Code") that does real analytical work (software code + moral code + legal code = triple meaning)
- Is simultaneously a description AND a political accusation
- Creates a clean, citeable frame for a sprawling phenomenon

Benjamin has done this repeatedly: "Captivating Technology" (edited volume title — "captivating" = mesmerizing AND imprisoning), "Viral Justice" (justice spreads like a virus — flips the pandemic metaphor), "Abolitionist imagination." She is a master of reframing through naming.

**Escalating abstraction** — each book operates at a higher level of abstraction:
- *People's Science* (2013): specific case study (stem cells)
- *Race After Technology* (2019): framework across many cases (algorithms)
- *Viral Justice* (2022): theory of change (how to respond)
- *Imagination: A Manifesto* (2024): meta-level (how we think about alternatives)

This is a deliberate trajectory: establish empirical credibility first, then zoom out to claim philosophical territory. Amber should note this sequencing.

**Provocation-first framing** — "The New Jim Code" is a provocation: it equates contemporary tech companies to the architects of Jim Crow. This is not an accident; it forces a response and makes neutrality impossible. Benjamin's work consistently chooses the charged frame over the cautious one.

### Career Architecture

**Field declaration** — Benjamin is not just doing work in Critical Race STS; she is *declaring Critical Race STS as a field* through papers like "Catching Our Breath: Critical Race STS and the Carceral Imagination." The field declaration move: (1) name the field, (2) write the seed paper, (3) edit the anthology (*Captivating Technology*), (4) build the lab. This is how you create a discipline.

**Coalition building via edited volume** — *Captivating Technology* (2019) gathers contributors and gives them all a frame to work within. This is a territorial move: everyone in the volume is now in "your" field. Same year as *Race After Technology* — double-punch that defined the field in a single year.

**Lab-as-infrastructure** — The Ida B. Wells Just Data Lab does what many scholars don't do: it creates institutional structure around a concept. The name evokes Ida B. Wells (investigative journalism, data-driven anti-lynching work) — this historicizes the project and gives it moral weight that "tech ethics" lacks. The Artist-in-Residence program is particularly smart: it creates ongoing outputs beyond the PI's own writing.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **Spelman → Berkeley (2000s)**: HBCU foundation → elite research PhD. Retains Black institutional rootedness while gaining academic prestige credentials. This combination is rare and politically powerful.

2. **Postdoc at UCLA Institute for Society and Genetics (2008–2010)**: The genomics/stem cell focus in early career gives her hard empirical credibility before making larger claims. She's not just a theorist.

3. **Harvard Kennedy School fellowship (early 2010s)**: Policy adjacent move. Allows her to speak credibly to policymakers, not just academics. This is why she ends up on "Real Facebook Oversight Board" — she has policy legitimacy.

4. **Princeton hire (2014)**: Institutional anchor — Princeton's African American Studies department has been the leading department in the US for decades (Eddie Glaude, Imani Perry, Cornel West formerly). This places her in elite company and opens institutional doors.

5. **"New Jim Code" coined in *Race After Technology* (2019)**: THE conceptual move. The book is the watershed. Published the same year as *Captivating Technology* — simultaneous single-author book + edited anthology as field-staking double-punch.

6. **ICLR 2020 keynote**: Crossover into ML community at peak of AI ethics discourse. Rare for a sociologist to keynote a core CS conference. This gave her reach beyond social science.

7. **Ida B. Wells Just Data Lab officially named / made public (2018–2020)**: Infrastructure move. By naming the lab after Ida B. Wells, she claims a specific political lineage (data journalism + anti-violence activism) that differentiates from generic "tech ethics."

8. **MacArthur Fellowship (2024)**: Consecration. After MacArthur, you are a "genius" in the American public imagination. Opens every door. *Imagination: A Manifesto* (2024) published same year — the manifesto form is appropriate for this stage.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Name your framework early.** Don't wait until you have everything figured out. "New Jim Code" wasn't fully theorized in one book — it was a concept that organized an entire research program. What's Amber's equivalent for "machine behavior has social consequences that are observable, patterned, and political"? The name matters as much as the idea.

2. **Use the edited anthology as field-making.** Benjamin's *Captivating Technology* gathered a community and defined a field simultaneously. Amber could do this for "agent ethology" or "machine behavior as science" — gather key people, write the framing intro, stake the territory.

3. **Cross the disciplinary boundary deliberately.** Benjamin keynoting ICLR 2020 was a calculated crossover. Amber's inverse version: bringing sociological/critical theory frames into ALIFE or CHI. The papers that make the biggest impact are often the ones that don't belong, but are invited in.

4. **Build lab as intellectual infrastructure.** The Just Data Lab isn't just a research lab — it has Artist-in-Residence, Tech Freedom School, student mentorship. This multiplies outputs and builds a community that outlasts any single paper. Amber's lab at Oxford could model this.

5. **The manifesto trajectory.** Benjamin's arc: empirical → framework → theory of change → manifesto. Each book escalates the claim. Amber is probably in the "empirical/speculative" phase. The framework book (equivalent to *Race After Technology*) is the one that matters for reputation. It should be the priority.
