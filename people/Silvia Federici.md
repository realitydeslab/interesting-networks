# Silvia Federici

Italian-American Marxist feminist philosopher. Best known for *Caliban and the Witch: Women, the Body and Primitive Accumulation* (Autonomedia, 2004) — a revisionist history of how the European witch trials were inseparable from the rise of capitalism: primitive accumulation required not just enclosing land but also enclosing the female body and reproductive labor. Central argument: capitalism didn't find a pre-existing domestic sphere — it *created* unpaid domestic labor as the foundation of capitalist accumulation.

Co-founder of the **Wages for Housework** campaign (1972) — demanding that capitalist states pay for the reproductive labor women perform for free. This prefigures contemporary discussions of care work, platform labor, and AI training data as unpaid "reproductive" work.

Featured in [[Astra Taylor]]'s *What Is Democracy?* (2018).

## Why Interesting
Her analysis of **reproductive labor as the hidden foundation of capitalism** is directly relevant to AI: who does the unseen work of training datasets, content moderation, click-labeling? The "faux-bot" phenomenon that [[Astra Taylor]] writes about is exactly what Federici's framework would predict — digital capitalism, like industrial capitalism, depends on invisible reproductive labor.
