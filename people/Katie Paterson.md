# Katie Paterson

Scottish artist working at geological and cosmological timescales. "Deep-time artist & ecological storyteller" (Long Now Foundation Council). Best known for [Future Library](https://www.futurelibrary.no/) (a Norwegian forest planted in 2014 that will be harvested in 2114 to print 100 books by writers including Margaret Atwood, David Mitchell, and Karl Ove Knausgård, who submit manuscripts unseen until then) and [All the Dead Stars](https://katiepaterson.org/portfolio/all-the-dead-stars/) (maps of all 27,000 dead stars visible from Earth). Works with extreme timescales — ice ages, stellar death, geological epochs.

**Source:** Discovered as Long Now Foundation Council member during Long Now Foundation deep research (2026-03-02).

## Why Interesting
Deep-time art at the scale Long Now cares about. Future Library is essentially a time capsule that activates civilizational-scale trust in future humans — very relevant to Amber's interests in more-than-human systems, machine mortality, and long-term responsibility. The project implicates the forest, the paper, future readers — a genuinely more-than-human collaborative artwork. Based in London (proximity bonus for Amber in Oxford/London).

## Key Works
- **[Future Library (Framtidsbiblioteket)](https://www.futurelibrary.no/)** (2014-2114) — 1,000 Norwegian spruce trees planted, books by 100 writers to be printed in 2114. Each year, one writer submits a manuscript (unseen until 2114). Margaret Atwood, David Mitchell, Elif Shafak, Han Kang, Karl Ove Knausgård among contributors. Truly 100-year artwork.
- **[All the Dead Stars](https://katiepaterson.org/portfolio/all-the-dead-stars/)** — maps of 27,000 dead stars. We see their ancient light without knowing they're gone.
- **[Earth-Moon-Earth](https://katiepaterson.org/portfolio/earth-moon-earth/)** — Beethoven's Moonlight Sonata bounced off the moon (gaps where moon absorbed signal).
- **[A Field in England](https://katiepaterson.org/portfolio/a-field-in-england/)** — meteorite from field in England
- **[Vatnajökull (the sound of)](https://katiepaterson.org/portfolio/vatnajokull-the-sound-of/)** — phone connected to Icelandic glacier; anyone could call the glacier.
