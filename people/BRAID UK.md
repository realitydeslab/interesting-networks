# BRAID UK (Bridging Responsible AI Divides)

**[BRAID](https://braiduk.org/)** is a £15.9M, six-year AHRC-funded programme that integrates Arts and Humanities research into the Responsible AI ecosystem, bridging the divide between academic, industry, policy, and regulatory work on AI. Led by the **University of Edinburgh** in partnership with the **Ada Lovelace Institute** and the **BBC**.

The core thesis: responsible AI discourse is dominated by a narrow pool of private-sector expertise. Arts and humanities researchers — working in data ethics, copyright law, digital design, qualitative analysis — have crucial contributions to make that are currently excluded from policy processes.

## Key Programme Elements
- **BRAID Fellowships** — competitive fellowships pairing university researchers with partner organizations from public, private, or third sector. First cohort: 17 fellows across UK universities. Partners include Adobe, Datamind Audio, Mozilla Foundation, Microsoft, Ada Lovelace Institute, Alan Turing Institute, BBC, Institute for the Future of Work.
- **Demonstrator projects, scoping projects, artist commissions, UK/US collaborations**

## Topics
- Responsible AI governance from humanities perspectives
- How cultural institutions can inform AI policy
- Arts-based prototyping for regulatory frameworks
- AI and the creative industries
- Collective data governance

## Why Interesting
BRAID represents the UK's most serious institutional effort to bring arts and humanities into AI governance. Mercedes Bunz's BRAID fellowship specifically targets the gap between gallery-based AI experimentation and public policy — arguing that arts commissions function as *prototypes* for regulatory thinking. The programme's existence signals that AHRC recognizes a structural failure: responsible AI discourse needs cultural sector expertise, not just technical ethics. For anyone working at the AI-art-policy nexus, BRAID's fellowship programme and publications are the primary UK venue.

## Funding
£15.9M over six years. Funded by Arts and Humanities Research Council (AHRC), part of UK Research & Innovation (UKRI).
