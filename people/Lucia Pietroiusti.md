Italian-born curator and ecological thinker based in London. Founded [[General Ecology]] at Serpentine Galleries in 2018 — the most rigorous ecology programme in the UK institutional art world. Ran it as Curator (2018–2023) then Head of Ecologies (2023–2025). Creator of *The Shape of a Circle in the Mind of a Fish* (six-edition interspecies symposia series, 2018–ongoing), co-curator of the Venice Golden Lion opera *Sun & Sea*, co-curator of the 13th Shanghai Biennale "Bodies of Water / 水体," and a driving force behind Back to Earth and the Infinite Ecologies Marathon. Now independent — her institutional phase appears complete and she is carrying the *Fish* series forward as a standalone programme. One of the most important ecological curators working today, distinctive for insisting that ecology must be structural/organisational reform, not just thematic programming.

## Background

Lucia Pietroiusti began her curatorial career at Serpentine Galleries before the ecology turn, working in public programmes. Her first major step into ecological territory was co-curating the **Extinction Marathon** (October 2014) with artist Gustav Metzger and Hans Ulrich Obrist — a two-day, 100+ participant event timed to coincide with Frieze Art Fair. That event established her as a curator willing to take radical stances: the Marathon literally asked what it means to programme art at the edge of extinction. Four years later, in 2018, she founded **General Ecology** — Serpentine's first dedicated cross-institutional ecology programme.

The General Ecology years (2018–2025) represent the most consistent and intellectually serious institutional ecology work in the UK art world during that period. Her model was explicitly organisational: not a silo ecology strand but a commitment to restructuring how the gallery thinks about infrastructure, procurement, travel, and networks — alongside producing the most rigorous research events on ecology and more-than-human thinking in the country.

She left Serpentine around 2024–2025. The *Fish* series, the most distinctive intellectual programme she built, continues independently.

## Key Works / Programmes

### The Shape of a Circle in the Mind of a Fish (2018–ongoing)
*The* defining project. A multi-year, interdisciplinary symposia series exploring intelligence, sentience, and existence across more-than-human worlds. **Named after** the puffer fish *Torquigener albomaculatus*, which creates elaborate geometric sand circles on the sea floor — behaviour rediscovered in 2011 and still contested between "mating ritual" and "artwork." Co-curated (Editions 1–5) with **[[Filipa Ramos]]**. Visual identity by **Giles Round**. Dedicated website: [themind.fish](https://themind.fish) (designed by Giles Round).

### General Ecology (2018–2023)
Founded at Serpentine Galleries in 2018. Not a standalone ecology strand but "a long-term, cross-organisational, multi-disciplinary and cross-media project dedicated to embedding ecological principles and commitments throughout the Galleries' programmes, networks and infrastructure." The framing was institutional transformation first, ecological programming second. She led it as Curator (2018–2023), then as Head of Ecologies when the programme was institutionalised as the Serpentine Ecologies department (2023). The programme evolved through the *Fish* series (research), *Back to Earth* (campaigns and advocacy), and the *Infinite Ecologies Marathon* (expanded interdisciplinary scope). See: [[General Ecology]].

### Back to Earth (2020–2022)
A multi-year, artist-led programme responding to the climate emergency. Over 60+ artists commissioned to devise actual campaigns, protocols, and initiatives as catalysts for change — not "climate art" for galleries, but real-world interventions. **Curators:** Rebecca Lewin, Hans Ulrich Obrist, Pietroiusti, Kostas Stasinopoulos, Jo Paton, Holly Shuttleworth, Sarah Hamed.

**Back to Earth Exhibition:** 22 June–18 September 2022, Serpentine North Gallery.

**Artists included:** Agnes Denes, Alexandra Daisy Ginsberg (*Pollinator Pathmaker* — living AI sculpture), Brian Eno, Carolina Caycedo, Cooking Sections (*CLIMAVORE*), Dineo Seshee Bopape, Formafantasma, Giles Round, Karrabing Film Collective, Sissel Tolaas, Tabita Rezaire/AMAKABA, Yussef Agbo-Ola/Olaniyi Studio, Superflux, and many more.

**Back to Earth podcast** (2020–2022): Co-hosted with **Victoria Sin**. Final episode "Can I Get Back To You?" (Nov 2020) with HUO, Rebecca Lewin, Kostas Stasinopoulos.

### Sun & Sea (Venice Biennale 2019)
Pietroiusti is listed as curator of the Lithuanian Pavilion opera-performance **Sun & Sea** by Rugilė Barzdžiukaitė, Vaiva Grainytė, and Lina Lapelytė, which won the **Golden Lion at the 58th Venice Biennale** (2019). This is one of the most critically acclaimed performances of recent years: an opera set on a beach inside a gallery, exploring climate collapse through sunbathers' sung thoughts. Pietroiusti's involvement connects her directly to the most prestigious performance distinction in contemporary art.

Co-curator of the 13th Shanghai Biennale at Power Station of Art ([[Power Station of Art]]), alongside Chief Curator Andrés Jaque ([[Andrés Jaque]]), Marina Otero Verzier, YOU Mi, and [[Filipa Ramos]]. ## Intellectual Lineage

General Ecology and the *Fish* series explicitly drew on:

1. **Donna Haraway** — *Staying with the Trouble*; "making kin not babies"; multispecies entanglement
2. **Anna Tsing** — *The Mushroom at the End of the World*; multi-species assemblages; patchy capitalism
3. **Félix Guattari** — *Three Ecologies* (environmental, social, mental) — Pietroiusti's "General Ecology" title directly echoes Guattari's opposition of "restricted" vs. "general" ecology
4. **Timothy Morton** — Dark ecology; ecology without nature; mesh
5. **Michael Marder** — Plant philosophy; "plant-thinking"; vegetal intelligence
6. **Karen Barad** — Agential realism; intra-action; material-discursive practices
7. **Rosi Braidotti** — Posthumanism; transversal subjectivity; zoe/geo/techno
8. **Isabelle Stengers** — Cosmopolitics; the question of intrusion; care for the question

The distinctive move: rather than importing this theory into art contexts, Pietroiusti *operationalised* it — turning posthuman philosophy into actual event design, institutional structures, and artist commissions.

## Research Taste Analysis

*(⭐⭐⭐ Section — methodology, career architecture, what Amber can learn)*

### Methodology: The Structural Insert

Pietroiusti's defining move is what might be called the **structural insert** — entering an institution not to run a programme *about* ecology but to make ecology the institution's *operating system*. This is intellectually demanding (it requires convincing institutional leadership that the stakes are existential, not thematic) and extremely rare. Most ecology programmes in the art world are thematic: "we made green art." Pietroiusti's approach was: "we restructured how the gallery procures suppliers, books flights, selects artists, and designs its events." The research programme (*Fish*) generated the ideas. The institutional programme (*General Ecology*) operationalised them.

### Career Architecture: The Three-Phase Model

Her career has a clear three-phase structure:
1. **Foundation phase** (pre-2018) — building institutional position at Serpentine through traditional curatorial work and the Extinction Marathon as a statement of intent.
2. **Programme phase** (2018–2024) — running General Ecology as Founder/Curator/Head of Ecologies; building the intellectual output (*Fish* series), the campaign output (*Back to Earth*), and the institutional output (Ecologies department).
3. **Independence phase** (2025–) — departing once the institution absorbed the lesson; carrying the *Fish* series forward as a standalone programme outside institutional walls.

The key insight: **she left when the work was institutionalised, not when she was exhausted.** That's a choice, not a necessity. By departing after the Serpentine Ecologies department was established, she ensured her work had structural legacy while reclaiming her intellectual independence.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Build the proof-of-concept inside an institution, then take it outside.** Pietroiusti needed Serpentine's resources and legitimacy to establish *General Ecology* and the *Fish* series as the reference model. Now she doesn't need Serpentine — the *Fish* series can run anywhere. Amber's parallel: use institutional affiliation to establish rigorous research credibility, then the work speaks for itself independently.

2. **The durational symposium as publishing strategy.** Each *Fish* edition accumulated a body of participants, recordings, and relationships. By Edition 5 you had Vint Cerf and Peter Gabriel and Anna Tsing in the same intellectual network. The series is itself a publication — an argument developed across years. Amber's machine behavior work could benefit enormously from this format.

3. **Name it precisely and strangely.** "The Shape of a Circle in the Mind of a Fish" is a perfect title: precise (it refers to a real organism), strange (you can't quite picture what a circle looks like from inside a fish's mind), and philosophical (it's about the limits of cross-species understanding). Amber should be that precise and strange in naming her own work.

4. **The curator as convener, not author.** Pietroiusti's intellectual contribution is in *who she puts in the room* and *what question she puts to them* — not in producing her own theory. She convenes. The convening *is* the intellectual work. This is under-recognised as a form of original contribution. Amber's interdisciplinary work requires the same move.

5. **Work the institutional-independent hybrid.** Pietroiusti worked within Serpentine but the *Fish* series had a life beyond it (dedicated website, international venues, independent publication). The institutional home provides resources; the independent brand provides freedom. Amber should build both simultaneously.

## Why Interesting

She solved the hardest problem in institutional ecology: **how do you make it structural rather than decorative?** The art world is full of ecological programming that amounts to "we made a show about trees." General Ecology was something different — it changed how the institution operated, not just what it exhibited. AND she produced the most intellectually rigorous interspecies symposia in the UK, and AND she curated the Venice Golden Lion, and AND she co-curated a major biennale in Shanghai. The density and coherence across all those outputs is rare.

The *Fish* series is the specific thing to study: six editions over seven years, each taking a different angle on the question "what does intelligence/consciousness look like from beyond the human?" From animal language (Ed. 1) to plant eroticism (Ed. 3) to dreaming across species (Ed. 5) to love and lament (Ed. 6) — it's a genuinely cumulative intellectual project, not an annual repeat. The series built a community of thinkers (Nicola Clayton, Vint Cerf, Ted Chiang, Michael Marder, Federico Campagna, Peter Gabriel) who wouldn't naturally be in the same room, and it held them together across years. That's extraordinary organising work dressed as curatorial work.

Now independent, she's in the most interesting phase of her career.
