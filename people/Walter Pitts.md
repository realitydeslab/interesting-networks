# Walter Pitts (1923–1969)

American autodidact mathematical prodigy. Ran away from home at 15 after memorizing Principia Mathematica in a library; found by Bertrand Russell, then taken under Norbert Wiener's wing at MIT. With Warren McCulloch, authored the most important paper in the intellectual prehistory of deep learning.

## Biography Notes

Born 1923 in Detroit. Autodidact — taught himself logic and mathematics, learned Greek and Latin as a child. At age 12, spent three days in a library reading *Principia Mathematica* and sent Bertrand Russell a letter pointing out errors. Russell invited him to Cambridge; he didn't go but resolved to become a logician.

At 15, he left home and traveled to the University of Chicago to attend Russell's lectures. Stayed there without registering as a student. Met Rudolf Carnap (Russell directed him there), then Nicolas Rashevsky's mathematical biophysics seminar. Met [[Jerome Lettvin]] in 1938. Was **homeless and penniless** throughout.

In 1941, [[Warren McCulloch]] moved to University of Illinois Chicago. In early 1942, he **invited Pitts to live with his family**. This act of hospitality produced the most important paper in the intellectual prehistory of deep learning. The University of Chicago awarded Pitts an Associate of Arts for the 1943 paper — his only earned credential.

In 1943, Lettvin introduced Pitts to Norbert Wiener at MIT. Pitts moved to Boston and joined the Research Laboratory of Electronics group (McCulloch, Lettvin, Pat Wall). He never formally enrolled until 1956–58 (electrical engineering PhD, never completed).

**The estrangement (1952)**: Wiener's wife Margaret became convinced McCulloch had corrupted a young woman they knew. Wiener suddenly cut off McCulloch and everyone associated with him — including Pitts. Pitts was devastated. He burned his doctoral dissertation (on probabilistic 3D neural networks, described as extraordinary by those who saw parts). Became increasingly isolated and alcoholic.

Died 1969 of esophageal varices — same year as McCulloch. 

## Why Interesting
The Pitts story is tragedy and science history intertwined. A runaway prodigy who co-authored one of the most consequential papers in 20th-century science, then had his career destroyed by social betrayal (not scientific failure). The McCulloch-Pitts paper launched neural network theory → Rosenblatt perceptrons → modern deep learning. Pitts never saw any of this. His later work — the burned dissertation — was probabilistic, anticipating the stochastic approaches that would eventually overtake the deterministic McCulloch-Pitts model. He was ahead of the curve twice: once with the 1943 paper, once with what he destroyed.
