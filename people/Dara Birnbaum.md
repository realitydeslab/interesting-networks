# Dara Birnbaum

Pioneering video and media artist whose work since the late 1970s has "talked back to the media" — reediting found television footage to expose and critique the power structures embedded in media form and content. One of the foundational figures of appropriation art and feminist video practice. Subject of the first US retrospective *[Reaction](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/695-dara-birnbaum-reaction)* curated by [[Lauren Cornell]] at CCS Bard/Hessel Museum (2022).

## Domains
- Video art (single channel and installation)
- Television appropriation and critique
- Feminist media art
- Conceptual and systems art
- Media and power analysis

## Background
Birnbaum's practice is rooted in her recognition, in the late 1970s, that television was the dominant ideological apparatus of her time — and that art could "talk back to the media" by hacking its own form and content. *Technology/Transformation: Wonder Woman* (1978–79) — reediting footage of the TV show — remains one of the most cited works in video art history. Her work anticipated the logic of social media critique by decades.

## Key Works
- ***Technology/Transformation: Wonder Woman*** (1978–79) — breakthrough video work; appropriated TV footage deconstructing gender and spectacle
- ***PM Magazine*** (1982) — three-channel video installation
- ***Rio Videowall*** (1989) — 25-monitor public video installation, Rio Shopping Center, Atlanta
- **[*Dara Birnbaum: Reaction*](https://ccs.bard.edu/museum/exhibitions/695-dara-birnbaum-reaction)** (CCS Bard / Hessel Museum, 2022) — first US retrospective, curated by Lauren Cornell; works 1975–2011

## Why Interesting
Birnbaum's work is the feminist-critical genealogy that much contemporary art about technology, media, and identity claims as its ancestor. Cornell's decision to organize her first US retrospective establishes a clear through-line: from Birnbaum's 1970s media critique to contemporary art dealing with social media, platforms, and representation.
