# McKenzie Wark

Australian-born media theorist, Situationist scholar, trans memoirist, and Marxist cultural critic. One of the most prolific and formally inventive voices in contemporary critical theory. Author of ~twenty books spanning: foundational media/class theory (*A Hacker Manifesto*); definitive Situationist International scholarship (*The Beach Beneath the Street*, *The Spectacle of Disintegration*); eco-Marxist recovery (*Molecular Red*); and an autotheoretical memoir arc (*Reverse Cowgirl*, *Philosophy for Spiders*, *Raving*, *Love and Money, Sex and Death*). Invented the **vectoralist class** concept — arguably the most useful framework for understanding tech-platform capitalism. Part of the **New York School of Media Theory** alongside [[Alexander Galloway]] and [[Eugene Thacker]]. Trans woman. Based at [The New School for Social Research](https://www.newschool.edu/nssr/) since the early 2000s.

## Background

McKenzie Wark was born in 1961 in [Newcastle, New South Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newcastle,_New_South_Wales), Australia — a working-class industrial city, not a cultural capital. Her mother died in 1967, when McKenzie was about six. Her father, **Ross Kenneth Wark**, was an architect, and raised her and her two older siblings. This provincial upbringing — formed by the labor movement, local bohemian scenes, and Australian cultural politics — shapes her intellectual habitus: she describes herself as "low theory," suspicious of academic patriarchy, committed to sidelong approaches and queer uncles rather than apostolic succession.

**Education:**
- **BA, [Macquarie University](https://www.mq.edu.au/)** (1985) — Sydney
- **MA, [University of Technology, Sydney](https://www.uts.edu.au/)** (1990) — UTS, Sydney
- **PhD in Communications, [Murdoch University](https://www.murdoch.edu.au/)** (1998) — Perth, Western Australia

In Australia, she was part of what critic Mark Gibson called the **"republican school"** of cultural studies — republican in the sense of *res publica*, the public thing, committed to intervening in the national-popular cultural space rather than just critiquing from the sidelines. She wrote a column for [*The Australian*](https://www.theaustralian.com.au/) newspaper and participated in culture wars debates. Early engagement appeared in the Australian post-Marxist quarterly [*Arena*](https://arena.org.au/) in the 1990s, where she debated real abstraction, meta-ideology, and post-structuralism.

She fell in love with New York artist **Christen Clifford** (met 1997 in Williamsburg), gave up tenure in Australia, emigrated to the US around 2000, and married Clifford that year. They have two children together. This was simultaneously a biographical upheaval and a theoretical reset: arriving in America with no public sphere access, nobody knowing her, she was free to abandon the popular-front cultural studies project and return to her earliest formation — Western Marxism and the avant-gardes — producing *A Hacker Manifesto* (2004).

She is a **trans woman**. In 2017, Wark began her gender transition; she started taking hormones in 2018. Anticipating that hormone therapy might affect her ability to write — or at least might change how she writes — she took leave from The New School and worked intensively, completing *Reverse Cowgirl*, *Philosophy for Spiders*, *Capital Is Dead*, and *Sensoria* in rapid succession. Between 2018 and 2022, she primarily wrote articles and commissioned essays, and became deeply involved with queer and trans rave scenes in Brooklyn — which generated *Raving* (2023). In 2022, she published a landmark essay in *Document Journal* mapping this trans bohemia.

She is currently **Professor of Media and Cultural Studies** at [The New School for Social Research](https://www.newschool.edu/nssr/) and [Eugene Lang College](https://www.newschool.edu/lang/), New York City.

## Key Works

### Early Australian Books (1994–2002)
- *[Virtual Geography: Living with Global Media Events](https://www.bloomsbury.com/us/virtual-geography-9780253208712/)* (Indiana University Press, 1994) — theory of the "weird global media event" (stock crash 1987, Tiananmen 1989, Berlin Wall 1989); introduces the concept of the **media vector** and "third nature" (telecommunications as something qualitatively different from first and second nature). Her earliest theoretical contribution.
- *[The Virtual Republic: Australia's Culture Wars](https://www.allenandunwin.com/browse/books/general-books/society/The-Virtual-Republic-McKenzie-Wark-9781864480184)* (Allen & Unwin, 1997) — culture wars as national identity crisis; Lyotard's *différend* applied to Australia
- *[Celebrities, Culture and Cyberspace: The Light on the Hill in a Postmodern World](https://www.plutobooks.com/9780745315669/celebrities-culture-and-cyberspace/)* (Pluto Press, 1999) — the transformation of social-democratic "popular" into media-driven pop culture
- *Speed Factory* — experimental work about distance and expatriation, co-written using the "speed factory" method: one author writes 300 words, emails it, next author has 24 hours to write the next 300 words; co-written with [John Kinsella](https://www.johnkinsella.org/), [Bernard Cohen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Cohen_(Australian_author)), and Terri-Ann White
- *[Dispositions](https://www.saltpublishing.com/)* (Salt Publishing, 2002) — GPS-tracked travel writing; Ned Rossiter called it "micro-empiricism" derived from Deleuze

### The Harvard Books (2004–2007)
- *[A Hacker Manifesto](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674015432)* (Harvard University Press, 2004) — **the major theoretical contribution**. 392 numbered theses about the class politics of information. Key concepts: the **vectoralist class** (those who own the means of distributing information — like feudalists owned land, capitalists owned capital) and the **hacker class** (those who produce new information, code, culture — but rarely own the vectors). Intellectual property creates an artificial scarcity in an inherently non-scarce field. Translated widely. Originally written in loose dialogue with Australian digital avant-gardes of the 1990s (nettime, etc.). Wark has called it her "first American book," even though it came from transnational digital community.
- *[Gamer Theory](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674025196)* (Harvard University Press, 2007) — first published by [Institute for the Future of the Book](http://www.futureofthebook.org/) as a networked book with specially designed interface before Harvard UP print edition. Gamespace as the utopian mirror of contemporary life: games actually deliver the level playing field and merit-based reward that capitalism promises but fails to provide. "Gamespace" as the total capture of experience by competitive logics.

### Situationist Scholarship (2008–2013)
- *[50 Years of Recuperation of the Situationist International](https://www.papress.com/html/product.details.dna?isbn=9781568987538)* (Princeton Architectural Press, 2008) — result of a lecture at [Columbia University](https://www.columbia.edu/); SI's influence on contemporary art, tactical media, anti-globalization. Pays close attention to figures beyond Debord: Constant Nieuwenhuys's utopian architecture, Pinot-Gallizio's painting, Jacqueline de Jong's *Situationist Times*, Michèle Bernstein's novels.
- *[The Beach Beneath the Street: The Everyday Life and Glorious Times of the Situationist International](https://www.versobooks.com/en-us/products/2197-the-beach-beneath-the-street)* (Verso, 2011) — the definitive rereading of the SI as radical *theory* not art movement. Recovers figures systematically sidelined by the Debord cult (Bernstein, de Jong, Pinot-Gallizio, Constant). Uses the SI as a labyrinth rather than apostolic succession.
- *[The Spectacle of Disintegration: Situationist Passages out of the Twentieth Century](https://www.versobooks.com/en-us/products/2295-the-spectacle-of-disintegration)* (Verso, 2013) — continues the SI *dérive* through the archive; focuses on the post-1968 moment: what do you do when revolution fails? More mournful and fragmented than *Beach*.
- *[Excommunication: Three Inquiries in Media and Mediation](http://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/E/bo14413838.html)* (University of Chicago Press, 2013) — co-authored with [[Alexander Galloway]] and [[Eugene Thacker]]. The foundational text of the **New York School of Media Theory**. Three linked essays: Galloway on three modes of mediation (Hermes, Iris, Furies — exchange, illumination, network); Thacker on "dark media" (horror and mysticism as communication with the inaccessible); Wark on **"Furious Media"** — the infuriated swarm as queer politics of heresy, distinct from both media theory and traditional left, celebrating deviation from academic and political patriarchies.

### Marxist Theory Cycle (2015–2020)
- *[Molecular Red: Theory for the Anthropocene](https://www.versobooks.com/en-us/products/61-molecular-red)* (Verso, 2015) — eco-Marxist theory through two unlikely pairs: First half: Soviet Marxist [Alexander Bogdanov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Bogdanov) (philosopher of organizing labor knowledge, founder of Proletkult) and Soviet writer [Andrei Platonov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrei_Platonov) (his novels as Marxist theory in practice). Second half: [[Donna Haraway]] (read as a Marxist thinker) and [Karen Barad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karen_Barad) and [Kim Stanley Robinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kim_Stanley_Robinson) (sci-fi as speculative Marxism). Crucial for recovering Bogdanov's relevance to today's collaborative-labor problems and Anthropocene thinking. Wark treats Haraway as part of a "Haraway cluster" of thinkers who share her approach.
- *[General Intellects: Twenty-One Thinkers for the Twenty-First Century](https://www.versobooks.com/en-us/products/442-general-intellects)* (Verso, 2017) — survey of 21 contemporary theorists including: Judith Butler, Wendy Brown, Bruno Latour, Donna Haraway, Paul B. Preciado, Slavoj Žižek, Fredric Jameson, and others. Wark's pedagogical "map" of what theory is doing now. Written as a guide for others, not a system.
- *[Capital Is Dead: Is This Something Worse?](https://www.versobooks.com/en-us/products/887-capital-is-dead)* (Verso, 2019) — builds on *A Hacker Manifesto*. Argues that the vectoralist class has become the *dominant* ruling class, displacing capitalists — i.e., we may no longer live in capitalism properly speaking, but in something else (vectoralism). Provocatively asks: is this *worse* than capitalism? Opens onto a different periodization of political economy.
- *[Sensoria: Thinkers for the Twenty-First Century](https://www.versobooks.com/en-us/products/880-sensoria)* (Verso, 2020) — companion/sequel to *General Intellects*; more thinkers on problems of the present. Includes figures working on sensation, perception, and affect.

### Autotheory / Trans Memoir Cycle (2015–2026)
- *[I'm Very into You: Correspondence 1995–1996](https://semiotexte.com/?p=829)* (Semiotext(e), 2015) — email correspondence between Wark and novelist [Kathy Acker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kathy_Acker) during their 1995 affair, with Wark's afterword. Originally titled as a 2007 publication but the Semiotext(e) book appeared 2015. Wark calls it "accidental autofiction" in the German edition's afterword.
- *[Reverse Cowgirl](https://semiotexte.com/?p=1147)* (Semiotext(e), 2020) — autofictional account of Wark's experiences of gender and sexuality on the cusp of her transition. Addresses her partner in second person throughout. Described as "on the threshold" — written at the moment of deciding to transition.
- *[Philosophy for Spiders: On the Low Theory of Kathy Acker](https://www.dukeupress.edu/philosophy-for-spiders)* (Duke University Press, 2021) — grew out of material originally intended for *Reverse Cowgirl*. A hybrid of theory, memoir, and literary criticism: uses Wark's personal relationship with Acker to do feminist/trans theory through Acker's texts. "Low theory" via a "penetrable body." Trans reading of Acker's refusal of binary gender. Praised by Sianne Ngai, Sarah Schulman, and many others.
- *[Raving](https://www.dukeupress.edu/raving)* (Duke University Press, 2023) — in Duke UP's "Practices" series (activities that make us human). First-person autotheory of queer trans rave scenes in Brooklyn and Berlin. Coins new vocabulary: "speaker demons," "rave condoms," "punishers," "sidechain time." Argues for raving as a trans aesthetic and ethical practice — **ressociation** (versus dissociation) as a way of being with others outside capitalism. Praised by Kodwo Eshun, Paul D. Miller (DJ Spooky), Simon Reynolds (*Energy Flash*), madison moore. Reviewed in *The New Yorker* (Emily Witt), *Vulture*, *Frieze*, *Vice*.
- *[Love and Money, Sex and Death: A Memoir](https://www.versobooks.com/en-us/products/3113-love-and-money-sex-and-death)* (Verso, 2023) — epistolary memoir addressed in second person to Wark's deceased mother, her sister, and various lovers. Works through loss, desire, trans experience, and class politics through intimate direct address. Part of the second-person project Wark views as enabling for trans literature (bypassing omniscient narrator).
- *[Life Story](https://hanumaneditions.com/books/life-story-mckenzie-wark)* (Hanuman Editions, ~2025/2026) — very recent short work.
- *[Leaving the Twentieth Century: Situationist Revolutions](https://www.versobooks.com/en-us/products/3097-leaving-the-twentieth-century)* (Verso, 2024) — returns to Situationist material.

## Why Interesting

The vectoralist class concept is the under-rated framework for AI political economy. Everyone reaches for "monopoly capitalism" or "surveillance capitalism" to describe what Google/Meta/Anthropic are doing, but Wark's formulation — that controlling the *vector* (the means of distribution of information) is a structurally *different* form of class power than controlling capital — is sharper. It explains why FOSS hackers and knowledge workers are in structural contradiction with platforms even when they share the same political values.

The arc from *A Hacker Manifesto* (2004) to *Capital Is Dead* (2019) is a 15-year meditation on whether information capitalism is capitalism-plus or something qualitatively new. This is now the central question in political economy of AI.

The Situationist scholarship matters because she didn't just import Guy Debord — she systematically recovered the women, the architects, the odd figures that Debord's masculinist narrative suppressed. This is a model for how to do recovery work without hagiography.

The autotheoretical turn after 2017 (transitioning, writing as/about trans experience) makes her an important figure in the growing field of autotheory — theory that uses the body and personal experience as its primary site. Her use of second-person address is a formal contribution to the genre.

The fact that she left Australia and gave up tenure makes her an interesting anti-institution figure in a way that's instructive: not by refusing the academy entirely, but by choosing a different relationship to it (writing for "queer uncles" not "academic patriarchs").

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Framework multiplication**: Wark specializes in taking two apparently unrelated archives and running them together to produce new third concepts. *Molecular Red*: (Bogdanov + Platonov) × (Haraway + Barad + Robinson) = collaborative-labor Anthropocene theory. *Raving*: (queer rave scene) × (media theory) = trans ethics of ressociation. *Excommunication*: three separate essays that produce "excommunication" as a new media concept through collision.

**Recuperation inversion**: Her major move is to "recuperate the recuperation" — to find what's been suppressed in canonical narratives (the SI beyond Debord, the Marxists beyond Marx, the trans beyond queer theory) and bring it forward, not as the *real* history but as an *alternative* archive. This is explicitly anti-canonical: she wants a "labyrinth, not an apostolic succession."

**Low theory as method**: Not building grand systems but occupying "writerly rather than scholarly" positions, creating "spaces" for others to inhabit. This is a deliberate choice against the system-builder model and toward the enabling-text model. Works like a map for others.

### Career Architecture

**Australian to American re-positioning**: When she arrived in the US with "no access to the public sphere," she understood she had to abandon the popular-front mode and go deeper — down into the theoretical formation (Western Marxism, avant-gardes) that had always underpinned her. The migration was intellectually enabling precisely because it removed the old platform.

**Leave of absence as productive rupture**: The pre-transition writing burst (4 books in ~2 years before hormones) shows how biographical necessity can become creative productivity. She anticipated change and worked the anxiety productively.

**Publishing circuit breadth as strategy**: Spanning Harvard UP, Chicago UP, Duke UP, Verso, Semiotext(e), Princeton Architectural Press, Hanuman Editions is unusual. It signals she never wanted to be owned by any single circuit (academic/mainstream, radical/alternative, experimental/micro). Each publisher reaches a different audience and credentializes differently.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **Mid-1990s**: Labor movement formation + Australian culture wars + *Arena* engagement → "republican school" of cultural studies. Gave her a commitment to public space that all the theory in the world can't reproduce.
2. **1994**: *Virtual Geography* introduces the **vector** as a concept — will be the seed of everything that follows. The media vector as a different kind of infrastructure from capital.
3. **2000**: Gives up tenure, emigrates. Freedom from the national-popular project; arrives in the context of 9/11, which accelerated the next move.
4. **2004**: *A Hacker Manifesto* — the vectoralist class. This is the foundational conceptual claim. The Harvard UP imprimatur, the Hegelian-dialectical prose style (numbered theses), the networked publication context all combine to make it widely read across hacker culture, media studies, and radical theory.
5. **2007–2013**: The SI trilogy (*50 Years*, *Beach*, *Spectacle*) consolidates her as the definitive Anglophone SI scholar. Importantly, she does this *against* the Debord cult — recovering the women, the eccentrics, the suppressed figures. This is canonical-while-anti-canonical.
6. **2013**: *Excommunication* creates a collective identity — "New York School of Media Theory" — around Galloway/Thacker/Wark. The text itself performs the network.
7. **2017**: Begins gender transition. Takes leave. Writes *Reverse Cowgirl*, *Capital Is Dead*, *Philosophy for Spiders*, *Sensoria* in intensive burst before hormones begin.
8. **2018–2022**: Embeds in Brooklyn trans scene. This is not a detour — it's the theory living in the world, generating the next book (*Raving*).
9. **2022–present**: The autotheoretical arc (*Raving*, *Love and Money*, *Life Story*) — theory and life inseparable. The second-person address as formal innovation.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **The vector concept** is worth internalizing as a general analytical tool: in any system, ask who controls the vector (the means of distribution) vs. who produces the content. This applies to AI pipelines, academic publishing, social media — everywhere.

2. **Recovery as strategy**: Rather than adding marginalia to canonical figures, identify the suppressed alternatives. Bogdanov over Lenin; the SI women over Debord. The recovery of a *path not taken* is always more generative than exegesis of the canon.

3. **"Leave big spaces"**: Instead of totalizing systems, write enabling texts — maps that invite others to inhabit them. This is a research strategy for someone who isn't trying to found a school.

4. **Second-person address**: The formal move of addressing your writing to a specific person (real or imagined) bypasses both academic omniscience and confessional navel-gazing. Worth experimenting with as a writing strategy.

5. **Use biographical disruption**: Wark turned each major life change (emigration, transition, rave embeddedness) into theoretical productivity rather than treating it as interruption. The body as a site where theory is generated, not merely illustrated.
