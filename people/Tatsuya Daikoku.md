# Tatsuya Daikoku 大黒岳彦

Associate Professor at the Next Generation Artificial Intelligence Research Center (AI Center), University of Tokyo. Adjunct faculty in the ISI Laboratory (Kuniyoshi Lab, Dept. Mechano-Informatics). Also affiliated with the **Centre for Neuroscience in Education, University of Cambridge** and the **Center for Brain, Mind and KANSEI Sciences Research, Hiroshima University**. 43 peer-reviewed papers as of 2026.

His core project: **how the brain learns patterns in music and language, and how that learning produces bodily sensation, emotion, and creativity**. He developed the **Hierarchical Bayesian Statistical Learning (HBSL)** model to mimic the brain's statistical learning process — and uses it to analyze jazz improvisation, children's songs across cultures, and creative individuality. He also pioneered a visual **body-mapping** method for studying how music literally moves the body: which chord progressions activate the chest, abdomen, or limbs, and what that says about interoception, mental health, and aesthetic experience.

The ALIFE 2025 Art Panel role — alongside [[Takashi Ikegami]] and [[Nao Tokui]] — is significant: Daikoku was positioned as the **"human sciences perspective on AI/ALife and sound/music,"** bridging empirical neuroscience with the artistic and computational concerns of the ALife community.

## Research Focus

**Statistical Learning in Music and Language:**  
The brain extracts transitional probabilities from sequences of sounds — in music, speech, and even poetry. Daikoku's lab studies this implicit learning using EEG (mismatch negativity / MMN paradigms), computational modeling (HBSL), and behavioral methods. Key finding: uncertainty modulates the *order* of statistical learning strategies — under higher uncertainty, the brain shifts to higher-order sequence chunking.

**Predictive Processing + Creativity:**  
Using the HBSL model, he analyzed 456 jazz improvisations (1905–2009, 78 musicians) and found era-specific temporal patterns of *surprise* (prediction error) and *uncertainty*, while rhythm sequences stayed consistent across eras. This maps onto the idea that **creativity emerges from adaptive learning at the edge of predictability** — a direct ALife/open-endedness theme.

**Body Mapping of Musical Emotion:**  
His most distinctive methodological contribution: participants physically mark where on a body silhouette they feel sensations while listening to music. He applies this to chord progressions (varying uncertainty/prediction error), sound pitch, and musical improvisation. Key findings: certain harmonic progressions activate the chest/cardiac area (linked to interoception and aesthetic appreciation); individuals with alexithymia or depression show *diffuse*, less localized body sensations in response to sound; higher musical uncertainty elicits more upper-torso sensations. This links **musical prediction → interoception → emotional well-being**.

**Cross-Domain Rhythm Structure:**  
With Usha Goswami (Cambridge), he applies the **S-AMPH (Spectral-Amplitude Modulation Phase Hierarchy)** computational model to compare rhythm structures across music genres, infant-directed speech (babytalk), and poetry. Finding: French and German poetry — considered linguistically distinct — actually share nearly identical acoustic/physical rhythm structures at the amplitude envelope level. Music, infant speech, and human vocal genres share nested AM modulation structures that nature sounds (birdsong, rain) lack.

**Neurodevelopmental Applications:**  
Neural statistical learning is reduced in dyslexia (Cereb Cortex 2025, with Sammler at Max Planck). Autistic individuals' speech rhythm differs in prosodic processing from neurotypical speech; non-autistic speakers *spontaneously imitate* the rhythm of autistic interlocutors (PLoS One 2023, with Kumagaya). This extends into divergent cognitive individuality and creativity trade-offs.

**For Amber specifically:**

1. **Bodily maps + somaesthetic design**: His body-mapping methodology — where people mark physical sensations triggered by music/prediction — is a rigorous empirical instantiation of somaesthetic inquiry. The finding that certain musical prediction patterns trigger chest/cardiac sensations linked to aesthetic appreciation has direct resonance with soma-based design.

2. **Statistical learning → creativity → ALife**: His HBSL model and the finding that creativity emerges from a trade-off between learning efficiency and novelty generation (hypo-sensitive = more creative, hyper-sensitive = fixated) is a precise computational analogue of open-endedness questions in ALife.

3. **Jazz improvisation as machine behavior**: His analysis of 456 jazz improvisations shows era-specific adaptation in uncertainty/surprise dynamics while rhythm stays constant — this is essentially **agent ethology** applied to musical creativity. The temporal dynamics of how artists adapt to cultural-emotional contexts mirrors how agents adapt to environments.

4. **Musical prediction + embodiment + mental health**: His autism speech research and body-mapping work on depression/alexithymia positions him at the intersection of neurodivergence, embodied communication, and more-than-human interaction design.

5. **Direct network bridge**: He sits between the [[Takashi Ikegami]] ALife/consciousness cluster and the Cambridge/Max Planck music neuroscience cluster — a genuinely unusual position. He can translate between experimental neuroscience rigor and ALife theoretical concerns.
