# Kathy Acker

American experimental novelist, poet, playwright, and performance artist. (1947–1997). Pioneer of transgressive postmodern fiction: combines cut-up technique, pastiche, bodily extremity, and feminist theory into something that refuses every category. Key figure in punk literary culture (blank generation), New Narrative movement, and — posthumously — trans literature. [[McKenzie Wark]] had an affair with her in 1995, published their email correspondence as *I'm Very into You* (Semiotext(e), 2015), and wrote *Philosophy for Spiders* (Duke UP, 2021) recovering Acker as a trans and feminist theorist.

## Background
Born Karen Lehman, April 18, 1947, New York City. Contested early life (in disputed circumstances); adopted the name Kathy Acker. Moved through San Diego, New York bohemia, San Francisco. Key figure in the downtown New York art scene. Died November 30, 1997 in Tijuana, Mexico, of breast cancer. She refused chemotherapy and sought alternative treatment.

## Key Works
- *Blood and Guts in High School* (1984) — canonical transgressive novel; schoolgirl protagonist, cut-up, pornography, politics
- *Great Expectations* (1982) — Dickens pastiche + cut-up + sexual politics
- *Don Quixote: Which Was a Dream* (1986) — gender inversion; language as resistance
- *Empire of the Senseless* (1988) — biker culture + Burroughs + feminist theory
- *In Memoriam to Identity* (1990)
- *My Mother: Demonology* (1993)
- *Pussy, King of the Pirates* (1996)
- Various essays and performances

## Why Interesting
Posthumously claimed as a major figure in trans literature — [[McKenzie Wark]] argues that Acker's writing explores gender in ways related to, but ultimately different from, cis feminist norms. The body as a site of inscription, violation, and transformation in Acker anticipates the autotheory turn. Her experimental prose — the cut-up, the borrowed text, the pornographic — is a practice of living outside literary convention. "Low theory" in Wark's sense: theory that works through the body and the text, not through academic argumentation.
