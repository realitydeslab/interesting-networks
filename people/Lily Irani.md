# Lilly Irani

Associate Professor of Communication and Science Studies at [[UC San Diego]]. One of the sharpest critical scholars of technology, innovation, and labor. Author of [*Chasing Innovation: Making Entrepreneurial Citizens in Modern India*](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691175140/chasing-innovation) (Princeton UP, 2019). Co-creator of [Turkopticon](https://turkopticon.net/) — a worker-designed tool that flips Amazon Mechanical Turk's surveillance apparatus, letting workers rate employers. Her work sits at the exact intersection of building tools and critiquing the systems those tools operate within, making her one of the rare scholars who is both a practitioner and a theorist of technology politics.

## Background

Lilly Irani's trajectory is unusual and revealing: she started inside Silicon Valley and then turned the critical lens back on it.

- **Stanford University** — BS and MS in Computer Science. During her time at Stanford (early 2000s), she published on women in computer science and gender/confidence in CS culture, publishing in ACM SIGCSE. This early feminist awareness shaped everything that followed.
- **Industry experience** — Worked as a user experience designer in Silicon Valley, including at companies like Google. This insider perspective gave her ethnographic material and credibility when she later wrote about design thinking as ideology.
- **[UC Irvine](https://www.uci.edu/)** — PhD in Information and Computer Sciences, where she studied under **[[Paul Dourish]]** in the Informatics department. At UCI, she co-created Turkopticon (2008) with **M. Six Silberman** and began the crowdwork research that would define her early career. Also co-authored the foundational "Postcolonial Computing" papers with Dourish.
- **[UC San Diego](https://ucsd.edu/)** — Joined the Department of Communication in 2013 as faculty, with a joint appointment in Science Studies. Now Associate Professor. UCSD's Science Studies program is historically significant — it was built by scholars like **Bruno Latour** (visiting), **Chandra Mukerji**, and others in the STS tradition. Irani's position bridges communication, STS, and computing in a way few departments allow.

## Key Works

### Book
- [*Chasing Innovation: Making Entrepreneurial Citizens in Modern India*](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691175140/chasing-innovation) (Princeton UP, 2019) — Ethnography of a Delhi design studio showing how "innovation" discourse produces new forms of citizenship and excludes craftspeople, workers, and activists. Winner of the ICA Outstanding Book Award and the Diana Forsythe Prize. 92 citations.

### Major Papers (by citation impact)
- ["Who are the crowdworkers?"](https://doi.org/10.1145/1753846.1753873) (with Joel Ross, M. Six Silberman, Andrew Zaldivar, Bill Tomlinson; CHI EA 2010) — 1,009 citations. Foundational demographic study of Amazon Mechanical Turk workers. Showed the shift from US-based to increasingly international (especially Indian) workforce.
- ["Turkopticon: Interrupting Worker Invisibility in Amazon Mechanical Turk"](https://doi.org/10.1145/2470654.2470742) (with M. Six Silberman; CHI 2013) — 758 citations. The paper behind the [Turkopticon](https://turkopticon.net/) tool. A worker-built browser extension that lets Turkers review and rate requesters. Paradigmatic example of "design from below."
- ["Postcolonial Computing: A Lens on Design and Development"](https://doi.org/10.1145/1753326.1753522) (with Paul Dourish; CHI 2010) — 694 citations. Introduced postcolonial theory to HCI. Argued that design and development projects for the "developing world" reproduce colonial power structures.
- ["The Cultural Work of Microwork"](https://doi.org/10.1177/1461444813511926) (New Media & Society, 2013) — 533 citations. Analyzed how Amazon Mechanical Turk's framing as "artificial artificial intelligence" obscures the labor and cultural politics of microwork.
- ["Difference and Dependence among Digital Workers: The Case of Amazon Mechanical Turk"](https://doi.org/10.1215/00382876-2831665) (South Atlantic Quarterly, 2015) — 348 citations. Extended the analysis of MTurk to global labor dependencies.
- ["Hackathons and the Making of Entrepreneurial Citizenship"](https://doi.org/10.1177/0162243915578486) (Science, Technology, & Human Values, 2015) — 308 citations. Showed how hackathons function as sites where entrepreneurial subjectivity is produced — participants don't just make apps, they learn to be "entrepreneurial citizens."
- ["We Are Dynamo: Overcoming Stalling and Friction in Collective Action for Crowd Workers"](https://doi.org/10.1145/2702123.2702508) (with Niloufar Salehi, M. Six Silberman, Michael S. Bernstein; CHI 2015) — 265 citations. Designed a platform for collective action among MTurk workers — turned research INTO organizing infrastructure.
- ["Postcolonial Computing: A Tactical Survey"](https://doi.org/10.1177/0162243910389594) (with Paul Dourish; Science, Technology, & Human Values, 2010) — 257 citations. The companion journal article to the CHI paper. A deeper theoretical treatment.
- ["Stories We Tell About Labor: Turkopticon and the Trouble with 'Design'"](https://doi.org/10.1145/2858036.2858592) (CHI 2016) — 163 citations. Self-reflexive paper analyzing the contradictions of designing tools for worker resistance within the same academic-industrial complex being critiqued.
- ["Ethics and Tactics of Professional Crowdwork"](https://doi.org/10.1145/1869086.1869100) (with M. Six Silberman; XRDS, 2010) — 149 citations.

9. **[[FAccT]]** — Fairness, Accountability, Transparency in computing.
10. **ACM Interactions** — Design magazine for HCI community. Accessible writing.

### Pattern Analysis
Irani's venue choices reveal someone who deliberately bridges **CS conferences** (CHI, CSCW) and **critical theory journals** (SAQ, Catalyst, ST&HV). She's not just publishing in CS venues with a critical lens — she's publishing in humanities/social science venues with CS material. This dual positioning gives her work unusual reach and allows her to bring STS critique *into* the systems-building community while also making computing legible to social theorists.

The inclusion of **South Atlantic Quarterly** and **Catalyst** is particularly telling — these are not places most HCI researchers publish. They signal an intellectual ambition beyond computing.

## Why Interesting

Irani is interesting because she occupies a position almost nobody else does: she has the **technical credentials** (Stanford CS, Google UX) to be taken seriously by the computing community AND the **critical theory chops** (postcolonial theory, feminist STS, labor studies) to produce genuinely rigorous critique. This is not an outsider throwing stones — it's someone who was inside, left, and turned the analytical tools of science studies on the industry she came from.

The Turkopticon project is paradigmatic: it's not just a paper ABOUT platform labor — it's a TOOL that changes labor conditions, AND a paper analyzing the contradictions of building that tool within academic incentive structures. The loop between building and critiquing is what makes her work live at the edge of chaos.

Her concept of **"entrepreneurial citizenship"** — the idea that hackathons, startup culture, and design thinking don't just produce technologies but produce SUBJECTS who identify as entrepreneurs — connects directly to Amber's interest in how technology shapes identity and governance.

The "Tech Lash to Tech Fash" framing at Aarhus 2025 names something that feels prophetic: the transition from liberal hand-wringing about technology's harms to a more urgent recognition that tech infrastructure is being weaponized for authoritarian ends.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Flipping the lens (counter-infrastructure):** Irani's signature move is taking a system and flipping its surveillance or power structure. Turkopticon takes Amazon's worker surveillance infrastructure and reverses it so workers can rate employers. This "inversion" strategy generates both a design artifact AND a critique simultaneously.

**Concept coining through provocation:** "Postcolonial computing," "entrepreneurial citizenship," "ghost work" (adjacent, popularized by Mary L. Gray who cites Irani's work), "tech lash to tech fash." Each is a two-word bomb that reframes a debate. They're designed to be uncomfortable for the community they address.

**Ethnographic grounding:** Unlike many critical computing scholars who work from theory, Irani does extensive ethnographic fieldwork — years in Delhi design studios, deep engagement with MTurk worker communities. This gives her claims empirical weight that pure theory lacks.

### Career Architecture

**Industry-to-academy pipeline:** Starting at Stanford CS and working in Silicon Valley before pivoting to critical scholarship at UCI/UCSD gives her unusual authority. She can't be dismissed as someone who doesn't understand technology because she built it.

**Bridge department:** Her Communication + Science Studies dual appointment at UCSD lets her publish in both CS venues (CHI, CSCW) and humanities/theory journals (SAQ, Catalyst). Most scholars are stuck in one or the other.

**Tool-as-theory:** Turkopticon functions simultaneously as research output, political intervention, and institutional proof of "impact." One project generates papers for a decade while also doing real-world work.

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **Stanford CS + Silicon Valley UX (early 2000s)** — Built technical credibility and insider knowledge. Without this, the later critique would lack authority.
2. **Turkopticon launch (2008-2009)** — Created with Six Silberman during PhD at UCI. A browser extension that lets MTurk workers rate requesters. Simultaneously a tool, a research project, and a political intervention. This single project generated 5+ papers and anchored her entire research identity.
3. **"Postcolonial Computing" papers (2010)** — Co-authored with Paul Dourish. Named something the HCI community couldn't ignore: that computing research for "developing" countries reproduces colonial power dynamics. 950+ combined citations.
4. **"We Are Dynamo" (2015)** — Extended Turkopticon into collective action infrastructure. Showed she could scale from individual tools to organizing platforms. Co-authored with Michael Bernstein (Stanford) — a strategic choice that bridges critical and mainstream HCI.
5. **UCSD appointment (2013)** — Moved from UCI to a Communication + Science Studies position, signaling a shift from HCI/CS to a broader social science framing. This let her write *Chasing Innovation* (an ethnographic book) rather than being constrained to CS paper formats.
6. **"Design Thinking" critique (2018)** — Published in feminist STS journal Catalyst, not in a CS venue. Called design thinking what it is: an ideology defending Silicon Valley's position at the top of global labor hierarchies. This was the move from critiquing specific platforms (MTurk) to critiquing the entire ideological apparatus of "innovation."
7. **"Tech Lash to Tech Fash" (2025)** — Named the political urgency of the current moment. Shifted from academic critique to strategic organizing language. Published at [[Aarhus Conference]], connecting to the Scandinavian participatory design tradition.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Build the counter-tool, then critique it:** Don't just write about problems — build something that addresses them, then write papers analyzing the contradictions of building it. This generates both "impact" and "theory" from one project.

2. **Industry experience as critical capital:** Irani's Silicon Valley years give her critique authority. Amber's technical background in XR/spatial computing could function the same way — critique from the inside is more powerful than critique from the outside.

3. **Venue bridging as intellectual strategy:** Publishing the same ideas in BOTH CS venues (CHI) and theory journals (SAQ, Catalyst) multiplies reach. The CHI paper introduces the idea to builders; the theory paper gives it philosophical depth. Amber should consider where else beyond standard venues her work could speak.

4. **Name the thing:** "Postcolonial computing," "entrepreneurial citizenship," "tech fash" — Irani names phenomena in ways that make them un-ignorable. Two-word concept coining is a powerful academic tool.

5. **Ethnographic depth over survey breadth:** Irani spent YEARS in Delhi design studios. The depth of engagement is what makes *Chasing Innovation* authoritative rather than superficial. Amber's deep engagement with specific communities (XR, spatial computing) could similarly produce ethnographic richness.
