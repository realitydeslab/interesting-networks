# Malcolm Le Grice

(15 May 1940 – 3 December 2024) The central architect of British structural-materialist film. Co-founder and chief shaping force of the [[London Film-Makers' Co-operative (LFMC)]]; BFI-described as "probably the most influential modernist filmmaker in British cinema." A painter-turned-filmmaker-turned-digital-artist who built the institutional infrastructure, theoretical framework, and 70+ film body of work through which an alternative, radical history of British cinema runs. Died December 2024, aged 84.

> "An entire alternative, parallel history of cinema can be constructed through the work of artist and experimental filmmaker Le Grice. He has explored the tropes of early cinema, shadow performances, features, multiple-projection work, digital and most recently 3D." — BFI, 2016

## Background

Born Plymouth, Devon, 15 May 1940. Studied painting at the **Arts University Plymouth** then the **Slade School of Fine Art**, UCL. Started as a painter and jazz improviser — both practices feeding his early instinct for improvisation, materiality, and non-representational structure.

Began making film in the **mid-1960s**, initially at Saint Martin's College of Art, where he taught the **first film course ever given in a British art college context** (1965). The art-college context was formative: Le Grice approached film as a fine art medium, not as a narrative vehicle, and built a pedagogy around that conviction from the beginning.

**LFMC involvement (1969–1976):**
- Joined the [[London Film-Makers' Co-operative (LFMC)]] committee in 1969.
- Chair of the Co-op 1970–73.
- **Physically built the Co-op's 16mm processing workshop** with Fred Drummond at the Robert Street Arts Lab — the moment that transformed the LFMC from a distribution-and-exhibition collective into a genuine production infrastructure.
- Simultaneously established film as a discipline at **Saint Martin's School of Art** and **Goldsmiths, University of London**, creating pipelines for experimental filmmakers.

**Later institutional career:**
- Dean of Media Art, **University of Westminster**
- Professor, then **Professor Emeritus**, **University of the Arts London** (UAL)
- Served on committees of the **British Film Institute** (including the BFI Production Board), the **Arts Council of Great Britain**, the **CNAA**, the **Higher Education Funding Council for England**, and the **Arts and Humanities Research Council**
- Wrote a regular column for art monthly **Studio International** in the 1970s, making structural-materialist ideas legible to the broader art world

**Personal:** Married to Judith Le Grice; two children.

## Theoretical Framework: Structural-Materialist Film

Le Grice's work is inseparable from his theory. The core position: **film is not a transparent window onto reality but a physical substrate — celluloid, emulsion, grain, light, projection mechanism — with its own material properties**. Avant-garde film must foreground these properties rather than use them to create narrative illusions.

His **1972 essay "Real TIME/SPACE"** is the key early theoretical document — it positions the "projection event" (the act of projecting the film in real time before a real audience) as the defining moment of cinema, distinct from the recorded illusion the film strip contains.

Key positions:
- Film language carries ideological freight from its theatrical and literary origins; experimental film must dismantle this freight
- The projection event is itself a material, temporal event — not a playback of a record but a live event with duration
- Computer animation and early digital media are natural extensions of this inquiry (he became a pioneer of **computer-generated filmmaking** from the 1970s)
- Unlike Peter Gidal's harder structural-materialist position (no representation, no readable images), Le Grice allowed lyricism, colour expressivity, even symbolism — but always grounded in material process

His move from 16mm to **video and digital media** in the 1980s–90s was contested but consistent with this logic: the new medium had new material properties to investigate.

## Key Works

### Early Films (1965–1969)
- **China Tea** (1965, 8mm, silent) — two-screen; object/material study; first proper film work. Drawing on the framing of painting, transposed to film.
- **Castle 1** (1966) — early structural work
- **Little Dog for Roger** (1967)
- **Yes No Maybe Maybenot** (1967)
- **Blind White Duration** (1967, silent)
- **Castle Two** (1968)

### The Core Structural-Materialist Period (1970–1977)
- **[Berlin Horse](https://lux.org.uk/work/berlin-horse/)** (1970) — **The defining British structural-materialist film.** Dual-screen (or single-screen, at the filmmaker's discretion); rephotographed found footage of a horse pulling a plough, colour-shifted through optical printing. The optical printing process exposes grain, temporal loops, and the material substance of the medium. Soundtrack by **Brian Eno** (one of Eno's earliest collaborators — then exploring post-Cage, post-serial composition with magnetic tape). BFI calls this a "film studies staple." Held in permanent collection of Centre Pompidou, among others.
- **Love Story 1** (1971, shadow play film)
- **Horror Film 1** (1971) — shadow-play performance film; projected film interacts with live shadow performance. Showman and magician qualities; "the guts of cinema being reformulated"
- **Horror Film 2** (1972, shadow play) — staged publicly at Raven Row in 2017 for first time since 1973
- **Whitchurch Down** (1972)
- **Blue Field Duration** (1972)
- **Matrix** (1973, loop film)
- **Four Wall Duration** (1973, loop film)
- **Gross Fog** (1973, loop film)
- **Principles of Cinematography** (1973, performance film)
- **After Lumière – l'arroseur arrosé** (1974) — structural deconstruction of early cinema (Lumière's *The Sprinkler Sprinkled*); interrogates the origins of cinema's grammar
- **After Manet – le dejeuner sur l'herbe** (1975) — structural deconstruction of art history's canonical images
- **Academic Still Life** (1976)
- **Time and Motion Study** (1976)
- **Blackbird Descending – tense alignment** (1977) — considered one of his best features; complex temporal structure

### Features & Later 16mm (1977–1984)
- **Emily – third party speculation** (1979)
- **Finnegans Chin – temporal economy** (1981) — broadcast on British television; later career major feature

### Video Period (1984–1999)
Le Grice's move to video in the mid-1980s fractured some assumptions about his work — "symbolism suddenly assumed more significance as he moved to consider memory and the passing of time far beyond 'the projection event'" (BFI obituary). The diaristic and expressionistic mode entered alongside the formal.

- **Digital Still Life** (1984–86, video)
- **Arbitrary Logic** (1984–86, video)
- **Like a Fox** (1988, co-edited with Gill Eatherley, video)
- **Rock Wave** (1988, video)
- **Juniper and the myths of origin** (1988)
- **Veritas** (1988, video)
- **Et in Arcadia Ego** (1988)
- **Rape** (1990)
- **Chronos Fragmented** (1995) — broadcast on British TV
- **Even the Cyclops Pays the Ferryman** (1998, video installation) — elegiac video about his recently-deceased father. "Haunted and haunting" (BFI). Layers of superimposition over darkness; fire, thunder, endings and cycles. Considered by many a "late work" despite being made 25+ years before his death.

Le Grice maintained painting practice throughout. Works include *After Manet 1* (1985), *Walking By* (1985), *Leonardo Triptych* (1993), *Berlin Horse Block* (1998), *Before you came After I've gone* (2003), and later digital painting works.

## Expanded Cinema: Filmaktion

One of the most historically significant phases of Le Grice's work was **Filmaktion** — the expanded cinema performance group he formed with **[[Gill Eatherley]]**, **[[Annabel Nicolson]]**, and **[[William Raban]]** in the early 1970s.

First major event: **Gallery House, London, March 1973** — a weekend of projections using 8mm, 16mm, and slide projectors, with film installations running continuously and live performance-based screening programmes.

Key venues in the original Filmaktion circuit (1973–1975):
- Gallery House, London (March 1973) — inaugural
- Scottish Arts Council Gallery
- Walker Art Gallery, Liverpool (a full week of events; documented in *Filmaktion Timelapse*, Raban, 1973)
- The Place
- ICA, London

The format: "itinerant viewer" film installations (loops running throughout the space) punctuated by live performance/multi-screen programmes twice daily. Projections used **multiple gauges simultaneously** (8mm, 16mm, slides). This is expanded cinema at its most literal: cinema expanded into the gallery, into duration, into space.

**2017 Reunion at Raven Row:** Le Grice, Eatherley, and Raban reconvened as Filmaktion at Raven Row (4–5 March 2017, curated by Mark Webber) in the context of the *This Way Out of England: Gallery House in Retrospect* exhibition. *Horror Film 2* was staged publicly for the first time since 1973. All original works from the 1973 circuit were performed/installed.

## Key Publications

### Books
- **[*Abstract Film and Beyond*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262620208/)** (Studio Vista / MIT Press, 1977) — **The first book-length history of experimental cinema written by a British practitioner.** Translated into Greek (Ekdozeiz Kaztanioth, 1982). Sets the LFMC's structural-materialist practice in international context from Richter, Léger, and early abstract film through American structural film to the British moment. Essential reading for understanding how Le Grice situated himself historically.
- **[*Experimental Cinema in the Digital Age*](https://www.bfi.org.uk/bfi-publishing/experimental-cinema-digital-age)** (BFI Publications, 2001/2011, ISBN 0-85170-873-0) — Collected essays spanning four decades. Covers the structural-materialist period, the move to digital, and Le Grice's ongoing theoretical engagement with what experimental film means in the age of computation.

### Key Articles & Essays
- **"Real TIME/SPACE"** (1972) — foundational essay on the "projection event" as the defining moment of cinema
- Regular column, **Studio International** (1970s) — made structural-materialist ideas legible to the broader art world
- Numerous essays collected in *Experimental Cinema in the Digital Age*

## Key Relationships & Collaborators

- **[[Brian Eno]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — provided the experimental soundtrack for *Berlin Horse* (1970); both exploring post-Cage, post-serial ideas simultaneously through different media (film / magnetic tape). - **[[Peter Gidal]]** ⭐⭐ — co-LFMC figure; the theoretical antipode to Le Grice. Gidal pushed harder-structural (zero representation); Le Grice allowed lyricism and colour. Their tension defined the LFMC's theoretical range. Both wrote major books on structural/materialist film.

- **[[Gill Eatherley]]** — core Filmaktion collaborator; co-edited *Like a Fox* (1988)

- **Fred Drummond** — technician; physically built the LFMC workshop with Le Grice at Robert Street Arts Lab. Also published *Filmmakers' Europe* (bi-monthly journal, 1977–81).

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Le Grice moved in **three interrelated circuits**:

### 1. British Structural/Experimental Film Circuit
The LFMC itself and its allied venues — co-op screenings, arts labs, later BFI Southbank (National Film Theatre), ICA. These were the primary venues for the structural-materialist work in the 1960s–70s. Small, hostile, often poorly attended, but generative.

### 2. International Avant-Garde Exhibition Circuit
Documenta 6 (1977), Biennale de Paris, X-Screen at MUMOK, Behind the Facts touring exhibition. Le Grice was one of very few British experimental filmmakers to achieve sustained exhibition at this level. His presence at Documenta signalled a successful crossover from underground film into international contemporary art.

### 3. Film Festival / Retrospective Circuit (later career)
Media City Film Festival (Windsor, Canada, 2015), REDCAT (Los Angeles, 2019), International Film Festival Rotterdam, Anthology Film Archives (New York, 2016). The retrospective mode — appropriate for a major historical figure — characterised the final decades.

**Small venues as signal:** Velarde Gallery (Devon) for his final exhibition is striking — a return to locality, to the South West of England where he was born, for the definitive late survey. The choice is intimate and territorial, not careerist.

## Writing & Ideas

### The Projection Event
Le Grice's key concept: film is not a stored record that gets "played back" — it is a **real-time material event** occurring in a specific space before a specific audience. This makes film closer to performance than to literature or photography. The quality of the projection event — the light in the room, the audience's duration with the work — is constitutive of the film itself.

This concept directly motivates:
- Multi-screen work (the spatial arrangement of the projection event is variable)
- Shadow performance work (the performance is part of the projection event, not separate from it)
- Loop films (the material endurance of the film loop becomes the structure)

### Against the Literary-Theatrical
Le Grice's sustained argument throughout his career: cinema was "lumbered" with literary and theatrical traditions by the introduction of sound in the 1920s. These traditions naturalised narrative, character, and dialogue as the medium's core. Experimental film's task is to recover what cinema was before that lumbering — and to discover what else it might become.

### Computer Film as Extension of Materialism
Le Grice was a **pioneer of computer-generated filmmaking** from the early 1970s. This was not a break from his materialism but a continuation: the computer is another material substrate with its own properties (binary logic, pixel grid, temporal sampling) that must be foregrounded, not used transparently. His exploration of computer animation predated the broader digital art turn by decades.

### Late Digital Work
The multi-projection video installations *The Cyclops Cycle* and *Treatise* extend the expanded cinema logic into gallery installation, with multiple simultaneous screens replacing the multiple simultaneous projectors of the Filmaktion period. The concerns remain identical: duration, the projection event, the material properties of the medium — now applied to digital video.

## Why Interesting for Amber

**🔧 Infrastructure Builder:**
Le Grice is a rare figure who combined rigorous artistic practice with institution-building at scale. He didn't just make films — he built a workshop, shaped a co-op, established film departments at two art colleges, sat on funding committees, and wrote a history. He is the model of an artist who builds the conditions for their field to exist. For Amber's interest in communities as research infrastructure, Le Grice is a concrete historical case: this is what it looks like to build field infrastructure from scratch.

**🎬 Material Investigation as Epistemology:**
Structural-materialist film treats the medium itself as the site of knowledge production. Le Grice's films don't represent things — they investigate what film *is*, what looking at film *does*, how cinema produces meaning through its material operations. This is epistemology through practice, not just philosophy about practice. The closest contemporary analogues are practitioners who treat code, data, or computational processes as sites of material investigation rather than transparent tools.

**⚡ The Projection Event:**
Le Grice's concept of the "projection event" as constitutive rather than representational maps directly onto questions about what AI-generated content *is* — is it a record, a performance, a live event? His framework, applied to computation, raises urgent questions about what it means for generative systems to "run" rather than just "produce."

**🌐 The LFMC Legacy:**
Understanding Le Grice means understanding the full genealogy of British artists' film — from the LFMC → Circles → Cinenova → LUX. This is the historical backbone of British experimental film distribution and the context in which contemporary British moving image artists (Hito Steyerl's European contemporaries, the Black Audio Film Collective, etc.) situate themselves.

**💀 Historical Moment:**
His death in December 2024 means the field is in an active moment of reflection. LUX has published a tribute; the BFI has published an obituary; the archive question is urgent. This is an optimal moment to understand who he was and what the LFMC means, because the conversation is happening now.
