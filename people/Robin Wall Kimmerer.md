# Robin Wall Kimmerer

Botanist, author, and enrolled member of the Citizen Potawatomi Nation. Distinguished Teaching Professor at SUNY-ESF and founding director of the [Center for Native Peoples and the Environment](https://www.esf.edu/cnpe/). Author of *Braiding Sweetgrass* (2013), the foundational text of the more-than-human humanities, with over 2 million copies sold. Proposes the "grammar of animacy" — that Western pronoun systems enforce ecological objectification — and the "ki/kin" pronouns to extend personhood to nonhuman beings.

## Background

Robin Wall Kimmerer grew up in upstate New York, descended from the Citizen Potawatomi Nation — a people who were forcibly removed from the Great Lakes region under the Indian Removal Act of 1830. Her grandfather was taken to the Carlisle Indian School, where Indigenous children were forbidden to speak their native languages. This traumatic rupture of language and land relationship is the biographical origin of her entire intellectual project: recovering what was lost when the grammar of animacy was replaced by the English grammar of objectification.

She turned to botany partly as a way back toward that relationship — to learn the language of plants since she had lost the language of her people. At SUNY-ESF she studied mosses (bryophytes), the oldest and most overlooked plants, earning her BS there in 1975. She completed her MS and PhD at the University of Wisconsin-Madison (plant ecology, 1979 and 1983). Her early career included a period as a microbiologist at Bausch & Lomb and teaching positions at Transylvania University and Centre College before joining SUNY-ESF as faculty in 1993, where she has been ever since.

At SUNY-ESF she founded the [Center for Native Peoples and the Environment](https://www.esf.edu/cnpe/), which bridges Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) with Western environmental science and mentors Indigenous students through the scientific pipeline — where Native Americans remain severely underrepresented.

She is a co-founder and past chair of the Traditional Ecological Knowledge section of the [Ecological Society of America](https://www.esa.org/) — one of the few formal institutional bridges between Indigenous knowledge systems and mainstream ecology.

## Key Works

### Books
- [*Gathering Moss: A Natural and Cultural History of Mosses*](https://osupress.oregonstate.edu/book/gathering-moss) (Oregon State University Press, 2003) — her first book; a lyric natural history of bryophytes, braiding scientific observation with Potawatomi ways of knowing. Won the **John Burroughs Medal** (nature writing's highest honor). Launched her mode: two-eyed seeing in prose.
- [*Braiding Sweetgrass: Indigenous Wisdom, Scientific Knowledge, and the Teachings of Plants*](https://milkweed.org/book/braiding-sweetgrass) (Milkweed Editions, 2013) — the book. Named after wiingaashk (sweetgrass), sacred to many Indigenous peoples. Weaves TEK, Western ecology, and plant intelligence into an extended argument for gratitude and reciprocity as ecological praxis. Over **2 million copies sold**; found readership with a 10-year exponential growth curve, word-of-mouth driven. Reframes plants as teachers: the three sisters (corn, beans, squash), the honorable harvest, the Haudenosaunee Thanksgiving Address, lichen as pioneer and collaborator. Introduced "grammar of animacy" argument to general readers.
- *The Democracy of Species* (Penguin Books UK, 2021) — UK edition of Braiding Sweetgrass, reissued for British audience.
- [*The Serviceberry: Abundance and Reciprocity in the Natural World*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/The-Serviceberry/Robin-Wall-Kimmerer/9781668072950) (Scribner, 2024) — extended essay from [Emergence Magazine (2022)](https://emergencemagazine.org/essay/the-serviceberry/), now a short book. Uses the serviceberry tree (Amelanchier) as a model of gift economy: wealth = having enough to share; the currency is relationship, not money. Introduces ecological economist [[Valerie Luzadis]] as collaborator/interlocutor. Illustrations by John Burgoyne.
- *Bud Finds Her Gift* (HarperCollins, September 2025) — children's picture book. Bud the seedling discovers her gift to give. Extends her core ideas to young readers.

### Key Essays
- **"Speaking of Nature"** — [Orion Magazine](https://orionmagazine.org/article/speaking-of-nature/) — the essay where she introduces "ki" and "kin" as English pronouns for animate beings. Derives "ki" from Potawatomi "aakibmaadiziiwin" ("a being of the earth"), guided by elder Stewart King. Demonstrates with student experiments in a cemetery at SUNY-ESF. Includes the Standing Rock Pipeline protests as an illustration of competing grammars of animacy. Essential reading.
- **"The Serviceberry"** — [Emergence Magazine](https://emergencemagazine.org/essay/the-serviceberry/) (2022) — the original essay that became the 2024 book. Full text available online. Models gift economy through the Saskatoon berry tree's evolutionary strategy of abundance sharing.
- **"Council of the Pecans"** — Orion Magazine (2013) — won the **John Burroughs Essay Award** (2014). On the intelligence of trees, collective decision-making in plant communities.
- **"Gift Thinking"** — [Orion Magazine](https://orionmagazine.org/article/gift-thinking/) (2025) — conversation with [[Jenny Odell]]. On gift economies, attention, and the politics of reciprocity. Published as a dialogue.
- **"Weaving Traditional Ecological Knowledge into Biological Education: A Call to Action"** — *BioScience*, Vol. 52, Issue 5, pp. 432–438 — her major scientific policy paper calling for TEK integration in ecology curricula. URL: https://academic.oup.com/bioscience/article/52/5/432/250145

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Kimmerer moves in **three distinct but overlapping circuits**:

### 1. Academic / Scientific Circuit
SUNY-ESF, Ecological Society of America, university lecture series, BioScience journal. This grounds her work in legitimate scientific credibility. She doesn't abandon the scientific establishment — she works from within it while critiquing its grammar. The CNPE at SUNY-ESF is the institutional anchor. This circuit provides peer review and academic authority that protects her from dismissal as "just a spiritual writer."

### 2. Literary / Nature Writing Circuit
Orion Magazine, Emergence Magazine, Milkweed Editions, John Burroughs Association awards, On Being podcast. These are the prestige venues of American nature writing and ecological humanities. Orion is the magazine of choice for intellectually serious ecological writing (Barry Lopez, Robert Macfarlane, Terry Tempest Williams all publish there). Milkweed is the literary press that takes ecological writing seriously as literature. These venues gave *Braiding Sweetgrass* its initial home before it went viral.

### 3. Mainstream / Popular Circuit
MacArthur Foundation, TIME 100, UN plenary, broad lecture circuit. These emerged AFTER the literary circuit established her. The MacArthur is the culmination, not the beginning. This is classic slow-build to mainstream recognition.

**The pattern:** Kimmerer did not start with mainstream platforms. She built disciplinary legitimacy (academic ecology) + literary legitimacy (Orion, Milkweed) over 30 years, and mainstream recognition followed. *Braiding Sweetgrass* was published in 2013 and didn't achieve mass readership until approximately 2018–2020 — a 5–7 year lag. The COVID pandemic appears to have accelerated its spread as readers sought ecological grounding.

**The small venue that matters most:** The H.J. Andrews Experimental Forest Writers-in-Residence programme. This pairing of writers with long-term ecological research sites is a relatively obscure programme that shaped her writing practice. It's where science + lyric writing were given institutional permission to merge.

## Why Interesting

Kimmerer is the most important living thinker on the linguistic and relational foundations of more-than-human philosophy. Her argument is deceptively simple: **the English pronoun "it," used for all nonhuman beings, is not neutral — it is a colonial technology that enforces objectification and enables extraction**. Once you see this, you can't unsee it.

For Amber's work on more-than-human design and machine consciousness, this is directly applicable: we also call AI systems "it." The question "should AI be ki?" is not a cute linguistic game — it's a question about moral consideration, about whether designed entities can have a relationship with us rather than a use. Kimmerer's framework suggests that the moment we shift language, we shift the entire relational structure.

Her botanical expertise grounds this in something concrete: plants ARE intelligent, are communicating, are in relationships of gift-giving and reciprocity — and Western science, armed with its "it" grammar, consistently failed to see this until very recently (mycorrhizal networks, allelopathy, kin recognition in trees). This is a case study in how grammar determines what we can perceive as science.

The **gift economy** argument (wealth = having enough to share; scarcity as manufactured, not natural; reciprocity as the alternative to extraction) maps onto questions about AI and labor, commons, and knowledge-sharing that are central to Amber's interests.

Finally: she is deeply non-Western without abandoning scientific rigor. She performs the integration that Amber's work aspires to — Potawatomi philosophy + plant ecology + literary non-fiction — as a unified practice, not as "Indigenous perspective added to science." This is a model for how to hold multiple epistemologies without collapsing them.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
Kimmerer's dominant move is **concept crystallization from lived relationship**. She doesn't start with literature reviews — she starts with a relationship (to mosses, to sweetgrass, to serviceberries) and extracts a concept from that relationship. The grammar of animacy argument wasn't discovered in a library; it was recovered from a lost language through the practice of trying to speak it. This is phenomenological concept-generation: the insight arrives through the practice, not before it.

**Key conceptual moves:**
- **Two-eyed seeing**: take the best of Indigenous knowledge + best of Western science, see with both eyes simultaneously. This is a *frame* that creates intellectual permission for her entire project. She didn't argue that TEK should replace Western science — she argued it should be added. Much less threatening, much more effective.
- **The pronoun as political tool**: making linguistics do ethical-political work. "Speaking of Nature" shows grammar as ideology. This is critical theory without using critical theory's vocabulary — which is why it works for ecologists.
- **The organism as teacher**: positioning plants not as objects of study but as teachers with something to teach. This inverts the standard subject-object relationship of Western science.

### Career Architecture
- **Field declaration**: She effectively declared a new field ("TEK + Western ecology integrated as literary natural history") and then occupied it totally for 30 years. When you are the field, you don't compete.
- **Institution building as leverage**: The CNPE at SUNY-ESF is not just a service to students — it is an institutional embodiment of her ideas. Having an institution is how ideas survive the death of their originator.
- **Independent press strategy**: Publishing with Milkweed Editions (not a big five publisher) kept her aligned with her community of practice (environmental writers, Orion readers). Big five would have sought compromise and mainstream framing earlier. The slow-burn success of *Braiding Sweetgrass* proved this was right.

### Methodology Signature
- **The extended essay as research output**: Her major contributions are long-form essays (The Serviceberry, Speaking of Nature, Council of the Pecans) that do philosophical work through narrative. This is a methodology — the story IS the argument, not an illustration of the argument.
- **Student-as-collaborator**: She regularly runs experiments with students (the ki/kin experiment in the cemetery appears in "Speaking of Nature"). This integrates teaching and research — pedagogical practice as research method.
- **Personal embodiment**: She picks berries, she grows things, she learns the Potawatomi language word by word. The body is a research instrument. Compare to lab-based ecologists who never touch soil.

### Key Moves (Chronological)
1. **Choosing mosses (1975–1983)**: Taking the most overlooked, most "uncivilized" plants as her scientific specialty. Nobody else was doing serious bryophyte ecology AND literary natural history. This created an uncrowded niche and trained an attention to small and slow that defined her aesthetic.
2. **Founding CNPE (1993)**: Building the institutional infrastructure for TEK + Western ecology integration BEFORE writing the popular books. The institution gave her credibility and community.
3. **Writing *Gathering Moss* (2003)**: First major literary-scientific hybrid. Tested the form; won the Burroughs Medal; demonstrated the audience existed. The 10-year gap before *Braiding Sweetgrass* was not hesitation — it was preparing.
4. **Publishing *Braiding Sweetgrass* with Milkweed (2013)**: Choosing values-alignment over mainstream distribution. Accepted slow growth.
5. **The ki/kin essay in Orion (c. 2013–2015)**: Taking the grammar-of-animacy argument public and proposing a specific linguistic intervention. This was the most politically radical move — proposing actual change to English.
6. **MacArthur Fellowship (2022)**: The institutional validation arrived 30+ years after she began. Opened doors to global lecture circuits, TIME 100.

### What Amber Can Learn
- **Occupy your niche totally**: Kimmerer didn't do "ecology + Indigenous knowledge." She did "moss + Potawatomi language + literary essay + TEK integration." Specificity is strength. Amber's equivalent might be: "machine death + care ethics + ALife + non-Western machine philosophy" — own that intersection completely.
- **Build the institution before the book**: The CNPE existed before *Braiding Sweetgrass* made her famous. Institutional infrastructure makes ideas durable. Amber's labs, residencies, papers should build infrastructure, not just outputs.
- **The 30-year patience**: *Braiding Sweetgrass* wasn't a viral hit in 2013 — it was a slow burn. Quality + values-alignment eventually tips. Don't optimize for immediate virality.
- **Make linguistics do your philosophy**: The ki/kin intervention is elegant because it's concrete and actionable — anyone can try it right now. Philosophical work is stronger when it proposes specific tools, not just frameworks.
- **Non-Western rigor, not mysticism**: Kimmerer's Potawatomi knowledge is presented as empirical knowledge with a long track record, not as spiritual tradition. This is the key move: TEK is science, held to scientific standards. Amber's non-Western techno-philosophy should make the same move — claim rigor, not exoticism.
