# Ai Hasegawa

Japanese artist and design researcher whose speculative practice probes the frontiers of biological reproduction, mortality, queer biotechnology, and technological ethics. One of the most provocative graduates of [[Design Interactions (RCA)]]. Famous for imagining humans gestating sharks, same-sex genetic children, and machines for confronting death. Based in Japan; orbit spans London, Boston, Tokyo, Kyoto.

## Background

Ai Hasegawa is a Japanese artist and researcher who trained in the legendary [[Design Interactions (RCA)]] programme under [[Anthony Dunne]] and [[Fiona Raby]], graduating ca. 2012–2013. Her graduation project — *I Wanna Deliver a Shark* — imagined surgically modified women gestating endangered shark embryos, instantly establishing her as one of the most provocative voices in speculative biodesign.

After RCA, she worked at and with **MIT Media Lab** (researcher and lecturer, likely in [[Sputniko!]]'s Design Fiction group), taught at **University of Tokyo**, and was a **Design Researcher in Residence at Kyoto Institute of Technology's KYOTO Design Lab** (2020–21). She has been nomadic across London, Boston, Tokyo, and Kyoto.

Her intellectual core can be described as: *what happens at the boundaries of reproduction — between species, between sexes, between life and death?* She uses speculative design not to solve problems but to generate public debate about bioethics, biotech governance, and who gets to decide.

She has also written prolifically in Japanese, and her 2020 book *20XX年の革命家になるには* (Diamond Publishing) is a major speculative design textbook incorporating her MIT and UTokyo curricula.

## Key Works

### Reproduction / Inter-species

**[I Wanna Deliver a Shark…](https://web.archive.org/web/20230922225909/https://aihasegawa.info/i-wanna-deliver-a-shark)** (2012)
RCA graduation project. A wearable/surgical system for a human woman to gestate a *Squalus acanthias* (spiny dogfish shark, IUCN vulnerable). Imagines uterine medication patches to prevent rejection, artificial uterus technology using maternal urine, and a galvanic skin response "stress sharing" system to bond mother and embryo. Medical consultants: Dr. Mark H. Sullivan (Imperial College London), embryologist Anastasia Mania. The speculative argument: giving birth to an endangered species might generate care and prevent extinction — but would that care then prevent consumption? Wildly provocative and anatomically researched.

**I Wanna Deliver a Dolphin…** (2013)
Follow-up to the shark project, extending the inter-species reproduction argument to another marine mammal with different extinction pressures.

**[(Im)possible Baby, Case 01: Asako & Moriga](https://web.archive.org/web/20231020082041/https://aihasegawa.info/impossible-baby-case-01-asako-moriga)** (2015)
Her best-known work. Collaborated with a real lesbian couple (Asako Makimura & Moriga) to simulate the genetically possible children they could have via iPS cell-derived gametes (not yet technologically possible but imminently so, following Cambridge/Weizmann Institute research). Used 23andMe genetic data + SNPedia to simulate faces, personalities, eye patterns, food sensitivities of two hypothetical daughters ("Mameko" and "Powako"). Produced a hardcover family photo album presented to the couple as a gift. Also developed a web-based baby simulator. Culminated in a 30-minute NHK documentary (*NEXT: 未来のために*, aired Oct 2015) followed by 950+ significant tweets and major public discussion. Production adviser: **Sputniko!** (MIT Media Lab). The project explicitly asked: *who decides whether same-sex reproduction is ethically permitted?*

**[Human X Shark](https://aihasegawa.info/human-x-shark)** (2017)
Further development of the human-shark intimacy/gestation theme.

**[Virgin Birth Simulator](https://aihasegawa.info/virgin-birth-simulator)** (2015)
Exploring parthenogenesis as a feminist speculative scenario.

### Environments / Love Hotels

**[The Extreme Environment Love Hotel, Jupiter Room](https://we-make-money-not-art.com/?s=ai+hasegawa)** (2012)
Proposes a Japanese love hotel room that simulates *impossible* environments — the surface of Jupiter, or Earth 300 million years ago (Carboniferous) — by manipulating atmospheric conditions, gravity, and sensory parameters. An intimate space for experiencing inhuman conditions. The argument: what is "nature" if we can simulate its most alien versions?

**The Extreme Environment Love Hotel, Carboniferous Room** (2012)
Companion piece — inhabiting deep geological time as an erotic/intimate act.

### Death / Mortality / Grief

**[Techno-thanatology: Speculative Noh Pilot Play 技術死生学：思索能試作](https://web.archive.org/web/20230922225933/https://aihasegawa.info/techno-thanatology)** (2021)
Video installation exploring the intersection of death-care technologies and traditional Japanese aesthetics. Uses Noh theatre (which is already a form where the living encounter the dead) as a lens for thinking about deepfake resurrection, digital afterlives, and other "technologies of death." Origin story: Hasegawa's first video work *an example01* was made about a 21-year-old friend who died of cancer — she found herself without religious frameworks for caring for death, and made art as a secular death-care practice. Produced as part of her Design Researcher in Residence at KYOTO Design Lab; supported by JST-RISTEX ELSI Program's "Techno-thanatology Project" (PI: Watanabe Maiko, Jichi Medical University). Exhibited at Terada Warehouse (Tokyo), designed by Fumihiko Sano Studio.

**[Memorial Picture Frame 2.0 供養絵額2.0](https://web.archive.org/web/20230922210801/https://aihasegawa.info/memorial-picture-frame20-20)** (2021)
Workshop and video work. Takes inspiration from *kuyo-e-gaku* — 19th-century Iwate Prefecture tradition of memorial painted frames offered to temples. Speculates on a near-future grief-care service using DeepFake video to animate memorial images of the deceased. The workshop asked participants to design their own future memorials and discuss the ethics of such services. Collaborators: Tsukuda Arina, Ozawa Ibuki; JST-RISTEX ELSI Program.

### Risk / Disaster

**[Mt. Fuji Eruption Tea Ceremony 富士山噴火茶席](https://web.archive.org/web/20231020082028/https://aihasegawa.info/mtfuji-eruption-tea-ceremony)** (2017, 2021)
A tea ceremony installation using the ritualized space of *chanoyu* to discuss the "grey rhino" risk of Mt. Fuji eruption. Each tea utensil represents a disaster scenario element: a kettle lid that inflates like a swelling volcano, tea bowls modelled on Mt. Fuji's pre-eruption and lava-flow topographies, felt mats representing 4–12cm volcanic ash accumulation depths at different Tokyo locations. Tea master: Matsumura Soryo. Architect: Sano Fumihiko (茶室「灰犀庵」). Commissioned by Kyoto Institute of Technology KYOTO Design Lab and Terada Warehouse. A meditation on invisible-but-known catastrophic risks and how we inhabit disaster-adjacent daily life.

### AI Ethics

**[Alt-Bias Gun](https://web.archive.org/web/20231020082028/https://aihasegawa.info/alt-bias-gun)** (2018)
Speculative gun whose trigger is locked for a few seconds when the target matches the algorithmic profile of "unarmed Black people historically killed by police." Trained on Washington Post police shooting data. Asks: is it ethical to encode counter-bias into lethal weapons? Does it shift responsibility from officer to machine? Raises questions of Neurolaw, algorithmic fairness, and who holds liability for bias-correcting systems. Technical support: Yuri Aisaka; JST ERATO Kawahara Project.

### Writing & Pedagogy

**[20XX年の革命家になるには─ Revolutionary 20XX](https://web.archive.org/web/20230922223940/https://aihasegawa.info/book-20xxrevolutionary-20xx)** (2020)
Major speculative design textbook published by Diamond (ダイヤモンド社). Collects and systematizes her teaching at MIT Media Lab and University of Tokyo. Covers the history of Design Interactions at RCA, speculative design methodology, Asian perspectives on speculative futures (Taiwan, Indonesia, China), ethics in AI and design, and includes a complete "20XX Revolutionary Toolkit" — 126 detachable cards and worksheets for speculative design ideation. Features essays by Anthony Dunne, and dialogues with SF author Fujii Taiyo, legal scholar Inaya Tatsuhiko. Explicitly positions speculative design as a tool for social revolution accessible to non-experts. The most accessible Japanese-language SD textbook.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Biological limits as philosophical premises.** Hasegawa doesn't just work "with biology" — she picks a specific biological impossibility (inter-species gestation, same-sex gamete production, resurrection via deepfake) and builds an entire argument inside it. The biology functions as a stress-test for existing ethics. This is methodology-by-limit: choose an edge case, push it until the ethical framework breaks, exhibit the fracture.

**Two-register operation.** Her best work operates simultaneously in the aesthetic register (beautiful, strange, intimate objects) and the scientific register (she actually consults embryologists, reads iPS cell papers, uses real 23andMe data). The aesthetic makes people care; the scientific credibility makes the ethical question unavoidable. Neither register alone would work.

**Provoking the decision-makers.** The explicit political move in (Im)possible Baby: "Who gets to decide whether same-sex reproduction is ethical? A few doctors and scientists, or us?" She doesn't answer — she designs a space where the question becomes uncomfortable to avoid.

### Career Architecture

**Graduation project as career-defining statement.** *I Wanna Deliver a Shark* was her RCA graduation work. It immediately circulated in Dezeen, Wired, New Scientist. This is the "bet everything on one provocation" strategy — risky but high-reward if the project is genuinely alien.

**Institutional anchoring via unlikely media partnerships.** NHK is not where speculative designers normally work. By partnering with national television for (Im)possible Baby, Hasegawa reached millions of Japanese people with a bioethics debate that was previously confined to academic and gallery spaces. This is a rare move in the speculative design world — most practitioners stay in the gallery circuit.

**Return to origin + field-definition.** By writing the major Japanese-language SD textbook, she defined the field in Japan rather than remaining a practitioner-exporter. This consolidates institutional power differently than UK/US counterparts who compete in the global gallery market.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **2012 — RCA graduation with *I Wanna Deliver a Shark*.** The provocation-first strategy. Immediately establishes her as a voice willing to go where others won't. Enables international media coverage and MIT Media Lab connection.

2. **2015 — (Im)possible Baby + NHK documentary.** Upgrades from gallery to broadcast media. Converts a speculative project into a genuine national bioethics debate in Japan. Produces 950+ significant public responses. This is the move that made her a public intellectual, not just an artist.

3. **2018 — Alt-Bias Gun.** Pivots to AI ethics, showing her framework works across domains. Supported by JST ERATO Kawahara Project — signals she can attract engineering-side funding for critical work, rare for a speculative designer.

4. **2020–21 — KYOTO Design Lab residency + book publication.** Institutionalizes her practice. The textbook crystallizes her methodology for pedagogy and creates a field-defining document in Japanese. The residency produces Techno-thanatology, extending her death work.

### What Amber Can Learn

- **The single provocation strategy**: one deeply researched, beautifully documented, genuinely alien project can do more career work than ten exhibitions. Amber's machine death work should be developed this way — one scenario, exhaustively argued, with both aesthetic and scientific/technical backing.
- **Media partnership as amplification**: NHK gave Ai reach no gallery circuit can match. Amber might think about BBC partnerships, Channel 4, or Japanese/Chinese media equivalents for any work addressing mortality or more-than-human care.
- **Real subjects, real stakes**: Ai's use of actual genetic data from a real couple makes the ethics undeniable. Amber's speculative work would be strengthened by anchoring scenarios in real people, real data, real institutions.
- **Japanese-language output matters**: If Amber wants influence in Japan's speculative design ecosystem (currently undervalued internationally), writing or co-authoring in Japanese would be surprisingly high-leverage given how few Western researchers do this.
