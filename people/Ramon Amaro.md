# Ramon Amaro

Critical theorist, researcher, and design leader. Exceptionally rare figure: trained engineer (General Motors, ASME) who crossed into cultural/critical theory — now the incoming Creative Director of [[Design Academy Eindhoven]] (Sept 2025). Author of *The Black Technical Object* (Sternberg Press, 2023), the landmark text on race and machine learning.

## Domains
- **Race and machine learning** — how statistical analysis encodes racial hierarchies into AI systems
- **Black studies / critical computing** — Fanon, Wynter, Ferreira da Silva read through technical practice
- **Hauntology and technology** — drawing on Derrida and Avery Gordon to think about algorithmic haunting
- **Design education** — reforming design pedagogy from within (DAE Creative Director)
- **Digital culture studies** — formerly at Nieuwe Instituut, research on digital infrastructure and power

## Background

Amaro's trajectory is genuinely unusual: **engineer first, critical theorist second**. He trained and worked as a quality design engineer at **General Motors** and held policy roles at the **American Society of Mechanical Engineers (ASME)** before pivoting to cultural theory. This grounding in actual industrial systems engineering — the metallurgy of production, the logistics of classification — is what gives his critique of machine learning a particular depth. He is not making cultural analogies from the outside; he knows exactly what the machines do.

He studied and eventually taught at **Goldsmiths, University of London** (Department of Visual Cultures), one of the critical theory and art theory epicentres of Europe — the department that houses Forensic Architecture, Research Architecture, and a generation of thinkers working on race, image, and power. His PhD research at Goldsmiths laid the groundwork for *The Black Technical Object*.

He moved to the **Nieuwe Instituut** in Rotterdam as Senior Researcher in Digital Culture, where he founded and directed **-1** — an interdisciplinary research space probing how architecture, design, and technology intersect with marginalised subjectivities. The Nieuwe Instituut is one of the premier European venues for design-technology-politics discourse.

In 2024, he was announced as the next **Creative Director of Design Academy Eindhoven** (DAE), Europe's most internationally prominent design school, succeeding Joseph Grima. His start date was September 2025. This is a major institutional position: the Creative Director shapes DAE's entire pedagogical and intellectual direction, including the annual Dutch Design Week.

## Key Works

### Books
- [*The Black Technical Object: On Machine Learning and the Aspiration of Black Thought*](https://www.sternberg-press.com/product/the-black-technical-object/) (Sternberg Press, 2023) — the central text. Introduces the history of statistical analysis and "scientific" racism into machine learning research. Argues that ML is preconditioned by a "prototypical Whiteness" embedded in data collection, taxonomic patterning, and classification. Juxtaposes practical analysis of ML with a theory of Black alienation — drawing on Fanon, Wynter, Ferreira da Silva — to generate alternative approaches to algorithmic practice. The title's double meaning is crucial: the "Black technical object" is both (a) the Black body as raw material for algorithmic training data and (b) a latent capacity within Black thought to reimagine technical practice. One of the most important texts in the critical AI / race-and-technology space. Referenced widely in FAccT, CSCW, critical data studies.

### Essays and Articles
- **"Haunting, Blackness, and Algorithmic Thought"** — *e-flux journal* #123 (December 2021), co-authored with **Ezekiel Dixon-Román**. Draws on Derrida's hauntology and Avery Gordon's concept of haunting to examine how racial histories "haunt" computational systems. Argues that algorithmic thought is both temporally discontinuous and viscerally affective — the past ghosts of racial science recursively reappear in ML outputs. References Gilbert Simondon on the technical object, Sylvia Wynter on autopoetic subjectivity, and Fanon's "fact of Blackness." This essay introduced Amaro to a broader art/theory audience beyond academia. [→ e-flux journal #123](https://www.e-flux.com/journal/123/437244/haunting-blackness-and-algorithmic-thought/)

- **"Contagion, Information, Territory"** — 2025 conference paper co-authored with **Jasbir Puar**. Examines how data infrastructures and algorithmic systems reproduce social hierarchies in relation to what Amaro theorises as "the black technical object." Indicates ongoing collaboration between Amaro's work and queer/crip theory (Puar's domain). Presented at UAB (Barcelona), 2025.

- **"Refusing Algorithmic Recognition"** — referenced in *European Journal of International Law* (35:4, 2023), on the entanglements between technology, data, and race. Amaro's genealogy of racial sorting argues that machine learning is preconditioned by a prototypical Whiteness.

## Intellectual Toolkit

Amaro's theoretical resources are recognisably within a post-colonial / Black radical tradition, but unusually filtered through philosophy of technology and mathematics:

- **Frantz Fanon** (*Black Skin, White Masks*) — the "fact of Blackness," the epidermal schema, Black alienation
- **Sylvia Wynter** — on autopoetic turn/overturn, Black knowledges, the "overrepresentation of Man"
- **Denise Ferreira da Silva** — Black feminist poethics, the racial ordering of the world, the post-Enlightenment subject
- **Achille Mbembe** (*Critique of Black Reason*) — decolonial critique of Western reason
- **Hortense Spillers** — Black interiority, the flesh/body distinction
- **Claudia Rankine** (*Citizen: An American Lyric*) — on the everyday violence of anti-Black perception
- **Ezekiel Dixon-Román** (*Inheriting Possibility*) — on social reproduction and quantification in education
- **Gilbert Simondon** (*On the Mode of Existence of Technical Objects*) — on how technical objects carry a logic that exceeds their intended use
- **Jacques Derrida** — hauntology, the "non-full" presence
- **Avery Gordon** (*Ghostly Matters*) — on haunting as a sociological category; dis/continuity of time as politically affective

**Key argument synthesis:** ML inherits the logic of 19th-century racial statistics (Francis Galton, Karl Pearson, eugenics) because it inherits the same epistemological framework — the reduction of bodies to measurable features for classification. The "Black technical object" names a site of double inscription: the body colonised by data, and the latent counter-technical knowledge that survives that colonisation.

## Venue Choice Analysis

Amaro's publication choices are telling. **Sternberg Press** (not MIT Press, not Bloomsbury) puts him squarely in the art-world-critical-theory space rather than the social science or STS space — he's talking to designers, artists, curators, as well as scholars. **e-flux** confirms this: it's the flagship journal of contemporary art discourse, not a peer-reviewed academic publication. He is deliberately addressing the art/design field from within a rigorous Black studies / philosophy framework.

His Goldsmiths affiliation (Visual Cultures, not Computer Science) signals the epistemic frame: this is cultural theory engaging technology, not the other way around. His move to DAE is the culmination of this: bringing critical race/technology theory into the heart of European design education.
