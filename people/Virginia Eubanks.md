# Virginia Eubanks

Political scientist and author known for rigorous empirical documentation of how automated systems harm poor and working-class communities. *Automating Inequality* (2018) studied three cases — Indiana welfare automation, Los Angeles homeless services, Pennsylvania child welfare prediction — showing how algorithmic governance disciplines and punishes the poor in ways invisible to the middle class.

## Domains
- Algorithmic harm and inequality
- Welfare, criminal justice, and social services automation
- Participatory research methods
- Technology and poverty

## Key Works
- [*Automating Inequality: How High-Tech Tools Profile, Police, and Punish the Poor*](https://us.macmillan.com/books/9781250074317/automatinginequality) (St. Martin's Press, 2018) — essential empirical case studies. More grounded than Noble, more policy-oriented than Crawford.
- [*Digital Dead End: Fighting for Social Justice in the Information Age*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/digital-dead-end) (MIT Press, 2011) — earlier work on technology and marginalized communities.

## Why Interesting
Her ethnographic, participatory methods provide a model for studying algorithmic harm in situ — not through abstract critique but through direct research with affected communities. Connects to participatory design tradition Amber is interested in. Relevant to [[Algorithmic Governance]], [[Data Feminism]].
