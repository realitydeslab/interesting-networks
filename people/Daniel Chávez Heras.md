# Daniel Chávez Heras

Lecturer in Digital Culture and Creative Computing at the **[Department of Digital Humanities, King's College London](https://www.kcl.ac.uk/digital-humanities)**. Mexican-born researcher who bridges the gap between film theory and computational practice with unusual depth — he both theorises machine vision and builds tools that deploy it. Core member of the **[[Creative AI Lab]]** (KCL + Serpentine Galleries). Author of *[Cinema and Machine Vision](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-cinema-and-machine-vision.html)* (Edinburgh University Press, 2024). His research orbits a central provocation: when machines become spectators, what do they actually see — and does it matter aesthetically, politically, epistemologically?

His job title encodes his research programme: "Digital Culture *and* Creative Computing" — the "and" doing the work of insisting that these domains are neither separate nor easily reconciled. He built one of the BBC's earliest AI documentaries (seen by half a million viewers), ran Mexico's first online film festival, and is currently leading a BFI-funded national project to rethink how UK film archives engage with AI.

## Key Projects

### [ISSA — Intelligent Systems for Screen Archives](https://movingpixel.net/Projects/ISSA) (2025–)
Funded by the **BFI Innovation Challenge Fund** (National Lottery). Brings together five UK film and television archive partners across four nations: **National Library of Scotland**, **National Library of Wales**, **Northern Ireland Screen**, **North West Film Archive**, and **Yorkshire Film Archive**, in collaboration with **King's Digital Lab**. Aim: develop the knowledge, tools, and skills for rethinking large audiovisual collections from a computational perspective — critically and technically. Currently active. Presented at Fantastic Futures 2025 (British Library). Includes experimental **DEERIN prototypes** — AI tools for screen archive analysis.

### [Made by Machine](https://movingpixel.net/Projects/Made-by-Machine) (2018, BBC R&D)
Invited to join a small team of developers, engineers and producers at **BBC R&D** to create "the world's first broadcast AI-TV programme." Developed four computational techniques to browse the BBC television archive and automatically concatenate new sequences of clips. Result: *Made by Machine: When AI Met the Archive*, broadcast on **BBC Four** (2018), seen by **nearly half a million people** in the UK. Nominated for a **Broadcast Tech Innovation Award**. He has since written extensively about this project in *Cinema and Machine Vision*.

### [Creanalytics](https://movingpixel.net/Projects/Creanalytics) (2022–2023)
A portmanteau of *creative* + *analytic*. Research project developing the idea that creative and analytic methods can coexist under a unified computational framework in the age of generative AI — "generation through prediction," analogous to the analytic-synthetic distinction in philosophy. Resulted in an article in *Convergence* (2023). Developed in collaboration with **Jonathan Gray** and **Mercedes Bunz** (KCL). Commissioned **Sam Lavigne** to develop his open-source [Videogrep](https://antiboredom.github.io/videogrep/) tool for the project — computational video essays as both creative output and analytic method.

### [Sculpting Time with Computers](https://movingpixel.net/Events/Sculpting-Time-with-Computers/Sculpting-Time-With-Computers) (workshops, 2023–2024)
Annual workshop series at KCL exploring temporal dynamics in large audiovisual collections. First edition: King's College London, 6–7 July 2023. Work on a major Soviet newsreel collection (1944–1992). Participants included: Andrea Farina, Belén Vidal, Carlo Bretti, Isadora Campregher, Jake Berger, Joel McKim, Mila Oiva, Nanne van Noord, Pauline van Mourik Broekman, Stephen McConnachie, Tom Brown. Results presented at EYE Museum International Film Conference (May 2024, Amsterdam). Second edition in 2024.

### [FrameSense](https://movingpixel.net/Projects/FrameSense) (ongoing)
A film analysis tool built with **Geoffroy Noël** at King's Digital Lab. Part of the broader infrastructure for computational moving image analysis.

### [FOFA](https://movingpixel.net/Projects/FOFA) (2016)
Festival Online Futuros Astimétricos — Mexico's first online short film festival. Animation, sound-art, net-art, and narrative short films from 20+ countries. 90 selected pieces, 18 international conferences. Directed this student-led project at UAEMex. Pre-figures current thinking about online-first cultural distribution.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Daniel moves between **four overlapping circuits**:

1. **UK digital heritage / national archives**: BFI, British Library (AI4LAM), King's Digital Lab, Yorkshire/NW/Northern Ireland/National Libraries — the institutional heritage infrastructure circuit. His ISSA project makes him central here.

2. **European media art / media theory**: [Transmediale](https://transmediale.de/) (Berlin, multiple panels), [EYE Museum](https://www.eyefilm.nl/) (Amsterdam), IFilNova (Lisbon) — the critical media art + film scholarship circuit. Transmediale is the most significant European media arts festival/conference for critical digital practice; having two distinct panel invitations there indicates genuine standing.

3. **European academic computational humanities**: CUDAN (Tallinn), Aarhus University (SHAPE), PPIG — the computational humanities academic circuit, distinct from media art.

4. **Mexico / Latin America**: UAEMex (early career), Ibero (Mexico City, 2025) — a circuit maintained across career stages, suggesting an ongoing responsibility to his origin context.

The absence from purely tech industry venues (no NeurIPS, no Google, no industry AI conferences) is significant: he's in the cultural institutions × academic × media art circuit, not the AI industry circuit.

## Research Taste Analysis

**The problem he's working on**: What does it mean for an AI to be a *spectator* — not just a detector or classifier, but an entity that "watches" films in a way that has aesthetic, historical, and political weight? And if spectatorship has always been theorized as a human-centric act (from Baudry to Mulvey to Metz), what happens to film theory when the spectator is a machine?

**Why this matters beyond film studies**: This is actually a question about the epistemology of machine vision at large — but he approaches it from the richest possible theoretical tradition (cinema studies, psychoanalysis, phenomenology, aesthetics) rather than from within computer vision itself. The result is a genuinely different set of questions: not "how accurate is the model?" but "what does accuracy even mean when applied to visual culture?"

**What makes him methodologically distinctive**:
- He codes. FrameSense, the focal length classifier, the supercut generator — these are real tools that require technical fluency. This isn't humanities scholars occasionally using Python; it's someone with actual machine learning practice.
- But he refuses to let the tools be the research. The tools are instruments for asking humanistic questions. "Critical technical practice" (from Phil Agre) is his governing concept.
- His CUDAN fellowship put him in a genuine science-of-culture environment where quantitative work was expected. He learned to work at scale (70,000 Soviet newsreel clips) without abandoning aesthetic concerns.

**The conceptual range**:
- Computational Spectatorship → questions what happens at the film-AI interface
- Creanalytics → questions the creative/analytic distinction in AI tools
- Computational Ekphrasis → reframes text-to-image AI within a 2,500-year-old tradition
- The Digital Pastoral → applies the pastoral tradition (idealization of nature) to critique of "minor tech" discourse — wonderfully contrarian

**The Mexican angle**: He's one of relatively few Latin American scholars at the senior level in UK digital humanities. His early career work in Mexico — running a digital festival, publishing in Spanish, working for the British Council — gives him a different relationship to the Anglo/European canon than his UK peers. His talk title "Ojos que no ven, máquinas que no sienten" (Eyes that don't see, machines that don't feel) — a pun on the Spanish proverb — shows this code-switching fluency.

**The "and" in his job title**: "Digital Culture *and* Creative Computing" — he has described this explicitly as the key to his profile, insisting that neither term should subsume the other. This is a design choice for the self, not just a bureaucratic description.

**Network position**: He's neither purely an artist nor purely a scientist — he occupies the gap. His collaborators span: philosophers (Zylinska), data scientists (van Noord), film archivists (BFI, EYE), media artists (Jäger, Creative AI Lab), political digital culture scholars (Cox, Andersen), and activists/artist-programmers (Lavigne, de Valk). This cross-circuit positioning is rare and worth mapping.

## Why Interesting for Amber

1. **Methodological parallel**: Like Amber's research taste, Daniel insists on combining critical frameworks with actual technical practice. The "and" in his title is structurally identical to the tension Amber navigates between AI capability research and critical/social concerns.

2. **Computational Ekphrasis**: His framing of text-to-image as a new form of ekphrasis is potentially relevant to art-science synthesis questions — it recasts generative AI as part of a very long tradition of image-text relations, which changes how we evaluate and situate it culturally.

3. **The C5 model**: The Creative AI Lab's framework (Creative/Critical/Cultural/Contextual/Collaborative) is one of the more rigorous academic frameworks for evaluating AI art practice. Worth engaging with directly.

4. **ISSA as model**: His BFI-funded national archives project demonstrates how to secure institutional support for AI × cultural heritage work in the UK — relevant if Amber is thinking about how to fund / embed her work in existing cultural institutions.

5. **The Transmediale connection**: Geoff Cox's Toward Minor Tech workshop is one of the few European academic-art venues where genuinely critical perspectives on AI are developed and published. Daniel is embedded there; this circuit overlaps with Frankfurt School–adjacent media theory (the "minor tech" discourse references Agamben, Stiegler, etc.).

6. **Latin American DH**: Daniel is building a bridge between UK/European computational humanities and Mexico — his return trips to Ibero, his Spanish publications, his early work at UAEMex. If Amber is thinking about non-Western AI art scenes, his network provides a Mexican/Latin American entry point.
