# Golan Levin

New media artist, engineer, educator, and cultural provocateur. Pioneer of computational art who has spent 30+ years asking how code can be a medium for expression, play, and political critique. Known for interactive audiovisual performances, information visualization, open-source fabrication (MoMA collection), and the definitive textbook on creative coding education. Director of CMU's Frank-Ratchye STUDIO for Creative Inquiry (2009–2023). A two-time TED speaker named one of "50 Designers Shaping the Future" (Fast Company, 2012).

## Background
Born 1972 in New York City. Designed his own undergraduate degree at MIT in 1994 — combining fine arts, computer science, architectural design, electronic music, video/animation, philosophy, cultural anthropology, and ethnomusicology. The self-designed degree is a signal: Levin has always been constitutionally cross-disciplinary.

Between his two MIT degrees, spent four years (1994–1998) at **[Interval Research Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_Research_Corporation)** in Palo Alto — Paul Allen's research lab. There he met **[Michael Naimark](http://www.naimark.net/)**, **[Brenda Laurel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brenda_Laurel)**, and **[Scott Snibbe](http://www.snibbe.com/)**, who introduced him to interactive new media art. This corporate R&D period is crucial — it gave him engineering rigor and an industry perspective unusual among art-world figures.

Returned to MIT for MS in Media Arts and Sciences (2000) under **[John Maeda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Maeda)**'s Aesthetics and Computation Group. Thesis: "Painterly Interfaces for Audiovisual Performance." Maeda's group was ground zero for computational aesthetics — [[Casey Reas]] (co-creator of Processing) also came from this environment.

**Teaching career (NYC → Pittsburgh):** After MIT, taught at Columbia University, Cooper Union, and Parsons School of Design (2000–2003). Joined Carnegie Mellon University in 2004 as Assistant Professor of Electronic Time Based Media. Promoted to Associate Professor (2007), then Professor of Electronic Art. Also holds courtesy appointments in School of Computer Science, School of Design, School of Architecture, and Entertainment Technology Center.

**STUDIO for Creative Inquiry (2009–2023):** Served as Director/Co-Director of CMU's [Frank-Ratchye STUDIO for Creative Inquiry](https://studioforcreativeinquiry.org/) — an interdisciplinary research unit for "atypical, trans-disciplinary, and inter-institutional research at the intersections of arts, science, technology and culture." Founded 1990 by Lowry Burgess. Levin led it for 14 years, making it one of the most significant art-technology labs in the US. Stepped down from directorship in 2023.

## Domains
- **Interactive audiovisual art** — systems where gesture, voice, movement generate simultaneous image and sound
- **Information visualization as critical inquiry** — making data visible to reveal power structures and social patterns
- **Computer vision and gesture** — early pioneer of CV as artistic medium
- **Open-source fabrication and design** — tactical use of 3D printing for political commentary

## Key Works

### Landmark Interactive Works
- **[Audiovisual Environment Suite (AVES)](http://acg.media.mit.edu/people/golan/aves/)** (2000) — Five interactive systems for real-time abstract animation + synthetic sound. His MIT thesis work. Award of Distinction, Prix Ars Electronica 2000.
- **[Dialtones: A Telesymphony](http://www.telesymphony.com/)** (2001) — Concert whose sounds are entirely performed through the carefully choreographed dialing and ringing of the audience's own mobile phones. One of the first artworks to use mobile phones as instruments at scale. Performed at Ars Electronica 2001. CD released by Ars Electronica Center (Staalplaat Records, 2002).
- **[The Secret Lives of Numbers](http://www.turbulence.org/Works/nums/)** (2002) — Information visualization revealing the relative popularity of every integer from 0–1,000,000 across the internet. Classic early data art piece.
- **[Re:MARK](http://flong.com/archive/projects/remark/index.html)** (2002) — Collaborative with Zachary Lieberman. Augmented reality that makes handwriting visible as interactive graphic forms.
- **[Messa di Voce](http://www.tmema.org/messa/)** (2003) — Performance and installation (with [[Zachary Lieberman]] as Tmema). Real-time speech visualization — everything said by performers is rendered as animated graphic forms. Performed at Ars Electronica, Royal Festival Hall London, Tate Modern, ICA London, The Kitchen NYC, Whitney Biennial. Award of Distinction, Prix Ars Electronica 2003 (Net Vision).
- **[The Manual Input Sessions](http://www.tmema.org/mis)** (2004) — Interactive installation exploring gestural vocabularies of hand-on-screen. Performed at The Kitchen NYC, Ars Electronica, RomaEuropa Festival.
- **[The Dumpster](http://artport.whitney.org/commissions/thedumpster/dumpster.shtml)** (2006) — Whitney Artport commission. Visualization of 20,000 blog posts about teen breakups — showing patterns in how young people express heartbreak online.
- **[Scrapple](http://flong.com/archive/projects/scrapple/index.html)** (2005) — Tabletop augmented-reality interface for spectrographic performance. Paper: "The Table is The Score" (ICMC 2006).
- **[Ursonography](http://flong.com/archive/projects/ursonography/index.html)** (2005) — With sound poet Jaap Blonk. Performed at Ultrasound (UK), RomaEuropa (Rome), Ars Electronica, Tokyo Sound Poetry Festival.
- **[Opto-Isolator](http://flong.com/archive/projects/optoisolator/index.html)** (2007) — Robotic eye that stares back at viewers. Explores gaze as mode of human-machine communication. Appeared in *Decode* at V&A.
- **[Double-Taker (Snout)](http://flong.com/archive/projects/snout/index.html)** (2008) — Interactive robotic installation exploring machine looking.
- **[Augmented Hand Series](http://flong.com/archive/projects/augmented-hand-series/index.html)** (2014) — Real-time system presenting dreamlike transformations of visitors' hands. Exhibited: Ars Electronica 2015, La Gaîté Lyrique Paris, Cinekid Amsterdam, Saxon Museum of Industry Chemnitz. Honorable Mention, Computer Animation, Prix Ars Electronica 2015.
- **[Terrapattern](http://flong.com/archive/projects/terrapattern/index.html)** (2016) — Open-source tool for visual query-by-example in satellite imagery using ML. Exhibited at Art Center NABI Seoul, Robotronica Brisbane. "Terrapattern: Machine Learning, Visual Culture and Geospatial Imaging" (alt-AI Conference, SFPC, 2016).
- **[Ghost Pole Propagator II](http://flong.com/archive/projects/gpp-ii/index.html)** (2016) — Exhibited at Day for Night Festival, Houston.

### Open-Source / Design Works
- **[Free Universal Construction Kit (F.U.C.K.)](http://flong.com/archive/projects/free-universal-construction-kit/index.html)** (2012, with Shawn Sims) — Open-source 3D-printable adapters connecting 10 incompatible toy construction systems (LEGO, Duplo, K'Nex, Lincoln Logs, Tinkertoys, etc.). Reverse-engineered with micrometre precision. **Permanent collection: MoMA + Cooper Hewitt Smithsonian Design Museum.** Named #1 Most Memorable Project of 2012 (Creative Applications). Included in MoMA's 2025 *Pirouette: Turning Points in Design* exhibition. "A grassroots interoperability remedy that implements proprietary protocols to provide a public service unmet by corporate interests." The conceptual politics: toy systems as proprietary ecosystems; the hack as civic act.
- **[NeoLucida](http://flong.com/archive/projects/neolucida/index.html)** (2013) — Affordable camera lucida revival for sketching. Core77 Design Award Winner, DIY Category. Presented at XOXO Conference 2014, Cooper Hewitt talk 2015.
- **[Moon Drawings](http://flong.com/archive/projects/moon-drawings/index.html)** (2015) — Exhibited at Meta.Morf Biennale, Trondheim.

### Educational Works
- **[Code as Creative Medium](https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/code-creative-medium)** (MIT Press, 2021, with [[Tega Brain]]) — Definitive textbook for teaching creative coding in art/design contexts. Organized by concept (iteration, randomness, visualization, etc.) with hands-on exercises. Has become a standard text in university creative computing courses.
- **[golancourses.net](http://golancourses.net/)** — Archive of all courses taught at CMU since 2004: Interactivity and Computation, Experimental Capture, Drawing with Machines, Foundations: Digital Media.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Levin moves through **three distinct circuits** simultaneously:

**1. Media Art Festival Circuit (European core):**
Ars Electronica (Linz) → ZKM (Karlsruhe) → Transmediale (Berlin) → NTT ICC (Tokyo) → Eyebeam (NYC)
This is the institutional spine of new media art — he's been embedded here since 2000.

**2. Design / MoMA-Adjacent Circuit:**
MoMA → Cooper Hewitt → Design Museum London → Bozar Brussels → Museu del Disseny Barcelona → Core77 Awards
The Free Universal Construction Kit was his crossover moment — it moved him from "media artist" into "designer" territory.

**3. Academic / Teaching Circuit:**
CMU → MIT Media Lab → Parsons → Columbia → Cooper Union → NAEA → SECAC → Eyeo Festival → ART && CODE
He's built pedagogical infrastructure — not just making work but training the next generation of computational artists.

Notable: Levin deliberately chose **Pittsburgh** over NYC or the coasts when he joined CMU in 2004. This allowed him to build a 14-year lab directorship and an influential teaching program outside the gallery market. It's a strategic bet on institutions over markets.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

Golan Levin is a **slower-burning, institution-building figure** compared to the theoretical fireworks of many ⭐⭐⭐ people in this network. His research strategy is worth studying because it shows how to build lasting influence through infrastructure rather than manifestos.

### Conceptual Strategy

**Medium-first thinking:** Levin's core intellectual move is to ask "what can computation do that nothing else can?" — specifically, what kinds of interactivity, nonverbal expression, and visualization are *intrinsically computational* rather than just computational approximations of analog things. This is a philosophical position (computation as distinct medium) that organizes his entire 30-year output.

**Two-word concept coining:** "Painterly interfaces," "audiovisual performance," "nonverbal communication protocols" — he consistently names the territory he's working in before others have a term for it. This is field-clearing work.

**Provocation through precision:** F.U.C.K. works because the name is aggressive AND the engineering is perfect. The conceptual and craft layers reinforce each other. He doesn't choose between rigor and politics.

### Career Architecture

**Platform building over solo career:** While building his own art practice, he simultaneously built the STUDIO for Creative Inquiry (a lab), ART && CODE (a symposium), dorkbot Pittsburgh (a community), and co-wrote the canonical textbook. He's creating the infrastructure for a field, not just participating in it.

**Pedagogical investment as influence:** His courses (golancourses.net, 2004–present) have trained hundreds of artists who now credit him. The textbook (*Code as Creative Medium*) reaches thousands more. Teaching is his longest-lasting contribution.

**The CMU bet:** Choosing Pittsburgh over NYC in 2004 was strategic non-conformism. He got a stable faculty position + lab directorship + the freedom to do politically provocative work without gallery market pressures. NYC would have absorbed him into the art market; Pittsburgh let him build an institution.

### Methodology Signature

**Engineering + concept simultaneously:** Every project is engineered to a standard most artists don't reach (micrometre precision on toy connectors; real-time computer vision in 2003). The technical quality amplifies the conceptual content.

**Open-source as practice:** He releases code, shares techniques via lectures, and publishes work at SIGGRAPH/CHI/NIME alongside art venues. He lives in both worlds (academic publication AND gallery exhibition) without compromising either.

**Residency as studio:** Multiple Ars Electronica Futurelab residencies (2002–2005) used industrial R&D infrastructure for art production. He imports corporate research tools into art contexts.

### Network Strategy

**Maeda lineage:** His positioning as a Maeda student at MIT's ACG means he's networked into a generation of foundational computational artists (Casey Reas, Ben Fry, Processing's origin story). This affiliation gives intellectual credibility.

**Cross-venue presence:** He exhibits at art institutions (Whitney, MoMA, ZKM) AND presents at engineering conferences (CHI, NIME, SIGGRAPH) AND teaches at universities AND speaks at TED. No single circuit owns him.

**Tmema collaboration:** His 5-year collaboration with Zachary Lieberman as "Tmema" was his most productive period. Lieberman went on to co-create openFrameworks, co-found SFPC. The collaboration seeded the creative coding community.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **Self-designed MIT undergraduate degree (1990–1994):** Refused to choose between art and computer science. Built the interdisciplinary identity before "interdisciplinary" was fashionable. This enabled everything.

2. **Interval Research Corporation (1994–1998):** Took the "corporate detour" and emerged with patents, engineering contacts, and a clear sense of what industry couldn't do (make art that matters). The corporate R&D period made him more rigorous, not less artistic.

3. **MIT Media Lab / Maeda ACG (1998–2000):** Positioned himself within the most important computational aesthetics research group of the era. His thesis launched AVES, which won Prix Ars Electronica 2000 immediately.

4. **Tmema collaboration with Lieberman (2002–2007):** Rather than solo career, formed a duo that produced the most significant interactive artworks of the period (Messa di Voce, Manual Input Sessions). The collaboration created more impact than either could alone.

5. **CMU / STUDIO directorship (2004–2023):** Turned down bigger coastal markets to build institutional infrastructure. Ran the STUDIO for 14 years, funding and hosting dozens of projects that wouldn't have existed otherwise.

6. **Free Universal Construction Kit (2012):** His most culturally viral work. Open-source political design — MoMA collection within a year. Crossover from "media artist" to "designer activist." The name was strategic provocation.
