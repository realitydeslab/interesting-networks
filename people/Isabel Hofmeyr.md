# Isabel Hofmeyr

Professor Emeritus at Wits/WISER and Distinguished Scholar in Residence at New York University. Pioneer of oceanic humanities, Indian Ocean literary and cultural history, and now elemental humanities. Closest scholarly collaborator of [[Sarah Nuttall]]; together they co-convene the three WISER forum series (Heated Conversations, Breathing In, Plant Lives).

## Background
Over three decades, she pioneered research on global, oceanic, and transnational forms of literary and cultural history that seek to understand Africa's place in the world. Was previously Professor of African Literature at Wits and Global Distinguished Professor at NYU. Co-directed the Oceanic Humanities project with Charne Lavery at WISER for 5 years.

## Key Works
- *The Portable Bunyan: A Transnational History of The Pilgrim's Progress* (1994) — foundational text in transnational literary history
- *Gandhi's Printing Press: Experiments in Slow Reading* (2013) — reading practices and political formation in colonial India/South Africa
- *Ten Books that Shaped the British Empire* (co-edited with Antoinette Burton, 2015)
- *Dockside Reading: Hydrocolonialism and the Custom House* (2022) — hydrocolonialism as a framework for reading water and colonial infrastructure
- "Reading for Water" — *Interventions* 24 (2022): 303-322 (with Sarah Nuttall and Charne Lavery) — the elemental methodology article

## Why Interesting
Hofmeyr's "hydrocolonialism" concept — treating water as both a material and epistemological medium of colonial power — is the foundation that Nuttall's elemental humanities builds on. For Amber: data centers, undersea cables, and cloud infrastructure are forms of "techno-colonialism" that could benefit from a hydrocolonialism-style analysis. Her work on reading practices (slow reading, reading from the South) also speaks to Amber's interest in non-Western epistemologies.
