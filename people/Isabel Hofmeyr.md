# Isabel Hofmeyr

Professor Emeritus at Wits University (WISER) and Distinguished Scholar in Residence at NYU. Pioneer of oceanic, transnational, and hydrocolonial literary history. Coined "hydrocolonialism" — the study of how colonial power flows through water, ports, and custom houses. Three decades of work reframing how Africa is understood globally through oceanic and transnational cultural flows. Co-directed the [Oceanic Humanities for the Global South](https://www.oceanichumanities.com/) project with Charne Lavery.

## Background

Christine Isabel Hofmeyr was born in 1953 in Potchefstroom in the former Transvaal Province of South Africa. Her first degree (1974) was a Bachelor of Journalism at [Rhodes University](https://www.ru.ac.za/) — a print-media foundation that would shape her career-long attention to materiality and media. After a year of clerical work in London, she returned to Wits for a BA Hons in English (1976), then an MA (1979) on mining, social change, and literature in South African fiction. She then took a two-year MA at the [School of Oriental and African Studies (SOAS)](https://www.soas.ac.uk/), London on a British Council scholarship, studying Area Studies with an African concentration.

In 1979 she worked as junior lecturer at the University of Durban-Westville, then left to join the **South African Committee for Higher Education (SACHED)** — a non-profit anti-apartheid organization where she co-wrote adult education materials on African studies (1980–1982). This anti-apartheid period shapes her commitment to the Global South as a perspective, not just a geography.

She returned to Wits in 1984 as a tutor in African Literature, became a lecturer in 1988, completed her PhD in 1991 (on oral historical narrative in a South African chiefdom), and was promoted to Professor in 1994 — then the youngest full professor in her faculty. She served as head of the African Studies department and assistant dean in the Wits Humanities Faculty, and was chairperson of the Wits University Press advisory committee (1997–2001).

From 2013 to 2021 she was Global Distinguished Professor at NYU (English department). She is now Professor Emeritus at Wits, based at [[WISER]], and Distinguished Scholar in Residence at NYU.

## Key Works

### Books
- [*We Spend Our Years as a Tale that is Told: Oral Historical Narrative in a South African Chiefdom*](https://witspress.co.za/) (Wits University Press, 1993) — adapted from PhD; explores oral tradition and literacy in a community facing colonial massacre. Shortlisted for the **1995 [Herskovits Prize](https://www.africanstudies.org/awards-and-prizes/herskovits-award/)** (African Studies Association, USA).
- [*The Portable Bunyan: A Transnational History of The Pilgrim's Progress*](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691120928/the-portable-bunyan) (Princeton University Press, 2004) — how John Bunyan's *Pilgrim's Progress* transformed as it traveled through African Protestant missions. Won the **International John Bunyan Society's 2007 [Richard L. Greaves Prize](https://www.bunyan.org.uk/)**.
- [*Gandhi's Printing Press: Experiments in Slow Reading*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/books/9780674072770) (Harvard University Press, 2013) — the material practices of the press at Gandhi's Phoenix ashram in Natal; how printing supported satyagraha philosophy.
- [*Ten Books that Shaped the British Empire: Creating an Imperial Commons*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/ten-books-that-shaped-the-british-empire) (co-edited with Antoinette Burton, Duke University Press, 2014) — print culture as a lens for empire.
- [*Dockside Reading: Hydrocolonialism and the Custom House*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/dockside-reading) (Duke University Press, 2022) — **the definitive hydrocolonialism text**. Tracks printed matter from ship to shore through colonial Custom Houses. Shows how copyright and censorship were shaped by colonial control of coastal waters. Also published by Wits University Press. Won the **2023 [NIHSS Best Non-Fiction Monograph Award](https://www.nihss.ac.za/)**.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Hofmeyr works primarily in **three circuits**:

1. **South African institutional circuit**: WISER/Wits (intellectual home), Wits University Press (publishing home), NIHSS awards. Johannesburg as base.

2. **Transatlantic academic circuit**: Princeton UP, Harvard UP, Duke UP (three of the most prestigious US academic presses — each book at a different one). NYU as US anchor (2013–2021). Yale, Chicago, London journals review her work. This is a career strategy: each book at a different top-tier press signals independence from any single institutional house.

3. **Global South / oceanic humanities circuit**: CODESRIA (African social sciences), Indian Ocean studies networks, Oceanic Humanities for the Global South platform. This is the circuit she's *building*, not just joining.

She deliberately avoids the European theory mainstream (no Verso, no Frankfurt School adjacency) and instead constructs a **Southern intellectual axis** — Johannesburg–Mumbai–London–New York. Print culture as the method; oceans as the geography.

The choice of the custom house and the dock — not the library, not the archive — as literary site is itself a venue choice: it forces encounter with colonial logistics, trade, and environment. Methodological venue selection as political statement.

## Why Interesting

Hofmeyr is interesting to Amber for a specific reason: she's doing **elemental humanities** — treating water, air, and plants as political and theoretical objects — at the same time as Amber is interested in more-than-human computing. They're approaching the same question from different disciplines.

*Hydrocolonialism* specifically: the argument that colonial power doesn't just travel on land but through water, through the control of ports and ships and what gets through customs. This maps onto Amber's interest in protocol studies and infrastructure — the argument that power lives in the boring middle layers, not the visible endpoints. The custom house = the TCP/IP stack.

Hofmeyr's career also demonstrates a model for building a scholarly life that is simultaneously rigorous and politically committed, without becoming purely activist or purely academic. She stayed institutionally anchored (Wits for 40 years) while building global networks (NYU, Duke, Princeton, Harvard). ## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy
**Long-game concept development**: Hofmeyr doesn't invent a concept and move on. "Hydrocolonialism" was seeded in *Gandhi's Printing Press* (the observation that Gandhi opposed copyright as private property → led to customs → led to water) and fully elaborated in *Dockside Reading* a decade later. Each book drills a new facet of the same question: *how does power travel through media?*

**Bureaucratic turn as method**: She consistently takes overlooked, "boring" institutions — the missionary press, the printing shop, the custom house — and runs critical theory through them. The custom house becomes the most politically interesting space in the empire. This is methodological judo: the less glamorous the site, the more explosive the theoretical yield.

**Two-word concept coining**: "Hydrocolonialism" is her most citeable move. Memorable, self-explaining, generates a whole research programme. Compare to "necropolitics" (Mbembe) or "entanglement" (Nuttall). She's learned from her colleagues.

### Career Architecture
**Press diversification**: Each of her four major monographs was published by a different press (Wits UP, Princeton UP, Harvard UP, Duke UP). This is deliberate — it signals independence, builds different audiences, and avoids capture by any single editorial culture. Each press brings a different readership and legitimacy.

**South-anchored globalism**: She stayed at Wits for 40 years (not typical in academic humanities), while building a parallel US presence (NYU 2013–2021). This dual anchoring gave her both the institutional security of a home institution and the global visibility of a US research university. The lesson: *don't choose between local and global — hold both.*

**Platform building**: The Oceanic Humanities for the Global South website is infrastructure, not just research output. It's a node in a network she's actively constructing — connecting artists, scientists, scholars working on ocean/environment/decolonial themes across the Global South.

**Field declaration**: The phrase "Oceanic Humanities for the Global South" is a field declaration, not just a project title. She's saying: *this discipline doesn't exist yet, and we're going to build it from Johannesburg, not from London or New York.*

### Key Moves (Chronological)
1. **1979-1982: Anti-apartheid NGO work** — Grounded her in social commitment before academic career. Not an ivory tower story.
2. **1991-1993: Oral history PhD → book** — Established her as a Wits-rooted, African-literature scholar with serious archival rigor.
3. **2004: The Portable Bunyan (Princeton UP)** — First major international press publication; the transnational move that put her on the global map. Moving from *oral* to *textual circulation*.
4. **2009: Acting Director, CISA** — Strategic institutional move into Indian Ocean Studies, establishing Africa-India axis.
5. **2013-2021: NYU Global Distinguished Professorship** — US anchor built during prime career years. Massive citation exposure, transatlantic network construction.
6. **2013: Gandhi's Printing Press (Harvard UP)** — Showed she could write for the most demanding US audience and win.
7. **2017: ASSAf Fellowship** — South African recognition; institutional validation in home country simultaneous with global career.
8. **2022: Dockside Reading (Duke UP)** — Culminating work introducing the hydrocolonialism concept at full scale. The book reviewers include Sunil Amrith (Yale), Leah Price — elite placement.
9. **2023: Festschrift published while still active** — *Reading from the South* is a festschrift before retirement, edited by collaborators. This is rare and signals the field has crystallized around her.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **Stay home, go everywhere.** Hofmeyr didn't leave Johannesburg for Oxford or New York. She built the world's interest in coming to her. Reality Design Lab in Oxford is the anchor — make Oxford the node, not the suburb.

2. **Coin your concept early, develop it slowly.** "Hydrocolonialism" took 30 years to fully emerge. Amber could do the same with "agent ethology" or "machine death" — plant the seed now, let each project drill a new angle, let the book be the culmination.

3. **Use boring bureaucratic artifacts.** The custom house is boring. The rate-limit, the API log, the permission system — also boring. Theoretical analysis of boring infrastructure is how you find the most important arguments.

4. **Build the platform, not just the research.** Oceanichumanities.com is a research infrastructure, not just a website. Amber's Reality Design Lab could develop similar infrastructure — a platform for agent ethology that connects artists, scientists, and scholars globally.

5. **Diversify your publishers.** Each book at a different top press. Don't get captured by a single editorial culture or readership.
