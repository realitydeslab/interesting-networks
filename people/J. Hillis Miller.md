# J. Hillis Miller

Distinguished Research Professor Emerita of Comparative Literature and English at **UC Irvine**. One of the central figures of **American deconstruction** — a member of the Yale School (with Paul de Man, Harold Bloom, Geoffrey Hartman, Jacques Derrida). Author of dozens of books on Victorian fiction, narrative, literature and ethics. In his late career, co-authored with [[Tom Cohen]] and [[Claire Colebrook]] on theory and the Anthropocene.

## Domains
- Deconstruction and literary theory (Yale School)
- Narrative theory
- Victorian fiction (Dickens, Hardy, Eliot)
- Literature and ethics
- Derrida scholarship
- Late work: Anthropocene, disappearing future

## Key Relationships
- **Paul de Man** — closest intellectual associate; together at Yale, co-founders of American deconstruction
- **Jacques Derrida** — long collaboration; *For Derrida* (2009)
- **[[Tom Cohen]]** — late-career collaborator; *Theory and the Disappearing Future* + *Twilight*
- **[[Claire Colebrook]]** — late-career collaborator; same two books

## Why Interesting
Miller brings the full weight of Yale School deconstruction — the most rigorous version of literary critical theory in 20th century America — to the question of the Anthropocene. His collaboration with Cohen and Colebrook represents a kind of passing of the torch from classical deconstruction to extinction studies. *For Derrida* is a beautiful late work. The *Twilight* book is more urgent than most contemporary climate theory because it asks: what does de Man's rhetorical reading teach us about how we talk about climate?
