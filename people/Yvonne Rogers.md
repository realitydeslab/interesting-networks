# Yvonne Rogers

FRS. Director of [[UCL Interaction Centre]] (UCLIC) and Professor of Interaction Design at UCL. Co-author of *Interaction Design: Beyond Human-Computer Interaction* — the most widely used HCI textbook on the planet, 300,000+ copies sold. Coined "research in the wild" — a methodology that transformed how HCI studies technology deployment in real-world contexts. Fellow of the Royal Society (2022), ACM Fellow, CHI Academy member. Most cited HCI researcher in the UK. Over 350 publications.

## Background

Yvonne Rogers grew up in Wales and took a BA in Psychology at the University of Wales (1982), followed by an MSc in Ergonomics at UCL (1983) — the same institution she now directs. Her PhD (1988, also University of Wales) was titled "Icon Design for the User Interface" — a signal of where computing was then: figuring out how to make GUIs comprehensible to humans.

Her career moved through the major UK CS institutions in succession: Sussex University (1992–2003), Indiana University in Bloomington (2003–2006), the Open University (2006–2011, where she directed the Pervasive Interaction Lab), and then UCL from 2011, where she has been Director of UCLIC ever since. This is a trajectory through the full range of UK (and briefly US) academic environments, building a network that spans all of them.

The deep pattern: she is a *field-builder* who works at the intersection of cognitive science and computing, repeatedly reframing what HCI is and what it could be. The textbook is the most visible instance: it replaced the old "human factors in computing" frame with "interaction design" — a shift that opened the door to design thinking, social science, and embodied cognition.

Her other defining move: the shift from lab-based usability testing to "research in the wild" — studying technology in actual contexts of use. This was a methodological declaration and also a political one: HCI needed ecological validity, not just controlled experiments.

## Domains

- **Ubiquitous computing** — extending computing beyond the desktop into everyday environments, objects, bodies
- **Research in the Wild** — in-situ technology deployment and ethnographic study methodology
- **Distributed cognition / external cognition** — how artifacts and environments support thinking (with Mike Scaife)
- **Human-Robot Interaction / AI interfaces** — more recent work on novel interaction paradigms for AI systems
- **Public displays and ambient interfaces** — interactive displays in public spaces, behavioral nudging through ambient technology
- **Embodied learning / educational technology** — physical and digital augmentation for learning outdoors and in schools
- **Behavioral change through technology** — ambient nudging (stairs vs elevator, energy use visualization)
- **HCI theory** — meta-level work on how theories, frameworks, and visions shape the field

## Key Works

### Books
- [*Interaction Design: Beyond Human-Computer Interaction*](https://www.wiley.com/en-gb/Interaction+Design%3A+Beyond+Human-Computer+Interaction-p-9781119901143) (with Helen Sharp & Jenny Preece, Wiley, 6th ed. 2023) — **the** HCI textbook. 300,000+ copies. Translated into Chinese, Korean, Portuguese, and more. Defines HCI education globally. The shift from "human factors" to "interaction design" in the title is itself a political act — it opened HCI to design practice, user experience, and social science.
- [*Research in the Wild*](https://books.google.com/books?id=_nObDgAAQBAJ) (with Paul Marshall, Morgan & Claypool, 2017) — methodological manifesto for in-situ HCI research. Documents the philosophical stance and practical tools for studying technology where it actually gets used, not in labs.
- [*HCI Theory: Classical, Modern, and Contemporary*](https://books.google.com/books?id=XpoUtAEACAAJ) (Morgan & Claypool, 2012) — intellectual history and analysis of HCI theory. Maps how the field thinks.
- *Human-Computer Interaction* (with Preece, Sharp, Benyon et al., Pearson, 1994) — the original textbook, 5,000+ citations.

### Key Papers
- ["External Cognition: How do graphical representations work?"](https://doi.org/10.1006/ijhc.1996.0048) (with Mike Scaife, *International Journal of Human-Computer Studies*, 1996) — 1,755+ citations. Foundational theory of how external representations (diagrams, maps, visualizations) support and extend cognition. Directly influenced HCI's move toward understanding representations as thinking tools, not just displays.
- ["Moving on from Weiser's Vision of Calm Computing: Engaging UbiComp Experiences"](https://doi.org/10.1007/11853565_24) (UbiComp 2006) — 679+ citations. Critical intervention: challenges Mark Weiser's "invisible computer" vision, argues UbiComp should engage and provoke rather than disappear.
- ["Enticing people to interact with large public displays in public spaces"](https://doi.org/10.1145/964696.964714) (with Harry Brignull, INTERACT 2003) — 960+ citations. Foundational work on large public displays.
- ["Distributed cognition: an alternative framework for analysing and explaining collaborative working"](https://doi.org/10.1057/jit.1994.12) (with Judi Ellis, *Journal of Information Technology*, 1994) — 534+ citations. Applied Hutchins' distributed cognition to collaborative work.
- ["Designing for or designing with? Informant design for interactive learning environments"](https://doi.org/10.1145/258549.258789) (with Scaife, Aldrich, Davies, CHI 1997) — 588+ citations. Participatory design methodology for educational tech.
- ["Ambient Wood: designing new forms of digital augmentation for learning outdoors"](https://doi.org/10.1145/1017833.1017873) (with Price, Fitzpatrick, et al., IDC 2004) — 430+ citations. Children using wireless probes to explore biological processes in a forest. Landmark in-the-wild educational technology deployment.
- "New Theoretical Approaches for Human-Computer Interaction" (*Annual Review of Information Science and Technology*, 2003) — 699+ citations.
- "Human-Computer Interaction in the Year 2020" (with Harper, Rodden, Sellen, Microsoft Research, 2008) — the "Being Human: HCI in 2020" manifesto.

## Key Projects

### Landmark Projects
- **[Equator Project](http://www.equator.ac.uk/)** (2000–2007) — Major UK EPSRC interdisciplinary collaboration on physical/digital boundaries. Rogers was a Principal Investigator. Projects ranged from mixed reality games to children's learning. Included Ambient Wood (below). Brought together UCL, Nottingham, Bristol, Glasgow, Southampton. One of the defining UK HCI research programmes of the 2000s.
- **[Ambient Wood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_Wood)** (2004) — Children equipped with handheld wireless probes exploring a forest, collecting data on organisms, soil, light, triggering audio/video responses via probes. Landmark in-the-wild educational technology deployment. Showed that digitally-augmented outdoor learning produced qualitatively different exploration than classroom learning.
- **ICRI Cities** (Intel Research Collaborative Research Institute) — Rogers was PI. Urban informatics, community data visualization, smart city interaction.
- **[Visualising Mill Road](https://visualisingmillroad.com/)** — Community data collection project visualized as street art in Cambridge. Participatory data visualization in public space.
- **[Tidy Street](https://www.theguardian.com/environment/blog/2011/apr/12/energy-use-households-monitor-electricity)** (featured in The Guardian) — Visualizing household energy usage on street pavement to encourage community-level efficiency. Early influential example of ambient behavioral nudging.
- **Lambent Shopping Trolley** — Ambient display clipped to supermarket trolleys to nudge buying decisions. Applied ubicomp in commercial setting.
- **AR Makeup Try-On** — Augmented reality for retail; studied how AR influences buying decisions. [Covered in Harvard Business Review](https://hbr.org/2016/04/what-marketers-need-to-understand-about-augmented-reality) (2016).
- **Staircase nudging project** — Ambient light to encourage stair use over elevators in a building. Classic ubicomp behavioral change.

## Research Taste Analysis

*How Rogers builds research — meta-strategies Amber can learn.*

### Conceptual Strategy: The Methodological Declaration
Rogers' most powerful move is declaring *how* to do research, not just reporting findings. "Research in the Wild" is not a paper; it's a paradigm shift announced in 2017 but practiced since 2000 (Equator, Ambient Wood). Her critique of Weiser (2006) is not a systems paper — it's a vision reframing. And the textbook is not a literature review — it's a curriculum construction.

**What this means:** She writes at the level of *how the field should work*, not just *what she found*. This gives her contributions a longer half-life than empirical papers. Amber should look for opportunities to make similar moves — "here is the framework for studying agentic behavior in the wild" rather than just "here are our findings."

### Career Architecture: Textbook as Infrastructure
The *Interaction Design* textbook (1994 first edition, now in 6th ed. with 300,000+ copies) is not just a book — it is *curriculum control*. Every HCI professor who assigns it is also assigning Rogers' framing of what HCI is. This is the most durable form of intellectual influence: you shape how the next generation thinks before they've done a single experiment.

**Key move:** She built the textbook infrastructure *before* the field was fully formed (1994). This is timing intelligence — you don't wait until a field is established to write its textbook; you write it early and your framing becomes the field.

**What Amber can learn:** Is there a field that Amber could similarly define? Machine behavior? Agentic interaction? HCI for AI systems? The moment of early crystallization is the moment to write the framework.

### Methodology Signature: In-Situ Over Lab
Rogers consistently chooses to study technology *where it lives*, not in controlled environments. Ambient Wood is forest-based. Tidy Street is street-based. Visualising Mill Road is community-based. This is not just methodological preference — it's a philosophical stance: real messy contexts produce real insights that lab studies miss.

**What Amber can learn:** The "in the wild" stance gives HCI a claim to ecological validity that pure engineering or lab studies lack. For Amber's AI agent work: where would agents be studied "in the wild"? In homes? In research labs? In social settings? The study design is as important as the system design.

### Network Strategy: Institution-Hopping with Intention
Rogers moved Sussex → Indiana → Open University → UCL. Each move was strategic: Sussex for the early-career community, Indiana for the US network, Open University for the Pervasive Interaction Lab directorship (first time building her own lab), UCL for maximum institutional prestige and the UCLIC directorship.

**Key insight:** She didn't stay anywhere that stopped growing her. Each move unlocked a new community, a new grant environment, or a new leadership opportunity. The directorship of UCLIC was only possible because she had built a track record of lab leadership at Open University first.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **1994: The Textbook.** *Human-Computer Interaction* with Preece, Sharp et al. Early, before HCI had a dominant textbook. Established her as a field-definer, not just a researcher.

2. **1996: External Cognition theory** (with Scaife). High-citation theoretical contribution. Established her as someone who does theory, not just empirical work. Necessary credential for becoming a senior figure.

3. **2000–2007: Equator Project leadership.** Massive multidisciplinary grant, shaped a decade of UK HCI. First "in the wild" work at scale. Built the methodology that became "Research in the Wild."

4. **2006: "Moving on from Weiser."** High-profile critique of the dominant UbiComp vision. Showed intellectual courage (challenging the founding mythology of the field) and produced a frequently-cited alternative vision.

5. **2011: UCL/UCLIC Directorship.** Institutional peak — becomes the leader of the UK's top HCI centre. Combines research leadership with institutional prestige.

6. **2017: *Research in the Wild* (with Marshall).** Methodological codification — turns years of practice into an explicit, citable framework. Creates curriculum materials for a generation of students.

7. **2022: FRS + Milner Award + SIGCHI Lifetime.** Triple recognition in a single year. Signals peak status — the field and the broader scientific community agree she is exceptional.

8. **2023: Honorary Doctorate, St Gallen.** European recognition. First CS at St Gallen = discipline-bridge achievement.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Write the framework, not just the findings.** Rogers' most-cited work is frameworks (External Cognition, Research in the Wild critique of Weiser) not empirical results. Amber should look for opportunities to write the framework paper for agentic interaction, machine behavior, or AI-human ecologies.

2. **The textbook strategy is still available.** No one has written "Agentic Interaction Design" or "Designing for Machine Behavior." These are open textbook opportunities. You don't have to write them now — but you can plant the seeds through workshop papers, position papers, and course syllabi.

3. **Timing the institutional move.** Rogers' move to UCL (from Open University) was after she had built a lab and track record. Amber is building that track record now (Oxford HCC, Max Van Kleek, HoloKit). The question is: when is the right moment to move to the institution that offers the directorship? Oxford itself might be it — or UCL, or elsewhere.

4. **Ecological validity as a differentiator.** In a field full of lab studies and simulation, the "in the wild" stance is a genuine differentiator. Amber can stake out the equivalent territory for AI agent research: studying agents in actual deployment contexts, with real users, in real environments, rather than in sandboxes.

5. **Productive critique of dominant visions.** Rogers' Weiser critique is a model: identify the founding myth of your field, show what it misses, propose an alternative. Amber's equivalent: the dominant vision of AI agents (obedient, helpful, aligned with user goals) misses something important (machine welfare, agent mortality, multispecies futures). Write that critique.
