# Kathryn Yusoff ⭐⭐⭐

> "No geology is neutral."

Professor of Inhuman Geography at Queen Mary University of London. Author of *A Billion Black Anthropocenes or None* (2018) and *Geologic Life* (2024). Co-curator of the British Pavilion at the Venice Architecture Biennale 2025 ("GBR: Geology of Britannic Repair"), which won a Special Mention for National Participation. One of the most important thinkers working at the intersection of race, geology, colonialism, and environmental humanities today.

---

## Background

Kathryn Yusoff received her PhD in Geography from **Royal Holloway University of London** (2005), where her thesis — *"Arresting Vision: A Geographical Theory of Antarctic Light"* — worked on the chrono-geographies of Antarctica. This early focus on polar regions led to her edited volume *Bipolar* (2008), a collaboration with Arts Catalyst and the British Library for International Polar Year.

She is currently **Professor of Inhuman Geography** and **Director of Graduate Studies** in the School of Geography (now School of Society and Environment, Department of Geography and Environmental Science) at QMUL. She is also **Co-Director of the Forum on DeCentering the Human in Humanities and Social Sciences**.

Career trajectory: geographer → climate aesthetics → Anthropocene theory → race/geology → geologies of extraction and colonialism → architecture/built environment. Each phase deepens and radicalizes the previous.

## Domains

- **Inhuman Geography** — her coined disciplinary position: thinking from the inhuman as a place to understand earthly relations and inhumane histories
- **Geologies of Race** — how colonial earth practices and the discipline of geology co-produced racial subjugation
- **Anthropocene Politics** — questioning who the "anthro" in Anthropocene actually is; Black feminist critique of geological epoch discourse
- **Extractivism & Colonial Ecologies** — mineral extraction as ongoing racial and colonial project
- **Environmental Humanities** — interdisciplinary bridge between earth sciences, critical theory, and arts
- **Geophilosophy** — drawing on Deleuze/Guattari, Sylvia Wynter, Saidiya Hartman, Frantz Fanon, Katherine McKittrick
- **Architecture & Built Environment** — increasingly active in architecture discourse (Venice Biennale)

## Why Interesting

Kathryn Yusoff is doing something **structurally unusual**: she is a geographer who has become one of the most important thinkers in contemporary art, architecture, and critical race theory — without abandoning the rigor of earth science. Her move from Antarctic light theory → Anthropocene geopolitics → Black feminist geology → architecture biennale curator is not opportunism; it's a coherent unfolding of a single question: *who gets to be a geological subject, and at what cost?*

Her book *A Billion Black Anthropocenes or None* (2018) is arguably the most important critique of Anthropocene discourse yet written. It exposed how the geological grammar of the Anthropocene (uniformitarianism, stratigraphy, the "human" as geomorphic agent) was built on the bones of enslaved and colonized people — and demands that any serious engagement with planetary change reckon with this inheritance.

*Geologic Life* (2024) extends this into a full theory of race as a geological process — not metaphorically but literally: how bodies were incorporated into rock strata, how colonial geology was the machine for racial capitalism.

She is also **building new institutions**: the "Inhumanities" as a call for epistemic restructuring of the humanities; the Forum on DeCentering the Human at QMUL; the Venice Pavilion as a diplomatic and material intervention in how Britain accounts for its geological crimes.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Key Intellectual Moves

1. **The Geologic Turn in Race Theory**: Instead of reading race through biology or law, Yusoff locates it in the practices of earth science — surveying, classifying, extracting. This is a genuinely original move that neither earth scientists nor race theorists had made.

2. **Critique Through Etymology**: "Anthro-pocene" = the epoch of the human. But which human? She presses on this with Black feminist theory: the human was always racially marked. The Anthropocene discourse invisibilizes this.

3. **Material Practice as Theory**: Her work combines close reading of geological texts and colonial archives with conceptual philosophy. The "mine" is not just a metaphor — she reads actual mining company records, geological surveys, colonial contracts.

4. **Counter-Poetics**: Yusoff argues for "counter-poetics" as a methodology — using language, art, and archival practice to destabilize the spatial imaginaries of colonial extraction. This is a move similar to what Amber calls "experiential futures."

5. **Disciplinary Transgression as Signal**: By moving into architecture curation (Venice), she is saying: the discipline of architecture, like geology, is built on colonial extraction — and needs to be thought differently from the ground up (literally).

### What Amber Can Learn

- **The "inhuman" as analytical position**: Not "post-human" (which still centers the human as origin), but "inhuman" — thinking from what the humanities has already classified as non-subject. Useful for Amber's more-than-human design and machine consciousness work.
- **Geological time vs. digital time**: Yusoff's work on deep time and the Anthropocene could enrich Amber's thinking about AI timescales, machine death, and longevity.
- **Art/architecture as intellectual argument**: Yusoff doesn't just write papers about colonialism and geology — she curates exhibitions, runs collectives, designs counter-archives. This is a model for Amber's practice-as-research.
- **Black feminist theory as design method**: Wynter's theory of the "overrepresentation of Man," Hartman's "speculative fabulation" — these are live methodologies, not just citations.
