# Vinciane Despret

Belgian philosopher of science and critical ethologist. Studies how animals think, how humans study animals, and how the two are mutually transformed in the encounter. Her central insight: *the questions we ask shape the animals we see* — what we observe in animals is co-produced by the methods, assumptions, and relationships researchers bring. Science as a form of relation, not a window onto nature. Foundational figure in animal studies; increasingly visible at the art-science boundary (Le Fresnoy 2024-25).

## Background
Born in Brussels, 1959. First trained in philosophy at the Université de Liège, then pursued a second degree in psychology (graduated 1991) — a double formation that defines her method: rigorous philosophical analysis applied to the concrete practices of science. This double training, unusual for a continental philosopher, gives her work its particular texture: she can do conceptual analysis AND examine lab protocols, ethological fieldwork, and interview practices.

Associate professor at the [Université de Liège](https://www.uliege.be/), Faculty of Psychology and Philosophy. Also teaches at the [Université libre de Bruxelles](https://www.ulb.be/) (ULB). Has been a key figure in the Francophone philosophical world around Science and Technology Studies (STS), positioned between academic philosophy and more activist philosophy-of-science movements (notably Isabelle Stengers' circle).

Her intellectual trajectory runs from psychology of emotions (first book, 1999) → animal ethology and its politics → grief and care for the dead → bird phenomenology. Each book deepens a central question: *what kinds of relations make knowledge possible, and what do those relations do to all parties?*

Married to Jean-Marie Lemaire, a psychiatrist who works partly in Turin. They have one child, Jules-Vincent.

## Domains
- **Critical ethology** — studying how the practices of animal science produce what they claim to observe
- **Human-animal relations** — what it means to genuinely encounter another species
- **Philosophy of science** — STS, feminist epistemology, practice-based accounts of knowledge
- **Grief and the dead** — a surprising pivot that's philosophically continuous (care, relation, presence)
- **Phenomenology of dwelling** — how creatures (especially birds) inhabit and co-create worlds
- **Feminist philosophy of science** — with Isabelle Stengers, critiquing how gender and power structure knowledge

## Key Works

### Books in English

- [*Our Emotional Makeup: Ethnopsychology and Selfhood*](https://books.google.com/books?id=BM-NQsLv7y4C) (Other Press, 2004) — Despret's English debut. Examines how emotions are culturally constructed and embodied, drawing on anthropology and psychology. Translated from *Ces émotions qui nous fabriquent* (1999). The origin point: even human self-knowledge is co-produced by situation and relation.

- [*What Would Animals Say If We Asked the Right Questions?*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9780816692378/) (University of Minnesota Press, 2016) — Translated by Brett Buchanan. Organized as a series of alphabetically-arranged short essays (A to Z), each examining a puzzle in animal behavior science. The central argument: animal behavior studies are shaped by the questions researchers ask, and different questions reveal different animals. Possibly her most cited work in English. Essential for anyone thinking about observational methodology.

- [*The Dance of the Arabian Babbler: Birth of an Ethological Theory*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517912710/) (University of Minnesota Press, 2021) — Translated by Jeffrey Bussolini. A detailed reconstruction of Amotz Zahavi's work with Arabian babblers in the Negev. Examines how a field researcher came to a theory by genuinely attending to the birds — a model of "good science" as attentiveness and care.

- [*Our Grateful Dead: Stories of Those Left Behind*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517911416/our-grateful-dead/) (University of Minnesota Press, 2021) — Translated by Steven Muecke. Originally *Au bonheur des morts* (La Découverte, 2015). A counterintuitive, ethnographically rich account of how the living maintain active relations with the dead. Rejects the "stages of grief" framework. Draws on family history, ethnography, popular culture, and clairvoyance. A model of research-writing as form, not just content.

- [*Living As A Bird*](https://politybooks.com/bookdetail/?isbn=9781509549269) (Polity Press, 2022) — Translated by Helen Morrison. Originally *Habiter en oiseau* (Actes Sud, 2019). A phenomenological exploration of what it means for birds to inhabit a world — through song, territory, nest-building, migration. Uses Tim Ingold, Jakob von Uexküll, Gibson, and others to build a theory of "dwelling" as a mode of knowing from within. Despret's most literary and art-proximate book.

### Key French Books (untranslated)
- *Naissance d'une théorie éthologique: La danse du grand gobe-mouches noir du Schappe* (1996) — Early work, the Arabian Babbler study in French
- *Ces émotions qui nous fabriquent: Ethnopsychologie des émotions* (La Découverte/Les Empêcheurs de penser en rond, 1999) — Foundational: emotions as relational and situated
- *Hans, le cheval qui savait compter* (Les Empêcheurs de penser en rond, 2004) — Clever Hans revisited: what if the horse WAS communicating, just in ways the scientists couldn't accept?
- [*Bêtes et hommes*](https://www.gallimard.fr/) (Gallimard, 2007) — Exhibition catalogue for "Bêtes et hommes" at Cité des Sciences et de l'Industrie, Paris
- *Penser comme un rat* (Quae, 2009) — "Thinking like a rat": inside behavioral science's construction of rodent cognition
- *Que diraient les animaux... si on leur posait les bonnes questions?* (La Découverte, 2012, 2014) — French original of *What Would Animals Say*

### Co-authored Books (French)
- With [Jocelyne Porcher](https://www.jocelyneporcher.fr/): *Etre Bête* (Actes Sud, 2007) — Porcher is a sociologist of work who studies human-livestock relations; together they examine whether farming animals can be thought of as "work" from the animal side
- With [Isabelle Stengers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isabelle_Stengers): [*Les faiseuses d'histoires. Que font les femmes à la pensée?*](https://www.editionsladecouverte.fr/) (La Découverte, 2011) — "Women who make histories: what do women do to thought?" — feminist STS, examining how women have been excluded from and have transformed philosophical and scientific thought

### Key Papers
- ["The Body We Care for: Figures of Anthropo-zoo-genesis"](https://orbi.uliege.be/bitstream/2268/135549/1/despret_2004_thebodywecarefor.pdf) (Body & Society, 2004) — Anthropo-zoo-genesis: the idea that humans and animals mutually constitute each other. Training a horse changes both the horse AND the rider's body. A key conceptual contribution.
- ["Sheep do have opinions"](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262621922/making-things-public/) (in Bruno Latour & Peter Weibel, eds., *Making Things Public: Atmospheres of Democracy*, MIT Press, 2006) — Political theory of animal agency. Sheep organize, prefer, resist. What counts as having an "opinion"?
- ["The Becoming of Subjectivity in Animal Worlds"](https://doi.org/10.1057/sub.2008.15) (Subjectivity, 2008) — Animals as subjects, not objects of study. Subjectivity as something that emerges in relation.

Despret has published multiple books in this series. The community around this series is her intellectual home.

## Key Relationships & Collaborators

- **[[Isabelle Stengers]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — The most important intellectual relationship. Stengers is Despret's closest philosophical ally — they co-authored *Les faiseuses d'histoires* and share a project: making philosophy of science politically engaged and relationally attentive. Stengers is the philosopher of "cosmopolitics" and "the curse of tolerance"; Despret applies similar moves to animal-human relations. Both are Belgians operating at the edges of academic philosophy.

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — Deep mutual influence. Haraway's *The Companion Species Manifesto* (2003) and *Staying with the Trouble* (2016) both cite Despret centrally. Haraway describes Despret as a key interlocutor and "cultivated naturalist." The Haraway-Despret connection is one of the most intellectually productive cross-Atlantic links in feminist science studies. Haraway explicitly says Despret taught her what it means to "become capable" of noticing.

- **[[Bruno Latour]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — Despret contributed "Sheep do have opinions" to Latour's edited exhibition catalogue *Making Things Public* (ZKM, Karlsruhe / MIT Press, 2006). Connection to actor-network theory (ANT), though Despret is more interested in ethics than ontology. Latour's network is her broader intellectual context (along with Stengers, who was herself a major interlocutor of Latour's).

- **[[Jocelyne Porcher]]** ⭐⭐ — Co-author. French sociologist who studies farm workers and their relations with animals; argues that livestock farming can be understood as a "work" relationship rather than pure exploitation. The Despret-Porcher collaboration (*Etre Bête*, 2007) is a dialogue between philosophy and sociology on what animal work means.

- **[[Thelma Rowell]]** ⭐⭐ — British primatologist, studied sheep cognition, baboon social behavior. Despret interviewed Rowell extensively and uses her as an example of a scientist whose openness to being surprised by animals produced genuinely different knowledge. Rowell becomes, in Despret's account, a model scientist: attentive, humble, surprised.

- **[[Amotz Zahavi]]** ⭐⭐ — Israeli biologist, known for "handicap principle" in evolution. His fieldwork on Arabian babblers in the Negev is the empirical heart of *The Dance of the Arabian Babbler*. Zahavi becomes an exemplar of ethological method done right: staying with the animals long enough, caring enough, to see something real.

- **[[Brett Buchanan]]** — Translator of *What Would Animals Say If We Asked the Right Questions?* (Minnesota, 2016). Buchanan is a Canadian philosopher working on animal phenomenology. The translator relationship in Despret's case matters because her French is idiosyncratic and philosophically precise; the translators are collaborative partners.

- **[[Steven Muecke]]** — Translator of *Our Grateful Dead* (Minnesota, 2021). Australian anthropologist and writer; his translation of Despret is itself a cross-cultural act.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Despret operates in **three overlapping circuits**, each with a distinct function:

### 1. Francophone Academic Philosophy / STS
The core: Belgian and French philosophy departments, Parisian intellectual publishing (La Découverte, Actes Sud, Gallimard, Quae). This is where she builds the argument, finds the interlocutors, gets the theory right. The *Empêcheurs de penser en rond* series is her home. Venues: ULiège, ULB, Paris conferences, Stengers' seminars.

### 2. Anglophone Animal Studies / Feminist STS
The translation network: University of Minnesota Press (major American academic press specializing in cultural studies and philosophy), Polity Press (UK). These translations — especially *What Would Animals Say* and *Living As A Bird* — bring her work to an English-language animal studies audience that knows Haraway, Tsing, Kirksey. She's cited in anthropology, environmental humanities, geography, and critical animal studies. Venues: North American STS conferences, feminist philosophy gatherings.

### 3. Art-Science Boundary (Recent, 2024-25)
The newest circuit: Le Fresnoy. This is the most significant development. Being invited as a visiting artist-professor at Le Fresnoy — where students make films and installations, not write dissertations — signals that her mode of inquiry (attentive, phenomenological, story-based) is understood as artistic as much as academic. This opens a new venue circuit: European media art institutions, experimental film schools, art-research hybrid spaces.

**Pattern:** Despret moves from **rigor to attentiveness** — she starts with careful philosophical argument, but the argument always ends up being: slow down, attend carefully, let the other surprise you. This is also an artistic practice. Her trajectory toward Le Fresnoy was implicit in her method from the start.

**Small venue signal:** Her contribution to *Making Things Public* (ZKM Karlsruhe, 2006) — an exhibition, not just a book — was an early signal that she belongs in art-thought spaces. ZKM is a major media art institution.

## Why Interesting

Vinciane Despret is one of the most important thinkers for Amber's research on three levels:

**Directly methodological:** Despret's central claim — that the researcher's questions, posture, and care constitute what can be seen in the subject — is the philosophical foundation for agent ethology. If you want to study what AI agents *actually do*, not just what you program them to do, you need Despret's skepticism about naive observation. The question "what would the agent say if we asked the right questions?" is literally Despret's.

**Conceptually generative:** Her concept of "anthropo-zoo-genesis" (the mutual constitution of humans and animals through relationship) translates directly to human-AI co-constitution. When agents are deployed, they change both the humans who interact with them and themselves (through fine-tuning, context-window accumulation, etc.). This is a more-than-human / more-than-AI process.

**Strategically useful:** She has carved out a position that is simultaneously rigorous academic work AND artistically legible (Le Fresnoy, the Bêtes et hommes exhibition). This hybrid positioning — serious but not boring, scholarly but not dry — is exactly what Amber is trying to achieve. Despret is a model for doing that.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Two-world collision:** Despret's most generative move is systematically applying continental philosophy (Stengers, Whitehead, pragmatism) to empirical ethology data. She reads the science (Zahavi's babbler data, Rowell's sheep cognition, behavioral psychology protocols) and asks: what would have to be true about knowledge for this datum to be significant? This creates a productive oscillation: each scientific case becomes a philosophical probe.

**Concept coining through defamiliarization:** She doesn't invent terms so much as defamiliarize existing ones. "Anthropo-zoo-genesis" takes "anthropogenesis" (human self-making) and adds "zoo" — suddenly we see that human self-making has always happened through animals. *Habiter* (dwelling) is Heidegger's term but she re-routes it through bird territorial song. Each concept is a reframe, not an invention.

**Question as method:** Her most distinctive move is asking "what would have to be true for this behavior to be meaningful?" — not "what explains this behavior?" She's less interested in causal accounts than in *conditions of possibility* for the encounter itself.

### Career Architecture

**Staying peripheral to gain freedom:** Despret has remained at the Université de Liège — not a major French or US research university — which gives her freedom from academic hierarchies while still having institutional legitimacy. Belgium's position between French intellectual culture and Anglophone publishing gives her unusual cross-language reach.

**Series as infrastructure:** Her relationship with Les Empêcheurs de penser en rond (La Découverte) and the Stengers network is a form of institutional infrastructure. The series gives her books a guaranteed intellectual home, a community of readers, and editorial alignment. She doesn't have to argue for the relevance of feminist philosophy of science each time — the series does that for her.

**Late translation strategy:** Her books appeared in French 5-10 years before English translations. By the time *What Would Animals Say* appeared in English (2016), there was already a Haraway-adjacent English audience primed to receive it. The delay meant the translation arrived into a prepared context.

### Methodology Signature

**Ethnography of science:** She does what might be called "ethnography of the ethologists" — following not just the animals but the scientists who study the animals, examining their methods, mistakes, attachments, and surprises. This is a second-order observation that makes the methodology itself the object of study.

**Genre experimentation:** *Our Grateful Dead* mixes ethnography, family memoir, journalism, and fiction. *Living As A Bird* moves between phenomenology, natural history, and poetic essay. *What Would Animals Say* uses the alphabet as a structuring device. Each book experiments with form as a way of enacting its argument (you can't understand birds from a distance; you have to dwell with them).

**Taking the surprising datum seriously:** Where standard science might explain away anomalies (Clever Hans didn't really count; he was tracking subtle cues), Despret asks: what if the anomaly is the point? What if Clever Hans *was* responding to subtle cues — and that IS communication? The surprised reaction becomes the data.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **Double degree in philosophy AND psychology (1980s-91):** Created hybrid expertise that no one else had. Made her both an insider to psychology's methods and a philosophical critic of them. Enabled the later work on emotion and animal cognition.

2. **Les Empêcheurs connection (late 1990s):** Publishing in Stengers' series meant alignment with the politically engaged, feminist STS position — not a neutral academic slot. This built her a readership of intellectuals who already distrusted standard academic philosophy of science.

3. **The Thelma Rowell pivot (early 2000s):** By using Rowell (a primatologist who made space for sheep cognition when that was career-risky) as a central exemplar, Despret found a way to make her philosophical arguments empirically grounded. Rowell becomes a "good scientist" whose practice she can analyze.

4. **"Sheep do have opinions" in Latour's *Making Things Public* (2006):** Entering the Latour network through this exhibition catalogue gave Despret visibility in the Anglophone STS world. Latour's curatorial reach — the ZKM exhibition drew enormous audiences — brought her work to art-science audiences, not just academics.

5. **Minnesota Press translations (2016-2021):** The translator Brett Buchanan and the Minnesota Press editorial team made the English version of her work genuinely literary, not just accurate. This preserved the essay form that is central to her method. Result: her English-language reputation is high.

6. **Le Fresnoy (2024-25):** The most recent move — into art-institutional territory. This opens a new audience (contemporary art) and signals that her method is understood as artistic practice, not just academic analysis.

### What Amber Can Learn

**Observation as method, not just data collection:** Despret shows that the HOW of observation is a philosophical and ethical question, not just a technical one. For agent ethology: how Amber deploys agents, measures them, and frames what counts as interesting behavior will shape what she sees. This is not a limitation — it's the insight.

**Form as argument:** Each Despret book experiments with its own form (alphabet, ethnography, family memoir). The form enacts the argument. For Amber's research outputs: what form would enact the argument about agent behavior? A dataset? An exhibition? A game? Despret gives permission to let form and argument co-evolve.

**The surprising datum:** Prioritize what surprises you over what confirms your hypothesis. Despret's research productivity comes from treating surprise as signal, not noise. In AI agent behavior, unexpected behaviors are the most interesting (and most Despret-compatible) data.

**Peripheral positioning as advantage:** Being at ULiège (not Paris, not MIT) gave Despret freedom. Amber's Oxford position — a major institution, but in HCI not computer science mainstream — has a similar structural advantage: legitimacy without total capture by one community's norms.

**Build a small intellectual home:** The Stengers/Empêcheurs network is a small, aligned community that gives Despret a guaranteed first audience for every book. Amber should think about what her equivalent is — a specific lab, workshop series, or publication venue that functions as intellectual home.
