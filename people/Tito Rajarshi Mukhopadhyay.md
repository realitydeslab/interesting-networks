# Tito Rajarshi Mukhopadhyay

Autistic poet, writer, and theorist. Born in India; based in Austin, TX. One of the most important autistic voices in the world — not because he represents autism but because his writing is genuinely philosophically rich and does something language rarely does.

Mukhopadhyay uses facilitated communication (typing) to produce work that exists at the intersection of poetry, philosophy, and memoir. His language is unusual, compressed, imagistic — it emerges from a different relationship to time, syntax, and meaning than neurotypical prose. This makes it philosophically interesting independent of any disability framing.

## Connection to SenseLab/Manning

He is the author of *Plankton Dreams: What I Learned in Special Ed* — published in the **Immediations** series ([[Open Humanities Press]], ~2015–2018). This is [[Erin Manning]]'s book series for research-creation. That he is included as an Immediations author rather than as a research subject is itself significant: Manning treats him as a co-theorist of neurodiversity, not a case study.

He features prominently in Manning's theoretical work in *Always More Than One* (2013) and *The Minor Gesture* (2016) as someone whose mode of knowing enacts the "autistic perception" she theorizes philosophically. He has also participated in SenseLab events.

He also appears in the *Inflexions* Pop Up 1 "Diversity in Diversity" (Sept 2018), alongside Adam Wolfond and others working at the intersection of neurodiversity and research-creation.

## Key Works

- *Plankton Dreams: What I Learned in Special Ed* (Open Humanities Press / Immediations series) — his most discussed work in the research-creation context
- *The Mind Tree: A Miraculous Child Breaks the Silence of Autism* (Arcade Publishing, 2003) — his first book; memoir of growing up autistic in India; written as a teenager
- *How Can I Talk If My Lips Don't Move?: Inside My Autistic Mind* (Arcade Publishing, 2008)
- *Beyond the Silence: My Life, the World and Autism* (with Soma Mukhopadhyay; National Autistic Society, 2000)

## Why Interesting

He is a demonstration rather than an argument. His existence as a poet, theorist, and Immediations author embodies the claim that neurodiversity is epistemic diversity — different modes of knowing, not lesser modes. His work is interesting on purely literary grounds: compressed, strange, philosophically resonant.

For anyone working on **inclusive design, accessibility, or non-standard sensing**, encountering his work directly (not just via Manning's theory of it) is valuable.
