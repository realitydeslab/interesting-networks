# Oron Catts

Artist, researcher, curator. Co-founded [[SymbioticA]] — the world's first university-based biological arts research laboratory. Together with [[Ionat Zurr]], leads the **Tissue Culture & Art Project (TC&A)**, which since 1996 has pioneered the use of tissue engineering, cell cultures, and living organisms as artistic medium. Coined the "Semi-Living" concept: entities simultaneously biological and constructed that exist in a gray zone between life and artifact. Predicted and performed cultured meat, victimless fashion, and biological chimeras decades before these became mainstream ethical debates.

## Background
- Born in **Helsinki, Finland**. Grew up in a context that made him sensitive to bodies-as-technology and life-as-material early on.
- Moved to **Perth, Western Australia**, where he has been based since the early 1990s.
- **BA (Hons) Industrial Design, [Curtin University of Technology](https://www.curtin.edu.au/)** (1996). Honours thesis: *"Living Surfaces: Biotechnology and the Design Way"* — asked whether living organisms could be used as design material. The research trajectory was already there from the beginning.
- **Research Fellow, Tissue Engineering and Organ Fabrication Laboratory, [Massachusetts General Hospital](https://www.massgeneral.org/) / [Harvard Medical School](https://hms.harvard.edu/)** (2000–2001) — where the Pig Wings project was developed. This is how he gained access to actual wet lab infrastructure: not as a scientist but as an embedded artist-researcher.
- Has worked with numerous biomedical labs across multiple countries.
- Co-founded **[[SymbioticA]]** in 2000 with [[Ionat Zurr]], biologist Miranda Grounds, and neuroscientist Stuart Bunt at UWA's School of Anatomy and Human Biology.
- **Visiting Professor, [Royal College of Art](https://www.rca.ac.uk/)** Design Interactions programme (2009–2012), London
- **Professor at Large in Contestable Design, [Royal College of Art](https://www.rca.ac.uk/)** (2015–2017), London
- Set up **Biofilia** — biological art lab at [Aalto University](https://www.aalto.fi/), Helsinki (2012–2013)
- Currently: **Academic Lead, [Institute of Advanced Studies, UWA](https://www.uwa.edu.au/)** and longtime Director of SymbioticA

## Domains
- **Biological arts** — using living tissue, cells, organisms as art material, not representation
- **Tissue engineering as art** — biodegradable polymer scaffolds seeded with living cells, grown into forms
- **Cultured meat and "victimless" food/fashion** — art as ethical provocation ahead of industry
- **"Semi-Living" entities** — objects that are partially alive and partially constructed; the philosophical category he invented
- **Critical biology / Neolifism** — interrogating how life is framed, instrumentalized, and commercialized in biotechnology rhetoric
- **Art-science methodology** — using artists-in-labs as a model for critical engagement with science

## Key Works

### Foundational TC&A Projects
- **[Stage 1 & 2](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/stage-1/)** (1996) — earliest experiments growing tissue over scaffolds as artistic exploration. The conceptual origin of everything.
- **[Semi-Living Steak](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/semi-living-steak/)** (2000) — lab-grown frog steak. Exploring the implications of lab-grown meat for food culture and animal ethics. *Preceded cellular agriculture by over a decade.*
- **[Stone Age of Biology](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/stone-age-of-biology/)** (2000) — with Ionat Zurr & Guy Ben-Ary. Tissue-based objects critiquing the "Stone Age" of our understanding of biological complexity.
- **[Pigs Wings](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/pigs-wings/)** (2000–2001) — with [[Ionat Zurr]] & [[Guy Ben-Ary]]. Three sets of wings grown from pig mesenchymal bone marrow stem cells over biodegradable polymer scaffolds, in a rotary bioreactor, over 9 months. Final scale: 4cm × 2cm × 0.5cm each. Preserved and coated with gold. Used three wing types — bird (angelic), bat (demonic), pterosaur (culturally neutral) — to probe what moral status we will assign to future semi-living objects. Developed at Harvard Medical School residency. **Collected by [MoMA](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/110251), New York.**
- **[Semi-Living Worry Dolls](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/worry-dolls/)** (2001) — with [[Ionat Zurr]] & [[Guy Ben-Ary]]. First tissue-engineered sculptures presented alive in a gallery context. Shown at [[Ars Electronica]] 2000.
- **[Disembodied Cuisine](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/disembodied-cuisine/)** (2003) — grown frog skeletal muscle (from live frog biopsy) as a steak; cooked and served at *[L'Art Biotech](https://www.lalieuunique.com/)* exhibition at [Le Lieu Unique](https://www.lalieuunique.com/), Nantes, France. Guests ate the cultured frog meat. **The world's first performance of cultured meat consumption** — predating Sergey Brin/Mark Post's burger by a decade. The frog was present, alive, throughout.
- **[Extra Ear ¼ Scale](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/extra-ear/)** (2003) — with [[Ionat Zurr]], [[Guy Ben-Ary]] & [[Stelarc]]. Human cells grown into a quarter-scale human ear over a polymer scaffold. A literal extension of Stelarc's Extra Ear body project into wet biological reality.
- **[Victimless Leather](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/victimless-leather/)** (2004) — stitchless coat grown from mouse stem cells over a polymer scaffold in the shape of a tiny jacket. "A prototype for a garment that does not involve killing an animal." **Exhibited at MoMA, New York (2008)**. Controversy: MoMA killed the living cells by ending the exhibition — the museum became responsible for a living entity's death. The NYT covered it. This irony was part of the work's intention.
- **[Semi-Living Zit](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/semi-living-zit/)** (2004) — commissioned by *[Wired Magazine](https://www.wired.com/)*.
- **[The Remains of Disembodied Cuisine](https://tcaproject.net/portfolio/the-remains-of-disembodied-cuisine/)** (2004) — documentation/aftermath of the 2003 performance.

## Venue Circuit Analysis
Catts operates across three distinct circuits simultaneously:

1. **Scientific infrastructure** (Harvard, MGH, CERN, Aalto Biofilia, multiple biomedical labs globally) — This is what distinguishes him from most bio-artists: he doesn't simulate the lab, he IS in the lab. Access to actual tissue engineering equipment is the precondition for the work existing at all.

2. **European media art festivals** (Ars Electronica, ISEA, transmediale, L'Art Biotech Nantes, Laznia Gdansk) — The circuit that VALIDATES bio-art as a serious critical practice. Winning the inaugural Ars Electronica Golden Nica for Hybrid Art was field-defining: it said "biological arts is a real category."

3. **Major collecting institutions** (MoMA, Mori, NGV, GoMA, AGWA) — The circuit that CANONIZES the work. MoMA collecting Pig Wings and exhibiting Victimless Leather puts TC&A in the art-historical record alongside any contemporary practice.

**The small venues that matter:** L'Art Biotech at Le Lieu Unique (Nantes, 2003) — this was the show where Disembodied Cuisine was first performed. A relatively small French new-media venue, but the work it enabled changed the conversation about cultured meat permanently.

**Key structural insight:** Unlike most bio-artists who use biological imagery, Catts' circuit is constrained by actual wet lab access. This means his collaborators are always partly scientists (Vacanti, Grounds, Bunt), and his venues often include laboratories. The work can ONLY exist in specific institutional contexts — this is both the limitation and the power.

## Why Interesting
**Bio-art pioneer who actually does the biology.** Catts isn't working with biological metaphors or imagery — he's in a wet lab, growing cells over polymer scaffolds, and the result is an actual living artifact. This is what makes TC&A the hardest to dismiss and the most philosophically potent: the "Semi-Living" entities are not simulations.

**Relevance to Amber's core threads:**
- **More-than-human**: TC&A's Semi-Living entities are literally "more-than-human" — chimeric, partially alive, between categories. Catts invented the philosophical vocabulary for these objects decades before the concept became mainstream in design theory.
- **Critical computing (biological version)**: What Catts does with biotech is structurally what Amber does with computing — enters the system, learns the language, uses it to expose the assumptions embedded in the technology. "Countering the Engineering Mindset" is an explicit critique.
- **Art-science bridging**: SymbioticA is THE model institution for artists-in-labs. If Amber wants to understand how art and science can genuinely interpenetrate (not just illustrate each other), this is the canonical example.
- **Speculative design as ethical practice**: Victimless Leather was speculative design long before the field used that term. It posed a design question — "what if we could make leather without killing?" — and then built the artifact, then put it in a gallery, then let it die. The arc of the project IS the ethical argument.

**Why now (2026)**: SymbioticA's 2022 defunding threat and survival is a signal that biological arts is at an institutional inflection point. The field Catts founded is both mature enough to be institutionalized and fragile enough to be defunded. That tension is live.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy
**Category invention**: Catts doesn't just make art — he invents the *category* the art inhabits. "Semi-Living" is not descriptive jargon; it's a philosophical category that forces a question: *what are our ethical obligations to entities that are alive but not sentient, biological but artificial, living but designed?* The term becomes more powerful than any single artwork. Compare to Haraway's "companion species" or Thacker's "biomedia" — these two-word coinages structure entire fields.

**Provocation-first, then rigor**: Disembodied Cuisine (2003) was absurd on its face — growing frog meat and eating it in an art gallery. But the absurdity was the point: it materialized a question that "serious" bioethics hadn't asked yet. The provocation generates the philosophy. By the time lab-grown meat became a Silicon Valley venture category (2013+), Catts had already done the serious ethics.

**Using the master's tools to expose the master's logic**: Catts spent a residency at Harvard Medical School learning tissue engineering in order to use tissue engineering to critique tissue engineering. This is methodologically unusual — most critical artists critique from outside. By mastering the technique, he can speak from inside the lab and his critique has more authority.

### Career Architecture
**Platform building over portfolio building**: Most artists build a body of work. Catts built *SymbioticA* — an institution, a graduate programme, a residency, a model. The MBiolArts degree is a decades-long investment in infrastructuring a field. This is the difference between being a good artist and being a field-founding practitioner.

**Strategic institutionalization**: Co-founding SymbioticA within the University of Western Australia's School of Anatomy and Human Biology (not the Art School) was a deliberate positioning move. It placed biological arts in scientific infrastructure, giving access to labs, equipment, ethics clearance. Most art-science programs are art schools that invite scientists to collaborate. SymbioticA reversed this.

**Field declaration**: The Golden Nica for Hybrid Art in 2007 didn't just validate TC&A — it named and legitimized "biological hybrid art" as a category at the world's leading media art festival. Winning the inaugural award in a new category is a field-declaration move.

### Methodology Signature
**Embodied research in hostile environments**: The work can only exist because Catts is willing to spend months in a tissue engineering lab learning sterile technique, bioreactors, and scaffold fabrication. This is the opposite of outsider critique — it's inside infiltration.

**The irony trap**: Almost every TC&A project is designed so that the artwork's life-and-death creates an irony that the host institution must live with. Victimless Leather at MoMA: the museum kills it by ending the exhibition. The art can't be shown without making the institution complicit. This is methodologically brilliant — the work uses institutional logic against the institution.

**Living work as philosophical argument**: The living cells in TC&A artworks ARE the argument, not illustrations of it. When Victimless Leather grows or dies, it literally enacts the question: who is responsible for this entity?

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **1996: Honours thesis on design and biotechnology** — established the intellectual framework that would drive 30 years of practice. Most artists don't arrive at their core question this early and this clearly.
2. **1996: Co-founded TC&A with Ionat Zurr** — choosing a long-term partner creates the durability and depth that solo careers can't achieve. The Catts/Zurr collaboration is one of the most productive in contemporary art.
3. **2000: Harvard Medical School residency** — physical access to real tissue engineering infrastructure. Without this, Pig Wings and everything that followed couldn't exist. Strategic residency as capability acquisition.
4. **2000: Co-founded SymbioticA at UWA** — this is the move that changed everything. Not just making art but building the institution that makes the art possible and trains the next generation.
5. **2003: Disembodied Cuisine at L'Art Biotech, Nantes** — first cultured meat performance, globally. Set the ethical agenda for a debate that wouldn't become mainstream for another decade. Temporal advantage through art.
6. **2007: Inaugural Golden Nica for Hybrid Art (SymbioticA)** — institutional validation that placed biological arts at the center of the European media art canon.
7. **2009–2017: Royal College of Art visiting professorships** — transferred the biological arts model to European design education. Design Interactions (Anthony Dunne's programme) was the most fertile design research environment in Europe; Catts' presence there cross-pollinated bio-art with speculative design.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **The long game of institution-building**: SymbioticA took ~25 years to become what it is. The MBiolArts degree, the residency programme, the PhD stream — each of these is a 5-10 year infrastructure investment. Amber should be thinking not just "what am I making?" but "what am I building?"

2. **Category coinage as the highest-leverage move**: "Semi-Living" did more for biological arts than any single artwork. Naming the territory is more powerful than populating it. What is Amber's equivalent? What category would name the territory she occupies and generate philosophical traction?

3. **Residency as capability acquisition, not just career signal**: Catts used the Harvard residency to get actual wet lab access. Residencies aren't just CV items — they're capability-building opportunities. Which residencies would give Amber access to infrastructure she couldn't otherwise reach?

4. **Inside infiltration vs. outside critique**: The most powerful critical practice in TC&A is entering the system and using its tools against it. This is a general methodology: learn the technique of the thing you're critiquing. Shallow critique from outside is easy; deep critique from inside is rare and more powerful.

5. **The irony trap as design principle**: Every TC&A project is designed so that its existence forces the audience into an uncomfortable position (eating cultured meat, displaying "victimless" leather, witnessing MoMA kill a living piece). What's Amber's equivalent — how can her work structurally position the audience so they MUST take a position?
