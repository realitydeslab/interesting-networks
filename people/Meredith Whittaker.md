# Meredith Whittaker

President of the [[Signal Foundation]]. Co-founder of the [[AI Now Institute]] (with [[Kate Crawford]]). Former Google researcher and co-organizer of the Google Walkout (2018). One of the most important critical voices on AI, surveillance, power, labor, and encryption in the world. Self-described: "I am a surveillance capitalist. I'm a labour organizer. I'm privacy advocate." A rare figure who bridged insider technical expertise (Google, M-Lab) with structural political critique — and then left the industry to build the tools and institutions to change it.

## Background
Born ~1977 in the United States. Grew up in a working-class household. **BA in Rhetoric and English Literature, [University of California, Berkeley](https://www.berkeley.edu/).** Whittaker's undergraduate education in rhetoric and literature — not computer science — explains her distinctively humanistic and structural critique of technology. She did not pursue a graduate degree but built her expertise through practice.

**At Google (2006–2018):** Joined Google in 2006 in an operational role, gradually becoming a technical researcher and leader. Founded **[Google Open Research](https://research.google/about/)** — a group collaborating with the open source and academic communities on net neutrality measurement, privacy, security, and social consequences of AI. Co-founded **[Measurement Lab (M-Lab)](https://www.measurementlab.net/)** — a globally distributed open network measurement platform providing the world's largest source of open data on internet performance. Whittaker represented Google on the **[Partnership on AI](https://partnershiponai.org/)** (PAI), an industry-wide AI ethics consortium. Served as **Minderoo Research Professor at NYU** while still at Google, beginning the AI Now work from within the system.

**Google Walkout (November 2018):** Co-organized the **[Google Walkout For Real Change](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2018/nov/01/google-walkout-global-protests-employees-sexual-harassment-scandals)** — a global employee protest in which over 20,000 Google workers across 50 cities and multiple continents walked out on November 1, 2018. The walkout protested Google's culture of shielding executives accused of sexual misconduct (including a $90M payout to VP Andy Rubin), and also arose from outrage over **Project Maven** (AI systems for US military drone targeting). Following the walkout, Google did not renew the Project Maven contract. Whittaker was one of the key organizers alongside **Celie O'Neil-Hart** and others.

**Retaliation and departure (2019):** After the walkout, Whittaker alleged retaliation by Google — specifically being told to "abandon her work" at the AI Now Institute and face restructuring that would eliminate her role. She resigned from Google in July 2019 after 13 years, citing this retaliation. She subsequently testified before the **U.S. House Committee on Science, Space, and Technology** on "Artificial Intelligence: Societal and Ethical Implications" (June 2019).

**AI Now Institute (2017–present):** Co-founded the **[AI Now Institute](https://ainowinstitute.org/)** at NYU with [[Kate Crawford]] in 2017, following a symposium hosted by the White House. AI Now became the dominant research center for critical AI studies in the US — producing annual landscape reports, policy briefs, and research on AI's social implications including bias, labor, surveillance, and rights. Current Co-Executive Directors: [[Amba Kak]] and [[Sarah Myers West]]. Whittaker remains Chief Advisor.

**FTC Advisor (2021):** Named Special Government Employee and senior AI advisor to **FTC Chair [Lina Khan](https://www.ftc.gov/about-ftc/commissioners-staff/lina-m-khan)**. Alongside [[Amba Kak]], advised the FTC on AI regulation, market power, and tech antitrust matters during a landmark period of renewed antitrust enforcement.

**Signal Foundation (2022–present):** Named President of the **[Signal Foundation](https://signalfoundation.org/)** and the Signal messenger app in October 2022. Signal is the gold-standard encrypted messaging application used by hundreds of millions, whose Signal Protocol underlies WhatsApp's encryption. As President, Whittaker is navigating the difficult challenge of keeping a nonprofit privacy-first messaging app financially sustainable without surveillance business models. Key moves: expanding small donor base, launching username feature (2023) for phone-number-private signups, investing in post-quantum cryptography research. Executive Chairman: **[Brian Acton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_Acton)** (WhatsApp co-founder, Signal's largest individual donor).

**Rhizome Seven on Seven (2016):** Participated in [[Rhizome]]'s [Seven on Seven](https://rhizome.org/editorial/2016/may/13/seven-on-seven-2016-pairings/) — paired with artist and infrastructure researcher **[[Ingrid Burrington]]**.

## Venue Circuit Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

Whittaker operates in **four distinct circuits** — unusually, these are almost entirely policy/institutional rather than art-tech:

### 1. Policy & Government Circuit (Primary)
FTC, Congressional committees, White House, European Parliament, City of New York, WEF Davos, G20 digital working groups. This is where Whittaker has the most impact — testifying, advising, shaping regulatory frameworks. Very few tech critics manage to stay in this circuit consistently without being co-opted; Whittaker has maintained her critical stance throughout.

### 2. Critical Tech Research Circuit
AI Now Institute, FAccT, ACM CSCW, Data & Society, Knight Institute. The academic-adjacent policy research circuit. Whittaker built the dominant institutional node (AI Now) and shows up at all the key satellite events. Her contributions are less "academic papers" and more "landmark reports" — a model of hybrid policy-academic production.

### 3. Activist / Labor Circuit
Google Walkout, Tech Workers Coalition, ACLU. Where the movement meets the institution. Whittaker is the clearest example of someone who moved from activist organizing back into institution building — not vice versa. The walkout gave her a mass following (and platform) that pre-empts her institutional work.

### 4. Tech Culture / Art Circuit (Minor)
Rhizome Seven on Seven (2016), RE:PUBLICA Berlin, World Summit on AI. Small but significant: Seven on Seven is deliberately art-tech crossover, and her partnership with Ingrid Burrington shows genuine interest in how art can produce critical knowledge. This circuit is less developed than Crawford's art practice, but present.

**Key observation:** Unlike Crawford, Whittaker's circuit is almost entirely US-domestic and policy-facing, with European appearances primarily in formal policy contexts (EU Parliament, WEF). She's building power inside institutions, not through the art-world circuit. Signal is itself a form of infrastructure as critical practice — building the alternative rather than just critiquing the existing.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy — How She Generates Ideas

**Structural framing over individual cases**: Whittaker's signature move is to take specific harms (Andy Rubin's payout, facial recognition in housing, AI in criminal justice) and zoom out to the **structural conditions** that produce them. Not "this algorithm is biased" but "AI systems reproduce and amplify structural inequality because they are built by structurally privileged people for structurally privileged purposes." This framing scales — every new AI controversy can be analyzed through it, making her work perpetually relevant.

**Negation as clarification**: Whittaker is exceptionally good at saying what AI is *not* — "AI is not neutral," "privacy is not a feature," "surveillance is not a business model, it IS the business." These negations are more powerful than definitions because they disrupt the enemy's framing first.

**Infrastructure as argument**: M-Lab (internet measurement as public good), AI Now (critical AI as institutional practice), Signal (privacy as product) — each is an argument made through infrastructure rather than text. The persuasion is in the building.

### Career Architecture — How She Builds Power

**Working from inside out**: Whittaker spent 13 years inside Google before becoming one of its most effective critics. The inside knowledge gives her critique specificity and credibility that no outside critic can match. She knows exactly how the decisions were made, who made them, and what the alternatives were.

**Organizing as institution building**: The Google Walkout didn't just produce labor demands — it produced a constituency. The 20,000+ workers who participated, and the millions who watched, became an audience for the subsequent AI Now work. Political organizing created an intellectual platform.

**Filling the institutional vacuum**: In 2017, there was no dominant US institution for critical AI research. Crawford and Whittaker created AI Now and filled that vacuum rapidly. By 2019 it was the authoritative source for AI accountability journalism. Speed of institution building mattered as much as quality.

**Strategic exit timing**: Leaving Google in July 2019 — after the walkout had its maximum impact and after her retaliation complaints had been reported — meant departing from a position of maximum moral authority. The departure amplified the critique more than staying would have.

**Moving to infrastructure operations**: Becoming Signal President is the most interesting move of her career. It shifts her from critic to operator — from "tech should do better" to "here is what better tech actually looks like." This is a move from rhetoric to practice, and it gives her arguments a material grounding no think-tank position can match.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **2006–2016: Build expertise from inside the belly of the beast**. Thirteen years at Google — not to advance within it, but to understand how it actually works. Founded M-Lab, led Open Research. Crucial: she built genuine technical credibility without losing her humanistic perspective.

2. **2016: White House symposium → AI Now founding**. The Obama White House AI symposium created a moment for critical AI voices. Whittaker and Crawford moved fast to institutionalize that moment into AI Now at NYU. Perfect timing — before the AI now discourse became saturated.

3. **2017: AI Now launch + first annual report**. By producing a rigorous annual report from day one, AI Now established itself as the authority. The report format (not academic paper, not op-ed — something in between) was itself a strategic choice: accessible enough for journalists, rigorous enough for academics.

4. **2018: Google Walkout**. The most visible single action of her career. 20,000+ people walking out simultaneously is unprecedented in the history of the tech industry. The political effect (Maven contract not renewed) was real. The reputational effect (Whittaker as critical tech hero) was massive.

5. **2019: Departure + Congressional testimony**. Leaving Google with maximum moral authority AND immediately testifying before Congress = converting labor-organizer credibility into policy credibility. The sequence matters.

6. **2021: FTC advisory role**. Advising Lina Khan signals that Whittaker's critique has moved from think-tank to executive branch. This is how critical research becomes policy — by the critic joining the regulator.

7. **2022: Signal presidency**. The culminating move: building the alternative. Takes on the hardest possible version of the argument — "privacy is possible AND usable" — by actually running it. No longer just a critic.

### What Amber Can Learn

**1. The inside-out strategy.** Whittaker's credibility comes from *having been* inside. Amber is building expertise working with AI systems, agents, and computational creativity from within the research ecosystem. This insider knowledge is an asset. Don't leave it behind when doing critical work — bring it in. Criticism that comes from deep practice is more powerful than criticism from the outside.

**2. Report-as-medium.** The AI Now annual report is one of the smartest research communication strategies of the past decade. Not an academic paper (peer-reviewed, slow, jargon-heavy), not a blog post (ephemeral, low-credibility), not a book (too slow) — but a substantial, annual document that journalists, policymakers, and academics all find useful. Amber could develop a "landscape report" format for her research areas (machine behavior, critical computing + interaction).

**3. Organizing as inquiry.** The Google Walkout produced knowledge that no survey or interview study could. The form of organizing (getting people to act collectively) revealed the structure of the institution in ways only confrontation can. For Amber's participatory design work, this suggests: the participatory process itself is a site of knowledge production, not just a means to a design outcome.

**4. Infrastructure over argument.** Building Signal > writing about surveillance capitalism. If Amber wants to argue that "interaction should be embodied" or "AI should be more-than-human," the most powerful version is to build something that embodies that. Tools, institutions, platforms, protocols — these make arguments that outlast any paper.

**5. The negation move.** Whittaker's most effective rhetorical strategy is negation: "AI is not X" (neutral, objective, inevitable). This is reusable for Amber's domains: "machine behavior is not random" / "computation is not value-neutral" / "AI agents are not just tools." Starting from what something is *not* forces the audience to question their assumptions before you offer an alternative.
