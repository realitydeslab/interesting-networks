# Michael Bernstein

Professor of Computer Science at Stanford, Bass University Fellow, Senior Fellow at [[Stanford HAI]]. One of the foremost researchers in social computing and HCI, famous for three consecutive field-defining arcs: crowd computing (2010–2018), critical algorithm studies (2017–2021), and — the work most relevant to AI agents — LLM-powered social simulation culminating in the 2023 **Generative Agents** paper. Currently on leave (2025–2026) as co-founder of [Simile.ai](https://simile.ai/), a startup commercializing generative agent research as synthetic user research for enterprise. Nationally bestselling author (MIT Press, 2025).

## Background

**Education & origin:**
- BS in [Symbolic Systems](https://symsys.stanford.edu/) (the Stanford degree combining CS, linguistics, philosophy, and cognitive science), 2006. Stayed through his PhD at MIT.
- MIT CS Master's (2008). PhD (2012). Advisor: Rob Miller, David Karger. MIT CSAIL crowd computing group.
- PhD thesis: *Crowd-Powered Systems* — won the **George M. Sprowls Award** for best doctoral thesis in Computer Science at MIT. The most prestigious CS thesis prize at MIT, given annually.

**Career trajectory:**
- 2012: Joined Stanford CS faculty. Built the [[Stanford HCI Group]] into one of the most productive and influential HCI labs in the world.
- 2015: Named Bass University Fellow — an endowed named professorship at Stanford.
- Named Senior Fellow at [[Stanford HAI]] (Stanford Institute for Human-Centered AI) — the university's flagship AI governance/research institute.
- 2022–2023: Co-authored the **Generative Agents** paper with PhD student [[Joon Sung Park]] and [[Percy Liang]]. Became one of the most cited AI/HCI papers of the decade.
- 2025: Published *Flash Teams* book (national bestseller). Co-founded [Simile.ai](https://simile.ai/). On leave from Stanford.

**What makes him unusual in HCI:**
Unlike most HCI researchers who build tools and evaluate them in small studies, Bernstein consistently asks *societal-scale questions*: How does Mechanical Turk reshape labor? How do algorithms reshape democracy? Can we simulate entire societies? His work oscillates between building systems and writing theory. He has a rare ability to define a new sub-field, publish the foundational papers, and then move on to the next frontier before anyone else knows there is one.

He was also one of the earliest HCI researchers to take NLP/LLM seriously as a methodological tool — not just an object of study. The Social Simulacra → Generative Agents arc (2022–2023) represents the fastest field-emergence in recent HCI history.

- **Human-AI interaction:** How people work with and trust AI systems, especially LLMs.

## Key Works

### The Three Arcs

**Arc 1: Crowd Computing (2010–2018)**
The foundational period. Bernstein invented key paradigms for human computation via Mechanical Turk and crowdsourcing.

- **[Soylent: A Word Processor with a Crowd Inside](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2010/soylent/soylent-uist2010.pdf)** (UIST 2010) — Embedded Mechanical Turk workers directly into a Microsoft Word plugin, enabling real-time text editing, proofreading, and shortening by crowd workers. Introduced the **Find-Fix-Verify** pattern. Best Student Paper at UIST 2010, later awarded the **UIST Lasting Impact Award**. CACM Research Highlight 2015.

- **[4chan and the Aesthetics of Ephemeral Image Boards](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2011/chanthropology/4chan-icwsm2011.pdf)** (ICWSM 2011) — Comprehensive empirical study of 4chan culture, anonymity, and ephemeral image boards. **Best Paper Award, ICWSM 2011**. Early landmark in studying toxic internet culture empirically.

- **[PhD Thesis: Crowd-Powered Systems](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2012/CrowdPoweredSystems/phd-thesis-msbernst.pdf)** (MIT 2012) — **George M. Sprowls Award** (best CS PhD thesis at MIT). Laid out the theoretical and systems architecture for crowd-powered computing.

- **[The Future of Crowd Work](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2013/CrowdWork/futureofcrowdwork-cscw2013.pdf)** (CSCW 2013) — With Aniket Kittur, Jeffrey Nickerson, et al. Visionary manifesto for the crowd work field. Identified key research challenges that still drive the community.

- **[Flash Teams](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2014/flashteams/flashteams-uist2014.pdf)** (UIST 2014, **Best Paper Award**) — "On-demand" expert teams that can be assembled and dissolved in real time for complex projects. With Daniela Retelny, Melissa Valentine, et al.

- **[Dynamo: Enabling the Formation and Behavior of Peer Groups in Systems like Amazon Mechanical Turk](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2015/dynamo/DynamoCHI2015.pdf)** (CHI 2015, **Best Paper Honorable Mention**) — With Niloufar Salehi, Lilly Irani, Ali Alkhatib, et al. A platform enabling crowd workers to collectively organize and advocate for better wages/conditions. One of the most politically engaged papers in HCI history.

- **[Flash Organizations: Crowdsourcing Complex Work by Structuring Crowds As Organizations](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2017/flashorgs/flash-orgs-chi-2017.pdf)** (CHI 2017, **Best Paper**) — Larger-scale successor to Flash Teams. With Melissa Valentine et al.

**Arc 2: Critical and Social Computing (2017–2021)**
As algorithmic systems became dominant, Bernstein shifted to critical analysis alongside system-building.

- **[Street-Level Algorithms: A Theory at the Gaps Between Policy and Decisions](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2019/streetlevelalgorithms/streetlevelalgorithms-chi2019.pdf)** (CHI 2019, **Best Paper**) — With [[Ali Alkhatib]]. Applied Michael Lipsky's "street-level bureaucracy" theory to algorithmic systems — arguing that algorithms, like street-level bureaucrats, must exercise discretion, and that this creates predictable points of failure. One of the most theoretically ambitious papers in HCI. Widely cited in critical computing and STS.

- **[Jury Learning: Integrating Dissenting Voices into Machine Learning Models](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2022/gordon_jury_learning_chi22.pdf)** (CHI 2022, **Best Paper**) — With Mitchell Gordon, Michelle Lam, Joon Sung Park, et al. Proposes a new paradigm for NLP annotation that explicitly models disagreement rather than forcing consensus — inspired by the legal jury system.

- **[PNAS 2021: On COVID-19 Research Ethics](https://www.pnas.org/content/118/52/e2117261118)** (PNAS 2021) — With Margaret Levi, David Magnus, Betsy Rajala, Debra Satz, Quinn Waeiss. Interdisciplinary paper on ethics of social/behavioral research during the pandemic.

**Arc 3: LLM Social Simulation (2022–present)**
The breakthrough arc — using large language models to simulate human social behavior.

- **[Social Simulacra: Creating Populated Prototypes for Social Computing Systems](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2022/Park_SocialSimulacra_UIST22.pdf)** (UIST 2022) — With [[Joon Sung Park]], Lindsay Popowski, Carrie Cai, Meredith Ringel Morris, [[Percy Liang]]. The predecessor paper to Generative Agents. Used LLMs to populate prototype social media communities with simulated users to test design decisions before launch.

- **[Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03442)** (UIST 2023, **Best Paper**) — With [[Joon Sung Park]], Joseph O'Brien, Carrie Cai, Meredith Ringel Morris, [[Percy Liang]]. **The paper that launched the LLM agent simulation field.** Created 25 computational agents that simulate believable human behavior in a sandbox "Smallville" environment (inspired by The Sims), demonstrating emergent social behaviors: spontaneous party planning, relationship formation, social coordination. Architecture: memory store + reflection mechanism + planning module. Cited thousands of times.

- **[LLooM: A Concept Induction Method for Large Language Models](https://hci.stanford.edu/publications/2024/Lam_LLooM_CHI24.pdf)** (CHI 2024) — With Michelle Lam, Janice Teoh, James Landay, Jeffrey Heer. A method for LLM-powered inductive topic modeling — extracts high-level concepts from large text corpora.

- **[Reranking Partisan Animosity in Algorithmic Social Media Feeds Alters Affective Polarization](https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.adu5584)** (*Science* 2025) — With Tiziano Piccardi, Martin Saveski, Chenyan Jia, Jeffrey Hancock, Jeanne Tsai. Published in *Science* — extremely prestigious. Shows that algorithmic reranking of social media feeds to reduce partisan hostility measurably reduces affective political polarization. Landmark result for computational political science.

- **[LLM Social Simulations Are a Promising Research Method](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.02234)** (ICML Position Papers 2025) — With Jacy Reese Anthis, Ryan Liu, Sean Richardson, Austin Kozlowski, Bernard Koch, **[[James Evans]]**, Erik Brynjolfsson. Key position paper arguing for LLM social simulation as a legitimate social science research method. Co-authored with Amber's key person James Evans.

- **[Finetuning LLMs for Human Behavior Prediction in Social Science Experiments](https://aclanthology.org/2025.emnlp-main.1530.pdf)** (EMNLP 2025) — With Akaaash Kolluri, Shengguang Wu, [[Joon Sung Park]]. Project: [stanfordhci.github.io/socrates](https://stanfordhci.github.io/socrates/)

**Book:**
- **[*Flash Teams: Leading the Future of AI-Enhanced, On-Demand Work*](https://www.amazon.com/Flash-Teams-Leading-AI-Enhanced-Demand/dp/0262049848)** (MIT Press, 2025) — With [[Melissa Valentine]]. **National bestseller.** About how AI is reshaping the future of work, specifically "flash teams" — expert teams that assemble and dissolve on demand. Draws on two decades of research in crowd computing and organizational behavior.

**Edited book:**
- **[*Handbook of Collective Intelligence*](http://cci.mit.edu/CIchapterlinks/)** (MIT Press, 2015) — Co-edited with Thomas Malone. Landmark anthology defining the emerging field of collective intelligence research.

### Other Notable Papers (selected)

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy — Sequential Field Colonization

Bernstein doesn't pursue one research program — he sequences through fields, colonizing each one before moving to the next. The pattern is:

1. **Identify an emerging phenomenon** (crowdsourcing in 2008, social simulation with LLMs in 2021)
2. **Build the first working system** (Soylent, Generative Agents)
3. **Name it with a memorable 2-word coinage** (crowd-powered systems, generative agents, flash teams, social simulacra)
4. **Write the theory paper** that maps the field's open problems (Future of Crowd Work 2013, LLM Social Simulations ICML 2025)
5. **Move on** once the field is established

This is extremely effective because it maximizes originality and citation impact at each step. He's never competing for incremental progress — he's always defining new territory.

### Career Architecture — Institutional Dominance

**Named professorship (Bass University Fellow):** This isn't just prestige — it signals institutional investment and permanent tenure security. Allows him to take risks (like founding a startup).

**HAI Senior Fellowship:** Positions him at the intersection of technical AI research and policy/society. Most CS professors are only visible within CS. Bernstein is visible to the broader Stanford institution.

**Founding Simile.ai:** Taking a co-founder leave from a top-10 CS department is unusual and signals real conviction. He's betting that his research has near-term commercial value. This kind of external validation of your own research is rare.

### Methodology Signature — Scale + Validation

**Scale play (multiple times):** 
- Crowd computing: Recruited hundreds/thousands of Mechanical Turk workers
- Generative Agents: 25 agents simulating a complete social environment  
- Science 2025 polarization paper: Randomized control trial with real users at scale

**Interdisciplinary import:** He imports theory from political science (street-level bureaucracy), law (jury system), organizational behavior (flash teams), social science (GSS survey methodology for agent validation). This is cross-discipline poaching executed systematically.

**System + theory pairing:** Every major paper builds a system AND argues for theoretical contributions. Soylent is a system AND a theory of crowd-powered computing. Street-Level Algorithms is pure theory. Generative Agents is system + agent behavior theory. This dual track makes his work citable by both system builders and theorists.

### Key Moves (chronological)

1. **2006: Stanford Symbolic Systems** — Unusual choice: an interdisciplinary degree that combines CS with linguistics and philosophy. This became the epistemological foundation for asking "how do systems shape human behavior?" rather than just "how do systems work?"

2. **2010: Soylent** — First landmark paper at UIST. Introduced "human computation embedded in tools" paradigm. Won Best Student Paper + later Lasting Impact Award. Established him as a crowd computing leader while still a PhD student.

3. **2011: 4chan ICWSM Best Paper** — The ephemeral/anonymous internet study. Showed willingness to study uncomfortable internet phenomena empirically.

6. **2022: Social Simulacra** — First LLM simulation paper. Used GPT-3 era models to populate prototype social communities. Established the methodological foundation.

7. **2023: Generative Agents** — The breakthrough. Right timing (GPT-4 era), right framing ("interactive simulacra"), right demo (Smallville with emergent social behaviors). Won UIST Best Paper. Became one of the most cited AI/HCI papers of the decade.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Name the field before occupying it:** Coining "generative agents" was a deliberate act of field-naming. Amber should think about what 2-word concepts she's developing that could define new research territories.

2. **Build systems AND write theory:** The most citable papers in HCI do both. A system without theory is a demo; theory without a system is speculation. The combination is what gets cited by both audiences.

3. **Import theory from unexpected disciplines:** Street-Level Algorithms imported Lipsky from public policy. Jury Learning imported from legal philosophy. This is how you make HCI papers interesting to non-HCI readers.

4. **Arc thinking:** Instead of pursuing one research program indefinitely, plan 5-7 year arcs: establish a field, write the canonical papers, win the awards, then move to the next edge. This generates more impact than incremental progress in one area.

5. **Simile as career move:** Bernstein validated his research commercially without selling out academically. He'll return to Stanford with empirical knowledge of how generative agents work in enterprise settings. This will make his future research papers more grounded.
