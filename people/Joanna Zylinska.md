# Joanna Zylinska

Professor of Media Philosophy + Critical Digital Practice at **[King's College London](https://www.kcl.ac.uk/people/joanna-zylinska)** and Director of the **[Centre for Attention Studies](https://www.kcl.ac.uk/research/centre-for-attention-studies)**. Author of nine books on art, philosophy, and AI. Artist and curator. The most philosophically rigorous voice working at the triple intersection of AI, photography, and posthumanism in the UK today. Co-founder of the **[Creative AI Lab](https://www.kcl.ac.uk/research/creative-ai-lab)** at King's + Serpentine Galleries. Champion of radical open-access publishing.

## Background
Born Joanna Żylińska on 5 May 1971 in Poland. Studied and received her PhD from the **[University of Wrocław](https://uni.wroc.pl/en/)**, where she also worked as a lecturer in her early career. During graduate studies she relocated to the UK.

She spent many years at **[Goldsmiths, University of London](https://www.gold.ac.uk/)** as Professor of New Media and Communications, and from 2017–2020 served as Co-Head of the Department of Media, Communications and Cultural Studies. In **2021 she joined King's College London** as Professor of Media Philosophy + Critical Digital Practice.

Her trajectory is a rare convergence: Polish philosophical formation (continental tradition), UK media studies empiricism, and genuine art practice — she is a practising artist, not just a theorist who writes about art.

**Visiting positions held:**
- Guest Professor, **[Shandong University](https://en.sdu.edu.cn/)**, China
- **Winton Chair** Visiting Scholar, **[University of Minnesota](https://twin-cities.umn.edu/)**, US
- **Beaverbrook** Visiting Scholar, **[McGill University](https://www.mcgill.ca/)**, Canada

**Central intellectual project:** The constitution of the human as both species and historical subject, examined through the probe of "deep time." Her work maps the emergence of the human in conjunction with technologies — tools, language, storytelling, ethics, art, and media. The planetary perspective of this work is grounded in the socio-political crises of today: ecology, economy, and the gendering/racialisation of apocalyptic narratives.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Concept-chain building**: Zylinska builds a conceptual chain across books, each extending the last. From *Life after New Media* (mediation as vital process) → *Minimal Ethics* (ethics scaled to the Anthropocene) → *Nonhuman Photography* (image-making beyond the human) → *The End of Man* (feminist counter-apocalypse) → *AI Art* (what creativity means when machines make images) → *The Perception Machine* (the photographic future between eye and AI). This is not meandering — it's a sustained longitudinal project where each book fills a gap the previous one revealed.

**Conceptual interference**: Her best moves combine two registers at once. "Nonhuman Photography" is not about photography *of* nonhumans — it's about photography *by* nonhuman processes. "Feminist counter-apocalypse" is not feminist critique *of* apocalyptic thinking — it's a feminist alternative *to* apocalyptic thinking. This slightly paradoxical naming creates productive confusion that drives theoretical work.

**Deep time probe**: Her use of "geological time" / "deep time" as a methodological device gives her a way to relativise human-centric assumptions without either sentimentality or mysticism. It's a rigorous philosophical move borrowed from geological science.

### Career Architecture

**Platform building through publishing infrastructure**: She didn't just write books — she co-founded Open Humanities Press and the Radical Open Access Collective, then became series editor of MEDIA:ART:WRITE:NOW. This means she controls the terms on which her intellectual community publishes. It's a permanent platform, not just a career achievement.

**Art practice as research legitimation**: By maintaining a genuine art practice (not tokenistic "artistic research" but exhibited work at Serpentine, Ars Electronica, Transmediale level), she can write about AI art from inside the practice, not just as an observer. This grants her authority in both theory *and* practice communities simultaneously.

**Strategic directorship**: Founding/directing the Centre for Attention Studies at King's (a newly identified research area that hadn't been named as such before) is classic field-declaration move. She didn't join an existing centre — she created one, making herself the institutional node around a new term.

**Transitio_MX curatorial move (2013)**: Taking on the Artistic Director role of the largest Latin American new media festival at the height of her Goldsmiths career was a geographic expansion that gave her global South networks most UK media theorists lack. Timing: before the "decolonise" movement made such moves common — she was early.

### Methodology Signature

**Theory-practice integration**: Her books contain art projects (Exit Man, View from the Window). This is methodologically unusual — the artwork is not illustration but argument. The images constitute philosophical claims that words alone couldn't make.

**Scale and accessibility**: MEDIA:ART:WRITE:NOW books are deliberately short (between essay and monograph). This is a distribution strategy: theory should be accessible, not just comprehensive. Open access reinforces this — she refuses to let economics gatekeep ideas.

**Critical speculative mode**: She doesn't predict futures — she proposes counter-apocalypses, alternative imaginaries, warped dreams. The speculative is not escapism but a philosophical method for testing assumptions.

### Network Strategy

**Curating the advisory board**: The MEDIA:ART:WRITE:NOW advisory board (Hito Steyerl, Eduardo Kac, Cornelia Sollfrank, Mark Amerika, Morehshin Allahyari, Oron Catts) is essentially her intellectual community made visible. Each board member represents a different node in the art-theory-technology network. The board is a map of her alliances.

**Institutional migration as upgrade**: Goldsmiths → King's is not just a job change. It gave her a directorship (Centre for Attention Studies), larger institutional resources, and proximity to the Serpentine Creative AI Lab. She moved when she had the leverage to move up, not laterally.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **2002: Edit *The Cyborg Experiments*** — enters the body-technology debate via Stelarc; establishes connections to body art, performance, and cyber-feminism communities at the high point of this discourse
2. **2009–2012: MIT Press books (*Bioethics*, *Life after New Media*)** — two MIT Press books in 3 years establishes her as the serious philosophical voice in UK media studies; *Life after New Media* with Kember is their joint theoretical manifesto
3. **2013: Artistic Director, Transitio_MX05** — expands geographic reach, builds Latin American network, demonstrates curatorial not just theoretical authority
4. **2014: Radical Open Access Collective + *Minimal Ethics*** — plants an open-access flag while other academics are still fighting for journal prestige; *Minimal Ethics* itself enacts open access by being free
5. **2017–2018: *Nonhuman Photography* + *The End of Man*** — two books in consecutive years that establish the "nonhuman" and "feminist counter-apocalypse" as her signature theoretical moves; crystallises her brand
6. **2021: Move to King's + Creative AI Lab** — at precisely the moment AI becomes the dominant cultural topic; she arrives with 8 books behind her and immediately establishes institutional infrastructure (lab, directorship)
7. **2023: *Science* article** — breaks out of humanities into flagship natural science venue; signals that AI art theory is legitimate scientific knowledge, not just cultural commentary

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **The book-as-platform strategy**: Nine books isn't nine separate projects — it's one extended argument. Each book creates the intellectual ground for the next. Writing a "position paper" book early (as Zylinska did with *Minimal Ethics*) stakes territory and invites engagement before the bigger, denser books arrive.

2. **Publishing infrastructure as intellectual power**: Creating a series (MEDIA:ART:WRITE:NOW) is not service work — it's how you curate your intellectual community and control what ideas circulate in your field. If Amber wants to shape discourse, editing a series is higher leverage than writing papers.

3. **Cross-register authority**: By being simultaneously theorist AND artist AND curator AND series editor, Zylinska cannot be dismissed by any one community. Each role legitimates the others. Amber's cross-disciplinary positioning (AI research × art × design) could be systematically developed the same way.

4. **The "new concept" move**: "Nonhuman Photography" and "Perception Machine" are memorable two-word concepts that become citable shorthand. Coining terms that others can use is one of the highest-leverage academic moves. Think about what 2-word concept captures the core of Amber's work.

5. **Open access as politics, not just pragmatics**: Zylinska's OA commitment is not just about reach — it's a claim about who knowledge belongs to. Making this claim explicitly (through the Radical OA Collective) turns a publication decision into an intellectual position, which is more interesting than just "publishing OA."
