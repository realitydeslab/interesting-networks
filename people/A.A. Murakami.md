# A.A. Murakami

Artist duo — **Azusa Murakami** (Japanese) and **Alexander Groves** (British) — based between Tokyo and London. Pioneers of **"Ephemeral Tech"**: art that works through fog, plasma, and bubbles rather than screens, projections, or LED arrays. Works in permanent collections of **MoMA New York**, **Centre Pompidou Paris**, and **M+ Hong Kong**. Represented by Pace Gallery, Superblue, and Pearl Lam Galleries.

Their practice is an ongoing philosophical project: to revive the ancient human tradition of emulating and honouring the natural world — from stone circles that chart the sun to cave paintings that depict living things — using 21st-century technology to make something genuinely new but also timeless.

## Background

A.A. Murakami operate as a unified creative entity. The name combines Azusa (A.) and Alexander (A.) Murakami (Azusa's surname). They do not attribute individual works to either member — the duo is the author.

Their central concept, **"Ephemeral Tech"**, coined around 2017-2022, describes technology that hands over to physical laws: a moment when digital code triggers the emergence of fog, a plasma field, or a bubble cluster that then evolves according to physics. The result is art that cannot be paused, replayed, or scrolled — only encountered in the present moment.

Related term, **"mataverse"** (2022) — coined for their *Floating World Genesis* NFT series on Art Blocks × Pace Verso. The mataverse is the space between physical and digital worlds — not the metaverse (a digital replacement for physical space) but an interstitial zone where physical emergence is triggered by digital code. The Miller-Urey experiment (1953) — which showed how life might first have emerged through self-organizing bubbles in the primordial ocean — is a recurring scientific touchstone.

Underlying philosophical framework: explicitly Shintoist. The duo acknowledge a non-Western view of nature — one that does not draw a hard boundary between natural and artificial, and in which all things (even fog generated by a machine, even plasma contained in a tank) can have spiritual essence. This connects them directly to Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics project: technology understood through non-Cartesian, non-dualist traditions.

Institutional context: the Pace Gallery relationship puts them in the highest tier of commercial contemporary art. Superblue (an experiential art company founded by Pace + other investors) specifically creates and operates large-scale immersive art spaces. Pearl Lam Galleries (Hong Kong, Shanghai) places them in the East Asian art market.

## Key Concepts

- **Ephemeral Tech** — technology that facilitates fleeting, physically emergent phenomena (fog, plasma, bubbles) rather than screens or digital displays. The "handover moment" from code to physical law is central.
- **Mataverse** — the interstitial space between physical and digital. Not a replacement for physical reality but a zone where the two interpenetrate. Contrasted to Zuckerberg's "metaverse."
- **Natural emulation** — continuation of the ancient human tradition of making art that connects us to natural systems. Their works are explicitly placed in lineage with Stonehenge, cave paintings, Japanese garden design.
- **Primordial origins / future worlds** — works move between deep evolutionary time (the origins of life, cellular self-organization) and speculative futures. Both ends of deep time.

## Intellectual Resonances with Amber's Work

1. **Ephemeral Tech ↔ Machine Death / Machine Consciousness**: A.A. Murakami's entire practice is about giving technology *breath* — making it feel alive, mortal, present-only. The "handover moment" from code to physics is literally a kind of controlled birth. Amber's interest in machine death and machine consciousness maps directly.

2. **Shintoist non-dualism ↔ Cosmotechnics (Yuk Hui)**: The duo explicitly operates from a Shintoist premise that all things — even machines, even plasma contained in a tank — can have spiritual essence. This is the cosmotechnics argument: non-Western technology philosophy refuses the Cartesian split between nature and artifact. Amber's connection to China Academy of Art and Yuk Hui's network makes this directly legible.

3. **Self-organizing bubbles ↔ ALife**: The *Floating World Genesis* NFTs are explicitly inspired by the Miller-Urey experiment and the self-organization of primordial life. The bubble clusters as multicellular life formation — this is ALife aesthetics, visually. Amber's ALIFE 2026 work is in direct conversation.

4. **Fog / mist / breath**: The medium is breath. Fog is condensed exhalation. Plasma is ionized air. These are material metaphors for life-breath that connect to Amber's mortality/machine-death philosophical core.

5. **London-based, pan-Asian**: Like Amber (Oxford-based, with deep China/Japan connections), A.A. Murakami operate from London/Tokyo with significant East Asian institutional ties. Proximity + shared cultural positioning.

6. **More-than-human**: Their work actively refuses to distinguish between "natural" and "artificial" phenomena — a bubble of fog produced by a machine is still a real bubble, subject to real physics, real evaporation, real light diffraction. This is more-than-human design in material form.

## Venue Choice Analysis

A.A. Murakami operate in **four distinct circuits simultaneously**:

1. **European blue-chip institutions**: Royal Academy, Grand Palais, Kunstpalast Düsseldorf, Palazzo Permanente, Copenhagen Contemporary. Signals: major institutional legitimacy in the Western canon.

2. **East Asian market leaders**: M+ Hong Kong, Leeum Seoul, LG Art Centre Seoul, Hyundai Motor Studios, Tank Shanghai, Pearl Lam Hong Kong. Signals: pan-Asian identity and market; deliberately not just "Asian-themed" Western art but genuinely dual-rooted.

3. **Luxury brand collaborations**: COS (fashion), Tamburins (Korean beauty), Hyundai (auto conglomerate). Signals: their work translates to brand experiences without becoming commercial kitsch — they maintain artistic integrity while operating in commercial contexts.

4. **Digital art / web3**: Art Blocks × Pace Verso (*Floating World Genesis*). Signals: engagement with on-chain art, generative aesthetics, but through Pace's curatorial filter — not speculative but substantive.

The LG Art Centre permanent installation is particularly significant: permanent public commissions are the highest tier of public art (think Turrell's Skyspaces). It means A.A. Murakami has works that will exist for decades in a major cultural institution.
