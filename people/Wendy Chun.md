# Wendy Hui Kyong Chun

Korean-Canadian media theorist, critical digital studies scholar, and institution builder. **Canada 150 Research Chair in New Media** at [Simon Fraser University](https://www.sfu.ca/communication/), School of Communication. Founder and Director of the [Digital Democracies Institute (DDI)](https://digitaldemocracies.org/). One of the most cited scholars on the intersection of **race, algorithms, data, and network theory**. Author of four MIT Press books on digital media, including *Discriminating Data* (2021) — the definitive account of how machine learning perpetuates segregation through homophily. Coming speaker at [[Research Network for Philosophy and Technology]] Dialogues (May 4, 2026). Fellow of the [Royal Society of Canada](https://rsc-src.ca/) and [International Fellow of the British Academy](https://www.thebritishacademy.ac.uk/).

## Background
Grew up in Canada (Ontario). Began studying **Systems Design Engineering at the University of Waterloo** with the intention to also study English Literature. The plan became urgent after the **1989 École Polytechnique massacre** (the "Montreal Massacre"), in which a gunman entered a Montreal engineering school, separated the men and women, and systematically shot the women. This formative trauma sent Chun toward the humanities to answer questions that engineering textbooks couldn't address. She eventually completed a **PhD in English Literature at Princeton** (with a presidential fellowship), synthesizing her engineering background and critical theory in a way few scholars have since replicated.

Career trajectory:
- Princeton PhD → Brown University, Dept of Modern Culture & Media
- ~2000–2019: Professor and eventually **Chair of Modern Culture and Media** at Brown — one of the most prestigious media studies departments in North America. Also held positions at Harvard History of Science Dept (visiting associate professor, then associate).
- 2019: Left Brown to accept **Canada 150 Research Chair in New Media at SFU** and found the **Digital Democracies Institute** — a strategic bet to build her own intellectual infrastructure in a new country, with a research mandate she controls.

Her origin narrative is foundational to her work: the 1989 massacre didn't just send her from engineering to humanities — it made the question of **what technology does to gender, race, and power** her life's research problem.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy: The Historical Archaeology Move
Chun's signature intellectual move: **take a concept from one field that appears neutral or technical, do a historical archaeology that reveals its racial/political origins, then show it's still operative in contemporary technical systems**.

The homophily chain is the clearest example:
- **1954:** Sociologists Lazarsfeld and Merton coin "homophily" studying white residents' attitudes toward biracial housing. They ignore Black residents' responses. The concept encodes racial attitudes as social science truth.
- **1973:** Granovetter's "strength of weak ties" builds on homophily for network theory. Racial origins forgotten.
- **2000s–2010s:** ML recommendation systems operationalize homophily as an algorithm. "Birds of a feather flock together" becomes an engineering default.
- **2019:** Chun and Kurgan publish "Homophily: The Urban History of an Algorithm" showing the full chain.
- **2021:** *Discriminating Data* gives it book-length treatment.

This is a **concept repatriation** move: the concept leaves critical theory, gets adopted by ML, loses its critical charge, and Chun reclaims it with historical receipts. Amber can use this move on other ML concepts (the history of "prediction," the racial origins of "correlation," the colonial origins of "simulation" in operations research).

### Career Architecture: The Trilogy + Pivot
Chun's four books follow a **trilogy then breakthrough** structure:
1. **Control and Freedom** (2006) — internet as control apparatus + freedom fantasy
2. **Programmed Visions** (2011) — software as medium of memory and vision
3. **Updating to Remain the Same** (2016) — habit as the mechanism of persistence through apparent change
4. **Discriminating Data** (2021) — pivot: apply the trilogy's insights to the machine learning era

The first three books each contribute a concept (control, visualization, habit) that Discriminating Data then assembles into a single account of ML discrimination. This is a **slow-build trilogy → synthesis** architecture. The reader of book 4 benefits from all three conceptual contributions even if they haven't read the earlier books. Lesson: **your early books are concept deposits; your synthesis book is the withdrawal.**

### Institution Building as Intellectual Strategy
The move from Brown (established department, tenured full professor, chair) to SFU (new chair, new institute) is counterintuitive by traditional academic logic. Brown's MCM department is one of the most prestigious in North American media studies. Why leave?

The Canada 150 Research Chair comes with:
- A named position with explicit mandate
- The authority to found an institution
- Research funding
- Institutional autonomy

Chun didn't leave Brown to go to SFU; she left to *found the DDI*. The institute is the product; the SFU position is the infrastructure. This is the **laboratory strategy**: instead of being an intellectual in someone else's institution, you build the institution that embodies your intellectual agenda. The DDI now employs ~20 people across 5+ active research projects.

Lesson for Amber: **The question isn't "what's the best department to be in?" but "what's the institution I should build?"**

### Network Strategy: Triangulation Across Circuits
Chun's collaborators span three incompatible prestige circuits:
- **Academic critical theory:** Yuk Hui, Patrick Jagoda, Bonnie Honig, Alberto Toscano
- **Art/architecture world:** Hito Steyerl, Laura Kurgan, e-flux
- **Policy/social science:** SSRC, Stephanie Dick, Parmit Chilana (HCI)

By holding relationships across all three, she can translate her arguments upward (theory → art), outward (theory → policy), and sideways (theory → design). Most scholars pick one circuit and stay. Chun deliberately maintains all three.

2. **~2000 — Brown, MCM Department** — Places herself in one of the most intellectually serious media studies departments. MCM is where digital humanities, film theory, and critical theory overlap. Smart positioning for "new media studies."

4. **2011–2016 — Concept deposits** — *Programmed Visions* and *Updating to Remain the Same* each contribute a concept. Neither tries to do too much. Each is a clean contribution to a building argument.

5. **2016 — Guggenheim Fellowship** — Signals shift: the fellowship was explicitly to analyze "race, gender, class, sexuality in the era of data analytics." She used the fellowship year to begin Discriminating Data research. **Using prestige fellowships as R&D funding for your next major work.**

6. **2019 — e-flux "Homophily" paper** — The public-facing version of the Discriminating Data argument. Published in e-flux Architecture, reaching a very different audience than MIT Press. Tests the concept before the book.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **The historical archaeology move is reusable** — Take an AI concept Amber cares about (generativity? simulation? emergence?) and do the archaeology. Where did it come from? What were its original political conditions? What does it smuggle into present-day technical systems? This move produces both papers AND claims about what we should do differently.
