# Premesh Lalu

Professor of History and Director of the [Center for Humanities Research (CHR)](https://www.uwc.ac.za/) at the University of the Western Cape (UWC), Cape Town, South Africa. One of the most important intellectual figures in African humanities — not as a representative of "African studies" but as a genuinely philosophical thinker working on archives, memory, postcolonial theory, and the fundamental question of what the university should be. Since 2024, intellectual partner of [[Erich Hörl]]; co-authored ["Unseating Mastery: The University and the Promise of the New"](https://doi.org/10.1177/02632764241289621) (*Theory, Culture & Society*, 2024).

## Domains
- Postcolonial historiography — especially South African
- Archives and memory — apartheid, liberation struggle, colonial
- The university question — what should the university be? decolonisation of knowledge
- Political theory of student movements — the Western Cape 1985 student movement
- "The becoming technical of the human" — where technics meets political struggle
- Philosophy of the postcolony

## Background

PhD, University of Minnesota, 2003 ("In the event of history"). US training in postcolonial and historical theory; returns to South Africa with a comparative, philosophically sophisticated frame.

Has spent his career at UWC — itself a deeply resonant choice. UWC was founded during apartheid as a university for "Coloured" people; it became a site of radical resistance and is now a site of rethinking what the post-apartheid university can be. Lalu's directorship of CHR is both institutional and intellectual: he is building the argument for a particular kind of humanities research that is embedded in its political context rather than abstracted from it.

## Key Works

- *[The Deaths of Hintsa: Postapartheid South Africa and the Shape of Recurring Pasts](https://www.ucpress.edu/)* — UC Press. Lalu's major monograph. On Hintsa, a 19th-century Xhosa king whose skull was taken by British colonizers and continues to haunt South African politics. A study in how colonial violence recurs, how the past is never past, and how history is implicated in contemporary politics.

- ["Unseating Mastery: The University and the Promise of the New"](https://doi.org/10.1177/02632764241289621) — with [[Erich Hörl]]. *Theory, Culture & Society* 41(7-8), 2024, pp. 219-238. Co-authored with Hörl after Hörl's appointment as Extraordinary Professor at CHR. Argues that the contemporary university faces a "disruptive condition" that requires moving beyond "mastery" (transmitting established knowledge) to opening toward the genuinely new.

- "What is the university for?" — *Critical Times* 2(2), 2019. Core text on the university question from the position of the post-apartheid Global South.

- "The Trojan Horse and the 'Becoming Technical of the Human'" — in *[Remains of the Social](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/remains-of-the-social/947FE13D815CF8A2D5ABDD3B4D8ED1F0)* (Wits University Press, 2017), pp. 249-274. On the 1985 Trojan Horse Massacre in Athlone, Cape Town — where apartheid police hidden in a truck killed student protesters. Lalu reads this as the "becoming technical of the human": the moment where political struggle and technical mediation intersect fatally.

- "Where Does Sadness Come From? Politics, Potentiality and a Possible Humanities" — *Journal of Asian and African Studies* 47(1), 2012. Spinozist reflection: what can the humanities do in the post-apartheid context?

- "The virtual stampede for Africa: Digitisation, postcoloniality and archives of the liberation struggles in Southern Africa" — *Innovation*, 2007. On the politics of digitizing apartheid-era archives — who controls the past?

- "Apartheid's University: Notes on the Renewal of the Enlightenment" — *Journal of Higher Education in Africa* 5(2-3), 2007.

- *[Remains of the Social: Desiring the Post-Apartheid](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/remains-of-the-social/947FE13D815CF8A2D5ABDD3B4D8ED1F0)* — co-edited with Maurits van Bever Donker, Ross Truscott, Gary Minkley. Wits University Press, 2017. Major edited volume on post-apartheid social theory.

- "The ends of boycott" — in *Students Must Rise* (Wits University Press, 2016). On the 1985 Western Cape student movement and the figure of the boycott.

## Why Interesting
Lalu is building something rare: a philosophy of the university that is also a practice. He is not theorizing from the outside — he is the director of an institution that embodies the argument he is making. The CHR IS the proposal for what a decolonial, critical, philosophically serious humanities institute looks like.

His concept of "the becoming technical of the human" (via the Trojan Horse essay) is the most philosophically interesting thread for Amber — it connects political violence, the apartheid state's technical apparatus, and the question of how humans are constituted through their encounters with technics. This resonates with more-than-human design, cyborg sociology, and machine behavior.

The Hörl-Lalu co-authorship is significant: it suggests genuine intellectual reciprocity, not just an institutional arrangement. "Unseating Mastery" represents a synthesis that neither could have written alone.
