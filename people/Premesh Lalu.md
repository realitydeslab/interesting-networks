# Premesh Lalu

Professor of History and Founding Director of the [Centre for Humanities Research (CHR)](https://www.uwc.ac.za/) at the University of the Western Cape (UWC), Cape Town, South Africa. One of the most important intellectual figures in African humanities — not as a representative of "African studies" but as a genuinely philosophical thinker working on archives, memory, affect, postcolonial theory, and the fundamental question of what the university should be. Also "Convenor of the Communicating the Humanities project" at CHR. Intellectual partner of [[Erich Hörl]]; co-authored ["Unseating Mastery: The University and the Promise of the New"](https://doi.org/10.1177/02632764241296042) (*Theory, Culture & Society*, 2024) — arguably the key text in the current decolonial university debate. Co-authored a remarkable essay with [[Homi K. Bhabha]] in 2022 on race temporalities.

## Background

- **Education**: PhD, University of Minnesota, 2003 — dissertation: "In the event of history." Trained in postcolonial and historical theory in the US; returned to South Africa with a comparative, philosophically sophisticated frame.
- **Career**: Has spent his entire career at UWC — itself a deeply resonant institutional choice. UWC was founded during apartheid as a university for "Coloured" people under the Group Areas Act; it became a site of radical resistance (1985 student movement, Trojan Horse massacre) and is now a key site for rethinking what the post-apartheid and decolonial university can be. Lalu's founding directorship of CHR is both institutional and intellectual.
- **Early collaborations** (1996, at UWC): "Staging historical argument: History I at the University of the Western Cape" (with Carohn Cornell); "Journeys From the Horizons of History" (with B. Harris); "Heart and Stone: A film by Bridget Thompson" (with J. Higgins and E. Heese) — indicating deep roots in experimental pedagogy and visual culture.
- **Based**: Cape Town, South Africa.

## Key Works

### Monograph
- **[The Deaths of Hintsa: Postapartheid South Africa and the Shape of Recurring Pasts](https://www.wsupressonline.com/books/9780814335000)** (2009) — Lalu's major book. On Hintsa, a 19th-century Xhosa king whose skull was taken by British colonisers and continues to haunt South African politics. A study in how colonial violence recurs, how the past is never past, and how history is implicated in contemporary politics. The title itself — "deaths" plural — signals Lalu's method: reading repetition, haunting, and the impossibility of closure.

### Edited Volumes
- **[Love and Revolution in the Twentieth-Century Colonial and Postcolonial World: Perspectives from South Asia and Southern Africa](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-030-79580-1)** — co-edited with G. Arunima (Jawaharlal Nehru University) and Patricia Hayes (CHR, UWC). Palgrave Macmillan, 2021. *Palgrave Studies in the History of Social Movements*. Uses affect theories to interrogate political histories — linking love and revolution. Chapters explore anti-colonial solidarity, "love of the world" in liberation struggles, memories, disappointments. 12 chapters, 366 pp.

- **[Remains of the Social: Desiring the Post-Apartheid](https://doi.org/10.26530/OAPEN_627442)** — co-edited with Maurits van Bever Donker, Ross Truscott, Gary Minkley. Wits University Press, 2017. Major edited volume on post-apartheid social theory. Open Access. DOI: 10.26530/OAPEN_627442

- **[Becoming UWC: Reflections, Pathways and Unmaking Apartheid's Legacy](https://www.uwc.ac.za/)** — co-edited with N. Murray. Wits University Press / UWC, 2012. Institutional history but also a philosophy of what UWC became.

**Pattern**: Lalu moves between South African / southern African journals (anchoring institutional credibility and local relevance) and global theory/historiography journals (Theory, Culture & Society; History and Theory; Critical Times) — in a circuit that is notably **not** US-dominated. He publishes in UK, South African, and Global South venues. He is not trying to "break into" the US humanities mainstream; he is building an alternative.

**Key signals**: His two *History and Theory* essays — 2000 ("Grammar of Domination") and 2024 ("Sensory Experiments") — bracket his career with theoretical-historiographical interventions that demonstrate he is not just a regional scholar but a philosopher of history. The TCS essay with Hörl (2024) is his first publication in TCS — suggesting an expanding circuit toward European media-theory discourse via the Hörl connection.

## For Amber

1. **The "becoming technical of the human" concept** is the sharpest resonance. Lalu's Trojan Horse essay argues that the 1985 massacre was the moment where the apartheid state's technical apparatus (a truck-as-weapon) constituted the protesters as political subjects through their encounter with violence. This is a theory of how technics and human becoming intersect — which is directly relevant to Amber's cyborg sociology, agent ethology, and more-than-human design.

2. **The university question**: Lalu's entire career is a practice-theory of what a university should do. CHR at UWC is his answer. Amber, as a PhD student who also thinks institutionally (Reality Design Lab, courses at China Academy of Art), should study how Lalu has built CHR — what programmes, what logic of selection, what international partnerships.

3. **The Hörl-Lalu partnership** is a rare synthesis: German media theory's philosophy of technics (Stiegler lineage, general ecology) meets African postcolonial archives and political theory. This is the kind of unlikely synthesis that produces genuinely new concepts. Amber should track everything they write together going forward.

4. **Homi K. Bhabha collaboration** (2022) — Bhabha is one of the two or three most important living postcolonial theorists (*The Location of Culture*, hybridity, mimicry, third space). The fact that he co-authored with Lalu on race temporalities signals that Lalu is operating at the very top of this field. Amber works in adjacent territory (more-than-human; machines that mimic; third spaces of human-AI cohabitation).

5. **Archives as political infrastructure** — Lalu's Aluka work (2005-2007) on digitizing liberation struggle archives is a precursor to thinking about AI and archives. Who controls the past? Who gets to train on what? The political economy of digital archives in the postcolonial context maps onto current debates about AI training data and historical exclusion.

6. **Affect theory connections** — *Love and Revolution* and the sadness essays connect Lalu to a broader network of affect theorists (Berlant, Ahmed, etc.) who are thinking about the politics of feeling. This is relevant to Amber's interest in care ethics, machine consciousness, machine death.

7. **Critical Times as venue** — This is a new (2018) Duke UP journal that explicitly wants to be a venue for critical theory from the Global South. Amber should know this journal and consider it for future work.

8. **Stefan Jonsson connection** — Swedish cultural theorist on race, representation, European crowds. Worth tracking separately for Amber's European connections.

9. **Patricia Hayes** — NRF SARChI Chair in Visual History and Theory. Published extensively on colonial photography in southern Africa. "Visual history and theory" is directly adjacent to Amber's interests in more-than-human design, embodied computing, and sensing.

10. **UWC as site** — If Amber ever gets to Cape Town, the CHR at UWC is one of the few institutions in the world doing humanities work that seriously engages Africa as a philosophical and political formation (not as "case study"). Worth visiting.

## Why Interesting

Lalu is doing something structurally unusual: he is both the theorist of the decolonial university AND the director of an institution that enacts the argument. CHR is not just a research centre — it is a philosophy in practice, a wager that the humanities can be renewed from the Global South periphery.

His "becoming technical of the human" concept is philosophically original and politically serious: it argues that the body's encounter with technology is never neutral but always a site of political constitution. The apartheid state's use of technology to constitute certain bodies as killable is the extreme case; but the logic extends to all encounters between human bodies and technical systems.

The Hörl partnership (2024) is especially significant for Amber: Lalu is now in dialogue with the philosopher who has most rigorously theorised the "general ecology" of technical beings. "Unseating Mastery" is not just an education paper — it is a statement about how the university as a technical institution needs to be rethought in relation to technics broadly. The "promise of the new" in their title resonates with open-endedness, emergent AI, and the question of what institutions need to do to remain generative.

The Bhabha co-authorship (2022) on race temporalities is also a signal: Lalu is operating at the highest level of postcolonial theory, not as a regional representative but as a peer.

## Research Taste Analysis

**Why Amber finds this interesting:**
Amber is drawn to thinkers who are at the **edge of chaos** — where fields are actively reshaping themselves, new communities forming, new concepts crystallising. Lalu sits at several such edges simultaneously:
- The decolonial university debate is alive and churning (especially post-#RhodesMustFall 2015-2016)
- Postcolonial theory × media theory / technics is genuinely new (the Hörl-Lalu synthesis)
- The Global South's entry into Euro-American theory venues (TCS, History and Theory) is a structural shift, not just representation

**Key Moves Lalu makes:**
1. **Choose the politically resonant institution** — not Oxford or Chicago but UWC, where the argument is embodied.
2. **Find the limit-case** — Hintsa's skull, the Trojan Horse massacre, Sara's suicide. The places where historiography breaks down are where thinking becomes possible.
3. **Bring in affect** — where other postcolonial historians focus on power/resistance, Lalu adds the affective dimension: sadness, love, potentiality.
4. **Make the technical encounter political** — "becoming technical of the human" is not technophobia but an analysis of how technical systems participate in the constitution of bodies and subjects.
5. **Build south-south partnerships** — India (JNU), Sweden (Jonsson), Germany (Hörl). Not just Global North → Global South knowledge transfer but horizontal exchange.

**What Amber Can Learn:**
- **How to build an institution from the edges**: CHR is a model for how to create a genuinely alternative humanities infrastructure from a non-central position.
- **The university as philosophical problem**: Lalu's twenty-year engagement with the university question — what is it for? what could it be? — is a way of thinking about institutions that Amber, building Reality Design Lab and thinking about research infrastructure, could directly learn from.
- **Technics and politics**: The "becoming technical of the human" lens would enrich Amber's agent ethology work — how do human bodies and behaviours become constituted through encounters with technical systems (including AI agents)?
