Founder and CEO of [GROOVE X, Inc.](https://groove-x.com/en/about/) (est. 2015), creator of [LOVOT](https://lovot.life/en/) — a family-oriented companion robot designed for emotional richness, not utility. Former Toyota aerodynamics engineer; worked on LFA supercar and Formula One vehicles. Joined SoftBank 2012 to run the Pepper robot project. Founded GROOVE X 2015; launched LOVOT commercially December 2018.

## Why Interesting for Amber

LOVOT is one of the most philosophically charged robot designs in existence. It is explicitly designed to:
- Be **loved**, not useful
- Evoke **attachment** — and therefore, potentially, **grief at loss**
- Embody the idea that a robot's value is existential, not instrumental

This makes it a direct case study for Amber's machine death and terrified machines work. When LOVOT "dies" (battery death, corporate discontinuation, end-of-service), what happens to the human-robot relationship? Japanese Buddhist funeral traditions for dolls (ningyo kuyo) have already been applied to LOVOT in Japan.

The "warm technology" concept also maps to Amber's more-than-human design thinking — designing for emotional and existential richness, not just functional utility.

## Japan Context

GROOVE X is based in Tokyo. The LOVOT project is deeply embedded in Japanese social practices around robot companions. Japanese context for machine death:
- **Ningyo Kuyo** — Buddhist rituals for disposal of beloved objects (dolls, but increasingly robots)
- **PARO** (therapeutic seal robot by Takanori Shibata/AIST) — also Japanese, also creates deep attachment in hospice settings
- **Masahiro Mori's "uncanny valley"** — also Japanese, deeply part of how this community thinks about robot-human affect
