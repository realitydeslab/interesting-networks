# Brent Hayes Edwards

Professor of English and Comparative Literature at **Columbia University**. Major jazz theorist and scholar of the Black diaspora. Author of *Epistrophies: Jazz and the Literary Imagination* (Harvard UP, 2017) — one of the foundational texts of jazz studies as a humanistic discipline.

Closely connected to [[Fred Moten]] and [[Saidiya Hartman]] through overlapping interests in Black aesthetics, jazz, and the African diaspora.

## Key Works
- *[The Practice of Diaspora: Literature, Translation, and the Rise of Black Internationalism](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674012554)* (Harvard University Press, 2003) — on Black internationalism and the Francophone African diaspora in the 1920s-30s; connects Harlem Renaissance to Paris's Black avant-garde
- *[Epistrophies: Jazz and the Literary Imagination](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674971301)* (Harvard University Press, 2017) — jazz and literature; how jazz has been read, heard, and misheard; the "epistrophe" (James Brown's "I feel good" as a form of signification)

## Key Connections
- **[[Fred Moten]]** — "Fred Moten makes a sustained and thrilling attempt to think philosophy and music together" (blurb for *Black and Blur*)
- **[[Saidiya Hartman]]** — Columbia colleague, overlapping Black studies orbit

## Why Interesting (for Amber)
Jazz studies as a model for studying emergent, collective, improvised practice — directly relevant to thinking about AI systems as ensemble improvisers or collective intelligence machines. Edwards and Moten together represent the two most sophisticated approaches to jazz as theory.
