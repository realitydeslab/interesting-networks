Media theorist, essayist, and critical infrastructure scholar. After 23 years in the academy (18.5 at The New School + 3 at UPenn), she resigned her professorship in 2025 to pursue research and practice outside the university — a dramatic and deliberate move. Now Director of Creative Research at the [Metropolitan New York Library Council](https://metro.org/), a civic infrastructure org connecting hundreds of NYC libraries and archives. Her work places computation, data, and "intelligence" in their physical, historical, and material contexts: treating cities, libraries, forests, and geological strata as forms of media, memory, and cognition.

## Background
- Held tenured full professorships in **three fields simultaneously**: media studies, anthropology, and art history — an unusual multi-disciplinary feat that reflects her genuinely cross-cutting intellectual project
- **The New School**, New York: Department of Anthropology + School of Media Studies (2004–2022). Directed the 600-student graduate program in media studies; launched and directed the graduate minor in Anthropology + Design; collaborated with Parsons School of Design
- **University of Pennsylvania**: Penn Presidential Compact Professor of Media Studies, with secondary appointment in History of Art (2022–2025)
- Left academia in 2025 after 23 years: "I resigned my position." A deliberate escape from the university to pursue work at civic scale
- Based between New York City and Columbia County (upstate NY)
- Her parents shaped her sensibility: her mother was a special education teacher and community organizer; her father owned a hardware store and makes furniture — the working-class material culture of maintenance, repair, and community infrastructure runs through all her work

## Domains
- **Media archaeology** — excavating the deep history of media infrastructure (5000 years, not 50)
- **Critical urbanism / smart city critique** — against algorithmic governance of cities; cities as complex adaptive systems not computation
- **Infrastructure theory** — libraries, pipes, tubes, repair manuals, trees, soil as media
- **Material media theory** — the physicality of information: clay tablets, dirt, geological archives, plexiglass, cardboard, sandpaper
- **Knowledge infrastructures** — libraries, archives, databases, indexes as civic and political systems
- **More-than-human media** — trees, fungi, geological formations as sites of cognition and memory
- **Maintenance and care** — repair manuals, infrastructure maintenance as political economy
- **Sonic/sensory media** — listening, sound, the acoustic city

1. **Critical/architectural humanities**: Places Journal, e-flux Architecture, Perspecta (Yale School of Architecture), Storefront for Art and Architecture, Bard Graduate Center — the "critical architecture theory" world that bridges art, design, and critical theory

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy

**Scale multiplication as method**: Shannon consistently takes a contemporary problem and stretches it to an unexpected temporal or material scale. Smart cities → 5000 years of urban media. Computer data → clay tablets and dirt. Library → geological archive. This "deep time" move defamiliarizes the familiar and makes new questions appear. It's not just historical — it's a way of revealing that what we think is new is actually ancient, and vice versa.

**Two-word concept coining**: "Media city," "deep mapping," "arboreal codes," "code and clay," "speculative syllabus" — she consistently coins paired concepts that are instantly memorable and cite-able. The form is always: unexpected juxtaposition of domains (code+clay, arboreal+codes).

**Object-as-essay**: The repair manual, sandpaper, plexiglass, the drinking fountain, the computer mouse pad — she consistently takes a mundane material object and uses it as an essay form, revealing the political/historical/sensory dimensions embedded in ordinary things. This is media archaeology from the bottom up.

### Career Architecture

**The institutional authority play**: She spent 23 years building institutional credibility (The New School, UPenn, tenured full professor in THREE fields) and then converted that credibility into freedom — leaving the university while keeping the prestigious chairs (Kluge at Library of Congress, Cooper Hewitt). She built the institutional capital before spending it.

**Strategic exit from the university**: In 2025 she resigned a prestigious professorship to become a "Director of Creative Research" at a civic institution. This is rare. It signals that she believes civic infrastructure (libraries, archives, METRO) is where the interesting work is now — and that the university is too constraining. This is a form of field declaration: "my field is civic knowledge infrastructure, not academia."

**The Places Journal column as platform**: For 10+ years she's written long-form essays for Places Journal — a critical architecture journal with a specific, high-quality audience. This is not just publishing; it's a regular platform that trains an audience to expect her perspective. The column is a slow-burn platform strategy.

### Methodology Signature

**Material media archaeology**: She does not just theorize — she goes into the archives, the storerooms, the geological record. Her Kluge Chair project was literally researching "how furniture and trees shape the way we think." This is hands-in-the-clay methodology.

**The speculative syllabus form**: Her "speculative syllabus" essays (Library Field, Modeling Doubt) are genuinely novel: a syllabus as design artifact, as world-building document. This is a new academic form she's invented.

**Cross-discipline poaching**: She moves between communication studies, anthropology, art history, architecture, library science, environmental humanities — taking methods from each but not being owned by any. The three-field professorship (media studies + anthropology + art history) was not accidental; it was the institutional expression of a genuinely interdisciplinary practice.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **2004**: Join The New School across two departments (Anthropology + Media Studies) — immediately signals cross-disciplinary positioning, not disciplinary capture
2. **2007**: First book on libraries as civic design (niche but real) — stakes a claim in a space no one else was treating seriously
3. **2014**: Places Journal "Library as Infrastructure" essay — reframes libraries through infrastructure theory; introduces the infrastructure lens to architectural discourse
4. **2017**: *Code and Clay, Data and Dirt* + "A City Is Not a Computer" essay (same year) — this is the breakout moment. Two major interventions that together define a whole critical position: smart city critique + deep time media archaeology. Strategic simultaneity.
5. **2022**: Move to University of Pennsylvania (Penn Presidential Compact Professor) — institutional elevation, more resources, new city (Philadelphia) enabling new local research (Philadelphia media, civic infrastructure)
6. **2025**: Resign professorship, take Kluge Chair + Cooper Hewitt residency + METRO Director — the pivot OUT of academia. Betting that civic institutions are the future base for serious research.

5. **Civic institution as research base**: Shannon's METRO move suggests that when the university feels constraining, civic institutions (libraries, museums, archives) can be alternative research bases with real access to communities, collections, and resources.
