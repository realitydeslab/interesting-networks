# Emily M. Bender

Linguist, NLP researcher, and AI critic. Thomas L. and Margo G. Wyckoff Endowed Professor of Linguistics at University of Washington; faculty director of the [CLMS](https://www.compling.uw.edu/) (Computational Linguistics Master of Science) program and director of the [Computational Linguistics Laboratory](https://depts.washington.edu/uwcl/). Co-author of the landmark [Stochastic Parrots paper](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445922) (FAccT 2021, 11,000+ citations) with [[Timnit Gebru]], [[Margaret Mitchell]], and Angelina McMillan-Major — the paper at the center of Gebru's firing from Google. Co-author of [*The AI Con: How to Fight Big Tech's Hype and Create the Future We Want*](https://thecon.ai) (HarperCollins, May 2025) with [[Alex Hanna]]. Co-host of [Mystery AI Hype Theater 3000](https://www.dair-institute.org/projects/mystery-ai-hype-theater-3000/) podcast/stream (230,000+ downloads, 170 countries) with Hanna. The only Stochastic Parrots co-author *not* employed at Google at the time of writing — which gave the paper its external independence.

## Background

Emily M. Bender's trajectory is rooted in formal linguistics — not CS. She did her **AB in Linguistics at UC Berkeley**, spent time at **Tohoku University in Sendai** studying Japanese linguistics, then completed a **PhD at Stanford** in the HPSG (Head-Driven Phrase Structure Grammar) and [LinGO](https://web.archive.org/web/20200620233841/https://lingo.stanford.edu/) projects at [CSLI](https://www-csli.stanford.edu/). This training gave her something rare in the AI criticism space: actual linguistic rigor grounded in formal grammar theory.

After her PhD she held postdoc/temporary positions at Stanford and UC Berkeley, worked briefly in industry at **YY Technologies**, then joined UW faculty in **2003**. She has been at UW ever since — a rare case of someone who stayed in the same institution and built deep institutional infrastructure there (the CLMS program, the Grammar Matrix project, the Computational Linguistics Lab).

She describes herself as coming to societal impacts of NLP around **2016**, when she began teaching seminars on the topic. This was *before* the current moment of AI ethics discourse — which means she helped shape it rather than react to it. The Stochastic Parrots paper in 2021 was the culmination of five years of building frameworks: data statements (2018), ethics in NLP teaching, ethics review processes for conferences.

Her pronouns are she/her. Her [Erdős number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erd%C5%91s_number) is 4.

## Key Works

### Core AI Critique
- **[On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big? 🦜](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445922)** (FAccT 2021) — with Timnit Gebru, Margaret Mitchell, Angelina McMillan-Major. The paper that coined "stochastic parrot" as a critique of LLMs. Raises concerns about: environmental costs, dataset bias, concentration of power, miscalibrated expectations, and the fundamental issue that models trained on form cannot learn meaning. 11,000+ citations. Started at Google Brain, got Gebru fired.

- **[*The AI Con: How to Fight Big Tech's Hype and Create the Future We Want*](https://thecon.ai)** (HarperCollins, May 2025) — with [[Alex Hanna]]. Sharp, witty takedown of AI hype across its forms: at work, in the marketplace, in media, in policy. Argues hype serves to justify data theft, surveillance capitalism, devaluation of human creativity, and concentration of power in a small set of actors.

- **[Mystery AI Hype Theater 3000](https://www.dair-institute.org/projects/mystery-ai-hype-theater-3000/)** (ongoing, DAIR) — podcast and livestream with Alex Hanna. Named after Mystery Science Theater 3000. They riff on AI media coverage, breaking down hype, separating fact from fiction. 230,000+ downloads, 170 countries. Episode archive and newsletter on [Buttondown](https://buttondown.com/maiht3k).

### Foundational NLP Critique
- **[Climbing towards NLU: On Meaning, Form, and Understanding in the Age of Data](https://aclanthology.org/2020.acl-main.463/)** (ACL 2020) — with Alexander Koller. "The Octopus Test" paper. Argues that a system trained only on form has *a priori* no way to learn meaning — the formal distinction that underpins the Stochastic Parrots critique. If an octopus with no language exposure were connected to a communication system and learned to respond to linguistic form, it would still lack semantic understanding.

- **[Data Statements for Natural Language Processing: Toward Mitigating System Bias and Enabling Better Science](https://aclanthology.org/Q18-1041/)** (TACL 2018) — with Batya Friedman. Proposed "data statements" as a design solution for NLP: documentation of who produced data, under what circumstances, for what purpose — to address bias and enable better science. Inspired by Datasheets for Datasets. Now a field-standard practice; Version 3 released 2024.

### Linguistics for NLP Practitioners
- **[*Linguistic Fundamentals for Natural Language Processing: 100 Essentials from Morphology and Syntax*](https://dx.doi.org/10.2200/S00493ED1V01Y201303HLT020)** (2013, Morgan & Claypool) — textbook making formal linguistics accessible to NLP practitioners. Widely used.

- **[*Linguistic Fundamentals for Natural Language Processing II: 100 Essentials from Semantics and Pragmatics*](https://www.morganclaypool.com/doi/abs/10.2200/S00935ED1V02Y201907HLT043)** (2019, Morgan & Claypool) — with Alex Lascarides.

### Grammar Engineering
- **[LinGO Grammar Matrix](https://matrix.ling.washington.edu/)** (ongoing) — open-source starter kit for building broad-coverage precision HPSG grammars. Supports linguistic typology research: trying to computationally cover the full range of grammatical variants across languages. Framework for NSF-funded projects on endangered languages.

- **[AGGREGATION project](https://depts.washington.edu/uwcl/aggregation/)** — automatic grammar creation from Interlinear Glossed Text (IGT) for endangered language documentation.

## Ideas & Research Taste Analysis

### The Stochastic Parrots Argument
The paper's core claim: **LLMs are stochastic parrots** — they recombine tokens probabilistically based on form, but cannot access meaning because meaning requires grounding in the world, which text alone cannot provide. The "parrot" metaphor (later challenged but sticking) says: parrots mimic language without understanding it; LLMs do the same at scale.

Subsidiary arguments the paper makes (often overlooked):
1. **Environmental cost** — training large models consumes massive resources; those costs are externalized
2. **Training data encodes bias** — scraped internet text encodes racism, sexism, etc.; "cleaning" data just papers over structural issues
3. **Research monoculture** — massive investment in LLMs crowds out other approaches; this is a power issue, not just resource allocation
4. **Miscalibrated expectations** — when people believe LLMs "understand," they use them inappropriately; this causes real harm

### Form vs. Meaning (The Octopus Test)
The ACL 2020 paper with Koller provides the formal philosophical foundation for Stochastic Parrots. The "Octopus Test": if you connected a language-savvy octopus (no human language background) to radio communications and it learned to respond appropriately to linguistic form, it would still not *understand* the language — it would be doing something like sophisticated pattern-matching. The argument is that form (the statistical distribution of tokens) is insufficient to constitute meaning (semantic content, communicative intent, grounding).

This isn't just a rhetorical position — it's grounded in formal semantics and philosophy of language traditions Bender trained in.

### Data Statements as Structural Intervention
Rather than just critiquing, Bender proposes tools. Data Statements (2018, Version 3 by 2024) are a practical framework requiring NLP researchers to document: who collected the data, from whom, under what conditions, for what purpose, what varieties of language are represented. This is a structural intervention: rather than just pointing at bias, it creates infrastructure for transparency.

The trajectory from Data Statements → Stochastic Parrots → *The AI Con* is coherent: document the problem (Data Statements), theorize the problem (SP), popularize the critique for non-specialists (*The AI Con*).

### "Linguistics For The Win" (LFTW)
An informal slogan encapsulating her method: using formal linguistics — which has rigorous conceptual distinctions honed over decades — to critique sloppy AI discourse. Example: NLP researchers saying models "understand" language, when formal semantics has very precise definitions of what understanding/meaning require. LFTW is a methodological stance: linguistics has tools that matter, and the AI field ignores them at its peril.

### AI Hype as Political Economy
In *The AI Con*, the argument scales up: AI hype isn't just misinformation, it's a **political-economic strategy** — a way for Big Tech to justify data theft, surveillance capitalism, labor replacement, and regulatory capture. Hype creates a permission structure for harmful practices. This connects to broader critical computing traditions: technology as power, not just tool.
