The founder of **soma design** — the discipline of interaction design grounded in felt bodily experience. Professor at KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm. Her book *[Designing with the Body: Somaesthetic Interaction Design](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262038744/designing-with-the-body/)* (MIT Press, 2018) is the canonical text that declares somaesthetic interaction design as a field. Bridges Richard Shusterman's somaesthetics philosophy with concrete HCI design methodology. The most influential embodied interaction researcher of her generation. Known personally as "Kia."

## Background

Born 1964. Swedish. Completed her BSc at Uppsala University (1987) and PhD at Stockholm University (1996), with a thesis titled "A Glass Box Approach to Adaptive Hyper-Media" — early work on adaptive systems that let users maintain transparency and control. Habilitation at Stockholm University in 2002. 

Joined **Swedish Institute of Computer Science (SICS)** as a researcher in 1990 — building the foundational network in Swedish computing research. Won the prestigious [ERCIM Cor Baayen Fellowship](https://www.ercim.eu/publication/Ercim_News/enw32/christina.html) in 1997 — Europe's most distinguished award for young researchers in informatics — recognizing her work on personalized and social navigation in information space.

Became **Professor at Stockholm University** in 2003. Moved to **KTH Royal Institute of Technology** in 2012, establishing the Interaction Design (IxD) group within the Media Technology and Interaction Design (MID) department — giving soma design a proper institutional home. Also works part-time at [RISE](https://www.ri.se/) (Research Institutes of Sweden, successor to SICS).

Her intellectual trajectory: early adaptive hypermedia systems → social navigation → affective computing → somaesthetic interaction design. The thread throughout: how does a computational system engage with the whole human being, not just cognition? By the late 2000s, she had landed on the body itself — not as a sensor array but as a site of knowledge, cultivation, and felt experience.

Personally: horseback rider, mother, grandmother, feminist. The equestrian practice is not merely biographical — it became methodological, explored in her paper "Transferring Qualities from Horseback Riding to Design" (NordiCHI 2010).

## Key Works

### Books
- [***Designing with the Body: Somaesthetic Interaction Design***](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262038744/designing-with-the-body/) (MIT Press, 2018) — THE definitive text. Part philosophy, part design methodology, part research manifesto. Synthesizes Shusterman's somaesthetics with HCI design practice. Declares a new subdiscipline. 731 Google Scholar citations. If you work at the intersection of body and computing, this book is required reading.
- [***Designing Information Spaces: The Social Navigation Approach***](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-1-4471-0141-7) (edited with Benyon & Munro, Springer, 2003) — landmark volume on social navigation in information spaces.
- [***Social Navigation of Information Space***](https://link.springer.com/book/9781852330620) (edited with Munro & Benyon, Springer, 1999) — earlier companion volume.

### Canonical Papers (most cited first)
1. [**"Strong Concepts: Intermediate-Level Knowledge in Interaction Design Research"**](https://doi.org/10.1145/2362364.2362371) — Höök & Löwgren (ACM TOCHI, 2012) — 793 citations. Introduces "strong concepts" as a knowledge contribution mode for design research: between concrete examples and high-level theory. One of the most methodologically generative papers in HCI.
2. [**"Steps to Take Before Intelligent User Interfaces Become Real"**](https://doi.org/10.1016/S0953-5438(00)00006-5) — Höök (Interacting with Computers, 2000) — 505 citations. Visionary early paper on the conditions for intelligent interfaces to be genuinely beneficial.
3. [**"Somaesthetic Appreciation Design"**](https://doi.org/10.1145/2858036.2858583) — Höök, Jonsson, Ståhl, Mercurio (CHI 2016) — 373 citations. The paper that first explicitly names and methodizes somaesthetic design. Describes three design concepts that instantiate somaesthetic appreciation.
4. [**"Social Navigation: Techniques for Building More Usable Systems"**](https://doi.org/10.1145/345190.345243) — Dieberger, Dourish, Höök, Resnick, Wexelblat (interactions, 2000) — 335 citations. Defines social navigation as a design approach.
5. [**"Experiencing the Affective Diary"**](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00779-009-0236-7) — Ståhl, Höök, Svensson, Taylor, Combetto (PUC, 2009) — 263 citations. Landmark affective computing project: using physiological data as diary material.
6. [**"Sense and Sensibility: Evaluation and Interactive Art"**](https://doi.org/10.1145/642611.642655) — Höök, Sengers, Andersson (CHI 2003) — 256 citations. Methods for evaluating interactive artworks.
7. [**"Embracing First-Person Perspectives in Soma-Based Design"**](https://doi.org/10.3390/informatics5010008) — Höök, Caramiaux, Erkut, Forlizzi, et al. (Informatics, 2018) — 216 citations. 21-author community manifesto for soma design. Declares first-person somatic practice as legitimate research methodology.
8. [**"The Sensual Evaluation Instrument"**](https://doi.org/10.1145/1124772.1124876) — Isbister, Höök, Sharp, Laaksolahti (CHI 2006) — 213 citations. Tool for evaluating embodied, affective experience in interactive systems.
9. [**"Mind the Body!"**](https://doi.org/10.1145/1858171.1858187) — Sanches, Höök et al. (DIS 2010) — 199 citations. Mobile stress management encouraging bodily reflection.
10. [**"Transferring Qualities from Horseback Riding to Design"**](https://doi.org/10.1145/1868914.1868973) — Höök (NordiCHI 2010) — 221 citations. Uses equestrian interaction as a model for subtle somatic communication — methodologically the earliest explicit soma design paper.
11. [**"A Glass Box Approach to Adaptive Hypermedia"**](https://doi.org/10.1007/BF01099820) — Höök et al. (UMUAI, 1996) — 217 citations. Early career foundation: adaptive systems that maintain user transparency and control.
12. [**"HCI and Affective Health"**](https://doi.org/10.1145/3290605.3300475) — Sanches, Janson, Karpashevich, et al. (CHI 2019) — 244 citations. Major survey charting a decade of body/health-focused HCI research.

## Why Interesting

Höök represents the most successful case of **philosophical pivot in HCI** — taking a dense phenomenological/pragmatist philosophical concept (somaesthetics) and turning it into a concrete, teachable, community-building design methodology. This is rare. Most researchers either stay theoretical (Dourish's embodied interaction is brilliant but abstract) or stay practical (building systems without theoretical grounding). Höök built the bridge.

For Amber, she is the closest existing precedent for what Amber is trying to do with "Somaesthetic Realities," more-than-human design, and design-based research at the intersection of philosophy and practice. The intellectual DNA is shared. The career pattern is instructive.

The 2025 SIGCHI awards sweep (Lifetime Service + two Special Recognitions from her own lab + two Outstanding Dissertations from KTH affiliates) confirms that soma design is not a fringe methodology anymore — it has become a mainstream HCI paradigm, and Höök built it. That is the scope of what field-building can achieve in 15–20 years.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
**Philosophy-to-practice bridging at scale.** Höök's core intellectual move is finding a philosophical framework deep enough to generate research for decades — Shusterman's somaesthetics — and operationalizing it into a methodology. This is not shallow citation. She spent years connecting soma design to felt sense, interoception, and somatic disciplines (feldenkrais, pilates, movement arts) to make the philosophy designable. The conceptual bet: *the body knows things the mind doesn't, and this knowledge is designable.*

**Field declaration via definitive text.** The 2018 MIT Press book is not a summary of research — it is a disciplinary declaration. By writing the book, Höök claims soma design as a field with its own ontology, epistemology, and methodology. The naming move ("somaesthetic interaction design") is a territorial act: it says this is a thing, it has a name, I named it, here is its founding document.

**Strong concepts as knowledge infrastructure.** The "Strong Concepts" paper (with Löwgren, 2012) is actually the methodological backbone of all soma design knowledge production. By defining a knowledge contribution mode intermediate between examples and theory, Höök gave the entire design research community a vocabulary for claiming intellectual contribution. This is meta-level genius: before you can build a field, you need a way to articulate what counts as knowledge in that field.

### Career Architecture
**Institutional migration at the right moments.** SICS (1990s-2000s) → Stockholm University (2003) → KTH (2012). Each move gave her more resources and visibility at exactly the moment she needed them. KTH in 2012 was the platform required for the 2018 book and subsequent community building.

**Coalition papers as territorial claims.** The "Embracing First-Person Perspectives" paper (21 authors, 2018) is a community manifesto in paper form — it's not one researcher's findings but an entire community declaring its identity. This is field-building via authorship coalition. 21 co-authors means 21 researchers publicly identifying as soma design researchers.

**Journal editorship as community infrastructure.** Becoming Editor-in-Chief of TOCHI (2018–2024) and tripling submission rates while doubling impact factor is not just service work — it builds institutional power and positions soma design at the center of HCI knowledge production. If you control the top journal, your community's work gets the best platform.

### Methodology Signature
**Somatic practice as design research.** The most radical methodological move: requiring designers to practice feldenkrais, pilates, or movement arts as part of the research process. Not as inspiration, but as epistemic method. This imports embodied knowledge from movement sciences into design research — a cross-discipline poaching that no one in HCI had done before.

**First-person methods.** Research through one's own body — attending to felt sense during design, treating the researcher's bodily experience as primary data. This is genuinely strange in an HCI field dominated by third-person empirical methods (user studies, controlled experiments). Höök normalized it.

**Exemplars as theory.** The Soma Mat, Breathing Light, Affective Diary are not just design artifacts — they are theoretical claims made material. Each system is an argument about what interaction can be when the body is centered. This is how soma design contributes theory: through design, not just text.

### Network Strategy
**Mentorship of feminist extension.** The most recent generation of KTH soma design researchers — Nadia Campo Woytuk, Jooyoung Park — are explicitly feminist and posthumanist. Höök's mentorship of this direction (and the community's recognition of it via 2025 SIGCHI awards) suggests she is consciously extending soma design into critical theory territory. She is building the next generation of soma design *and* giving it a political valence.

**Strategic deployment across venue types.** CHI for impact, DIS for theory, TEI for materials, NordiCHI for community, TOCHI for permanence. Each venue serves a different function in the community-building strategy. Her keynoting pattern covers the full circuit of the embodied HCI world.

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **1996: PhD on transparent adaptive systems** — established technical credibility and the central thesis (give users control, maintain transparency) that would later appear as first-person soma design methods
2. **1997: ERCIM Cor Baayen Fellowship** — European recognition that positioned her as a researcher to watch; opened international network
3. **Early 2000s: Affective computing pivot** — moved from information navigation to emotion and body awareness; the crucial direction change
4. **2010: "Horseback Riding" paper (NordiCHI)** — first explicit soma design paper; used her own embodied practice as design research material. Career-defining methodological declaration.
5. **2012: "Strong Concepts" paper with Löwgren** — built the knowledge infrastructure that would allow soma design to claim theoretical contributions; 793 citations confirms the reach
6. **2012: Joined KTH** — institutional platform that provided resources for the community-building phase
7. **2016: "Somaesthetic Appreciation Design" (CHI)** — the paper that named soma design explicitly; planted the flag
8. **2018: MIT Press book + "Embracing First-Person" paper (21 authors)** — dual-move: book declares the field, manifesto claims the community. Both in the same year. Maximally coordinated.
9. **2018: Became Editor-in-Chief of TOCHI** — community infrastructure move; positioned soma design at the center of HCI knowledge production
10. **2025: SIGCHI Lifetime Service Award + PhD student recognitions** — vindication of the full strategy; soma design is now HCI mainstream

### What Amber Can Learn
- **Name the field first.** Before the book, before the community, you need a name that claims territory. "Somaesthetic interaction design" = "somaesthetic realities" → Amber could benefit from explicit naming moves in her own work.
- **Build the knowledge infrastructure before building the field.** The "Strong Concepts" paper (2012) preceded the book (2018) by 6 years. First establish how knowledge will be measured, then produce knowledge in that mode.
- **Use your own practice as research data.** Höök used horseback riding as a design research site. Amber's HoloKit development, his teaching practice, and his equestrian-equivalent embodied practices are legitimate research data, not peripheral biography.
- **Coalition papers as community formation.** A 21-author manifesto is a community *becoming visible to itself*. If Amber wants to build the more-than-human/somaesthetic design community in Asia, a multi-author paper might be the vehicle.
- **Mentor the next generation in the direction you want the field to go.** Höök's feminist extension isn't accidental — she mentored it. Strategic mentorship shapes fields.
