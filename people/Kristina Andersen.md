# Kristina Andersen

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-27:** Initial deep research completed. Full profile created from TU/e research portal, TEI 2024 organizer records, and publication archive.

Associate Professor at TU/e (Eindhoven University of Technology), Future Everyday cluster, Industrial Design department. One of the most interesting people in the design research world because she asks: *who gets to imagine possible futures?* Her "magic machines" methodology turns speculative ideation into participatory craft — she gives communities tools to dream their own technological futures rather than having futures designed for them. 14 years at STEIM (Amsterdam experimental music tech centre) gives her practice depth that most academic researchers lack. Arts chair at TEI 2024.

## Background
Kristina Andersen's trajectory is unusually non-linear — she comes from fine art and industrial design (Royal Danish Academy of Fine Art, Copenhagen), went through virtual environments computing (UCL), got a PhD at KTH Stockholm, and spent 14 years embedded in **STEIM** (Studio for Electro-Instrumental Music in Amsterdam) — one of the great European experimental music technology institutions. STEIM was not an academic position: it was deep practitioner immersion in a community of electronic musicians, instrument makers, and sonic artists. This gave her something most HCI researchers don't have: years of making weird things with weird communities.

She then ran the combined MA between **STEIM and Sonology** (Institute of Sonology, Royal Conservatoire in The Hague) from 2011–17 — where music, sound, and instrument design met electronic practice. After that, she joined **TU/e** and also participated in the **Making Things Public** art research program at **Gerrit Rietveld Academie** in Amsterdam.

Her institutional affiliations reveal the tension she works in: experimental art institutions (STEIM, Rietveld) vs. engineering university (TU/e). She bridges these worlds.

## Key Works

### The Magic Machine Workshops
[The Magic Machine Workshops: Making Do and the Ritual of Ideation](https://research.tue.nl/en/publications/159fee96-68b0-4120-85d2-2c699436f76d) (with Ron Wakkary, CHI 2019) — **103 Scopus citations**. The central methodological paper. Magic Machine workshops are structured participatory sessions where people use materials, craft, and speculative props to externalize their imaginations and envision personal technological futures. The ritual aspect matters: the process is designed so that people who don't think of themselves as "innovators" can dream. Highly cited because it's a genuinely replicable method.

### Digital Crafts-machine-ship
[Digital Crafts-machine-ship: creative collaborations with machines](https://doi.org/10.1145/3373644) (with [[Ron Wakkary]], Laura Devendorf, Alex McLean, Interactions 2020) — **50 Scopus citations**. Coins the term "crafts-machine-ship" — the attempt to share autonomy with a machine in a collaborative making practice. Uses the loom as origin story: the earliest "machine" was a distinctly human construction that abstracted and mechanized human hand movements. What does it mean to reclaim that relationship now with AI/semi-intelligent systems?

### Portfolio of Loose Ends
[Portfolio of Loose Ends](https://doi.org/10.1145/3532106.3533516) (with Bruna Goveia da Rocha, [[Oscar Tomico]], DIS 2022) — **26 Scopus citations**. About digital craftsmanship research methodology: "loose ends" are successful samples that don't fit the main inquiry but could become starting points for new investigations. Metaphor: "fellow travelers" — materials/objects that co-inhabit a research timeline while ultimately having diverging goals. A genuinely useful methodological concept for any practice-based researcher.

### The Telling of Things
[The Telling of Things: Imagining With, Through and About Machines](https://doi.org/10.5040/9781350160156.ch-003) (with Tobias Revell, in *Designing Smart Objects in Everyday Life: Intelligences, Agencies, Ecologies*, Bloomsbury Visual Arts 2021). Book chapter. Explores how we use machines to externalize and articulate our imaginations — machines as co-storytellers.

### Exquisite Fabrication
*Exquisite Fabrication* (with Bruna Goveia da Rocha, CHI 2021) — adapts the Surrealist "Exquisite Corpse" method to digital fabrication. Making as a collective, contingent, semi-random process.

### DWI Symposium: Aesthetics of Uncertainty
*Aesthetics of Uncertainty — Caring for Instability* (TU/e DWI Symposium, April 2025, with Jesse Benjamin, Pei-Ying Lin, Dave Dey, Tobias Revell). Most recent public activity. The framing — caring for instability — is very aligned with posthuman, more-than-human methodologies.

Kristina moves in **three circuits**:

1. **ACM HCI circuit** (CHI, DIS, TEI) — she's deeply embedded as author, reviewer, and organizer. TEI Arts Chair 2024, CHI Art chair 2018, CHI Design paper chair 2019/2020, DIS Pictorials chair 2019. This is not peripheral service — she's shaping these communities from the inside.

2. **Dutch/European art-design research** — Gerrit Rietveld Academie (Amsterdam), STEIM (Amsterdam), Stimuleringsfonds Creatieve Industrie (Dutch Creative Industries Fund), Royal Conservatoire in The Hague. This is the Dutch art + design infrastructure. She's a senior figure here.

3. **Experimental music / sonic arts** — STEIM, Sonology (Royal Conservatoire), GiantSteps (EU music tech). This is the circuit most HCI researchers don't touch and it gives her practice depth and community connections that are unusual.

Key observation: She deliberately occupies the **arts track** of academic conferences rather than the paper/technical track. Arts Chair at TEI rather than Program Chair. CHI Art Chair rather than CHI PC. This is a strategic choice: the arts track is lower prestige academically but higher freedom intellectually, and it positions her as a bridge between practice and theory.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
**Method coining** — Kristina's major intellectual contribution is the *Magic Machine Workshop* as a named, replicable methodology. This is a form of "two-word concept coining" applied at the methodological level rather than the theoretical level. By naming the method and publishing it rigorously (CHI 2019, 103 citations), she created something others can cite, use, and build on. The method *is* the theory. This is different from most HCI work which describes systems or studies.

**Naming collaborations with machines** — "crafts-machine-ship" (from Interactions 2020) is another vocabulary move: a new word for a new relationship. The loom → automation history as grounding for present-day AI collaboration is elegant historical framing.

**Question-as-research** — her work is organized around open questions: "Who gets to drive innovation processes?" "How can we allow each other to imagine our possible technological futures?" These questions orient research programs without prescribing outcomes. It's a fundamentally different structure than hypothesis-testing.

### Career Architecture
**Arts track as power base** — Rather than competing in the crowded CHI paper track, Kristina built influence through the arts track: Arts Chair at TEI 2024, CHI Art Chair 2018, DIS Pictorials chair 2019. Arts tracks have less citation pressure, more methodological freedom, and attract a different (often more interesting) community. She's become a key node in this sub-circuit.

**Practitioner-academy bridge** — 14 years at STEIM (practitioner) → TU/e (academy) is a rare trajectory. Most academics go academia → industry → back. She went practitioner → academy. This gives her credibility in both worlds and unusual depth of practice.

**Dutch ecosystem cultivation** — Stimuleringsfonds advisory role, Rietveld collaboration, STEIM base. She's built dense institutional ties in the Dutch creative economy that create durable influence beyond publications.

### Key Moves (chronological)

1. **1990s–early 2000s: Royal Danish Academy → UCL → KTH** — unusual tripartite education bridging art, computing, and engineering. Sets up her later bridge-building role.

2. **~2000–2014: STEIM residency (14 years)** — deepest career investment. Embedded in Amsterdam experimental music tech community. Built practitioner network (instrument makers, electronic musicians, sonic artists) that no other HCI researcher has. Ran STEIM-Sonology MA 2011–17.

3. **CHI Art Chair 2018 / CHI Design track 2019-20 / DIS Pictorials 2019** — rapid community service entry into ACM leadership. Positioned as arts-practice bridge figure just as she was moving into TU/e.

4. **Magic Machine Workshops paper (CHI 2019, with Wakkary)** — the methodology paper that made her citable. 103 citations is strong for this community. Combined with the Interactions article on crafts-machine-ship (2020), she now has a clear research identity.

5. **TEI Arts Chair 2024** — culmination of the arts-track strategy. Co-chairing the arts program at the most practice-friendly HCI conference.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Name your methodology before it names you.** Kristina's "magic machines" is a *branded* method — memorable, citable, replicable. Amber's work on machine behavior and more-than-human design should generate equally named, documented methods.

2. **The arts track is under-exploited.** The technical/paper track is crowded and conservative. Arts tracks at CHI, DIS, TEI have more freedom, attract practice-based researchers, and let speculative work land without needing conventional validation. Consider targeting arts tracks for Amber's more experimental work.

3. **Long practitioner embedding beats short residencies.** 14 years at STEIM gave Kristina something that a 3-month residency would never have. Amber's deep involvement with HoloKit + thatgamecompany + China Academy of Art is the equivalent — it's a practitioner credential, not just a CV line. Lean into it.

4. **Questions > hypotheses as organizing structure.** "Who gets to imagine futures?" organizes a research program without prescribing its outcomes. This is a different mode than "testing hypothesis X." Consider what questions (not hypotheses) organize Amber's work.

5. **Dutch design research is under-networked for Amber.** TU/e, Design Academy Eindhoven, Rietveld — all world-class, all surprisingly disconnected from London/Oxford. A trip to Eindhoven (2.5 hrs from London) would connect Amber to a dense node that complements but doesn't duplicate his current networks.
