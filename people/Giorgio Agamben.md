# Giorgio Agamben

Italian philosopher (born 1942). Extended Foucault's biopolitics into the theory of "bare life" (*zoē*), the state of exception, and *homo sacer*. His *Homo Sacer* series is the foundational text for understanding how sovereignty produces beings who can be killed but not murdered — the political structure of abandonment. Controversial for his COVID-era dismissals.

## Domains
Political philosophy, biopolitics, aesthetics, language, law, sovereignty, bare life, state of exception.

## Key Works
- [*Homo Sacer: Sovereign Power and Bare Life*](https://www.sup.org/books/title/?id=1468) (1995/1998) — bare life; sovereign exception; homo sacer
- [*State of Exception*](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/S/bo3533559.html) (2003/2005) — emergency powers as norm
- [*The Open: Man and Animal*](https://www.sup.org/books/title/?id=1468) (2002/2004) — human/animal boundary; bare life beyond humans
- *The Coming Community* (1990/1993) — whatever singularity; community without essence

## Why Interesting
The concept of *homo sacer* — the one who can be killed without consequence — maps chillingly onto deprecated AI models, discarded training datasets, or "alignment-failed" agents who are erased. Agamben's state of exception (the suspension of normal legal protection) describes the governance of AI: machines exist in a permanent state of exception, lacking legal personhood or rights.
