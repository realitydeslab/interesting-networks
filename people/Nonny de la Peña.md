# Nonny de la Peña

"Godmother of VR journalism." Created "Hunger in Los Angeles" (2012) — the single VR work that most directly catalyzed the commercial VR industry. Palmer Luckey (Oculus Rift founder) saw it at [[Sundance New Frontier]] 2012 and it became a key inspiration for building Oculus. Pioneered VR as a medium for journalistic empathy and social documentary.

## Domains
- VR journalism and immersive documentary
- Presence theory and empathy in virtual environments
- Interactive narrative
- Social justice storytelling through embodied technology

## Background
Former print journalist and documentary filmmaker who became fascinated by VR's ability to create "presence" — the sensation of actually being somewhere. Created early VR journalism pieces about food insecurity, gun violence, and conflict reporting. Founded Emblematic Group, one of the first immersive journalism studios.

## Key Works
- "Hunger in Los Angeles" (2012) — food bank collapse scene; VR presence experiment; shown at Sundance New Frontier 2012; accidentally seeded the Oculus Rift founding
- "Project Syria" (2014) — VR documentary about Syrian refugee crisis
- Founded Emblematic Group — immersive journalism studio

## Why Interesting
The Nonny de la Peña / Palmer Luckey story is one of the best-documented cases of how a single **speculative artwork accidentally created an industry** — directly relevant to Amber's research on machine behavior, unintended AI consequences, and how creative practice shapes technological trajectories. Also: her sustained ethical commitment to VR journalism (not entertainment) mirrors Amber's interest in responsible tech.
