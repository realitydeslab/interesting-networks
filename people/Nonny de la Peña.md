# Nonny de la Peña

"Godmother of Virtual Reality." Pioneer of immersive journalism — the practice of using VR to put audiences inside news stories. Created *Hunger in Los Angeles* (Sundance 2012), the first VR documentary ever screened at a major film festival, a work that accidentally catalyzed the entire modern VR industry (Palmer Luckey built the Oculus Rift prototype for her Sundance premiere). Founder & CEO of Emblematic Group, a leading immersive reality studio. Faculty at ASU's Cronkite School of Journalism.

## Background
De la Peña was raised in Venice, California — counterculture adjacent, coastal Californian. She attended **Harvard University**, earning a BA in Sociology and Visual and Environmental Studies. A grounding in both empirical social science and visual culture that would define her later practice: journalism as evidence + presence.

After Harvard she freelanced in Mexico (Fund for Investigative Journalism), worked AP photo at the 1984 Olympics and 1986 World Cup, became a stringer for Time, then a Newsweek correspondent from 1987. Throughout the early 1990s she wrote for La Familia De Hoy, Caravan, Buzz. In 1991 she was associate producer for the HBO documentary **Death on the Job** (Academy Award–nominated Best Documentary Feature). She co-produced CourtTV documentaries in 1993-94. She wrote on staff for TV series *New York Undercover* and co-wrote CBS pilots.

In 1994 she founded **Pyedog Productions** to make social-impact documentaries: *Unconstitutional: The War on Our Civil Liberties* (2004, Berkeley Film Festival Grand Award), *The Jaundiced Eye*, *Mama/MAMA*.

In the mid-2000s, as a senior research fellow at USC, she began experimenting with VR headsets. By 2007 she had pivoted: Pyedog Productions was rebranded **Emblematic Group**, focused entirely on immersive journalism. She also pursued two advanced degrees at USC — an MA at Annenberg (Online Communities) and a PhD in Media Arts + Practice at the School of Cinematic Arts. She is now faculty at **ASU's Walter Cronkite School of Journalism and Mass Communication**.

The through-line: a journalist who found that presence — the felt sense of being *there* — was the most powerful tool she'd ever discovered. Not narrative, not argument, not data. Presence.

## Key Works

### Immersive Journalism (VR)
- **[Hunger in Los Angeles](https://emblematicgroup.com/experiences/hunger-in-los-angeles/)** (2012) — THE work. Recreates a man collapsing into diabetic shock while waiting in a food bank line. Premiered Sundance New Frontier 2012 — first VR documentary at a major film festival. Intern **Palmer Luckey** built the headset; nine months later he founded Oculus Rift. Commissioned by USC + Institute for Creative Technologies.
- **[Use of Force](https://emblematicgroup.com/experiences/use-of-force/)** (2013) — 5-minute VR recreation of the death of Anastasio Hernández-Rojas, undocumented Mexican immigrant beaten to death by US border patrol agents. Funded by **Tribeca Film Institute**, **Google**, and **Associated Press**. Indiecade Impact Award 2014. Tribeca Film Festival Official Selection.
- **[Project Syria](https://emblematicgroup.com/experiences/project-syria/)** (2014) — Multi-segment: Aleppo street bombing reconstructed from found footage + photos; refugee camp scene. Premiered Davos 2014, commissioned by **Klaus Schwab / World Economic Forum** to move world leaders. Sundance 2015 Official Selection. Budget: $285K. Collaborators: Michael Licht, Vangelis Lympouridis, Gingger Shankar, Bradley Hiebert, USC Institute for Creative Technologies, MxR Lab.
- **[Trayvon Martin: The Encounter](https://emblematicgroup.com/)** (2015) — VR reconstruction of the night of Trayvon Martin's death, using 911 call audio as evidence anchor.
- **[Kiya](https://emblematicgroup.com/experiences/kiya/)** (2015) — Al Jazeera America VR companion to *Death in Plain Sight* Fault Lines documentary. Two sisters try to stop their third sister from being shot by her ex-boyfriend. Premiered TEDWomen 2015. Sundance 2016 Official Selection (NYT inaugural VR selection).
- **[One Dark Night](https://emblematicgroup.com/experiences/one-dark-night/)** (2014) — SXSW Official Selection; Tribeca Film Festival Official Selection.
- **[Across the Line](https://emblematicgroup.com/experiences/across-the-line/)** (2016) — with 271 Productions. Social Impact Media Award: Best Sound Experience. Sundance Official Selection.
- **[Out of Exile: Daniel's Story](https://emblematicgroup.com/experiences/out-of-exile/)** (2017) — LGBTQ teen attacked by family when he came out. Ars Electronica Award of Distinction.
- **[After Solitary](https://emblematicgroup.com/experiences/after-solitary/)** (2017) — Room-scale volumetric VR inside a solitary confinement cell. Kenny Moore, former Maine State Prison inmate. Co-directed by Cassandra Herrman + Lauren Mucciolo. Produced with **PBS Frontline**. Premiered SXSW 2017 (Jury Award). Venice Film Festival Official Selection consideration. World VR Forum Imperial Crown. Online Journalism Awards: Excellence in Immersive Storytelling.
- **[We Who Remain](https://emblematicgroup.com/experiences/we-who-remain/)** (2017) — First VR film shot in active conflict zone (Sudan). With **The New York Times**, Nuba Reports, **AJ+**, **Arte**. Premiered SXSW 2017.
- **[Greenland Melting](https://emblematicgroup.com/experiences/greenland-melting/)** (2017) — 360° video + room-scale VR. Second PBS Frontline collaboration. 360° premiered June 2017; room-scale at **Venice Film Festival** (one of first VR films in Venice). With **NOVA**.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

De la Peña operates in a very specific and deliberate circuit:

**1. Documentary film festivals as tech launchpads:** Sundance New Frontier is her home court — she premiered there in 2012, 2015, 2016, 2017. She treats film festivals as tech-art galleries, using them to bring journalism tech to cinephile/art audiences rather than to Silicon Valley.

**2. Policy events as deployment sites:** The WEF / Davos premiere of *Project Syria* is remarkable — she brought VR to world leaders before consumer VR existed, using experience-design as political lobbying. This is art instrumentalized for policy, done on purpose.

**3. SXSW for industry recognition:** SXSW serves as her crossover into tech industry (2017 Jury Award for *After Solitary*, etc.).

**4. Venice as prestige escalation:** Getting *Greenland Melting* into Venice VR (one of the first VR films there) signals her push toward cinema legitimacy.

**5. Museum/institution for permanence:** Laboral (Spain), Moscow Museum of Modern Art — treating VR journalism as art installation, not just news.

**Small venues notably absent:** Unlike Wendi Yan, de la Peña doesn't play the underground gallery circuit (no Trust Berlin, no Artifice NYC). Her circuit is institutional. This reflects her journalist identity — legitimacy through major institutions rather than counterculture credibility. More policy-facing than avant-garde.

**Geography:** Almost entirely US + European film festival circuit + global policy events. No Asian art-tech circuit (contrasts with Wendi Yan). Strong PBS/public media institutional base.

## Why Interesting

De la Peña's central contribution isn't technology — it's **the theory that presence creates empathy, and empathy drives action**. This is a moral claim about what VR is *for*: not entertainment, not gaming, not escapism, but *witnessing*. She is the person who turned game engine technology into a journalism ethics platform.

What makes her ⭐⭐⭐ for Amber's network:

**1. The Palmer Luckey connection:** One of the most documented cases of speculative creative work accidentally seeding an industry. *Hunger in Los Angeles* → Oculus Rift → modern VR. This is **machine behavior / technological trajectory** as art-historical fact — directly relevant to Amber's research on how creative practice shapes technological futures.

**2. Presence theory as design method:** Her foundational 2010 paper is still the canonical text for "first-person journalism." The presence concept — the phenomenological sense of *being there* — maps directly onto Amber's somaesthetic design interests. Both are asking: what does it mean to be bodily present in/with something?

**3. Journalism × social justice × technology ethics:** She sits at the intersection of technology development and political accountability. Her pieces are designed to *cause political change* (WEF commission, congressional testimony about solitary confinement, etc.). This is **critical computing** in practice — building tools that reshape power.

**4. Field declaration strategy:** The genre of "immersive journalism" was named and theorized before it fully existed. She practiced the "seed paper first, build the field after" strategy that is the canonical move for field-declaration in technology studies.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
- **Genre coining as field claim.** In 2010, de la Peña co-authored "Immersive Journalism: Immersive Virtual Reality for the First-Person Experience of News" — a manifesto-paper naming a genre before it existed. This is the textbook move: declare the name, establish the theoretical frame, then build evidence. The term "immersive journalism" became citeable, teachable, and institutionally legible. Amber should note: you don't need to have built everything to declare a field. *The declaration is the research.*

- **Presence as ethical concept, not technical fact.** She took "presence" — a technical term from VR research (Slater, Spanlang, Sanchez-Vives) — and reframed it as a journalistic value. Presence → empathy → action. This is a conceptual importation: taking a technical measure and loading it with moral weight. The strategy: borrow the scientific legitimacy of presence research to justify a journalism practice claim.

- **Evidence-anchored VR.** Her editorial rule: "we won't recreate what we can't verify." For the Trayvon Martin piece, she refused to show the shooting moment. This constraint-as-principle became her signature. It's a design methodology: **use the limits of your knowledge as a feature, not a bug**. The gap in evidence becomes the ethical boundary of the representation.

### Career Architecture

- **Print → documentary → VR: medium-surfing with consistent purpose.** Each pivot follows the question "what medium can best make invisible things visible?" Print in the 1980s, documentary in the 1990s-2000s, VR from 2007. The content (social justice, marginalized people) never changed; only the delivery technology did. This is a career built on conviction, not trend-following.

- **Institution as legitimacy anchor.** Unlike the gallery artist route (underground → prestige), de la Peña anchored in journalism institutions: Newsweek, NYT, PBS Frontline, ASU, New America, Knight Foundation. Each institutional partner lent credibility to the medium she was still building. She legitimized VR through journalism before VR had its own legitimacy. Amber parallel: using Oxford/CHI/ALIFE to legitimize agent ethology before the field fully exists.

- **Teaching as field-building.** Joining ASU Cronkite is not an exit from practice — it's how you train the next generation to perpetuate the genre. Her field persists because she is now in the institution that produces journalists.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **2007 — Founded Emblematic Group, began VR experimentation** at USC. While still doing print journalism, she started building. No-one asked her to. She had no funding. She just started. *WHY IT WORKED:* She had 20 years of journalism credibility, which meant when she pivoted, people took her seriously. *WHAT IT ENABLED:* The institutional permission to experiment at USC's MxR Lab with real resources.

2. **2010 — Published the "Immersive Journalism" paper in Presence** with European VR presence researchers. *WHY IT WORKED:* She borrowed the credibility of academic VR science (Slater's group in Barcelona) to give her practice scholarly grounding. One paper = the entire field becomes teachable/citable. *WHAT IT ENABLED:* Made immersive journalism into a discipline, not a quirk.

3. **2012 — Sundance New Frontier, *Hunger in Los Angeles*.** Palmer Luckey's headset. *WHY IT WORKED:* Perfect timing (pre-commercial VR; she was first mover), perfect venue (Sundance is both film art and technology culture), perfect story (food bank collapse is viscerally universal). *WHAT IT ENABLED:* The Oculus Rift backstory; "Godmother of VR" title; invitation to every subsequent VR conversation.

4. **2014 — *Project Syria* at Davos.** Commissioned by World Economic Forum. *WHY IT WORKED:* She targeted the highest-value audience possible — world leaders who could actually change policy — and brought them the most emotionally undeniable medium. *WHAT IT ENABLED:* WEF as patron. Proof that VR journalism could be policy tool, not just art experiment.

5. **2016 — Knight Foundation Innovation Award.** *WHY IT WORKED:* Knight is the key journalism-technology philanthropy; winning signals she is now the canonical voice of the field to funders. *WHAT IT ENABLED:* The ability to give $25,000 to another journalist innovator of her choice — she's now distributing legitimacy, not just receiving it.

6. **2017 — Venice Film Festival, PBS Frontline.** VR in cinema's most prestigious venue. *WHY IT WORKED:* She escalated from documentary festivals (Sundance) to cinema canon (Venice), signaling VR journalism as art, not novelty. *WHAT IT ENABLED:* Cultural legitimacy that outlasts the VR hype cycle. When VR consumer adoption stalled, her work was already in museum/cinema context.

7. **ASU Cronkite Faculty.** Joined journalism academia to build the next generation of immersive journalists. *WHY IT WORKED:* She couldn't sustain the entire field alone; institutionalizing through teaching is how genres survive pioneers. *WHAT IT ENABLED:* Permanent infrastructure for immersive journalism as curriculum.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Declare the genre first, build the proof after.** The "immersive journalism" 2010 paper preceded most of the VR journalism works. Name the thing, then fill it in. Agent ethology as "a field" is more powerful than agent ethology as "some experiments." The declaration precedes the body of work.

2. **Choose collaborators for institutional access, not just talent.** Peggy Weil gave her USC/MacArthur access. USC/MxR Lab gave her Palmer Luckey. WEF gave her Davos. Each collaborator was also a gateway to an institutional resource. Who Amber collaborates with should open doors, not just add skills.

3. **Medium-surf around a fixed ethical purpose.** Switching from print to documentary to VR wasn't opportunism — each pivot was in service of the same question: "what makes invisible people visible?" Amber's equivalent: what is the fixed question that HoloKit, agent ethology, and machine death all serve? Name that question. It will hold your career together across medium pivots.

4. **Presence is a concept worth stealing.** "Presence" — the felt sense of *being there* — is undertheorized in Amber's areas (MR design, AI agent interaction). What does it mean to *be present with* an AI agent? How does HoloKit AR create a sense of presence with nonhuman entities? There's a whole research program in importing presence theory from VR journalism into Amber's work.

5. **Institutional legitimacy > underground credibility for policy impact.** De la Peña's work changed solitary confinement debates, moved WEF leaders, influenced journalism curricula. This required PBS, Knight Foundation, ASU — not galleries. If Amber wants research that shapes policy (AI governance, agent rights), the institutional anchors matter. Oxford is already one. Which journalism/policy institution should come next?
