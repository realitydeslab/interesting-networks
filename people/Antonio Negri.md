# Antonio Negri

**Toni Negri** (1 August 1933 – 16 December 2023) — Italian Marxist political philosopher and the defining theoretical architect of *operaismo* (Italian workerism) and later autonomist Marxism. Co-author with [[Michael Hardt]] of the *Empire* trilogy and five other books — the most ambitious Marxist political theory project of the late 20th century. Developed foundational concepts now standard in political philosophy: constituent power, immaterial labor, the multitude, biopolitical production. Imprisoned 1979–1983 on disputed terrorism charges, exiled to France 1983–1997, lived and wrote until his death at 90 in Paris. With [[Mario Tronti]]'s death in August 2023, their two deaths within four months ended the founding generation of operaismo.

## Background

**Origins:** Born in Padua (Veneto, Italy) on 1 August 1933. His father, a communist worker from Bologna, died when Negri was 2; his mother, a teacher, was the primary influence. He was raised partly in Catholic Italy; in the early 1950s he participated in the Italian Catholic youth movement (GIAC), then converted to communism after working at a kibbutz in Israel in 1955. Joined the Italian Socialist Party (PSI) in 1956. This early trajectory — Catholic formation → kibbutz encounter → Marxist radicalization — is unusual and helps explain his later synthesis of Catholic traditions of community, Spinozist immanence, and Marxist political economy.

**Education:** Laurea in Philosophy, University of Padua, 1956. Immediately hired as a professor of *dottrina dello stato* (state doctrine / philosophy of law) at Padua. Rose to full professor of political science — one of the most important radical intellectuals in Italian academia in the 1960s–70s.

**Intellectual Trajectory — Five Periods:**

**1. Early operaismo / workerism (1961–1969)**
In 1961, co-founded (with [[Mario Tronti]], Raniero Panzieri, Romano Alquati, Alberto Asor Rosa, and others) the journal *Quaderni Rossi* (Red Notebooks) — the founding document of Italian operaismo. The project: use Marx's *Capital* as a tool for analyzing the contemporary Italian factory system from the workers' perspective. In 1964, he and Tronti split from *Quaderni Rossi* to found *Classe Operaia* (Working Class). In 1968, Negri co-founded (with Alberto Asor Rosa) *Contropiano*, another influential journal.

Key operaismo innovations:
- **Workers' inquiry (conricerca):** Direct investigation of factory conditions, workers' struggles, class composition. Not social science from above but workers' self-research.
- **Class composition analysis:** Distinguish "technical composition" (how capital organizes work) from "political composition" (how workers self-organize resistance). The passage between them is always a recomposition.
- **Mass worker:** The dominant figure of 1960s Italian factories (Fiat, Petrolchimico Marghera). Homogeneous, de-skilled, assembly-line. Their collective power was the refusal: the wildcat strike, absenteeism, sabotage.

**2. Autonomia Operaia and the "Years of Lead" (1969–1979)**
After 1969 (the "Hot Autumn" of factory uprisings), Negri co-founded *Potere Operaio* (Workers' Power, 1967–1973) with Franco Piperno, Oreste Scalzone, [[Franco Berardi]] (Bifo), and [[Paolo Virno]]. When Potere Operaio dissolved in 1973, Negri became central to *Autonomia Operaia* (Workers' Autonomy, 1973–1979) — a decentralized network running pirate radio stations (Radio Alice in Bologna; Onda Rossa in Rome), publishing newspapers (*Rosso* in Milan, *I Volsci* in Rome).

Key theoretical innovation: the **social factory**. With the crisis of Fordism, capital extended factory logic to all of social life. The whole of society became a factory. Reproduction (domestic work, education, social care) was also production. The working class now included students, the unemployed, women doing domestic labor. The *refusal of work* (rifiuto del lavoro) became a political strategy: not just striking inside factories but refusing the entire organization of capitalist life.

**3. Prison, Parliament, and the Paris Exile (1979–1997)**
On April 7, 1979 — known as "April 7" — the Italian state carried out mass arrests of autonomists. Negri was arrested and charged with being the "moral brains" behind the Red Brigades, including the kidnapping and murder of Christian Democrat prime minister Aldo Moro (1978). Most serious charges were later dropped — a senior Red Brigades member testified that Negri "had nothing to do with the Red Brigades" — but the prosecution was widely seen as a political attack on radical theory. Michel Foucault commented: "Isn't he in jail simply for being an intellectual?" Gilles Deleuze and Félix Guattari co-signed the *Appel des intellectuels français contre la répression en Italie* (1977).

While imprisoned (Rebibbia prison, Rome), he wrote *The Savage Anomaly* (1981) — his reading of Spinoza. Michael Hardt later translated it into English (1991), writing the foreword — the beginning of their 40-year collaboration.

In 1983, elected to the Italian Parliament as a Radical Party MP — parliamentary immunity allowed release from prison. Before the immunity was lifted, he fled to France (with help from Félix Guattari), beginning 14 years of exile. The **[Mitterrand doctrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitterrand_doctrine)** (1985) protected him from extradition.

In Paris, taught at the **Collège international de philosophie** (co-founded by Jacques Derrida) and at **Université Paris VIII** (Vincennes/Saint-Denis) — the experimental post-May '68 university. Met Michael Hardt at the Collège in the early 1980s. Co-founded the journal *Futur Antérieur* (1989–1998) with [[Maurizio Lazzarato]], Yann Moulier Boutang.

**4. Empire and global recognition (1997–2009)**
Returned voluntarily to Italy in 1997 to serve his remaining sentence (which became house arrest, then conditional release in 2003). *Empire* (2000) was written across Italian house arrest and collaboration with Hardt at Duke — it became a sensation: translated into 24+ languages, hailed as "the Communist Manifesto of the 21st century," described by Žižek as "the most important work of left political theory in decades." *Multitude* (2004) and *Commonwealth* (2009) followed.

**5. Late work (2003–2023)**
After 2003 lived primarily in Paris, with his partner the French philosopher [[Judith Revel]]. He had a daughter, **Anna Negri**, a film director. Continued publishing prolifically in Italian and French. With Hardt: *Declaration* (2012, responding to Occupy) and *Assembly* (2017). Solo: *Spinoza for Our Time* (2013), *Marx and Foucault* (2017), *From the Factory to the Metropolis* (2018). Died December 16, 2023, in Paris, age 90.

## Key Works

### The Empire Trilogy (with [[Michael Hardt]])
- [*Labor of Dionysus: A Critique of the State-Form*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/labor-of-dionysus) (1994, University of Minnesota Press) — precursor to the trilogy; critiques the state as mediator of class struggle
- [*Empire*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674006713) (2000, Harvard University Press) — the defining work. Argues global sovereignty operates as a decentered, deterritorialized "Empire" — no longer centered in nation-states. Sovereignty now manages life (biopolitics); the "multitude" is the new political subject. Translated into 24+ languages. Hailed by Žižek as "the most important work of left political theory in decades"; praised by London Review of Books as "the most successful work of political theory to come from the Left for a generation."
- [*Multitude: War and Democracy in the Age of Empire*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/296416/multitude-by-michael-hardt-and-antonio-negri/) (2004, Penguin Press) — develops the concept of the multitude as an active political subject; analyzes "permanent global civil war"; argues for democratic alternatives.
- [*Commonwealth*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674060289) (2009, Harvard University Press) — completes the trilogy. Centers "the common" (natural resources + languages + knowledges + affects) as the basis for a communist politics. Introduces "altermodernity" vs. anti-modernity. Critiqued by David Harvey (on class and identity politics) and Alex Callinicos (from orthodox Marxist perspective).

### Second Diptych (with [[Michael Hardt]])
- *Declaration* (2012, Argo Navis) — short pamphlet responding to Occupy Wall Street and the Arab Spring; diagnoses four dominant subjectivities produced by neoliberalism: the indebted, the mediatized, the securitized, the represented
- [*Assembly*](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/assembly-9780190677961) (2017, Oxford University Press) — responds to movements of the 2010s (Occupy, BLM, climate, Podemos/Syriza). Argues for "entrepreneurship of the multitude."

### Solo Works (Selected)
- *Descartes politico, o della ragionevole ideologia* (1970) — political reading of Descartes
- *Il dominio e il sabotaggio* (1977) — domination and sabotage in the factory
- [*Marx Beyond Marx: Lessons on the Grundrisse*](https://www.autonomedia.org/products/marx-beyond-marx) (1979/trans 1984, Autonomedia/Pluto) — seminars on Marx's *Grundrisse* at the École Normale Supérieure. Key analysis of "real subsumption," "general intellect," and how the Grundrisse anticipates post-Fordist capitalism. Still widely assigned.
- [*The Savage Anomaly: The Power of Spinoza's Metaphysics and Politics*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/the-savage-anomaly) (Italian 1981, written in prison; trans. Michael Hardt, 1991, University of Minnesota Press) — his major Spinoza book. Reads Spinoza's *Ethics* as an anomaly within bourgeois philosophy — a materialism of immanence against transcendent sovereignty. Spinoza's *potentia* (productive power of the multitude) vs. *potestas* (constituted power, sovereignty). Philosophical engine of all subsequent work.
- [*Insurgencies: Constituent Power and the Modern State*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/insurgencies) (Italian 1992, trans. Maurizia Boscagli, 1999, University of Minnesota Press) — traces constituent power through Machiavelli, Harrington, Spinoza, Marx, and Lenin. The distinction between constituent power (the living creative power that makes constitutions) and constituted power (what is instituted) becomes central to all subsequent analysis.
- [*Time for Revolution*](https://www.bloomsbury.com/uk/time-for-revolution-9780826458452) (trans. 2003, Continuum/Bloomsbury) — translation of two Italian texts: *Trentatré lezioni su Lenin* (1972–73) + *Kairòs, Alma Venus, Multitudo* (2000)
- *Negri on Negri: In Conversation with Anne Dufourmantelle* (2004, Routledge) — extensive interview autobiography
- [*The Porcelain Workshop: For a New Grammar of Politics*](https://www.semiotexte.com/?p=607) (Italian 2006, trans. 2008, Semiotext(e)) — meditations on revolutionary language and new subjectivities
- [*Goodbye Mr. Socialism: Radical Politics in the 21st Century*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/Goodbye-Mr-Socialism/Antonio-Negri/9781416562702) (Italian 2006, trans. 2008, Seven Stories Press; with Raf Scelsi) — wide-ranging conversations on the state of the left
- [*Spinoza for Our Time: Politics and Postmodernity*](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/spinoza-for-our-time/9780231148818) (trans. 2013, Columbia University Press) — collected Spinoza essays
- [*Marx and Foucault: Essays, Volume 1*](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Marx+and+Foucault%3A+Essays%2C+Volume+1-p-9780745688466) (trans. 2017, Polity)
- [*From the Factory to the Metropolis: Essays, Volume 2*](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/From+the+Factory+to+the+Metropolis%3A+Essays%2C+Volume+2-p-9780745688558) (trans. 2018, Polity)

### Key Concepts Developed
- **Refusal of work** (rifiuto del lavoro) — workers' refusal of capitalist work as a political act
- **Social factory** — all of society as a site of value production, not just the factory
- **Immaterial labor** — labor that produces immaterial products: information, communication, relationships, affects (developed with [[Maurizio Lazzarato]])
- **Affective labor** — care work, relational work, producing social relationships
- **Constituent power** (potere costituente) — the living power that creates constitutions, always exceeding what it institutes
- **Multitude** — the new political subject: singularities that share a world but resist unification
- **Empire** — decentered, deterritorialized global sovereignty (distinct from nation-state imperialism)
- **Biopolitical production** — production of life itself, social relations, affects (transforms Foucault's biopolitics from discipline to productivity)
- **The common/commonwealth** — natural resources + language + affects + knowledge as basis for communist politics

## Awards & Recognition

Negri was institutionally marginalized — imprisoned, exiled, never awarded mainstream academic prizes. His recognition came in different forms:
- **Michel Foucault's defense**: "Isn't he in jail simply for being an intellectual?" — perhaps the most prestigious intellectual endorsement of the era.
- **The Appel des intellectuels français** (1977): Foucault, Sartre, Deleuze, Guattari co-signed the French intellectuals' petition against Italian political repression.
- **Mitterrand doctrine** (1985): France formally protected him from Italian extradition — recognition encoded in state policy.
- **Election to Italian Parliament** as Radical Party MP (1983): the Italian left's institutional protection strategy.
- *Empire* is one of the most cited works of political philosophy of the 2000s — assigned in political theory courses worldwide. Translated into 24+ languages including Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Arabic.
- Named as one of the 100 most influential public intellectuals (various lists, 2001–2010 era).

## Key Relationships & Collaborators

- **[[Michael Hardt]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — The collaboration. Met at the Collège international de philosophie, Paris, early 1980s. Hardt (young American Duke PhD student) translated *The Savage Anomaly* (1991) before they co-authored. Together wrote six books over 40 years — the most sustained radical theory collaboration of the era. Their intellectual relationship restructured global left political theory. Hardt is at Duke and still active.
- **[[Paolo Virno]]** ⭐⭐ — co-member of Potere Operaio, Autonomia Operaia. Co-edited *Luogo Comune* journal early 1990s. Developed related concepts: "general intellect," "virtuosity," "exodus," "post-Fordism." His *A Grammar of the Multitude* (2004) is an important counterpart to Empire/Multitude.
- **[[Maurizio Lazzarato]]** ⭐⭐ — younger Italian philosopher based in Paris; in Negri's orbit at Paris VIII. Extended "immaterial labor" in seminal 1996 essay "[Immaterial Labour](https://www.generation-online.org/c/fcimmateriallabour3.htm)" — probably the most widely cited essay from the autonomist tradition. Co-founded Futur Antérieur and Multitudes.
- **[[Sandro Mezzadra]]** ⭐⭐ — Italian political theorist (borders, migration). Close collaborator in later years. Co-author with Brett Neilson of *Border as Method* (2013) — extends Negri's frameworks to migration.
- **[[Mario Tronti]]** ⭐⭐ — co-founder of operaismo. They diverged in 1964: Tronti toward PCI and "autonomy of the political"; Negri toward autonomism. Both died in 2023 — four months apart. The two poles of operaismo ended together.
- **[[Franco Berardi]] (Bifo)** ⭐⭐ — early Potere Operaio comrade; founded Radio Alice (Bologna pirate radio, 1976); exiled after 1979; developed the "cognitariat" and "semiocapitalism." Still prolific.
- **[[Judith Revel]]** — French philosopher (Foucault scholar), Negri's partner and wife in later years. Professor at Paris I Panthéon-Sorbonne. Her work on Foucault and subjectivity directly connects to Negri's biopolitics framework.
- **Yann Moulier Boutang** — French economist/philosopher, theorized "cognitive capitalism." Co-founded Futur Antérieur and Multitudes.
- **Félix Guattari** — befriended during Paris exile. Helped facilitate the practical conditions of exile; key bridge between Italian autonomists and French radical philosophy. Major conceptual influence.
- **Gilles Deleuze** — deep philosophical influence. Deleuze's concepts of immanence, desire, and Spinoza reading were central to Negri's framework. "Postscript on the Societies of Control" (1992) was in direct dialogue with the autonomist tradition.
- **Michel Foucault** — biopolitics framework. Foucault's biopolitics = discipline and normalization of life; Negri's extension = also productivity and resistance. Foucault publicly defended Negri.
- **Romano Alquati** — early operaismo sociologist who developed the "workers' inquiry" method (conricerca).
- **Anne Dufourmantelle** — French psychoanalyst. Co-authored *Negri on Negri* interview book (2004).

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Negri's "venues" were political movements, jails, and journals — not academic conferences.

**Phase 1 (Italy, 1956–1979): The Factory Circuit**
- University of Padua (chair)
- Italian factories in the Veneto and Milan (workers' inquiry, direct organizing)
- Italian left journals (Quaderni Rossi, Classe Operaia, Contropiano)
- Far-left organizations (Potere Operaio, Autonomia)
- **Rebibbia prison, Rome** (1979–1983): wrote *The Savage Anomaly* in prison — the most productive "residency" of his career

**Phase 2 (Paris exile, 1983–1997): The French Philosophical Circuit**
- Collège international de philosophie — the most prestigious non-institutional philosophy space in France
- Université Paris VIII (Vincennes/Saint-Denis) — the radical post-1968 university
- UQAM (Montréal) — occasional lectures
- *Futur Antérieur* journal (1989–1998) — primary publishing venue
- Frankfurt Book Fair, international solidarity events

**Phase 3 (Global, 1997–2023): The Anti-Globalization/Academic Circuit**
- Duke University (through Hardt)
- Harvard University Press and Oxford University Press
- World Social Forum, Porto Alegre — key venue for the anti-globalization movement era
- European radical philosophy events (Paris, Berlin, Venice)
- Italian left conferences and book fairs
- *Multitudes* journal (Paris)

He deliberately avoided the mainstream Anglo-American academic conference circuit. His venues were radical presses, political movements, and continental European philosophy spaces.

## Why Interesting

**1. Critical computing / digital labor:** Negri's concept of *immaterial labor* is the direct philosophical ancestor of debates about digital labor, platform capitalism, and the gig economy. When we ask whether YouTube creators, Mechanical Turk workers, or social media users are "workers," we are asking a Negrean question. His 1980s analysis of the transition from mass worker to "socialized worker" to "immaterial worker" predicted the political economy of the contemporary internet with uncanny precision.

**2. The social factory → the platform economy:** The operaismo insight that all of society becomes a factory — that reproduction, care, communication are all forms of value production — maps directly onto platform capitalism. Instagram, TikTok, and Twitter are the social factory. Negri and Lazzarato's framework is the best existing theoretical tool for analyzing why social media companies are so valuable.

**3. Constituent power as AI governance framework:** Negri's distinction between constituent power (living, creative, productive) and constituted power (what is instituted, frozen, juridified) offers a rich framework for thinking about how AI systems codify particular power arrangements — and what kinds of "multitude" can contest them. The question of who gets to *constitute* the rules that govern AI is structurally identical to the constituent power question.

**4. Non-Western / non-Anglophone theory:** Negri's operaismo emerged from a specifically Italian tradition (Gramsci, Machiavelli, Spinoza, Italian factory system) — not from Anglophone political science or German Critical Theory. He offers a non-Frankfurt-School model for critical social theory.

**5. Journals as theoretical infrastructure:** Negri's model of founding journals as intellectual infrastructure (Quaderni Rossi → Classe Operaia → Contropiano → Futur Antérieur → Multitudes) is a sustained example of using publication infrastructure to create intellectual communities and test ideas in movement — highly relevant as a model for community-building in research.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
**Framework multiplication at maximum intensity.** Negri's method: take a major tradition (Marxist political economy), identify its blind spots, import an unexpected philosophical framework (Spinoza's ontology), generate a new vocabulary, and deploy that vocabulary to name phenomena others can't see. The sequence:
1. Operaismo → new framework for reading factory politics (Marx + workers' inquiry)
2. Spinoza → constituent power, potentia, immanence (power as productive rather than negative)
3. Foucault → biopolitics (extends labor analysis to life itself)
4. Deleuze → singularity, multiplicity (the multitude as non-unifiable)

Each import solved a problem the previous framework couldn't handle. This isn't eclecticism — it's systematic framework acquisition for strategic purposes.

**Two-word concept coining as intellectual infrastructure.** Every major theoretical move produced a citeable, two-word concept: "immaterial labor," "constituent power," "social factory," "affective labor," "biopolitical production," "cognitive capitalism." These became the vocabulary that structured an entire field. The concepts are memorable, strange enough to need explanation, but clear enough to be used by others.

### Career Architecture
**Journal-as-movement.** Every time Negri found himself in a new context, he co-founded a journal. Five journals across 40 years: *Quaderni Rossi* (1961), *Classe Operaia* (1964), *Contropiano* (1968), *Futur Antérieur* (1989), *Multitudes* (2000). Each journal was a node in a network, a place to test ideas, assemble collaborators, reach a specific audience. The journal came *before* the book — concepts were developed in journal form, then synthesized in books. The journal was the living tissue; the book was the archive.

**The collaborator who multiplied reach.** The Hardt partnership is a deliberate architecture decision. Hardt: younger, Anglophone, academic, Duke-based, with access to Harvard University Press and the English-speaking academy. Negri: older, Italian, exiled, with deep theoretical roots but limited English-language access. The collaboration was asymmetric and productive: Hardt brought the platform; Negri brought the depth. *Empire* could not have been written by either alone.

**Exile as advantage.** Negri's forced removal from Italy placed him in Paris at exactly the moment when Deleuze, Guattari, Foucault, and the post-structuralist scene were at their peak. He synthesized Italian operaismo with French post-structuralism in a way that wouldn't have been possible had he remained in Padua. Exile became the condition for the most productive period of his career.

### Key Moves (Chronological)
1. **1961: Co-founding Quaderni Rossi** — enters the founding generation of operaismo as a co-equal theorist alongside Tronti. Journal-as-movement, not academic paper.
2. **1979: Writing *The Savage Anomaly* in Rebibbia prison** — converts political defeat (arrest) into theoretical breakthrough. The Spinoza book becomes his most influential solo work. Treat adversity as a writing retreat.
3. **1981–1983: Using parliamentary immunity to flee** — tactical use of constitutional form against the state. Use the system's own logic to survive it.
4. **Early 1980s: Meeting Hardt in Paris exile** — the most consequential collaboration of his life. Find the younger intellectual who can be a platform for your ideas in a different language and context.
5. **1989: Co-founding Futur Antérieur** — creates the intellectual infrastructure for the post-operaismo synthesis in exile. Journal-as-movement, second iteration.
6. **1997: Voluntarily returning to Italy** — accepts short-term sacrifice (house arrest) for long-term platform access. Cleared the legal obstacles that had prevented him from being a public intellectual.
7. **2000: *Empire*** — the synthesis. Everything since operaismo condensed into a single framework. Zoom out from labor analysis to global sovereignty; change the scale of the question entirely.
8. **2000: Co-founding *Multitudes*** — third iteration of journal-as-movement. This one is still running 25 years later.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **Concept coining is infrastructure.** The most durable thing Negri produced was vocabulary. "Immaterial labor," "multitude," "Empire," "constituent power" are now standard terms. If Amber can coin 2-3 such concepts in her area, they will outlast any individual paper.
