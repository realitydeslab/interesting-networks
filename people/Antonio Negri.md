# Antonio Negri

<<<<<<< HEAD
**Toni Negri** (1 August 1933 – 16 December 2023) was an Italian political philosopher — one of the foremost theorists of autonomist Marxism and, with [[Michael Hardt]], co-author of the *Empire* trilogy (2000–2009). His work fused Spinozist ontology, Marxist labor theory, and a reading of Foucauldian biopolitics to produce a distinctive theory of global governance, class composition, and the revolutionary potential of the "multitude." He died in Paris aged 90, leaving behind a corpus that continues to animate debates in political theory, labor studies, platform capitalism, and commons-based governance.

Italian Marxist political philosopher (1933–2023). The defining theoretical architect of *operaismo* (Italian workerism) and later autonomist Marxism. Co-author with [[Michael Hardt]] of the Empire trilogy and five other books — the most ambitious Marxist political theory project of the late 20th century. Developed foundational concepts — constituent power, immaterial labor, the multitude, biopolitical production — that restructured contemporary political philosophy. Imprisoned 1979–1983 on disputed terrorism charges, exiled to France 1983–1997, lived and wrote until his death at 90.

## Background

<<<<<<< HEAD
Born in **Padua** (Veneto, Italy) in 1933 to a communist father from Bologna who died when Negri was two. His mother was a teacher. He was introduced to Marxist ideas early through family memory. In the 1950s he began as a militant in the Roman Catholic youth movement (GIAC), then became communist after working at a kibbutz in Israel in 1955. Joined the Italian Socialist Party (PSI) in 1956; graduated and was immediately hired as a professor of *dottrina dello stato* (state doctrine / philosophy of law) at the **University of Padua**.

In the early 1960s he joined the editorial group of **Quaderni Rossi**, one of the founding journals of *operaismo* (workerism), then moved to **Classe Operaia** with [[Mario Tronti]]. In 1969, with Oreste Scalzone and Franco Piperno, he co-founded **Potere Operaio** (Workers' Power), the most prominent organization of the *operaismo*-to-autonomism transition. After Potere Operaio dissolved in 1973, he became a key figure in **Autonomia Operaia** through the late 1970s.

**The April 7th Case (1979).** On 7 April 1979, in the wake of the Red Brigades' kidnapping and murder of Aldo Moro (1978), Italian state prosecutor Pietro Calogero arrested Negri and dozens of activists, accusing him of being the mastermind of the Red Brigades and the Moro assassination. Most serious charges were quickly undermined — a senior BR member later testified Negri "had nothing to do with the Red Brigades" — but he was convicted of being "morally responsible" for the 1970s wave of political violence and sentenced to 30 years. *Michel Foucault* commented: "Isn't he in jail simply for being an intellectual?" *Gilles Deleuze* and *Félix Guattari* co-signed the *Appel des intellectuels français contre la répression en Italie* (1977).

In 1983, while still awaiting trial, Negri was elected to the Italian parliament as a member of the **Radical Party**, gaining parliamentary immunity, and fled to France with help from *Félix Guattari* and Amnesty International. The Chamber of Deputies later revoked his immunity; he remained in French exile for 14 years, protected by the **Mitterrand doctrine** (France does not extradite political refugees from EU countries).

**Paris exile (1983–1997).** Taught at **Paris VIII (Vincennes)** and the **Collège international de philosophie** (co-founded by *Jacques Derrida*), alongside Foucault, Deleuze, and Guattari. Wrote prolifically in exile, producing *The Savage Anomaly*, *Marx Beyond Marx*, and *Insurgencies* during this period.

In 1997, he voluntarily returned to Italy to serve his sentence, hoping to draw attention to hundreds of other political exiles. His sentence was reduced to 13 years through plea bargain. He continued writing in prison and was released in 2003, after which he alternated between Venice and Paris with his partner, the French philosopher **[[Judith Revel]]**. He had a daughter, **Anna Negri**, a film director.
=======
**Origins:** Born August 1, 1933 in Padua, Italy. His father, a communist, died when Negri was 12. His mother died when he was 2. Raised in a working-class Catholic family in Padua. This early encounter with loss and the communist tradition shaped his entire intellectual life — a philosopher who always understood theory as inseparable from lived political struggle.

**Education:** Laurea in Philosophy, University of Padua, 1956. Became a professor in the philosophy faculty at Padua, rising to full professor of political science — making him one of the most important radical intellectuals in Italian academia in the 1960s–70s.

**Intellectual Trajectory — Five Periods:**

**1. Early operaismo / workerism (1961–1969)**
Joined the Italian Communist Party (PCI) as a teenager but quickly grew disillusioned with its accommodation to parliamentary politics. In 1961, co-founded (with [[Mario Tronti]], Raniero Panzieri, Romano Alquati, Alberto Asor Rosa, and others) the journal *Quaderni Rossi* (Red Notebooks) — the founding document of operaismo. The project: use Marx's *Capital* as a tool for analyzing the contemporary Italian factory system from the workers' perspective, not as abstract theory. In 1964, he and Tronti split from Quaderni Rossi to found *Classe Operaia* (Working Class). In 1968, Negri co-founded (with Alberto Asor Rosa) *Contropiano*, another influential journal.

Key operaismo innovations:
- **Workers' inquiry (conricerca):** Direct investigation of factory conditions, workers' struggles, class composition. Not social science from above but workers' self-research.
- **Class composition analysis:** Distinguish "technical composition" (how capital organizes work) from "political composition" (how workers self-organize resistance). The passage from one to the other is always a recomposition.
- **Mass worker:** The dominant figure of 1960s Italian factories (Fiat, Petrolchimico Marghera). Homogeneous, de-skilled, assembly-line. Their collective power was the refusal: the wildcat strike, absenteeism, sabotage.

**2. Autonomia Operaia and the "Years of Lead" (1969–1979)**
After 1969 (the "Hot Autumn" of factory uprisings), Negri helped found *Potere Operaio* (Workers' Power, 1967–1973) with Franco Piperno, Oreste Scalzone, [[Franco Berardi]] (Bifo), and [[Paolo Virno]]. This was a far-left organization explicitly committed to autonomy from the PCI and the unions. When Potere Operaio dissolved in 1973, Negri became a central figure in *Autonomia Operaia* (Workers' Autonomy, 1973–1979) — a decentralized network of radical groups across Italy, running pirate radio stations (Radio Alice in Bologna; Onda Rossa in Rome), publishing newspapers (*Rosso* in Milan, *I Volsci* in Rome), and theorizing the transition from the mass worker to the "socialized worker."

Key theoretical innovation of this period: the **social factory**. If operaismo had analyzed the Fordist factory as the site of value extraction, the autonomists argued that with the crisis of Fordism, capital had extended factory logic to all of social life. The whole of society became a factory. Reproduction (domestic work, education, social care) was also production. The working class was no longer just factory workers — it included students, the unemployed, women doing domestic labor, those in the informal economy. The *refusal of work* (rifiuto del lavoro) became a political strategy: not just striking inside factories but refusing the entire organization of capitalist life.

In 1977, during the movement of students and precarious workers that erupted across Italy (the "Movement of '77"), French philosophers Foucault, Sartre, Deleuze, and Guattari signed petitions in solidarity with the Italian autonomists when the carabinieri surrounded Bologna university.

**3. Prison, Parliament, and the Paris Exile (1979–1997)**
On April 7, 1979 — known as "April 7" — the Italian state carried out mass arrests of autonomists. Negri was arrested and charged with being the "moral brains" behind the Red Brigades, including the kidnapping and murder of Christian Democrat prime minister Aldo Moro (1978). The charges were extraordinary — Negri had no direct connection to the Red Brigades, and many on the Italian left and internationally saw the prosecution as a political attack on radical theory. (The most serious charges were later dropped; he served time on other charges related to inciting violence.)

While imprisoned, he wrote *The Savage Anomaly* (1981) — his reading of Spinoza, exploring potentia vs. potestas, that would become one of his most important works. Michael Hardt would later translate it into English (1991) and write its foreword — this was the beginning of their collaboration.

In 1983, Negri was elected to the Italian Parliament as a Radical Party MP — parliamentary immunity allowed him to be released from prison. Before the immunity was lifted, he fled to France, beginning 14 years of exile in Paris.

In Paris, Negri taught at the **Collège International de Philosophie** (co-founded by Derrida) and at **Université Paris VIII** (Vincennes/Saint-Denis) — the experimental university founded after May 1968. He co-founded (with [[Maurizio Lazzarato]], Yann Moulier Boutang, and others) the journal *Futur Antérieur* (1989–1998). He met Michael Hardt at the Collège International de Philosophie in Paris in the early 1980s — beginning the most important collaboration of his life.

He was protected by the **[Mitterrand doctrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitterrand_doctrine)** (1985) — François Mitterrand's policy of not extraditing Italian far-left exiles who had broken with violence.

**4. Empire and global recognition (1997–2009)**
Negri returned voluntarily to Italy in 1997 to serve his remaining sentence (which became house arrest and then a conditional release in 2003), a decision that cleared the legal obstacles and allowed him to be a public intellectual again. *Empire* (2000, with Hardt) was written across the Italian house arrest and collaborative work with Hardt at Duke — it became a sensation. Hailed as "the Communist Manifesto of the 21st century," it was translated into 24+ languages and became the defining work of anti-globalization theory. *Multitude* (2004) and *Commonwealth* (2009) followed.

**5. Late work (2009–2023)**
After 2003 Negri lived primarily in Paris. He continued publishing prolifically — solo essays and books in Italian and French, with English translations following. With Hardt: *Declaration* (2012, responding to Occupy and the Arab Spring) and *Assembly* (2017, responding to the movements of the 2010s). Solo: *Spinoza for Our Time* (2013), *Marx and Foucault* (2017), *From the Factory to the Metropolis* (2018). Died December 16, 2023, in Paris, at age 90.

## Intellectual Trajectory

**Phase 1 — Operaismo (1961–1969):** Joins *Quaderni Rossi*, then *Classe Operaia*. Core insight: working-class struggle *drives* capital's development, not the reverse. Studies the Fordist factory system empirically ("workers' inquiry"). Central figure alongside [[Mario Tronti]].

**Phase 2 — Potere Operaio and Autonomia (1969–1978):** Co-founds Potere Operaio (1969–1973). After its dissolution, becomes leading theorist of Autonomia Operaia. Develops "social factory" thesis (capitalism has extended beyond the factory into all of social life) and the "social worker" as successor to the "mass worker." [[Franco Berardi]] and [[Paolo Virno]] are fellow travelers in this period.

**Phase 3 — Prison (1979–1983):** Arrest by Italian state. Despite confinement, produces major theoretical work. Writes *The Savage Anomaly* (Spinoza), "The Constitution of Time," and key autonomist essays. Prison becomes his most productive writing environment.

**Phase 4 — French Exile (1983–1997):** At Paris VIII Vincennes and Collège international de philosophie alongside Foucault, Deleuze, Derrida, Guattari. Produces *Insurgencies*, *Marx Beyond Marx*, *The Politics of Subversion*. Develops the constituent power theory and begins the biopolitics framework.

**Phase 5 — Return to Italy (1997–2003):** Returns voluntarily to serve sentence. Continues writing from prison. Completes *Empire* manuscript (co-written with [[Michael Hardt]] from prison).

**Phase 6 — Empire Era (2000–2012):** *Empire* (2000) becomes global phenomenon, timed perfectly with anti-globalization movement (WTO protests, World Social Forum). Completes trilogy with *Multitude* (2004) and *Commonwealth* (2009). Engages Occupy movement with *Declaration* (2012).

**Phase 7 — Final Decades (2012–2023):** Continues writing in Venice and Paris. *Assembly* (2017) returns to organizational questions. Remains intellectually active until near death. Dies Paris, 16 December 2023.

## Key Concepts

### Multitude
The multitude is not the people (unified, homogeneous political body), the masses (passive, manipulable), or the crowd (irrational). It is a multiplicity of singularities that cooperate productively while remaining internally differentiated. Taken from Spinoza (*multitudo*), it names the political subject capable of democratic self-organization against Empire. The question *Assembly* returns to: can the multitude govern itself, or does it always need leadership?

### Constituent Power / Potestas vs. Potentia
Central distinction in Negri's political philosophy. *Potestas* (constituted power) is the power of the state, law, institutions — transcendent, fixed. *Potentia* (constituent power) is the living force of the multitude — immanent, generative, never fully exhausted. Every constitution is an attempt to fix constituent power into constituted form; but constituent power always exceeds its constitution. This has direct relevance to questions of AI governance and democratic legitimacy.

### Empire (vs. Imperialism)
Classical imperialism = extension of nation-state sovereignty. **Empire** = a de-centered, deterritorialized form of sovereignty without a single center. The US is its monarchy, multinationals its oligarchy, NGOs and the UN its democracy — but no single node commands. This is why resistance to Empire cannot take the form of "our nation-state against yours." The enemy is structural, not located.

### Immaterial Labor
Post-Fordist capitalism has made cognitive, communicative, and affective labor hegemonic. "Immaterial labor" produces ideas, knowledge, relationships, affects — not physical commodities. Key: immaterial production is inherently *social* (language, ideas circulate), inherently *common* (knowledge as non-rival), and harder to measure/quantify. This creates both the conditions for capital's expansion and its potential undoing.

### The Common
Neither private property (market) nor public property (state). The common is what we produce together and share: language, knowledge, code, urban space, ecological systems. Communism, for Negri and Hardt, is the political project of the common against both capitalist enclosure and statist nationalization.

### Biopolitics (post-Foucault)
Where Foucault analyzed biopolitics as discipline of individual bodies and management of populations, Negri extends this: in post-Fordist capitalism, *all* life becomes productive. Not just the factory-worker's labor-power, but her affects, desires, relationships, creativity. Life itself is the terrain of exploitation and resistance.

## Awards & Fellowships

Negri was notably non-institutional — he received no major academic prizes (his politics precluded it; he was literally imprisoned and exiled). His "recognition" came in a different form:
- **International solidarity campaign** (1979–1983): Michel Foucault, Jean-Paul Sartre, Gilles Deleuze, Félix Guattari all signed petitions for his release. A political recognition that towers above any prize.
- **Protection under the [Mitterrand doctrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitterrand_doctrine)** (1985): France formally protected him from Italian extradition — recognition at the level of state policy.
- **Election to Italian Parliament** as Radical Party MP (1983): the Italian left's way of creating immunity for him.
- *Empire* became required reading across left political theory courses worldwide — one of the most cited works of political philosophy of the 2000s.
- He was one of the most interviewed and discussed philosophers of the anti-globalization movement era (1999–2010).

### Conceptual Strategy

**Spinoza as a renewable conceptual resource.** Negri returned to Spinoza across five decades — *The Savage Anomaly* (1981), *Spinoza for Our Time* (2013), and throughout Empire trilogy. He used Spinoza not as historical subject but as living conceptual ammunition: *potentia/potestas*, immanence vs. transcendence, *multitudo*, *conatus*, joy. The strategy: find a canonical figure who has been marginalized by the tradition, demonstrate they contain what the tradition excludes, use them to name what cannot otherwise be named. Spinoza was the "savage anomaly" — the system that should have been impossible.

**Concept pairs as political maps.** Negri consistently thought in opposition pairs: constituent power / constituted power; multitude / people; Empire / imperialism; common / public/private; immanence / transcendence; *potentia* / *potestas*. Each pair is a map of a political terrain. The first term is always the name for what exists but cannot be represented; the second for what has been institutionalized and fixed.

**The long collaboration.** The 23-year collaboration with [[Michael Hardt]] (1994–2017) is a methodological lesson: Negri provided the European philosophical tradition, radical political biography, and Spinozist ontology; Hardt provided American literary-theoretical training, Deleuzian philosophy, and access to Anglophone audiences. Neither could have written Empire alone. The collaboration also helped force both toward accessibility.

### Career Architecture

**Prison as epistemological laboratory.** The April 7th case (1979) could have ended Negri's intellectual career. Instead he used it: the early 1980s prison writings (*The Savage Anomaly*, "The Constitution of Time") are his most philosophical work. Distance from activist demands, enforced solitude, and extreme constraint produced conceptual innovation. Not advice to seek imprisonment — but a model for: *how do you convert constraint into productivity?*

**Exile as vantage point.** The 14 years in Paris (1983–1997) gave Negri access to the post-structuralist constellation (Foucault, Deleuze, Derrida, Guattari) at the moment they were most intellectually alive. Being forced to leave Italy gave him a transnational, de-centered perspective that was precisely what the Empire thesis required. The argument that global sovereignty is de-centered could only have been made by someone who had experienced the de-centered nature of exile.

**Timing the moment.** *Empire* (2000) was written in the mid-1990s from prison/Paris and published at exactly the moment the anti-globalization movement reached its peak (WTO Seattle 1999, Davos protests, World Social Forum 2001). Negri did not time the book to the moment — the moment arrived to meet the book. But the lesson is: sustained conceptual work on long-term structural questions will eventually find its historical moment.

**Writing for movements, not just academia.** Negri consistently wrote for multiple audiences simultaneously — rigorous philosophical work (*Insurgencies*, *The Savage Anomaly*) alongside more accessible interventions (*Goodbye Mr. Socialism*, *Declaration*). He addressed the World Social Forum; he engaged Occupy directly. This dual register — theory that is also intervention — is a distinctive strategic choice.

### Conceptual Strategy
**Framework multiplication at maximum intensity.** Negri's method: take a major tradition (Marxist political economy), identify its blind spots, import an unexpected philosophical framework (Spinoza's ontology), generate a new vocabulary, and then deploy that vocabulary to name phenomena others can't see. The sequence:
1. Operaismo → new framework for reading factory politics (Marx + workers' inquiry)
2. Spinoza → constituent power, potentia, immanence (allowed him to think power as productive rather than just negative)
3. Foucault → biopolitics (allowed him to extend labor analysis to life itself)
4. Deleuze → singularity, multiplicity (allowed him to think the multitude as non-unifiable)

Each import solved a problem the previous framework couldn't handle. This isn't eclecticism — it's systematic framework acquisition for strategic purposes.

**Two-word concept coining.** Every major theoretical move produced a citeable, two-word concept: "immaterial labor," "constituent power," "social factory," "affective labor," "biopolitical production," "cognitive capitalism." These became the vocabulary that structured an entire field. The concepts are memorable, strange enough to need explanation, but clear enough to be used. This is concept architecture as intellectual infrastructure.

### Career Architecture
**Journal-as-movement.** Negri's career move at every transition point was to found a journal. Five journals across 40 years: Quaderni Rossi (1961), Classe Operaia (1964), Contropiano (1968), Futur Antérieur (1989), Multitudes (2000). Each journal was a node in a network, a place to test ideas, assemble collaborators, and reach a specific audience. The journal came before the book — concepts were developed in journal form, then synthesized in books. This is the opposite of the academic model (papers → journal → book); for Negri, the journal was the living tissue, the book was the archive.

**The collaborator who multiplied his reach.** The Hardt partnership is worth analyzing as an architecture decision. Hardt: younger, Anglophone, academic, Duke-based, with access to Harvard University Press and the English-speaking academy. Negri: older, Italian, exiled, with deep theoretical roots but limited English-language access. The collaboration was asymmetric and productive: Hardt brought the platform; Negri brought the depth. *Empire* could not have been written by either alone.

**Exile as advantage.** Negri's forced removal from Italy placed him in Paris at exactly the moment when Deleuze, Guattari, Foucault, and the post-structuralist scene were at their peak. He synthesized Italian operaismo with French post-structuralism in a way that wouldn't have been possible had he remained in Padua. Exile became the condition for the most productive period of his career.

### Key Moves (Chronological)
1. **1961: Co-founding Quaderni Rossi** — enters the operaismo founding generation. Establishes himself as a co-equal theorist alongside Tronti. The move: journal-as-movement, not academic paper.
2. **1979: Writing *The Savage Anomaly* in prison** — converts political defeat (arrest) into theoretical breakthrough. The Spinoza book becomes his most influential solo work. The move: treat adversity as a writing retreat.
3. **1981–1983: Using parliamentary immunity to flee** — the tactical use of constitutional form against the state. The move: use the system's own logic to survive it.
4. **1980s: Meeting Hardt in Paris exile** — the most consequential collaboration in his life. The move: find the younger intellectual who can be a platform for your ideas in a different language and context.
5. **1997: Voluntarily returning to Italy** — cleared the legal obstacles that had prevented him from being a public intellectual. The move: accept short-term sacrifice (house arrest) for long-term platform access.
6. **2000: *Empire*** — the synthesis. Everything since operaismo condensed into a single framework: Empire, multitude, biopolitics, commons. The move: zoom out from labor analysis to global sovereignty, change the scale of the question.
7. **2009: *Commonwealth*** — the most philosophically mature book. Introduces the "common" as a third term beyond private and public. The move: generate the positive concept, not just the critique.
