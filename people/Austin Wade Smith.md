## Background

Austin Wade Smith describes themselves as an "artist, ecologist, writer, and technologist." Prior to leading Regen Foundation, they taught critical methodologies on climate justice in art and engineering at universities in New York. This interdisciplinary roots — straddling art, ecological science, technology, and critical theory — is the foundation for an unusual conceptual position: that digital addressing technologies and animist ontologies are not at odds but are natural allies in building ecological governance infrastructure.

Their research explores the interrelation of **mutualism, animism, and bioregionalism** in decentralized technical and economic infrastructures. This triangulation is the engine of their intellectual work:
- **Mutualism** — reciprocal relationships, not extraction, as economic base
- **Animism** — the world as full of agents, not as background to human agency  
- **Bioregionalism** — place as defined by watershed and ecosystem, not by political boundary

Personal: cultivates orchids, flies kites. Based in Brooklyn, New York.

## Domains
- **Ecological institutions** — governance frameworks where living systems (forests, rivers, wetlands) hold layered legibility across law, economics, and technology
- **Animist organizations** — institutions that treat Earth as participant, not backdrop; organizations that can carry the interests of non-human beings
- **Living covenants** — DAOs as encoded civic covenants with ecosystems, not just investment vehicles
- **Bioregional governance** — organizing social and economic life around watershed- and ecosystem-scale boundaries rather than political borders
- **Regenerative Finance (ReFi)** — financial instruments that prioritize ecosystem regeneration over extraction

- **Critical legibility** — how we make the living world *visible* to human socio-technical systems without commodifying it

## Why Interesting

Austin Wade Smith is one of the most practically-grounded **more-than-human governance** thinkers and builders anywhere. Unlike many theorists, they are deploying actual infrastructure — blockchains that encode ecological stewardship, forests with crypto wallets, rivers holding communal funds, wetlands serving as governance bodies. This is not speculative design fiction; it is implemented institutional design in collaboration with Indigenous and grassroots communities across four continents.

**The conceptual move is original:** Austin doesn't argue for "nature-inspired computing" but for the inverse — recognizing that "our infrastructure can also be recast and understood as living systems if we understand that there isn't a separation between nature and people." The digital and the ecological are not at odds; they are the same problem space viewed from different registers.

**The legibility framing is sharp:** Austin identifies "legibility" — the capacity to be seen by human socio-technical systems — as the key bottleneck for more-than-human governance. Not rights, not value, but *legibility*. And blockchain addressing systems solve this first-order problem: a Douglas fir gets an endpoint in a digital addressing system, a treasury, a way to participate in the balance sheet. Once legible, non-humans can be ignored less.

**The mutualism lineage is unusual:** By connecting to Sara Horowitz (Freelancers Union, Mutualist Society) and East African chama traditions (via Shaila Agha), Austin grounds the work in historical mutual aid movements rather than crypto-speculation. This is a deliberate rhetorical and intellectual strategy: the ecological institution is continuous with the credit union, the cooperative, the commons.

**The endorsement by Audrey Tang is a strong signal:** Tang is the person who built Taiwan's digital democracy experiment (pol.is, vTaiwan) and understands governance-at-scale. Their enthusiastic endorsement of *Ecological Institutions* signals that this project is legible as serious governance innovation, not just movement art.

**For Amber specifically:** Austin's work directly addresses the "more-than-human" thread in Amber's research. They're asking the same question from the other side — not "how do we design AI that respects non-human agency?" but "how do we build institutions that give non-humans actual governance participation?" These are naturally complementary frames that could produce a powerful collaborative inquiry.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Animist-technical fusion** — Austin's core intellectual move is taking animist ontology (the world as full of agents, everything as alive) and fusing it with technical substrate (digital addressing, blockchains, smart contracts). Neither side is diluted: the animism is not metaphorical ("like" organisms), and the technical implementation is not superficial. The result is a genuinely new institutional category — the "animist organization" — that has no direct precedent.

**Legibility as the key variable** — Rather than arguing at the level of rights, ethics, or philosophy, Austin zeros in on *legibility* as the actionable concept. If a river can be seen by the balance sheet, it cannot be ignored. This operationalizes the more-than-human argument in a way that works inside existing economic systems. It's a judo move: using the logic of economic legibility *against* the assumption that only commodities are worth valuing.

**Three-axis triangulation (mutualism × animism × bioregionalism)** — Austin doesn't argue from any single tradition; they use three registers simultaneously. Mutualism grounds it in economic history (cooperatives, mutual aid). Animism grounds it in non-Western ontology. Bioregionalism grounds it in place and ecology. The synthesis is harder to dismiss than any single argument would be.

**"Undual" as rhetorical move** — Audrey Tang uses the word "undual" in their endorsement, which appears to come from Austin's framing. Rather than arguing *against* the digital/natural separation, they propose *undualing* it — treating the binary as a conceptual error rather than a substantive distinction. This is more effective rhetorically than a critique.

### Career Architecture

**Teacher → researcher → institution builder** — Austin accumulated cultural and pedagogical credibility as a critical theory educator at NY universities, then transitioned into movement-building and institution design at Regen Foundation. Unlike academics who remain primarily in institutions of knowledge production, Austin crossed into institutions of governance production. This is a rare transition that creates unusual power.

**Nonprofit as research vehicle** — Regen Foundation is simultaneously a nonprofit organization and a research unit. Austin leads it as if it were a speculative design lab doing "real" things. The *Ecological Institutions* book is the flagship research output; the IDK is the methodology toolkit; the three prototype institutions are the applied research. This integration of research and institution is unusual and powerful.

**Movement-building as strategy** — The Regen Builder Lab (monthly), BioFi conference participation, and Metacrisis Salon talks are not primarily for building Austin's personal brand. They are tools for building the field. Austin seems to understand that ecological governance institutions will only be possible when there are enough practitioners, funders, and theorists oriented around the same questions.

**Scarcity as design principle** — The *Ecological Institutions* book is a gift, not a commodity. Only 500 copies, not purchasable. This is a deliberate design choice that enacts the book's own theory. It also creates a particular kind of legitimacy signal: the project is not primarily commercial, and access is structured through relationship rather than transaction.

### Methodology Signature

**Institution-building as research** — Unlike academics who study institutions or designers who prototype artifacts, Austin *builds actual institutions* as research method. The Community Staking DAO is not a simulation; it actually controls 35% of Regen Network governance power. The prototype institutions in Aotearoa, East Africa, and the Americas are not case studies; they are actual governance experiments. Research happens through implementation.

**Cross-medium publication strategy** — The *Ecological Institutions* book as a physical, tear-out card set that you play as a governance workshop is a carefully designed research output. It is simultaneously a book, a workshop kit, and a governance artifact. The medium *is* the message: this is what an ecological institution looks like even before it exists.

**Indigenous and grassroots partnership as epistemic method** — Austin's research happens in "close, direct relationships" with collaborators across five continents, and "the degree of intermediation is nil." This is not just ethics; it is epistemology. The knowledge about ecological governance comes from the practitioners who have always governed ecologically. Austin's role is to build the translation layer between those practices and contemporary technical/legal infrastructure.

### Network Strategy

It is intensely focused on the mutualism × animism × bioregionalism cluster, with strong connections to Kei Kreutler (composable governance / blockchain tech), BioFi Project (bioregional finance), and Cascadia movement (bioregional organizing). Unlike researchers who maintain broad cross-disciplinary networks, Austin builds deep within a specific movement.

It distinguishes Regen Foundation's work from crypto-speculative or purely academic approaches.
