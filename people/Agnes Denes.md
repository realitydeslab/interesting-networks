# Agnes Denes

Pioneering Land Art, Ecological Art, and Computer Art practitioner — active since the late 1960s. One of the foundational figures connecting art, ecology, and computation. *Wheatfield—A Confrontation* (1982, Manhattan) is her iconic work: growing wheat on two acres of landfill in Lower Manhattan overlooking the skyline, then donating the harvest to fight world hunger. Her 1970s Philosophical Drawings use mathematical/logical notation to map human knowledge; her Computer Art predates most contemporary generative art by decades. A rare figure whose ecological vision remained visionary even 50 years later — as her Shed retrospective (2022) made clear.

## Domains
- Land Art / Environmental Art
- Ecological intervention at civic scale
- Philosophical Drawings (mathematical/logical structures)
- Computer Art (1970s generative work)
- Cosmogony, knowledge cartography

## Background
Hungarian-born, immigrated to the USA. Developed her practice in parallel with Conceptualism (late 1960s) but diverged toward ecological concerns when that wasn't fashionable. Her "Human Argument" series (1969+) uses formal logic notation as artistic medium. Wheatfield (1982) is both ecological protest and civic gesture. Decades ahead of land art, ecological art, and computational art as recognized fields.

## Key Works
- *Wheatfield—A Confrontation* (1982) — 2 acres of wheat on Manhattan landfill; harvest donated to world hunger campaign. [Documentation](https://agnesdenesstudio.com/works7.html)
- Philosophical Drawings series (1969–) — visualization of human knowledge using mathematical/logical structures
- *Tree Mountain—A Living Time Capsule* (1992–96) — Finland; 11,000 trees planted by 11,000 people, managed for 400 years
- *Absolutes and Intermediates* retrospective (The Shed, 2022) — 50+ year survey

## Why Interesting
Denes is the ur-ancestor of ecological/more-than-human/computational art. Her practice integrates:
- More-than-human design (multispecies, long time horizons)
- Computational art (1970s Computer Art)
- Participatory ecology (Tree Mountain involves 11,000 humans + 11,000 trees over 400 years)
- Critique of human knowledge systems through art

For Amber: Denes's Computer Art lineage, her ecological systems thinking, and her use of mathematics as artistic medium are all directly relevant to questions of machine behavior as ecology, and artificial life as artistic medium.
