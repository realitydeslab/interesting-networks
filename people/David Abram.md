# David Abram

Philosopher, ecologist, and sleight-of-hand magician — the person who coined "the more-than-human world." Abram is the most important single figure connecting phenomenology (Merleau-Ponty), ecological science (Gaia, Margulis), and Indigenous animist cosmologies into a unified philosophy of sensuous participation with the animate Earth. His 1996 book *The Spell of the Sensuous* is the foundational text of ecological phenomenology. His phrase "the more-than-human world" has become the defining term of multispecies studies, more-than-human design, and ecological humanities worldwide. He argues that alphabetic literacy — and now digital technology — systematically captures and misdirects the human animal's deep-seated, instinctive animism, pulling it away from lived reciprocity with the more-than-human terrain.

## Background

Born on Long Island, New York. Grew up a suburban kid — an origin he mentions with a note of shock when he found himself kayaking Prince William Sound and confronting its colossal wildness for the first time. His early passion was stage magic: sleight-of-hand, street performance. This gave him a lifelong fascination with perception, misdirection, and the permeability of what we take for granted as "real."

In his early twenties, Abram traveled through Southeast Asia as an itinerant magician — living and working with indigenous healers (dukuns) in Bali, Sri Lanka, Indonesia, and Nepal. This is the famous opening of *The Spell of the Sensuous*: standing outside his hut in Bali at night, stars reflected in the rice paddies, fireflies dancing between. He was studying these healers' magic professionally, but found that what they actually were was something more fundamental — ecologists of perception. Their magic worked precisely because they sustained a felt reciprocity between the human community and the more-than-human surroundings. The healer was, in Abram's framing, a "specialist in the wild" — a person whose job was to maintain the boundary between the human and the other-than-human.

He returned to the US and wrote "The Perceptual Implications of Gaia" (1984) at Yale School of Forestry — a paper that brought him into contact with James Lovelock, Lynn Margulis, and William Irwin Thompson's Lindisfarne Fellowship. He received his PhD from Stony Brook University (1993, philosophy). He has lived in the American Southwest for many years, currently based in the foothills of the Southern Rocky Mountains / northern New Mexico high desert.

He is a practitioner, not just a theorist: he leads immersive retreats in wild places — canoeing the Canadian wilderness, descending into Tuscany, working with San Bushmen in the Kalahari — designed to restore direct sensory attunement to the living landscape.

## Key Works

### Books
- **[*The Spell of the Sensuous: Perception and Language in a More-than-Human World*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/319/the-spell-of-the-sensuous-by-david-abram)** (1996, Pantheon) — Lannan Literary Award for Nonfiction. The foundational text: covers Merleau-Ponty phenomenology applied to ecology, the link between alphabetic literacy and the eclipse of animist perception, how oral cultures maintain linguistic reciprocity with the landscape. Isabelle Stengers translated it into French (2013).
- **[*Becoming Animal: An Earthly Cosmology*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/318/becoming-animal-by-david-abram)** (2010, Pantheon) — runner-up for inaugural PEN/E.O. Wilson Literary Science Writing Award. More lyrical and embodied than *The Spell*; develops the "commonwealth of breath," shows how our creaturely bodies are continuous with the animate landscape.

### Key Essays (all at davidabram.org)
- **"Magic and the Machine"** — his most significant recent essay (~12,000 words). Argues that digital technology is a misfire of animism: we instinctively try to recreate an animate world (talking refrigerators, Siri, IoT "enchanted objects," GPS) but this misfires because it offers only human-programmed pseudo-otherness, not the radical alterity of a humpback whale or an old-growth forest. Brilliant critique: "As we enter more deeply into the world of ubiquitous computing, we increasingly seal ourselves into an exclusively human zone of interaction. We enter into a bizarre kind of intraspecies incest." Essential for AI/more-than-human design researchers.
- **"Creaturely Migrations on a Breathing Planet"** — on salmon, cranes, butterflies; the deep somatic intelligence underlying migration; how the GPS-dependent human is unlearning the ancestral skill of earthly orientation
- **"Depth Ecology"** — distinguishes deep ecology from environmentalism; traces the movement's philosophical roots
- **"Ground of Our Unknowing"** — written during the first weeks of COVID-19 pandemic; on the living earth catching its breath
- **"Storytelling and Wonder: The Rejuvenation of Oral Culture"** — on why oral culture is an ecological necessity, not a romantic nostalgia
- **"The Ecology of Magic"** (in *Orion Magazine*, 1983) — the earliest major essay; the Balinese healer/shaman as specialist at the boundary between human and more-than-human communities
- Routledge Handbook interview — on why identity conflicts prevent us from recognizing our shared ecological crisis; "we use identity conflicts to hide what's really at stake today — which is our deeper identity as parts of Earth"

## Key Concepts

- **"The more-than-human world"** — coined in subtitle of *The Spell of the Sensuous* (1996); designates earthly nature as including and exceeding human culture. Now the central term of multispecies studies, more-than-human design, ecological humanities. Most people using the phrase don't know where it came from.
- **The commonwealth of breath** — his recent central concept: the atmosphere as a living commons co-created and shared by all breathing organisms; each exhalation is an active contribution to a shared body. A political ecology of air.
- **Ecophenomenology** — applying Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology (the primacy of embodied, sensory perception) to human-nature relations; the body is always already embedded in a field of other sensing, sensuous bodies.
- **Alphabetic inscription and perceptual narrowing** — his most original scholarly contribution: alphabetic literacy (not literacy in general, but specifically the phonetic alphabet) short-circuits the instinctive animism of human perception, redirecting it to the self-referential system of written signs. The letters speak to us the way trees and rivers once did. In becoming literate, the landscape "fell silent."
- **Animism as default setting** — far from being a primitive projection, animistic perception (sensing the surrounding world as alive, expressive, sentient) is the normal mode of human sensory experience. Technologies — especially the alphabet — capture and redirect this instinct. The more we engage digital tech, the more we seal ourselves in an exclusively human cocoon.
- **Wild ethics** — ethics grounded not in rules but in a humble, attentive openness to the inexhaustible otherness of earthly beings. The animate earth, not human consensus, is the ground of ethics.
- **Oral culture as ecological imperative** — oral cultures are inherently local and place-based; their stories are memory-systems keyed to the landscape. Revitalizing oral culture is not nostalgia but ecological necessity: it's the soil in which literacy and digital culture must be rooted to remain sustainable.

- **🏠 Geographic proximity:** Abram teaches at Schumacher College (Dartington, Devon, UK) — a short trip from Oxford. His 2026 teaching schedule is active. Look for intensive programs there.
- **💡 The "Magic and the Machine" essay is must-read for AI researchers:** His argument that digital tech misdirects animism without satisfying it — because it offers no radical otherness — maps directly onto questions about AI agency, machine behavior, and more-than-human design. The question of whether AI systems can function as genuinely "other" beings (not just reflections of human programming) is exactly his question.
- **🔮 AWE as potential collaborator:** The Alliance for Wild Ethics mission — using "arts, often in tandem with the natural sciences, to provoke deeply felt shifts in the human experience of nature" — resonates with Amber's use of MR/AR technologies to create more-than-human encounters. AWE might be interested in HoloKit or mixed reality as a medium for the perceptual transformation they seek.
- **📚 Reading order:** (1) *The Spell of the Sensuous* ch. 1 & 4; (2) "Magic and the Machine" essay; (3) "Creaturely Migrations on a Breathing Planet"; (4) *Becoming Animal* (for "commonwealth of breath")
- **🌍 Intellectual framing:** Abram's career shows how to position ecological phenomenology as culturally transformative — not academic paper-making but "a rapid transformation of culture." This is the register Amber's more-than-human design work reaches toward. His framing of ethics as grounded in sensory reciprocity (wild ethics) is relevant to Amber's work on care ethics and machine behavior.
- **👁️ Alert:** His analysis of GPS vs. indigenous navigation — how GPS abolishes the embodied, sensory attunement to place that is our evolutionary inheritance — applies directly to questions about whether AI extends or short-circuits human ecological intelligence. What does it mean for more-than-human attunement when you mediate all perception through a screen?

## Why Interesting

Abram is the person who coined the term that defines Amber's entire research area. The "more-than-human world" framework didn't emerge from design studies or HCI — it came from phenomenology, ecology, and Indigenous knowledge systems. Understanding Abram's genealogy (Merleau-Ponty → Gaia science → Indigenous animism → "more-than-human") gives Amber the intellectual depth behind the term that most design researchers are missing. He is not a nostalgic primitivist: his work is a sophisticated philosophical argument about perception, technology, and what gets lost when our animistic instincts are misdirected by our own tools. The "Magic and the Machine" essay reads as though it were written for people working in AI and more-than-human design — it's uncannily relevant. He also offers a practical model for how to work at the intersection of philosophy, ecology, and embodied practice without losing the thread to either rigor or lived experience.
