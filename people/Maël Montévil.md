# Maël Montévil

Theoretical biologist and philosopher of biology at CNRS / ENS Paris. Builds rigorous mathematical alternatives to computational and reductionist models of life. Works at the crossroads of experimental biology, mathematics, and continental philosophy. Foundational figure in the **biological organization** research programme (with Longo, Mossio, Soto, Sonnenschein). Develops the framework of **closure of constraints**, **anti-entropy**, **extended criticality**, and **biological disruption** — the most serious attempt to give mathematical teeth to what critics of "AI-as-life" can only say intuitively.

## Background
Trained in physics and mathematics. Did his PhD at ENS Paris / Université Paris Descartes (2011) under [[Giuseppe Longo]] in the doctoral school "Frontières du vivant" — thesis: *Temps biologiques et transitions critiques étendues* (Biological time and extended critical transitions). This foundational training shaped his central move: not treating biology as "physics with complications" but developing a genuinely different mathematics for living systems.

**Career trajectory (postdocs → CNRS position):**
1. **Postdoc, Tufts University School of Medicine** — Dept. of Anatomy & Cell Biology, in [[Ana M. Soto]]'s group. Developed quantitative software for morphometric analysis of mammary gland whole mounts. This is where he learned to bridge philosophy with bench biology, building tools still used in endocrine disruption research (2025 *Reproductive Toxicology* paper on ketoconazole).
2. **Postdoc, IHPST, Université Paris I Panthéon-Sorbonne** — DIM-ISC (Île-de-France Interdisciplinary Sciences) grant. With [[Matteo Mossio]] (CNRS). Developed the "closure of constraints" formalism — their 2015 *Journal of Theoretical Biology* paper is the most-cited result (406 mentions).
3. **Postdoc, MSC, Université Paris VII Diderot** — Labex "Who AM I?" grant. In the Organisms research programme with Soto, building first principles of organismal biology from biological inertia / default state theory.
4. **Research fellow, [[IRI (Institut de recherche et d'innovation)]], Centre Pompidou** — with [[Bernard Stiegler]]. Developed the anti-entropy/technics connection; contributed to Plaine Commune contributive learning territory ([[Territoire Apprenant Contributif (TAC)]]).
5. **CNRS Chargé de Recherche, IHPST, Université Paris I** — funded by Cogito Foundation grant: "Understanding the Anthropocene: towards a philosophy of biological disruptions."
6. **Current: Centre Cavaillès, République des Savoirs UMR 8241, CNRS / ENS Paris** — the main philosophy of science research centre at ENS.

He publishes extensively in both French (158 items) and English (174 items) — rare bilingual theoretical biologist with simultaneous standing in the French tradition (Canguilhem, Simondon) and international theoretical biology.

## Domains
- **Theoretical biology** — what makes organisms irreducible to physics or computation; the mathematics of organization, time, and historicity in living systems
- **Anti-entropy and biological organization** — organisms as history-constituted entities that generate and maintain organized complexity; anti-entropy as the biological analogue to physical entropy's second law
- **Extended criticality** — organisms exist in permanent (not punctual) critical transitions, unlike physics where criticality is a special state; biological organization is inherently at the edge of chaos
- **Philosophy of biology** — rigorous critique of computational/informational models; the epistemology of biological measurement; what theory means in biology
- **Disruption biology** — formalizing how anthropogenic changes (endocrine disruptors, climate change, digital technologies) impair biological organization at organism and ecosystem levels
- **Canguilhem lineage** — normality, pathology, normativity in biological systems; extending Canguilhem's concepts into the Anthropocene
- **Stiegler/pharmacological connection** — connecting biological disruption to techno-social disruption; anti-entropy as a framework spanning biology and organology

### Key Articles (selection)
- [**"Biological Organisation as Closure of Constraints"**](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jtbi.2015.02.029) (with Matteo Mossio, *Journal of Theoretical Biology*, 2015) — **406 mentions. THE foundational paper.** Defines biological organization as a closed system of constraints that mutually depend on and maintain each other. Constraints are defined at a given time scale and are distinct from processes. This paper is the mathematical core of the whole programme.
- [**"Entropies and the Anthropocene Crisis"**](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00146-021-01221-0) (*AI & SOCIETY*, 2023) — introduces anti-entropy as the key concept for the Anthropocene. Biological organizations generate organized complexity (anti-entropy) against the thermodynamic trend; disruption decreases anti-entropy. Best entry-point for the anti-entropy framework.
- [**"What Drives the Brain? Organizational Changes, FEP and Anti-Entropy"**](https://doi.org/10.13133/2532-5876/19211) (with Marie Chollat-Namy, *Organisms*, 2025) — direct challenge to Friston's Free Energy Principle. Argues FEP misapplies information-theoretic concepts (predefined phase spaces, optimization) to biology. Introduces psychedelics as a test case: paradoxically elevated cerebral entropy induces organizational *improvements*, contradicting FEP's minimization story.
- [**"Historicity at the Heart of Biology"**](https://doi.org/10.1007/s12064-020-00320-8) (*Theory in Biosciences*, 2020) — philosophical foundation. Biological objects are constituted by their history; this forces a conceptual reconfiguration when modeling. Not just the platitude that "biology has history" — a rigorous epistemological argument about what happens when we try to mathematize historical objects.
- [**"From Physics to Biology by Extending Criticality and Symmetry Breakings: An Update"**](https://doi.org/10.14428/aes.v9i1.56043) (with Giuseppe Longo, *Acta Europeana Systemica*, 2020) — the extended criticality argument. Organisms are in permanent critical transitions; symmetry changes are the norm, not the exception. Best accessible update of the Longo-Montévil framework.
- [**"Disruption of Biological Processes in the Anthropocene: The Case of Phenological Mismatch"**](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10441-025-09496-2) (*Acta Biotheoretica*, 2025) — first formal mathematical schema for disruption. Plant-pollinator synchrony as case study; Boltzmann-inspired entropy framework applied to biological possibility spaces. Technical definition: disruption = moving from narrow viable region of possibility space to generic configurations where populations are not viable.
- [**"Computational Empiricism: The Reigning Épistémè of the Sciences"**](https://www.philosophy-world-democracy.org/computational-empiricism) (*Philosophy World Democracy*, 2021) — the sharpest critique of contemporary scientific practice. Characterizes mainstream science as "computational empiricism" — empirical data + computational models, no renewal of theory. Leading to "theory disruption" across fields. Essential for understanding the epistemological problem with AI-as-biology.
- [**"From the Century of the Genome to the Century of the Organism"**](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pbiomolbio.2015.12.002) (with Ana Soto, Giuseppe Longo, Denis Noble, Carlos Sonnenschein, Matteo Mossio, Arnaud Pocheville, Paul-Antoine Miquel, *Progress in Biophysics and Molecular Biology*, 2016) — a coalition manifesto. Multiple theoretical biologists declaring a paradigm shift from genome-centric to organism-centric biology. Important as a field-declaration move.
- **"Modeling Organogenesis from Biological First Principles"** (with Ana M. Soto, in *Organization in Biology*, ed. Matteo Mossio, Springer, 2023) — applies the three-principle framework (default state, variation, closure of constraints) to mammary ductal morphogenesis.
- **"Conceptual and Theoretical Specifications for Accuracy in Medicine"** (*Personalized Medicine in the Making*, Springer, 2022) — extends the theory to precision medicine; critiques how precision without theory isn't accuracy.
- **"Understanding Living Beings by Analogy with Computers or Understanding Computers as an Emanation of the Living"** (*Trópos*, 2021) — reinterprets computation through biological theory; Turing machines as constraints, not algorithms.

## Research Taste Analysis

Montévil's research strategy is unusually coherent and worth studying. He operates on multiple axes simultaneously:

### Conceptual Strategy
**Framework stacking (pyramidal):** He builds concepts that require each other. The logic: organisms have *historicity* (not just variation) → their organization is *closure of constraints* (not just complexity) → they generate *anti-entropy* (not just lower entropy locally) → Anthropocene disrupts *anti-entropy* (not just biodiversity). Each concept is minimal but opens new questions. This creates a cumulative programme where papers are not islands but contributions to a building.

**Two-word concept coining:** "Closure of constraints," "extended criticality," "anti-entropy," "biological default state," "computational empiricism" — each is a precisely defined two-or-three-word phrase that becomes citable. He's very good at naming concepts so they stick.

**Inversion moves:** He consistently inverts conventional assumptions. Physics: symmetry conservation is the norm, breaking is special → Biology: symmetry *change* is the norm. Physics: criticality is rare and special → Biology: organisms are *always* critical. Computation: default state is inertia/quiescence → Biology: default state is proliferation/variation. Each inversion does conceptual work.

### Career Architecture
**Trans-institutional positioning:** He has deliberately worked in experimental biology (Tufts), formal philosophy (IHPST), mathematical biology (MSC/Paris VII), political philosophy (IRI), and cognitive science (current FEP work). This is not randomness — it's a systematic strategy to occupy multiple nodes and be uncategorizable. Hard to marginalize.

**Coalition papers as territorial claims:** The 2016 "Century of the Organism" manifesto paper (with Noble, Soto, Sonnenschein, Longo, Mossio, Pocheville) is a multi-author declaration that biological theory is changing — not a hypothesis paper but a position paper. Very effective for field definition. Montévil has used this strategy multiple times.

**Stiegler network leverage:** By positioning himself at IRI and connecting biological theory to Stiegler's work, he gained access to an entirely different social network (humanists, artists, technologists, policy) while remaining credible as a biologist. The cross-pollination allowed him to publish in *AI & SOCIETY*, *EcoRev'*, *Philosophy World Democracy* — venues no pure biologist would reach.

### Methodology Signature
**Theory-experiment dual track:** He simultaneously writes formal philosophy of biology AND develops quantitative software (the mammary gland morphometry R code). This keeps him grounded and allows him to speak credibly to experimentalists.

**R code as philosophy:** He publishes his R code (Zenodo, 2020) for the disruption analysis. This is rare for a philosopher. It means his mathematical schemas are not just metaphors — they're computable.

**French philosophical tradition as resource, not constraint:** He reads Canguilhem, Simondon, Bergson, Nancy — the French tradition — not as historical background but as living tools. This lets him import distinctions (normativity, individuation, élan vital as biological time) that Anglophone analytic philosophers of biology often miss.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **Minimal formal schemas open big territories.** The "closure of constraints" paper is 12 pages with a precise definition. 406 citations. Amber's work could benefit from distilling core concepts to their minimal formal expression — not trying to say everything in one paper.

2. **Build across institutional orbits deliberately.** Montévil's trans-institutional career was not accidental — each postdoc placed him in a different community. Experimental biology, philosophy of science, mathematical biology, political philosophy. Each orbit contributed vocabulary. This is a strategy for being uncategorizable in a good way.

3. **Coalition papers are field-building, not just research.** The 2016 manifesto with 7 co-authors is not primarily a research contribution — it's a declaration of a field. Amber's interdisciplinary position might benefit from similar coalition papers (manifestos, position papers, special issues) that declare territory.

4. **Anti-entropy as conceptual transfer.** The move of taking a physics concept (entropy) and carefully redefining it for biology/society is very portable. Amber could use similar transfers — take precise concepts from one domain (physics, computation, information theory) and carefully redefine them for her domain of interest.

5. **Publish in French AND English.** If Amber is in the UK but has continental connections, publishing in French-language venues (even occasionally) opens completely different networks. Montévil's French circuit connects him to philosophers, artists, and political theorists that pure English publishing misses.
