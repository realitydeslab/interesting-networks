# Teresa Margolles

Mexican conceptual artist whose practice centers on death, forensic evidence, and the bodies of those killed by structural violence — particularly victims of Mexico's drug wars, trans women, and the urban poor. Trained in forensic medicine before becoming an artist. Works with forensic materials: water used to wash corpses, fat from liposuctions, blood from crime scenes, soil from execution sites, clothing of murder victims. Her studio is adjacent to a morgue in Mexico City. Her practice makes the social and political conditions that produce death legible through aesthetic displacement — the morgue as a mirror of society.

## Background

Born 1963 in Culiacán, Sinaloa — a state that has been ground zero of Mexico's drug violence. "There's not one family who don't have friends or relatives who've been murdered," she has said. The proximity to violence and death began in childhood.

As a teenager, she followed an older sister to Mexico City, intending to study photography. She enrolled at the **Faculty of Political Science, [UNAM](https://www.unam.mx/)** to satisfy her family, then began spending time at the neighboring medical school, attending anatomy classes and dissections. This led to formal training in **forensic science and forensic medicine** at UNAM, and she went on to qualify as a forensic technician — not to become a pathologist, but to gain legitimate, sustained access to the morgue. She also studied art at the **Dirección de Fomento a la Cultura Regional del Estado de Sinaloa** in Culiacán.

"Mainly I was an artist and observer developing this idea of the dead body as a reflection of society." Her training is genuine — she holds academic degrees in science communication and forensic medicine from UNAM — but the morgue was always the laboratory, not the career.

In **1990**, she co-founded **[[SEMEFO]]** (an acronym for the Mexican coroner's office, *Servicio Médico Forense*), an artists' collective that used "the detritus of murder investigations to confront violence in the country." Key members included Arturo Angulo and Carlos Lopez, though membership was loose. SEMEFO was conceptual and provocative, using decomposing materials, morgue detritus, and visceral interventions to shock Mexican cultural complacency about death. She left SEMEFO in the late 1990s to pursue a solo practice.

Her studio remains attached to a morgue in Mexico City, where she has maintained privileged access for over three decades. "The work of Teresa Margolles has always taken the human body and its liquid components as protagonists; they serve as vehicles for a relentless indictment of the growing violence in the world at large and in her own native country in particular." — Letizia Ragaglia, MUSEION, 2011.

## Key Works

- **[*Muerte Sin Fin*](https://www.mmk.art/en/whats-on/teresa-margolles/) (Death Without End)** (2004, MMK Frankfurt) — first major solo exhibition. Seven installations, each using morgue materials. Includes *En el aire*, *Papeles*, and other works. The show that established her international reputation.

- **[*En el aire*](https://www.mmk.art/en/whats-on/teresa-margolles/)** (2003/2004) — a hallway filled with soap bubbles from a machine. The soap water contains the dissolved residue of corpses washed in the morgue. Visitors walk through, unaware they are moving through the dissolved remains of murder victims. Formally beautiful; materially horrifying. One of the most conceptually sharp works in contemporary art.

- **[*Papeles*](https://www.mmk.art/en/whats-on/teresa-margolles/)** (2004) — rows of paper made with water from the morgue, incorporating traces of blood and fat from bodies. Essentially portraits made from human residue.

- **[*¿De qué otra cosa podríamos hablar?*](https://www.artnews.com/art-in-america/features/what-else-could-we-speak-about-teresa-margolles-at-the-mexican-pavilion-57746/) (What Else Could We Speak About?)** (2009, Venice Biennale, Mexican Pavilion) — curated by [[Cuauhtémoc Medina]]. Floor-cloths soaked in water used to wash murder victims' bodies; the outside of the pavilion bore a blood-soaked national flag. Visitors walked on the cloths unknowingly. The show was nearly expelled from the pavilion for being too explicitly critical of Mexico's drug war. Became one of the defining Venice presentations of its era.

- **[*Flag I*](https://www.tate.org.uk/art/artworks/margolles-flag-i-t14735)** (2009) — version of the blood-soaked flag from Venice. Now in the **[Tate Modern](https://www.tate.org.uk/)** collection.

- **[*37 Bodies*](https://www.tate.org.uk/art/artworks/margolles-37-bodies-t14736)** (year) — short pieces of surgical thread knotted together to form a single line, each piece memorializing a Mexican murder victim. In the **[Tate Modern](https://www.tate.org.uk/)** collection.

- **[*Frontera* (Frontier)](https://ro.uow.edu.au/ltc/vol11/iss1/2/)** (2010, Fridericianum Kassel) — sand from crime scenes in Ciudad Juárez exhibited in a glass container. The entire border region made present through its soil.

- **[*PM 2010*](https://www.mmk.art/en/whats-on/teresa-margolles/)** (2010) — photographs culled from Mexico City tabloids documenting homicide victims. The tabloid page as forensic archive.

- **[*Pistas de Baile*](https://jamescohan.viewingrooms.com/artworks/21787-teresa-margolles-pistas-de-baile-2016/)** (Dance Floors) (2016) — photography project in Ciudad Juárez, documenting the ruins of once-glamorous nightclubs where Frank Sinatra and Nat King Cole performed, now rubble, alongside the women who once worked in them. This project brought her into contact with **Karla La Borrada**.

- **[*Mil Veces un Instante*](https://www.london.gov.uk/programmes-strategies/arts-culture/fourth-plinth-trafalgar-square) (A Thousand Times in an Instant)** (2024, Fourth Plinth, Trafalgar Square, London) — a 3.3-metric-ton cube covered in 726 face masks taken from trans, nonbinary, and gender-nonconforming people from Mexico and the UK, many of them sex workers. Displayed concave-side-out (the inside of the face visible), not the outside. References the Mesoamerican **tzompantli** (skull rack). Dedicated to **Karla La Borrada**, a trans singer and sex worker murdered in Juárez in December 2015, who had been Margolles' collaborator and friend. The casts are designed to decompose in London's rain — "we know how it's going to start. But we don't know how it will finish."

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Margolles operates in **three overlapping circuits**:

1. **European Major Institutions**: MMK Frankfurt, Fridericianum Kassel, MUSEION Bolzano, PAC Milan, Migros Zurich, Kunsthalle Krems, Centre Pompidou collection — the legitimating axis of international contemporary art from a Latin American perspective. She's been in these institutions since 2004.

2. **Biennials (top-tier)**: Venice Biennale (Mexican Pavilion 2009, group 2019), Liverpool Biennial 2006, Cuenca Biennale 2023. The biennial circuit is where her work travels and where its political context reaches international audiences.

3. **US Major Museums + Public Art**: Guggenheim 2005, LACMA 2010, MoMA collection, PAMM Miami, Fourth Plinth 2024. The US museums have been slower than Europeans; the Fourth Plinth was a breakthrough into the UK/public art arena.

**What's notable in her circuit choices:** She is largely absent from the media art festival circuit (no Ars Electronica, no Transmediale, no ISEA) and from the commercial art fair mainstream. Her chosen spaces are institutional — museums, biennials, public commissions. This reflects her positioning: she is a serious institutional artist, not a commercial or media-art practitioner. The choice to represent Mexico at Venice in 2009 — despite the near-rejection by the Mexican government — was a defining act of institutional courage.

The **Fourth Plinth** (2024) marks an unusual expansion into London's civic public space. The work is permanent for two years and was prominently reviewed by the Guardian, BBC, and AP.

## Why Interesting

Margolles is doing something that most political art fails to do: **making the social conditions of death tangible as material**. The water in *En el aire* is not a metaphor — it literally *is* the dissolved residue of murdered bodies. This is a form of radical materiality that connects directly to several of Amber's research interests:

1. **More-than-human materiality**: The body as object, the morgue water as agent, the crime scene soil as archive. Margolles collapses the distinction between subject (the dead) and medium (their material traces) in a way that resonates with new materialism, Karen Barad-style intra-action, and material witness.

2. **Art-science bridging**: She is genuinely dual-trained — forensic pathologist AND artist. Not a collaboration, not a "science-informed" project, but a single practitioner who lives in both worlds. This is a model for how the art-science combination creates genuinely new perception.

3. **Mortality as research**: Her entire career is an investigation into death as a social phenomenon. This connects to Amber's interest in machine death, machine consciousness, and mortality as a philosophical category. How do you make death legible? Margolles offers one answer: by making it materially present.

4. **Practice as political witness**: She uses the morgue as a research laboratory for Mexican society. "Looking at the dead you see society." This methodological stance — using an unusual access point to illuminate social structure — is transferable to many domains.

5. **Trans women, femicide, and structural violence**: The Fourth Plinth work puts her at the center of a global conversation about trans femicide, border violence, and the invisibility of marginalized bodies. This connects to critical theory traditions Amber engages with.

6. **London presence**: She's now visible in London (Tate collection + Fourth Plinth 2024). She has a gallery in NYC. This makes her more accessible to Amber.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Radical Materialism as method.** Margolles generates artistic intensity not through representation but through literal material transfer. The conceptual innovation is: if the medium IS the murder victim's body, then the aesthetic encounter is a direct encounter with political violence — not a metaphor for it. This creates an unavoidable ethical demand on the viewer. It's almost forensic phenomenology.

**Forensic science as artistic methodology.** She imports the protocols of forensic evidence — chain of custody, material specificity, witness documentation — into the art world. The rigor of forensic science becomes a curatorial/ethical protocol.

**The morgue as social mirror.** Her conceptual frame — "looking at the dead you see society" — is a powerful Marxist-adjacent insight: the most vulnerable, most abandoned bodies end up at the morgue, and the pattern of death reveals the pattern of social exclusion. This is structural analysis through the body.

### Career Architecture

**Dual credential strategy.** By genuinely training as a forensic pathologist AND as an artist, she occupied a position no one else could. The forensic training was not decorative — it was necessary access. This gave her both a unique vocabulary and unique physical access that couldn't be replicated through institutional art education alone.

**SEMEFO as collective launchpad.** Starting a collective rather than going solo in 1990 was a strategic move: it provided institutional weight, shared risk, and the ability to make work that would have been legally and logistically impossible for a solo artist. When she left SEMEFO in the late 1990s, she took its methodology but added refinement.

**The Venice gambit (2009).** Accepting the commission to represent Mexico at Venice knowing the work would directly critique the Mexican state's drug war — and then nearly being expelled from the pavilion — was high-risk and high-reward. It defined her as an artist willing to use institutional platforms against the institution's implicit interests. This is the key move of her career.

**Working-class/marginalized bodies as subjects.** Her consistent choice of the murdered poor, trans women, sex workers, and migrants as subjects — rather than aestheticizing violence from a safe distance — is a political and ethical position that has defined her audience and her reception.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **1990: Co-founding SEMEFO** — established forensic art as a collective practice in Mexico. Created a body of work that had no analog elsewhere. Enabled access to materials that solo practice couldn't sustain.

2. **Late 1990s: Leaving SEMEFO for solo practice** — signal that she had internalized the methodology and no longer needed the collective framework. Solo practice allowed greater refinement and international mobility.

3. **2004: *Muerte Sin Fin* at MMK Frankfurt** — first major European institutional solo. European museums gave her the platform that Mexican institutions were not yet ready to give. This followed the typical Latin American artist's path: European recognition before domestic recognition.

4. **2009: Venice Biennale, Mexican Pavilion** — career-defining. The near-expulsion by the Mexican government made international news. [[Cuauhtémoc Medina]]'s curatorial framing was essential in transforming the work from provocation into indictment.

5. **2012: Prince Claus + Artes Mundi double** — formal international legitimation. The Prince Claus Fund (Netherlands) and Artes Mundi (UK/Wales) both deal with art under difficult conditions. The double award cemented her status as the leading voice on political violence in contemporary art.

6. **2016–present: Ciudad Juárez and trans community work** — deepening specificity. *Pistas de Baile* and then the Fourth Plinth work mark a turn toward the trans community in Juárez specifically, building long-term relationships rather than one-off interventions.

7. **2024: Fourth Plinth, Trafalgar Square** — entering civic, non-art-world space in the UK. The most publicly visible commission of her career. Guardian review was glowing.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Forensic training as artistic method**: The most powerful art-science combinations happen when the artist doesn't just "consult" scientists but actually trains in the methodology. Margolles spent years in morgues before making major work. The depth of methodological commitment is what makes the work irreducible.

2. **The body as social evidence**: In Amber's terms — what if the AI system IS the body? What if machine behavior, machine death, machine consciousness can be approached forensically — tracing material evidence, chains of causality, structural inequalities embedded in code? Margolles' methodology is transferable.

3. **Choosing the right platform at the right moment**: The Venice move in 2009 was not a traditional career-building move — it was a platform with exactly the right institutional weight to make the drug war legible to an international audience. How to choose the right platform for politically charged work is something Margolles has mastered.

4. **Duration and relationship-building**: The Juárez work took years. The trust required for *Mil Veces un Instante* (getting trans people to lie down and have their faces cast) required years of community relationship. This is methodologically significant — there is no shortcut.

5. **Radical material specificity**: The difference between "water" and "water used to wash murder victims" is everything. In research/design terms: what is the equivalent move? Not simulating machine death but making machine death materially present? The commitment to literal material transfer as conceptual force.
