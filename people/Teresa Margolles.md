# Teresa Margolles

Mexican artist whose practice centers on death, forensic evidence, and the bodies of violence's victims — particularly those killed in Mexico's drug wars. Trained as a forensic pathologist before becoming an artist. Works with forensic materials: water used to wash corpses, fat from liposuctions, soil from crime scenes, clothing of murder victims. Her practice makes the social and political conditions that produce death legible through aesthetic displacement.

## Key Works
- *¿De qué otra cosa podríamos hablar?* (2009, Venice Biennale) — floor-cloths soaked in water used to wash murder victims' bodies; visitors walked on them unknowingly. Curated by [[Cuauhtémoc Medina]].
- *PM 2010* (2010) — photographs of homicide victims from Mexico City tabloids
- *Frontera* (2010) — sand from Juárez crime scenes exhibited in a glass container
- *En el aire* (2003) — bubbles made with soapy water from corpse washing; the soap film contained the dissolved material of human death

## Key Relationships
- **[[Cuauhtémoc Medina]]** — primary curator; the Venice 2009 collaboration is the defining moment in how her work was framed for international audiences.

## Why Interesting
Her practice represents one of the most radical materializations of political art: the materials themselves carry the history. The work asks: what do we owe to those whose deaths we aestheticize? How can art be a form of witness? Relevant to thinking about how art can engage structural violence without aestheticizing it.
