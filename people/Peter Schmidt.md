# Peter Schmidt

Berlin-born British artist, painter, color theorist, and pioneering multimedia exhibitor. Best known as co-creator (with [[Brian Eno]]) of **Oblique Strategies** (1975) — the oracle card set that became one of the most influential creative process tools of the 20th century. Teacher at Watford College of Art; part of a generation of British art school teachers who profoundly shaped students who went on to transform art and music. Died suddenly January 1980, just before his planned collaborative exhibition with Eno in Brussels.

## Key Works
- **[Oblique Strategies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblique_Strategies)** (with Brian Eno, 1975–) — oracle cards with aphorisms for overcoming creative blocks. Six editions. Used in David Bowie's Berlin Trilogy sessions. Based on Schmidt's own "Thoughts Behind The Thoughts" system.
- **PROGRAM series** (from 1963) — concept paintings; systematic/rule-based approach to painting.
- **Cybernetic Serendipity** (1968, ICA) — Musical director for this landmark cybernetics-meets-art exhibition curated by Jasia Reichardt.
- **Portrait of Eno with Allusions** — watercolor portrait of Brian Eno; considered for *Before and After Science* cover.
- **64 I Ching hexagram drawings** (1972) — series of 64 drawings based on I Ching hexagrams.
- **1500 silk screen portraits of Eno** — four used on *Taking Tiger Mountain (By Strategy)* cover.

## Key Relationships
- **[[Brian Eno]]** — lifelong friend and collaborator. Met at Ipswich in late 1960s as visiting lecturer. Combined their independently-developed aphorism systems to create Oblique Strategies.
- **Hansjörg Mayer** — publisher/collaborator
- **Mark Boyle** — collaborated on "Son et Lumière" performances

## Why Interesting
Schmidt is the under-acknowledged half of one of the most important creative process artifacts in history. His "PROGRAM" concept — painting from systems and rules before rule-based art was a movement — connects cybernetics to artistic practice. His death at 48, just days before their joint Brussels exhibition, is one of the great "what ifs" of art history. The Oblique Strategies artifact itself deserves serious study as an intersection of art, game design, and cognitive tools.
