# Jennifer Gabrys

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Full deep research completed. All works, collaborators, projects, venue circuit, Research Taste Analysis.

**Chair in Media, Culture and Environment** in the Department of Sociology at the University of Cambridge. Pioneer of digital and computational environmental humanities. Leads the [Planetary Praxis](https://planetarypraxis.org) research group. Principal Investigator on three ERC-funded projects: [Citizen Sense](https://citizensense.net) (Starting Grant, €1.5M), [AirKit](https://citizensense.net/projects/airkit/) (Proof of Concept), and [Smart Forests](https://smartforests.net) (Consolidator Grant, €2M). Author of four monographs, co-editor of the *Planetarities* book series (Goldsmiths Press/MIT Press), and managing editor of *Big Data & Society*. Her work investigates how environmental sensing technologies, IoT devices, and citizen science platforms reshape ecological knowledge, governance, and participation.

## Background

Jennifer Gabrys's trajectory moves from art/design practice through media theory to sociology of environments. Her earliest works (early 2000s) were practice-based: book art, parking lot installations at the University of Minnesota (Jerome Foundation fellow), online exhibitions. Her first monograph, *Digital Rubbish* (2011), established her as a media archaeologist of electronic waste — treating discarded devices as sites of environmental and political inquiry.

She was based at **Goldsmiths, University of London** from at least 2012, where she won the ERC Starting Grant for Citizen Sense (2013–2018). The Citizen Sense project marked a pivotal shift: from writing about sensing to *doing* sensing — deploying actual air quality monitors with communities in Pennsylvania (fracking) and Deptford, London (urban pollution). This practice-based turn produced *Program Earth* (2016) and *How to Do Things with Sensors* (2019).

She moved to **Cambridge** (likely around 2019–2020), taking up the Chair in Media, Culture and Environment — a position that signals Cambridge Sociology's investment in digital-environmental research. At Cambridge she won the ERC Consolidator Grant for Smart Forests (€2M, 2021–2026), her most ambitious project: investigating how digital technologies transform forest governance globally.

Her intellectual trajectory shows a consistent widening of scale: electronic devices → sensor networks → urban air → planetary forests. Each step builds on the previous while expanding the frame from media studies to environmental sociology to planetary governance.

## Domains
- **Environmental sensing and citizen science** — core domain. How sensors change what counts as environmental knowledge and who gets to produce it
- **Computational environments / "Program Earth"** — environments as computational systems; sensors don't just observe but *program* environments
- **Smart forests and digital ecology** — how forests become data infrastructure; acoustic monitoring, IoT, satellite sensing in forests
- **Environmental data politics** — "just good enough data," data citizenship, the politics of environmental evidence
- **Media archaeology of waste** — electronic waste as environmental-cultural artifact
- **More-than-human politics** — lichens, plastic bags, trees as political actors in sensing networks
- **Participatory design for environmental justice** — toolkits (Dustbox, AirKit, Frackbox) as democratic infrastructure
- **Planetary governance** — the "planetarities" framework; planetary-scale thinking beyond globalism

## Why Interesting

Gabrys is one of the very few scholars who works rigorously on both computation AND ecology — not "digital humanities applied to environment" but genuine engagement with what computation means for non-human agency and ecological knowledge. Her core question — *what happens when environments become computational systems?* — is directly analogous to ALife questions about distributed cognition, emergence, and machine agency.

What makes her exceptional:
- **Practice-based theory**: She doesn't just write about sensors — she builds and deploys them. This loop between making and theorizing is exactly Amber's mode
- **Scale-spanning**: From lichens to planetary governance, she works at every scale simultaneously
- **Infrastructure builder**: Citizen Sense, Smart Forests Atlas, AirKit — she builds platforms, not just papers
- **Political commitment**: Environmental justice is not a topic but a method — working with fracking communities, air pollution victims, forest communities
- **Open knowledge**: All books on Manifold, GitHub repos for tools, multilingual atlas. Radical openness

The *Program Earth* argument — that when we deploy environmental sensors, we don't just observe environments but *transform them into computational systems* — is the most precise academic bridge to Amber's ALife work. Distributed sensing = distributed cognition. Environmental programming = emergence. The parallels are exact.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
- **Practice-to-theory loop**: Every concept emerges from fieldwork and tool-building, not pure theorizing. "Just good enough data" came from actual deployment experience. "Citizen sensing" came from watching people use sensors
- **Environment-as-computational-system**: The core conceptual innovation. Not "digital tools for environment" but "environments AS computational entities." This reframing is what makes *Program Earth* field-defining
- **Scaling through projects**: Each book corresponds to a project phase — Digital Rubbish (media archaeology) → Program Earth (sensing theory) → How to Do Things with Sensors (practical guide) → Citizens of Worlds (political synthesis). The project IS the concept

### Career Architecture
- **Project-to-grant-to-field building**: Citizen Sense (ERC Starting) → AirKit (Proof of Concept) → Smart Forests (ERC Consolidator). Each grant builds on previous, creating institutional infrastructure that outlasts individual papers
- **Platform as field declaration**: Smart Forests Atlas isn't just a research output — it's a *field infrastructure*. By creating the atlas, she defines what "smart forests studies" looks like
- **Strategic institutional migration**: Goldsmiths (creative, practice-based) → Cambridge (prestige, sociology). This move reframes her work from "digital arts" to "environmental sociology" without changing what she actually does
- **Editorial gatekeeping**: Managing editor of *Big Data & Society* + editor of Planetarities series = she shapes what counts as legitimate in two adjacent fields

### Methodology Signature
- **Toolkit methodology**: Every project produces a *kit* — Frackbox, Dustbox, AirKit, Phyto-Sensor Toolkit. These are simultaneously research instruments, publications, exhibition objects, and citizen tools. Quadruple-use methodology
- **Participatory fieldwork at scale**: Not lab work, not pure theory — actual deployment with communities in Pennsylvania, Deptford, forests worldwide. But systematized enough to publish in top journals
- **Atlas as method**: The Smart Forests Atlas is both output and methodology — a living, collaborative, multilingual research platform that generates its own research questions

### Network Strategy
- **Postdoc pipeline as research multiplication**: Her team (Westerlaken, Simlai, Lewis Hood, Fatimah, Urzedo, Ritts) each bring distinct disciplinary expertise (interaction design, conservation geography, Black/Indigenous studies, STS, political ecology, sound studies). She publishes with ALL of them, creating a multi-authored body of work that covers more ground than any individual could
- **Series editorship as coalition building**: The Planetarities series brings Glissant, Vergès, Elaine Gan into her orbit — postcolonial and decolonial thinkers who expand her environmental work's political frame
- **Cross-circuit presence**: STS journals + Environmental humanities + HCI conferences + Art exhibitions + Policy briefings. She is legible in five different worlds simultaneously

1. **Early 2000s: Art practice** — Book art, installations at Minnesota. Established practice-based credentials
