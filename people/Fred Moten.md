# Fred Moten

Poet, theorist, and Professor of Performance Studies at **New York University (Tisch)**. Co-author (with Stefano Harney) of *The Undercommons: Fugitive Planning and Black Study* — one of the most influential texts in Black radical theory and post-academic philosophy of the last decade. A singular figure who moves between poetry, jazz criticism, performance theory, and philosophy.

Moten's work centers on **blackness as an aesthetic and social resource** — not as a problem to be solved but as a generative outside that has always exceeded the systems that attempt to capture it. The undercommons is the fugitive space of study and life that happens around, below, and despite institutional structures.

## Key Concepts

- **The Undercommons**: the fugitive social space of Black study, music, improvisation, and collective life that exists around the university but refuses to be domesticated by it. Co-developed with Stefano Harney in *The Undercommons* (Minor Compositions, 2013).
- **Blackness as errancy**: blackness is not a fixed identity but a form of excess and movement that undoes fixed categories. This resonates with [[Erin Manning]]'s "minor gesture" — both theorize the minor, the surplus, the excessive-beyond-norm.
- **Performance as ontology**: performance is not something that happens to subjects; it is how the social happens. Black performance (jazz, improvisation, experimental music) is the model for a non-representational theory of social life.
- **Study without schooling**: learning as a collective activity that doesn't require institutional authorization. This directly feeds Manning's alter-pedagogy.

## Key Works

- [*The Undercommons: Fugitive Planning and Black Study*](http://www.minorcompositions.info/wp-content/uploads/2013/04/undercommons-web.pdf) (with Stefano Harney; Minor Compositions, 2013) — free download; one of the most circulated texts in Black studies, education theory, and radical politics. The undercommons as a space that is not outside institutions but beneath and around them.
- *In the Break: The Aesthetics of the Black Radical Tradition* (University of Minnesota Press, 2003) — his first major book; Black music, performance, and the aesthetics of resistance.
- *Consent Not to Be a Single Being* (3-vol. series; Duke UP, 2017–2018): *Black and Blur*, *Stolen Life*, *The Universal Machine* — massive trilogical work on blackness, philosophy, poetry, music.
- *The Service Porch* (Letter Machine Editions, 2016) — poetry.
- *Stolen Life* (Duke UP, 2018) — part of the trilogy; explores racial capitalism, debt, and alternative social forms.
- *All That Beauty* (Letter Machine Editions, 2019) — poetry.

## Connection to Manning

Moten blurbed [[Erin Manning]]'s *The Minor Gesture* (Duke UP, 2016). Manning's engagement with black life, fugitivity, and the undercommons in *The Minor Gesture* and *For a Pragmatics of the Useless* (2020) draws directly on Moten and Harney's work. The formulation "all black life is neurodiverse life" in Manning's work creates a resonance between Moten's blackness theory and her neurodiversity theory. This is one of the more generative cross-theoretical encounters in recent humanities.

## Why Interesting

Moten is one of the most significant living theorists of **what happens at the edge of institutional life** — the fugitive, the excessive, the irreducibly collective. His work is directly relevant for anyone thinking about alternative knowledge production, non-standard subjectivities, and what counts as "study" or "intelligence" outside mainstream frameworks.

His connection to the SenseLab/Manning orbit is significant: it shows that process philosophy and Black radical theory are not separate intellectual traditions but can be read as addressing the same ontological problem (the minor, the excessive, the surplus) from different vantage points.
