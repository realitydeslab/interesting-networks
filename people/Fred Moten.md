# Fred Moten

Poet, cultural theorist, and Professor of Performance Studies & Comparative Literature at **NYU Tisch**. Co-author (with [[Stefano Harney]]) of *[The Undercommons: Fugitive Planning & Black Study](http://www.minorcompositions.info/wp-content/uploads/2013/04/undercommons-web.pdf)* (2013) — one of the most-circulated texts in radical academia over the last decade, passed hand to hand like samizdat among adjuncts, students, and artists. A **MacArthur Fellow (2020)**. Works simultaneously as a poet and a philosopher; his books resist the genre boundary between criticism and verse.

Moten's project: to take sound — specifically the sounds of Black life, Black music, Black performance — as the primary matter of thought. Not the image, not the text, but the sound. Phonic substance precedes and exceeds the symbolic order. What does it mean that Frederick Douglass heard the screams of his Aunt Hester as "the entrance to the hell of slavery"? Moten's answer: *that scream is a form of resistance*, not just of suffering. The Black radical tradition is not reducible to politics; it is an aesthetic practice of survival, refusal, and joy that has always already exceeded the systems that attempted to contain it.

## Background

Born August 18, 1962 in **Las Vegas, Nevada** — raised in the segregated Black neighborhood on the western side of the city, in a transplanted Deep South community. His parents were part of the Great Migration: father originally from Louisiana, mother from Arkansas. Father worked at the **Las Vegas Convention Center** (later for Pan American Airlines as bus driver/porter); mother was a grade school teacher, the first in her family to attend college. Raised Catholic.

Moten traces his earliest poetic training to his grandmother, who recited Paul Laurence Dunbar and John Keats *while cleaning white women's houses* — a figure he has returned to many times as the origin point of a certain way of holding together beauty and labor, vernacular and canonical.

Enrolled at **Harvard** in 1980 as an economics major; was drawn off-track by Noam Chomsky, political activism, and general sociopolitical curiosity. Required to take a year off after his first year. During that year, **worked as a janitor at the Nevada Test Site** and discovered T.S. Eliot and Joseph Conrad — began writing poetry. Returned to Harvard as an English major, more focused. Met future collaborator **Stefano Harney** through the Harvard Advocate literary magazine. Studied under **Helen Vendler** (Modern American Poetry) and **Barbara Johnson** (Deconstruction — "the first-year deconstructionist").

**BA Harvard, 1984. PhD (English Literature) UC Berkeley, 1994.**

After Berkeley, taught at **University of Iowa**, then **Duke University** (as Professor of Modern Poetry — the position he held when *The Undercommons* was first published), then **UC Riverside** (Distinguished Professor, now Emeritus). Joined **NYU Tisch Department of Performance Studies** in 2017. As of the Griffin Prize profile (2024), holds joint appointment in **Performance Studies and Comparative Literature** at NYU.

Lives in New York with his partner — poet and theorist **Laura Harris** — and two children.

## Domains

- **Black Studies / Black Radical Tradition** — aesthetics of Black music, literature, performance; the political-aesthetic project of understanding blackness as a generative force rather than a problem
- **Performance Studies** — performance as ontology; the study of how the social happens through acts, gestures, sound
- **Poetics / Poetry** — shaped prose; polyphonic verse influenced by jazz and the Black vernacular; the Pound tradition filtered through Black performance
- **Critical Theory** — engages Kant, Adorno, Hegel, Marx, Fanon, Derrida; consistently routes European theory through Black aesthetic practice
- **Jazz Studies / Improvisation Theory** — deep engagement with Cecil Taylor, John Coltrane, Billie Holiday, Ornette Coleman, Curtis Mayfield; improvisation as the model for collective, non-hierarchical study
- **Queer Theory** — engaged with Jack Halberstam and the trans studies orbit; was on board of directors of CLAGS (Center for Lesbian and Gay Studies, CUNY)
- **Fugitivity / Alternative Institution Critique** — the undercommons, study without schooling, fugitive planning as modes of living otherwise within and against institutions

## Key Works

### Theoretical / Critical Books

- [*In the Break: The Aesthetics of the Black Radical Tradition*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/in-the-break) (University of Minnesota Press, 2003) — His first and most cited scholarly book. Begins with Frederick Douglass's account of the screams of his Aunt Hester at the hands of a plantation overseer: Moten hears this not as background noise to the "spectacle" of slavery but as the foundational moment of Black performance — the scream that exceeds the system, that generates what he calls "phonic substance," sound as the site of a radical excess that cannot be fully captured by representation. Moves through Billie Holiday, Robert Hayden, Amiri Baraka, Sun Ra. Radically reframes what counts as "the aesthetic" in African American cultural production.

- *[The Undercommons: Fugitive Planning & Black Study](http://www.minorcompositions.info/wp-content/uploads/2013/04/undercommons-web.pdf)* (with Stefano Harney; Minor Compositions / Autonomedia, 2013) — **Free download**. 166 pp. Introduction by Jack Halberstam. Six essays + interview with Stevphen Shukaitis. Critiques the university as a mechanism for converting insurgents into professional-managerial class subjects; proposes the *undercommons* — the fugitive social life that happens beneath and around the institution — as the site of genuine study. *The shipped* essay draws an analogy between modern logistics and the Middle Passage. Circulated virally among adjuncts, precarious academics, and artists as a kind of samizdat; Maggie Nelson listed it as one of her ten favorite books. Spanish translation by Cristina Rivera Garza (2018).

- **[*consent not to be a single being* trilogy](https://www.dukeupress.edu/the-universal-machine) (Duke University Press, 2017–2018)**
  - [*Black and Blur*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/black-and-blur) (2017) — essays on Black aesthetics, philosophy, and music; takes the Kantian sublime and routes it through Black cultural production. "Blur" as a figure for the resistance to definition that characterizes Black life and art. Named Best Book of 2018 by multiple critics.
  - [*Stolen Life*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/stolen-life) (2018) — essays on fugitivity, racial capitalism, debt, and the essay form itself as a practice of pursuit; Nathaniel Mackey calls it "diagnostic, ministerial, rhapsodic." The title riffs on Harriet Jacobs and on the ongoing theft of Black life.
  - [*The Universal Machine*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/the-universal-machine) (2018) — completes the trilogy; the "universal machine" is both the Turing Machine and a critique of the universalism that has organized Western philosophy through Black exclusion. Together the trilogy was named by Maggie Nelson and Jess Row as some of the most important theoretical work of the decade. Named by *Vulture* as reinventing theory and criticism.

- *[All Incomplete](https://www.minorcompositions.info/)* (with Stefano Harney; Minor Compositions, 2021) — follow-up to *The Undercommons*; includes a 2021 conversation with Harney and Stevphen Shukaitis published in *e-flux Journal* #116 ("Refusing Completion: A Conversation"), continuing the project of thinking study, planning, and Black social life as sites of radical incompletion and non-closure.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Moten moves in **four overlapping circuits** simultaneously, which is unusual:

1. **Academic Black Studies / Performance Studies circuit**: Duke, NYU, UC Riverside, Iowa, Berkeley, Columbia, Harvard. His presence at these institutions gives him both the material support (salary, research money) and the scholarly legitimacy to be taken seriously in theory. The move from Duke (English/Poetry) to NYU (Performance Studies) in 2017 was a signal: he was expanding from literature into the broader study of performance, sound, and social life.

2. **Small press / experimental poetry circuit**: Pressed Wafer, Leon Works, Letter Machine Editions, Wesleyan UP, Wave Books. Moten self-funded the publication of *The Feel Trio* with research money and published it through a friend's press — an act that embodies the undercommons thesis itself. The move to Wave Books (2023) broadens this circuit to a more nationally visible audience. His poetry readings are in small venues (Zinc Bar, etc.) — he has not moved to the stadium-reading circuit.

3. **Art world adjacent circuit**: New Museum advisory, e-flux essays, Wu Tsang collaboration, Theaster Gates connections. The art world pays for writing and shows in ways the poetry world doesn't — Moten has noted this explicitly ("I was shocked that they pay you to write"). He maintains a position of productive distance from the art market — he's the "activists' theorist, the contemporary art institution's darling" (Full Stop) but also wary of being a "content provider" who adds monetary value to artworks without his consent.

4. **Radical/Left intellectual public sphere**: *The New Inquiry*, Brooklyn Rail, e-flux, the minor compositions / autonomedia orbit. The undercommons as samizdat — distributed freely as PDF, circulated at protests, in occupied universities, among precarious academics. His engagement with Palestine solidarity, his 2017 Toronto conversation with Kelley, are part of this circuit.

**Cross-circuit dynamics**: The unusual thing about Moten is that he doesn't privilege one circuit over others. He is simultaneously a serious academic theorist (Duke UP trilogy), a small press poet (Letter Machine), an art world intellectual (e-flux, New Museum), and a radical public intellectual (Undercommons as free PDF, Palestine solidarity). This multi-circuit presence amplifies each circuit — academic legitimacy gives weight to the radical positions; the radical positions give edge to the academic work; the poetry gives language to the theory; the theory gives depth to the poetry.

## Why Interesting

Moten is one of the most significant living theorists of **what happens at the edges and undersides of institutional life**. His concept of the undercommons is not just an academic concept: it's a description of how the most interesting intellectual and artistic work has always happened — outside and despite the official channels. This is directly relevant to Amber's research interests:

- **Alternative knowledge production**: the undercommons is a model for how communities generate knowledge outside credentialing systems — directly relevant to Amber's interest in communities as research infrastructure
- **Participatory / democratic study**: "study without schooling" is a form of radical peer-to-peer learning that parallels the Scandinavian participatory design tradition but from a very different genealogy
- **Fugitivity as method**: Moten's concept of fugitivity — making ways through impossibility, finding routes through structures that weren't designed for you — resonates with design practice as making space for what doesn't yet exist
- **Sound as medium of resistance**: the emphasis on phonic substance over visual representation is a challenge to design's ocularcentrism; what would it mean to design for sound, for the unseen and unseeable?
- **Manning connection**: the Moten-Manning orbit (undercommons ↔ minor gesture) is one of the most generative cross-theoretical encounters available to Amber; both are at NYU (Manning's SenseLab orbit, Moten at Tisch); both published by Duke UP

The New Yorker profile captures it well: Moten is doing a "radical critique of the present" from within the institutions he critiques — not by exiting, but by practicing fugitivity from within.

## Research Taste Analysis

**This section analyzes HOW Moten builds his research, not just WHAT he researches.**

### Conceptual Strategy

**Phonic turn as founding move**: Moten's entire project is built on a single insight that he has elaborated for 20+ years: *take sound, not sight, as the primary matter of interpretation*. Most cultural criticism reads images, texts, meanings. Moten *listens* — to screams, to jazz solos, to the sound of words before their meaning resolves. This is his version of a "two-word concept" move: "phonic substance" — what sound carries that exceeds and precedes language. The concept is simple but the elaborations are endless.

**Framework multiplication**: Moten routinely takes a European framework (Kant's sublime, Hegel's dialectic, Adorno's negative dialectics, Derrida's pharmakon) and runs it through Black aesthetic practice until it either fails or gets transformed. The result is not a "Black critique of Kant" — it's a new reading of the sublime that happens to make Kant's framework inadequate. This is a powerful move: use the canonical framework as a foil, let Black aesthetics transform it, end up with something neither fully Western philosophy nor fully cultural criticism.

**Poetics as theory**: Moten writes his theoretical essays in a prose style that is itself a form of poetry — dense, musical, associative, punning. This is not an accident or an affectation. It embodies the argument: if phonic substance precedes semantic content, then the *sound* of theoretical prose is itself meaningful. Readers either love this or find it impenetrable; both responses confirm his method.

### Career Architecture

**Dual-track publication strategy**: Moten publishes poetry and theory simultaneously but from different publishers. Academic theory → Duke University Press (prestige, credentialing, citation). Poetry → small independent presses (Letter Machine, Wesleyan, Wave Books). This is unusual. Most theorists who write poetry publish it quietly or with minor presses that no one in theory reads. Moten makes both tracks legible — the poetry is reviewed and wins prizes; the theory is read by poets. The mutual contamination is the point.

**Free distribution as accumulation**: *The Undercommons* was made freely downloadable as a PDF by Moten and Harney. They paid for it themselves. The result: viral circulation, samizdat status, adoption in protest movements, art school curricula, and adjunct organizing. The book has been read more widely than any academic press book could have achieved. This is a deliberate move — they prioritized reach over royalties.

**Slow-build trilogy**: The *consent not to be a single being* trilogy (2017–2018) collected 15 years of essays written at Duke and UC Riverside. Moten is not a fast-cycle academic — he doesn't publish papers annually to optimize citation counts. He writes slowly, deeply, and then releases accumulated work in large packages. The trilogy was received as a major event precisely because of this density.

**Institutional prestige as protective cover**: Moten's move from Duke to NYU (2017) was a consolidation of institutional position at the highest level. But he doesn't use this position to become a mainstream commentator — he uses it as protective cover for radical work. The MacArthur Fellowship in 2020 amplified this: he can now say things about Palestine, about the university, about anti-capitalism, with the authority of a MacArthur Fellow, which makes it harder to dismiss.

### Methodology Signature

**The fugitive method**: Moten practices what he theorizes. His essays don't arrive at conclusions — they *move through* material without settling. The structure of an essay is often associative, recursive, inconcludable. He will take a photograph (Eakins #308), a scream (Aunt Hester), a jazz solo (Cecil Taylor), and follow the sound through philosophy, history, and poetics without resolving into a thesis. This is "fugitive planning" as an intellectual method.

**Listening as close reading**: Where literary critics do close reading of texts, Moten does close listening to sounds. His method involves attending to the sound-dimensions of written texts (rhythm, repetition, puns, polyvalence of spoken words) as well as literal auditory performances (recordings, live improvisation).

### Network Strategy

**Poetry-theory bridge as unique position**: The overlap of Moten's two audiences (academic theorists and experimental poets) is nearly unique. Nathaniel Mackey is the other great example. This overlap creates unusual citation patterns — he's cited in education theory, labor studies, queer theory, Black studies, poetry criticism, and performance studies. No single disciplinary community "owns" him.

**Art world as secondary but productive adjacency**: Moten is careful about the art world — wary of being a "content provider" who adds monetary value without consent. But he maintains productive adjacency: e-flux essays, New Museum advisory, artist friendships. This gives him access to institutions (and their budgets) without full capture.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **(2003) *In the Break***: Established the phonic substance / Black aesthetics framework with a single book that combined music studies, literary criticism, and performance theory. Named Frederick Douglass's aunt's scream as the founding moment of Black performance — a move so powerful it has been cited thousands of times. **Why it worked**: the argument was simple and shocking; it reframed the entire archive of African American aesthetics.

2. **(2013) *The Undercommons* as free PDF**: Released the most radical book of the decade as a free download, paying for it themselves. **Why it worked**: It reached people (adjuncts, precarious academics, artists, activists) who couldn't afford $25 books and had no institutional library access. This is not altruism — it's a distribution theory. **What it enabled**: viral adoption, citation across disciplines, the book becoming a touchstone of the post-2008 precarity moment.

3. **(2014) *The Feel Trio***: National Book Award finalist, LA Times Prize. A poetry collection that competed on mainstream terms while maintaining his experimental mode. **Why it worked**: showed that the Black experimental poetry tradition could be taken seriously on its own terms, not just as a footnote to theory. **What it enabled**: credentialing in the poetry world, broader readership.

4. **(2016-2018) Guggenheim + FCA + Trilogy**: Collected 15 years of essays into the *consent not to be a single being* trilogy. Three books in two years from Duke UP. **Why it worked**: the accumulation of material created a gravitational event; critics had to respond to the scope. Maggie Nelson, Jess Row, Lidija Haas all named it the intellectual event of 2018.

5. **(2020) MacArthur Fellowship**: The $625,000 grant gave him financial freedom and mainstream recognition simultaneously. **Why it worked**: Timing — 2020 was the year of George Floyd uprisings; the MacArthur Foundation chose Moten as an explicit statement about the importance of Black intellectual life. **What it enabled**: could fund projects, "do stuff financially" he couldn't do before; amplified his public intellectual presence.

6. **(2023) *perennial fashion presence falling* with Wave Books**: Moving to Wave Books — a major independent poetry publisher — signals consolidation of his presence in the poetry world as distinct from (but not opposed to) the academic theory world. Griffin Prize longlist confirms this.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Single founding insight, infinite elaboration**: Moten's career is built on one methodological move (listen, don't just read) that he has elaborated across every domain. The power is in the consistency and depth of the single insight, not in constantly generating new ones. For Amber: identify the 1-2 genuine methodological contributions, and elaborate them deeply across domains rather than always moving to new questions.

2. **Free distribution is a power move, not a sacrifice**: The Undercommons PDF has been more influential than any royalty-generating book. For Amber: consider what it would mean to release a key framework, tool, or dataset freely and early, before establishing institutional precedent, as a way of building audience and influence.

3. **The dual-track as protection**: By publishing in both academic and poetic registers, Moten cannot be fully captured by either. Neither academics nor poets can claim him entirely. For Amber: maintaining practice in multiple registers (research papers, design artifacts, public writing, code) creates similar protection against being captured by any single community's norms.

4. **Fugitive planning over strategic planning**: Moten practices "fugitive planning" — not a plan that maximizes a known outcome, but a way of moving through and making use of available structures without being determined by them. For design research: this is a model for how to build a research program that genuinely exceeds the institution's expectations while still operating within it.

5. **Study without schooling as pedagogical practice**: Moten's teaching (and his broader impact) operates not through official curricula but through the text-as-collective-study-object — *The Undercommons* as a text that people organize reading groups around, discuss in occupied buildings, share in WhatsApp groups. Consider: what would it mean for your own research output to function as a collective study object rather than a paper to be cited?
