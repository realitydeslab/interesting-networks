# Annabel Nicolson

British expanded cinema artist, performance-film pioneer, and feminist infrastructure builder. Made the legendary *Reel Time* (1973) — the canonical example of structural-materialist film as feminist performance. Co-founded [[Circles Cinenova|Circles]] (1979), the UK's first feminist film distribution network. Core member and cinema programmer of the [[London Film-Makers Co-operative (LFMC)]] across three distinct periods (1974, 1976–77, 1992/93). A perfect synthesis of formal rigour and feminist politics within the British avant-garde.

## Background

Annabel Nicolson was born in 1946 and received an unusual interdisciplinary formation: painting at **Hornsey College of Art** (1964–65), then **Edinburgh College of Art** (Drawing and Painting, 1965–69), then postgraduate film at **St Martins School of Art** (1970–71) — exactly when St Martins was becoming one of the key sites of British experimental film under [[Malcolm Le Grice]]'s influence. This education — painting + film + fine art postgraduate — explains the visual and material sensibility that runs through her work.

Before completing her formal education, Nicolson had already taken an active institutional role: from **1969–70 she ran the gallery at the [New Arts Lab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_for_Research_in_Art_and_Technology)** (Robert Street Arts Lab, Camden Town) — the pivotal London counterculture space that also housed the LFMC distribution office and workshop. This put her at the heart of London's experimental film and arts scene from its most formative moment.

She then became one of the LFMC's core figures, serving as **cinema programmer at the Co-op in 1974, 1976–77, and 1992/93** — an unusual three-period involvement that bridges the peak structural-materialist era, the feminist rupture of 1979, and the Co-op's later institutional consolidation.

Alongside her filmmaking practice, Nicolson contributed to two important publications of the period: as **editorial contributor to *Musics* magazine** (1976–79) and as **co-editor and publisher of *Readings* magazine** (1977). These situate her as an active intellectual participant in the experimental film discourse of the 1970s, not just a practitioner.

She was awarded the **John Brinkley Fellowship at Norwich School of Art** (1980–81) — a career recognition that arrived after the founding of Circles, reflecting her established status in the experimental film community.

Her teaching career has been sustained across many years as a **part-time and visiting lecturer** at: Cheltenham, Chelsea, Falmouth, Ravensbourne, Glasgow, Wolverhampton, and St Martins. This breadth — across England and Scotland, across art and film schools — reflects a decentralised pedagogy characteristic of the LFMC generation.

**Quote:** *"It is what happens to things when they are not being looked at that puzzles me."* — a statement that works across ontological registers: the unseen moment in the film loop, the overlooked labour of domestic life, the unobserved process of production.

## Key Works

- **[*Reel Time*](https://lux.org.uk/artist/annabel-nicolson/)** (1973) — The canonical work of British expanded cinema performance. A domestic **sewing machine** is used to sew through a loop of 16mm film while it is simultaneously projected. The needle holes created by the sewing machine appear on screen as **light-events** — punctures through which light passes, becoming image. The material trace of production (the sewing machine's path through the celluloid) is identical to the projected image. No representation: the film literally *is* the sewing action made visible. A perfect synthesis of structural-materialist principle and feminist attention to domestic labour — the sewing machine is simultaneously a tool of women's domestic work and an instrument of formal film deconstruction. One of the most celebrated performances in British avant-garde history; now held in the collections of the **Belgian National Film Archive** and the **British Film Institute**.

- **"Women and the Formal Film"** (1979) — Manifesto co-authored with Felicity Sparrow, Jane Clarke, Jeanette Iljon, [[Lis Rhodes]], Mary Pat Leece, Pat Murphy, and Susan Stein. Published in Phil Drummond (ed.), *Film as Film* (Arts Council of Great Britain, 1979), p. 118. This text emerged from the feminist artists' withdrawal from the Arts Council's *Film as Film* exhibition, documenting the painstaking research they had done on women's contributions to experimental film history (Alice Guy, Germaine Dulac, Maya Deren, Lois Weber) and the marginalisation of that research in the final curatorial frame. The withdrawal and statement became the founding act of Circles.

- **"Women and Creativity"** (recordings, early 1980s) — lecture or documentary recordings distributed through the early Circles catalogue, appearing in the first touring programme alongside films by Susan Stein, Pat Murphy, and Tina Keane's *Clapping Songs*. The specific format (tape recording or film) reflects Circles' deliberately broad definition of distribution media: "offering tape recording, video tapes, slide/tape and performance works for hire alongside film."

- **[*Escaping Notice*](https://collections.vam.ac.uk/)** (artist's book) — held in the collection of the **Victoria and Albert Museum**, London. The title resonates with her quote and her broader preoccupation with what passes unobserved.

- Various performance and film works shown at: [[London Film-Makers Co-operative (LFMC)]], [Acme Gallery](https://www.acme.org.uk/), London; [Stedelijk Museum](https://www.stedelijk.nl/), Amsterdam; Museum of Modern Art (Mumok), Vienna; and in national and international exhibition contexts since the 1970s.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Nicolson's venue circuit is entirely **artist-run infrastructure + European cultural institutions**, with no commercial gallery presence. This is paradigmatic of the LFMC generation:

1. **Artist-run institutions** (home circuit): London Film-Makers' Co-op, New Arts Lab, Acme Gallery, Four Corners Film Workshop — all non-commercial, collectively governed or artist-managed spaces.

2. **Major European modern art museums**: Stedelijk Museum (Amsterdam), Museum of Modern Art Vienna. The Stedelijk in particular was the principal European museum for LFMC-era British structural film — an important validation from the continental museum world that the Co-op generation could not get from British institutions.

3. **Feminist distribution infrastructure**: Circles screenings at Four Corners — women-only, with artist Q&As. A deliberately counter-programmatic circuit that rejected mainstream exhibition conditions.

The key insight: she shows **where the work can be what it needs to be**. *Reel Time* requires a projector and a sewing machine; it is a live performance, not a screened film. This means it can only happen in spaces that accept performance-film and live action. The Stedelijk and Mumok Vienna are the international validation of this kind of work; the LFMC is the home.

No evidence of commercial gallery representation, festival circuit, or institutional commission model. Entirely within the cooperative/cultural-institutional paradigm.

## Writing & Ideas

**Published work:**
- **"Women and the Formal Film"** (with Sparrow, Clarke, Iljon, Rhodes, Leece, Murphy, Stein), in Phil Drummond (ed.), *[Film as Film: Formal Experiment in Film 1910–1975](https://www.artsinstitution.co.uk/)* (London: Arts Council of Great Britain, 1979), p. 118. *The* founding feminist manifesto of British experimental film.
- **Editorial contributor to *[Musics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musics_magazine)* magazine** (1976–79) — Musics was a UK magazine covering new music and performance; Nicolson's involvement connects her to the experimental music/sound scene of that period.
- **Co-editor and publisher of *Readings* magazine** (1977) — a publication embedded in the LFMC/experimental film theoretical context.

**Core ideas in her practice:**
1. **Film-as-material, not film-as-representation** — *Reel Time* refuses to depict anything. It makes the film strip itself the subject: the holes become light, the process becomes image. This is structural-materialist theory enacted rather than argued.
2. **Domestic labour as formal device** — The sewing machine in *Reel Time* is not symbolic; it is functionally destructive of the film. But it carries the full weight of domestic labour politics: the device of women's work (sewing) is used to work on (and work through) the apparatus of cinema.
3. **Visibility as political question** — Her quote ("It is what happens to things when they are not being looked at that puzzles me") is simultaneously a formal claim (what happens to unseen film?) and a feminist claim (what happens to overlooked women's work?). This double valence runs through everything.
4. **Infrastructure as activism** — Circles was not a lobbying campaign; it was a distribution catalogue. The feminist position was enacted through building the institution, not by arguing for it.

## For Amber

1. **🔧 Professional inspiration** — Nicolson represents a mode of artistic practice that combines formal rigour with political commitment without collapsing either into the other. *Reel Time* is a technically radical work that is also obviously feminist — not because it illustrates feminist theory but because the formal choice (sewing machine through film loop) *is* the feminist insight. This kind of double encoding — where formal and political are identical — is a model worth studying.

2. **💡 Career inspiration** — The Circles model: when a structure marginalises your community, don't argue your way in — build parallel infrastructure. The "Women and the Formal Film" withdrawal and the founding of Circles is a complete strategic sequence: document the marginalisation, issue a statement, found the institution. This happened in under a year. The speed matters.

3. **📚 Learning from their path** — Nicolson's three-period engagement with the LFMC (1974, 1976–77, 1992/93) shows a long-term commitment to artist-run infrastructure that persists across 20 years of change. She wasn't just an early member — she returned as cinema programmer after Circles. This longitudinal engagement with the same institution is unusual and worth noting as a career model.

4. **🌍 Areas to know** — The LFMC/Circles/Filmaktion cluster is the deep history of UK artist-run moving-image infrastructure. Understanding it — especially the 1979 feminist rupture — is essential context for anyone working in UK experimental film, feminist art history, or artist-run cultural infrastructure.

5. **🔮 Future involvement** — Cinenova ([cinenova.org](https://cinenova.org/)) is the living successor to Circles and holds Nicolson's distributed works. It continues to operate as a feminist distribution archive; there may be opportunities to engage with their screening programmes or archive access.

## Why Interesting

Annabel Nicolson sits at the intersection of three of the most generative things in 1970s British art:

**1. Structural-materialist film at its most sophisticated.** *Reel Time* is not a demonstration of structural theory — it is structural theory made physical. The sewing machine doesn't represent anything; it acts on the film in real time, producing the image as a by-product of destruction. Watching it, you understand what "film-as-material" means in a way no amount of reading can give you.

**2. Feminist experimental practice at its most tactical.** The "Women and the Formal Film" withdrawal is one of the great institutional manoeuvres in British art history. It was not a protest — it was an action that immediately produced an alternative institution. Circles was founded within months of the withdrawal. The feminist critique of structural film's gender politics didn't dissolve into theory; it built a distribution catalogue.

**3. Artist-run infrastructure at its most enduring.** Circles ran for 12 years and became Cinenova, which runs today. The LFMC ran for 36 years and became LUX, which runs today. Nicolson's involvement with both — as co-founder and as cinema programmer — means she is connected to the longest-running strand of British experimental film institution-building. Her quote (what happens to things when they are not being looked at?) is also the question that drives archival and distribution infrastructure: what happens to films when no one screens them? Circles' answer: they get forgotten. The solution: build the distribution network.

**Connection to Amber's taste:** The combination of formal rigour + critical politics + infrastructure building is exactly the pattern in Amber's research taste. This is the experimental film equivalent of participatory design or critical computing: not theory separate from practice, but theory *as* practice.

## Research Taste Analysis

*(Annabel Nicolson is a historical figure, so this section reads her career retrospectively as a strategic model.)*

### Conceptual Strategy
**Dual encoding:** Nicolson's key conceptual move is finding the formal device that carries maximum political weight without becoming illustration. *Reel Time*'s sewing machine could have been a symbol of domestic oppression — instead it is a functional instrument that performs domestic labour *on the film strip itself*, producing both image and destruction simultaneously. The political and formal are identical, not parallel. This is a more sophisticated position than either pure formalism (ignore politics) or illustrative politics (use form to depict content).

### Career Architecture
**Infrastructure as the work:** Nicolson doesn't separate her administrative/organizational role from her artistic practice. Co-founding Circles is not a side project — it is the political dimension of her expanded cinema practice enacted at an institutional level. Running the LFMC gallery, editing *Readings*, contributing to *Musics* — all of this is the texture of a practice that exists in the community as much as on screen.

**Three-period institutional commitment:** The pattern of returning to the LFMC across 1974, 1976–77, and 1992/93 suggests a commitment to specific institutions over long periods, not the residency-hopping model. She is a builder who revisits and sustains, not an opportunist who moves on.

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **1969–70: Take the New Arts Lab gallery job** — positioned herself at the centre of the London experimental scene before completing her postgraduate study. Intelligence move: learn the infrastructure before making the work.
2. **1973: Make *Reel Time*** — the work that defines her. Created the canonical synthesis of structural-materialist film + feminist domestic labour. Everything else in her career is understood in relation to this.
3. **1974, 1976–77: LFMC cinema programmer** — moved from artist to institutional programmer; shaped what got seen and who programmed it.
4. **1976–79: *Musics* editorial, *Readings* co-editor** — embedded in the theoretical discourse; not just making films but shaping the intellectual framework.
5. **1979: "Women and the Formal Film" + Circles** — the productive rupture. Turned the marginalisation of women's work into a founding act. The withdrawal from *Film as Film* is the action; Circles is the institution.
6. **1980–81: John Brinkley Fellowship, Norwich** — institutional recognition; career consolidation moment.
7. **1992/93: Returns to LFMC as cinema programmer** — long-term institutional fidelity; brings the early period's concerns into the later institutional context.

### What Amber Can Learn
- **The founding-by-withdrawal pattern:** When you are marginalised within an institution you value, the move is not to lobby for inclusion but to document the marginalisation (the statement), exit (the withdrawal), and build the alternative (the institution). This is faster and more durable than accommodation.
- **Form and politics as identical, not parallel:** The most powerful work doesn't illustrate its politics; it enacts them in the formal decision. The sewing machine doesn't *represent* domestic labour — it *performs* domestic labour. Ask: what form would make my political point *identical* with my formal point?
- **Infrastructure IS the practice:** The LFMC, Circles, the magazines — these aren't side projects. They are the community structure that makes the work possible for everyone. Maintaining them IS doing the work. Amber, thinking about communities as research infrastructure, should notice that the LFMC generation built it literally: the workshop, the catalogue, the projector.
