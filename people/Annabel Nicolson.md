# Annabel Nicolson

(born 1946) British expanded cinema artist and feminist filmmaker; best known for *Reel Time* (1973), one of the most celebrated works in British avant-garde performance/film. Co-founder of [[Circles]] (1979), the UK's first feminist film distribution network; core member of the [[London Film-Makers' Co-operative (LFMC)]].

## Domains
Expanded cinema performance; structural/materialist film; feminist experimental practice; artist-run distribution; film as material process.

## Background
Studied Hornsey College of Art 1964–65; Edinburgh College of Art (Drawing and Painting) 1965–69; St Martins School of Art post-graduate film-making 1970–71. From 1969–70 ran the gallery at the New Arts Lab, London. Cinema programmer at LFMC 1974, 1976–77, and 1992/93 — bridging early and later periods. Co-founder of [[Circles]] – Women's Film in Distribution, 1979 (with Lis Rhodes, Jo Davis, Felicity Sparrow). Editorial contributor to *Musics* magazine 1976–79; co-editor/publisher of *Readings* magazine 1977. John Brinkley Fellowship, Norwich School of Art 1980–81.

Part-time and visiting lecturer at: Cheltenham, Chelsea, Falmouth, Ravensbourne, Glasgow, Wolverhampton, St Martins.

Works in collections of: Belgian National Film Archive; British Film Institute; Canterbury University; Women Artists' Slide Library. Artists' book *Escaping Notice* held by V&A.

## Key Works
- **Reel Time** (1973) — legendary expanded cinema performance. A domestic sewing machine passes through a loop of 16mm film while simultaneously projecting it. The needle holes appear on-screen as light-events; the material trace of production (the sewing machine's path through the film) becomes the image. A perfect synthesis of structural-materialist principle and feminist attention to domestic labour. Canonical work of British expanded cinema.
- Various performance/film works shown at LFMC, Acme Gallery London, Stedelijk Amsterdam, Museum of Modern Art Vienna.

## Quote
"It is what happens to things when they are not being looked at that puzzles me."

## Key Communities
- [[London Film-Makers' Co-operative (LFMC)]] — cinema programmer, core member
- [[Circles]] — co-founder (1979); the feminist film distribution network
- [[Cinenova]] — successor to Circles; Nicolson's distributed films now in Cinenova catalogue

## Key Relationships
- [[Lis Rhodes]] — co-founded Circles together; both LFMC cinema programmers
- "Women and the Formal Film" statement co-signatories: Felicity Sparrow, Jane Clarke, Jeanette Iljon, Lis Rhodes, Mary Pat Leece, Pat Murphy, Susan Stein

## Why Interesting
Nicolson is the perfect synthesis of formal rigour and feminist politics within the LFMC. *Reel Time* is a structural-materialist work — it exposes the material of film — but it does so through the domestic labour instrument of the sewing machine, turning structural film's austerity inside out. She is also a crucial figure in the institutional history of feminist distribution: without her and Rhodes, Circles doesn't happen, and without Circles, a major strand of women's experimental film would have had no UK distribution in the 1980s.
