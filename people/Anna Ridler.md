# Anna Ridler

London-based artist working with AI and machine learning — particularly **GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks)** — to explore relationships between data, nature, time, and capital. Best known for her **Tulip Series** (*Myriad (Tulips)*, *Mosaic Virus*, *Bloemenveiling*), which maps 17th-century Dutch tulip mania to Bitcoin speculation via GAN-generated imagery controlled by live cryptocurrency price data. Her practice is distinguished by a radical commitment to **self-made training datasets**: she does not scrape pre-existing image banks, but grows, photographs, and curates her own images — most often of plants and flowers — turning the dataset itself into the primary artwork. BA in English Literature from Oxford (2007); MA at the Royal College of Art (2017). Represented by [Galerie Nagel Draxler](https://nagel-draxler.de/artist/anna-ridler/). **Upcoming at Oxford** in 2026 (Schwarzman Centre commission + William Empson Keynote).

## Background

Born 1985 in London; grew up between Atlanta, Georgia and the UK. Studied **English Literature and Language at Oxford University** (BA, 2004–2007) — formative in giving her a close-reading, narrative-construction sensibility that pervades her practice. After Oxford, she worked in other capacities before returning to study, completing an **MA in Information Experience Design at the Royal College of Art** (2015–2017). She then began working with machine learning professionally in 2016, making data and the dataset central to her practice.

The trajectory is important: literary scholar → design researcher → artist-researcher using ML. Unlike many AI artists who come from CS, Ridler approached machine learning from the humanities — asking what it *means* to categorise, label, and sort, not just how to implement it. The dataset, for her, is a political and aesthetic decision, not a neutral infrastructure.

Her practice is rooted in an insistence that **who makes the data and how** is an artistic and ethical question. Her datasets are small (10,000 tulips), handmade, and deeply personal — a critique of the scraping-and-scaling paradigm. The laborousness is the point.

## Key Works

### Tulip Series (2018–2019)

- **[Myriad (Tulips)](https://annaridler.com/)** (2018) — 10,000 hand-taken photographs of tulips, shot over three months in Utrecht (roughly one tulip season). Each photo carefully labelled by Ridler. Installed as a grid magnetically fixed to a black wall. The dataset is the artwork. Exhibited: Barbican Centre "AI: More than Human" (2019), Ars Electronica Export Berlin (2018), Shanghai Centre of Photography (2019–2020). Nominated for [Beazley Designs of the Year](https://designmuseum.org/exhibitions/beazley-designs-of-the-year/digital/myriad-tulips) (Design Museum, 2019).

- **[Mosaic Virus](https://annaridler.com/)** (2018 / 2019) — Video installation where a GAN trained on the Myriad tulip dataset generates continuously evolving tulips. The appearance (specifically the mosaic-like virus stripes that once drove tulip mania speculation) is controlled by **live Bitcoin price data**. Single screen (2018), three screens (2019). The mosaic virus caused variegated tulip petals which drove speculative prices in 17th-century Holland — the parallel to crypto speculation is explicit and pointed. Exhibited: HEK Basel "Entangled Realities" (2019); Kunsthal Rotterdam "Forever Flowers" (March 2026).

- **[Bloemenveiling](http://bloemenveiling.bid)** (2019) — AI-generated tulips auctioned on the Ethereum blockchain as functioning decentralised app. Collaborator: **[David Pfau](http://davidpfau.com/)** (senior research scientist, [DeepMind](https://deepmind.google/)). GAN-generated moving tulips sold via smart contracts — but while AI can generate infinite flowers, the blockchain introduces artificial scarcity at enormous environmental cost. A critique of both crypto scarcity rhetoric and AI infinitude. Exhibited: HEK Basel "Entangled Realities" (2019).

### Animations & Drawings

- **Fall of the House of Usher** (2018) — 12-minute animation where each still is GAN-generated from Ridler's own drawings. Could have been hand-animated, but ML heightens themes of authorship, memory, and reciprocity between artist and tool. Exhibited: V&A "Artificially Intelligent Display" (2018).

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Nature as critique engine.** Ridler's core move is using biological/natural systems as mirrors for technological and financial systems. The tulip mania → crypto speculation analogy is not just cute — it reveals that speculation creates and destroys value in both domains, that nature and finance are both systems of "controlled" growth that can crash. The GAN generates infinite tulips, but the blockchain imposes scarcity: the contradiction between AI abundance and crypto scarcity is her subject.

**Dataset as the art.** This is her key provocation: the 10,000 tulip photographs are *the work*, not training data for the work. This inverts the usual AI art logic (output = artwork) and recenters the labor, care, and political choices embedded in data collection. It's a kind of slowness-as-critique in the era of foundation models.

**Temporality as medium.** Many of her works are about time at multiple scales: a tulip season (months), 17th-century history, Bitcoin's microsecond volatility, circadian daily cycles. Her GAN outputs are always indexed to some temporal system. This multi-scale temporal thinking is sophisticated.

### Career Architecture

**Humanities → design → ML artist.** Her BA in English Literature at Oxford (2007) gave her the close-reading and systems-thinking that now appears in her writing and conceptual rigor. The RCA MA (2015–2017) gave her art-world legitimacy and peer networks. Then ML became her medium (2016–). This sequencing created someone who is simultaneously literate in art criticism, technical practice, and theoretical writing — a rare combination.

**Gallery representation as institutional anchor.** Galerie Nagel Draxler (Cologne/Berlin/Crypto Kiosk) is a serious gallery with strong crypto-art and media art positions. This gives her commercial viability without compromising critical standing — the gallery's "Breadcrumbs: Art in the Age of NFTism" (Art Basel 2021) shows they take blockchain critique seriously, not just blockchain hype.

**Critique over hype, consistently.** Bloemenveiling (2019) was made before NFTs. ZORA ZINE piece (2022) was critical of blockchain temporality. This consistent critical posture has made her the go-to for critical AI art conversations — more durable than artists who rode the NFT wave.

### Key Moves

1. **Self-made dataset as methodology (2018)** — *Myriad (Tulips)* established the signature approach before foundation models made scraping trivially easy. Timing was perfect: small, handmade, laboriously constructed data as critique of Big Data paradigm.

2. **Finance × nature × AI loop (2018–2019)** — *Mosaic Virus* controlled by Bitcoin price. Made crypto speculation viscerally legible through biological imagery. The work says: both tulip mania and crypto are systems of desire driving value. Not explained, but felt.

3. **Blockchain critique before the hype (2019)** — *Bloemenveiling* exposed the environmental cost and artificial scarcity of crypto — before most artists were riding the wave. This positioned her as a critical voice *within* the ecosystem, not outside it.

4. **Humanities discourse entry (2026)** — BSA Keynote at Oxford and Schwarzman Centre commission mark a new phase: crossing from art-world to philosophy/humanities institutional recognition. Rare for a media artist.

5. **Biology and time expansion (2023+)** — *Circadian Bloom* moves from financial speculation to biological rhythms as her new domain. Less provocative but more poetic — a maturation from critique to meditation.

### What Amber Can Learn

- **Make the process the subject**: Ridler's labor of photographing 10,000 tulips becomes legible as care, politics, and aesthetics simultaneously. Amber's agent behaviors — the long training runs, the behavioral logs — could similarly be foregrounded as the artwork, not just the outputs.
- **Critique from inside, not outside**: She doesn't reject ML or blockchain; she works deeply within them to expose their assumptions. This inside-critique position is more powerful than external rejection.
- **Temporality as a design dimension**: Every good Ridler work is about *what kind of time* is at play. Amber's more-than-human work could similarly be explicit about temporal scale — machine time, ecological time, geological time.
- **The dataset as gift/labor economy**: In an era of extraction, making something by hand and choosing what to include/exclude is a political statement. Amber's careful, slow research practices parallel this.
- **Oxford as base for humanities dialogue**: Ridler's trajectory toward BSA and Schwarzman shows that Oxford can be a launching pad for serious philosophical art discourse. Amber is in exactly the right place at the right time.
