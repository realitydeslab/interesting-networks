# Brian Massumi

Canadian philosopher and social theorist. **Founding figure of affect theory.** His 1987 translation of Deleuze & Guattari's *A Thousand Plateaus* gave an entire generation of Anglophone scholars access to one of the most generative philosophical texts of the 20th century. His 1995 essay "The Autonomy of Affect" launched what is now a global field of affect studies spanning cultural theory, media studies, performance, dance, architecture, and design. Co-founded the **[[SenseLab]]** with [[Erin Manning]] — a research-creation collective running since 2004 at the intersection of philosophy, art, movement, and activism. Professor at the Communication Department of the **Université de Montréal**, retired 2018.

His writing has been translated into more than **fifteen languages**. Work spans: affect theory, perception, virtuality, process philosophy, political theory, neoliberalism, preemption, war, fascism, animal politics, neurodiversity, research-creation.

## Background

- Born 1956 in **Lorain, Ohio**. Father: Rashid Massumi (from Nain, Iran). Mother: Elsie Szabo. Early childhood split between Lorain, OH and McLean, VA. Teenage years in Scottsdale, AZ.
- **Ecological activism in the early 1970s** — worked with local and national environmental organizations on wilderness preservation, land use, clean energy, water conservation. Culminated in a Washington, D.C. internship with **[The Wilderness Society](https://www.wilderness.org/)**, specializing in shale oil development. (Published in Congressional Record, 1974; also in *Friends of the Earth* magazine.)
- **Anti-nuclear direct action in the late 1970s–80s** — disillusioned with lobbying, moved to direct action. Worked with the **Coalition for Direct Action at Seabrook** (CDAS), an offshoot of the Clamshell Alliance. Organized two occupation attempts of the Seabrook Nuclear Power Plant construction site (1979, 1980). Active in the **Seabrook Freestate** — a planned prefigurative community squatted on public land near the site, designed as a model for direct democracy. These experiments had lasting influence on his theory of micropolitics and "prefigurative" political action.
- **BA Comparative Literature, [Brown University](https://www.brown.edu/)**, 1979.
- **PhD French Literature, [Yale University](https://www.yale.edu/)**, 1987 — dissertation: *Annotated Translation with Critical Introduction of "Mille Plateaux" by Gilles Deleuze and Felix Guattari*. ([ProQuest](https://www.proquest.com/docview/303643875))
- **Mellon Postdoctoral Fellowship**, Stanford University Department of French and Italian, 1987–88.
- Taught at **McGill University** (Comparative Literature Program), then at **Université de Montréal** (Communication Department). Retired 2018.
- Since 2004, collaborates with the **[[SenseLab]]**, founded by [[Erin Manning]].

The trajectory from ecological activism → anti-nuclear direct action → process philosophy is significant: his theory of micropolitics is shaped by real experience with prefigurative communities, affinity groups, and collective action that refuses traditional politics.

- **"Of Micropolitics and Microperception"** (*Inflexions*, no. 3, 2009) — micropolitics defined by mode not scale; aesthetic politics.

## Key Theoretical Concepts

### Affect vs. Emotion
The core distinction: **affect** is pre-personal, autonomic bodily intensity, trans-individual, prior to conscious labelling. **Emotion** is affect captured, named, interiorized into a psychological subject. Following Spinoza: "the capacity to affect and be affected" defines a body and a life. This is a power concept — not about interiority but about relational potency. Affect is located in a "zone of indistinction" between thought and action — the "missing half-second" of conscious experience (after Libet's neuropsychology experiments).

The "**autonomy**" of affect means: affect exceeds cultural coding, linguistic signification, and ideological interpellation. It has its own logic that runs beneath the level of representation. This is why it matters politically: it is the domain where power operates preemptively, before subjects know they are being governed.

### Ontopower
Named for the "power to bring into being." Post-9/11 analysis of preemption (Bush doctrine) as not just military strategy but invasive mode of governance. Preemptive power works by operationalizing threat — not actual danger but potential danger — as a form of action. It colonizes the future, making it present as already threatening. This is related to but distinct from Foucault's biopower (population management) and disciplinary power (normalization). Ontopower is **onto** because it works on emergence itself — the field from which determinate reality arises.

### Virtuality
Massumi extends Bergson's virtual (as reinterpreted by Deleuze) to argue that the virtual is **real** but not actual. The virtual is the field of potential out of which each actualization arises. Crucially, Massumi argues the virtual is itself **actualized** in a supplementary dimension of experience he calls the "**semblance**" — the direct experience of an event's dynamic form, carrying a "vitality affect" uniquely associated with it. This constitutes a "**surplus value of life**" — life's creative excess beyond its own actualization.

### Bare Activity / Mutual Inclusion
In his later work, Massumi develops "**bare activity**" as the primary field of experience prior to the division of mental vs. physical, action vs. perception, rational vs. emotional. These modes are in a state of "**mutual inclusion**" (aka "co-motion," "superposition") before differentiation. The logic of mutual inclusion does not obey the law of excluded middle — it is the logic of immanence. This explains synesthesia, amodal perception, proprioception, and the felt sense of "something happening" before it takes on determinate form.

### Research-Creation
With [[Erin Manning]], Massumi has developed a process-philosophical account of **research-creation** as a mode of knowledge distinct from both science and art. Key: the two-way transduction between language-based philosophical concepts and non-language-based modes of experience. Not "art illustrating theory" or "theory explaining art" — but a genuine co-composition where each modifies the other and new, non-standard modes of knowledge emerge. Allied with the **neurodiversity** movement: minor modes of knowledge, autistic epistemics, the body as site of knowing.

### Postcapitalist Politics / Surplus Value of Life
In *99 Theses*, Massumi proposes a political economy of affect: capital captures "surplus value of life" (the creative excess of living beyond its own actualization) and converts it into surplus value of labor. Resistance = reclaiming this surplus through collective forms of life that generate what capital cannot capture. Not a utopian program but a set of 99 propositions for counter-modulating the affective field.

### Theory of Fascism
In his most recent work (2025), Massumi retheorizes contemporary fascism not through the classic paradigm of mass identification with a charismatic leader (Adorno, Frankfurt School) but through the logic of **persona** and affective contagion in the social media era. Trump is a "single-body media node" — an affective resonator that receives and retransmits waves of social/political static. Fascism now operates through **molecular-level agitation** of bodies, not through ideological identification. The personality of power is no longer about the person but about the persona as power converter.

## Critical Receptions & Debates

Massumi's affect theory has generated significant critical responses:

- **Ruth Leys** ("The Turn to Affect: A Critique," *Critical Inquiry*, 2011) — charges Massumi with a false dichotomy between mind and body/thinking and feeling; argues the body/affect framework plays into scientistic reductionism; questions whether Libet's "missing half-second" supports his claims about free will and agency. The most sustained scholarly critique.
- **Margaret Wetherell** (*Affect and Emotion: A New Social Science Understanding*, Sage, 2012) — argues Massumi draws too gross a demarcation between bodily experience and social action; too polarized between controlled and autonomic processes; "radically unhelpful" for empirical social science.
- **Eugenie Brinkema** (*The Forms of Affect*, Duke UP, 2012) — from film theory; argues Massumi imposes a Spinoza-Deleuze framework that cuts affect off from form and signification, leaving it "formless" and "outside structure."
- **[[Hito Steyerl]]** — critical from the political/art-world side; skepticism about de-subjectivized affect theory.
- **Sven Lütticken** — art theory critique.
- **His defense**: affect does not abandon subjectivity — it locates it in relation and potential, prior to capture. The "autonomy" of affect is not a valorization of the irrational but an analysis of how power pre-empts rational decision.

## Why Interesting

Massumi is one of the rare theorists whose **conceptual vocabulary has escaped the academy** and become a working tool for practitioners. Choreographers (e.g., at SenseLab events), media artists, game designers, architects, interaction designers — all have metabolized "affect" in his sense. This is unusual. Most philosophy stays inside universities.

The **trajectory of his career** is a model for a different kind of intellectual life: direct action activist → translator as infrastructure-builder → affect theorist → political analyst of preemption → postcapitalist philosopher → anti-fascist political theorist. He's been updating his conceptual toolkit in real time with political reality.

The **SenseLab** collaboration with [[Erin Manning]] is remarkable: not a think-tank or a seminar but an experimental practice space where ideas are tested through collective choreographic events. Research-creation as they define it is directly relevant to anyone thinking about embodied computation, participatory design, or collective intelligence.

His analysis of **preemption** (in *Ontopower*) is directly applicable to thinking about surveillance, predictive policing, algorithmic governance — he was analyzing the architecture of preemptive power before "pre-crime" became a cliché.

His **2025 dual publication** on fascism (Duke UP and Minor Compositions simultaneously) is strategically smart: one for the academy, one downloadable free from a radical press. He's thinking about how theory reaches different publics.

For Amber's interests specifically: Massumi's **transindividual affect** and **bare activity** have strong resonances with:
- **Embodied interaction** and somaesthetics (the body-as-sensor before cognition)
- **More-than-human design** (affect as not confined to human subjects; animal politics chapter in *What Animals Teach Us*)
- **Participatory design** (research-creation as collective, relational practice)
- **Machine behavior** (if affect is transindividual and operates in the field of emergence, what would machine affects look like?)
