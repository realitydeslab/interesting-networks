# Timnit Gebru

Eritrean-Ethiopian computer scientist, AI ethics researcher, and institution-builder. One of the most significant figures in critical AI globally. Best known for co-authoring [Gender Shades](http://proceedings.mlr.press/v81/buolamwini18a/buolamwini18a.pdf) with Joy Buolamwini (2018), the [Stochastic Parrots](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445922) paper that got her fired from Google (2021), and [Datasheets for Datasets](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3458723) — infrastructure work with 3,884+ citations. Founded [DAIR Institute](https://www.dair-institute.org/) in 2021 as an independent, community-rooted AI research organization after being ousted from Google. A symbol of resistance against corporate control of AI ethics research.

## Background

Born 1982/83 in Addis Ababa, Ethiopia. Father (electrical engineer, PhD) died when she was five. Raised by her mother, an economist. Both parents are from Eritrea. At age 15, during the Eritrean–Ethiopian War, she fled Ethiopia when family members were deported to Eritrea and conscripted. Briefly denied a US visa, she lived in Ireland before receiving political asylum in the USA. Settled in Somerville, Massachusetts for high school, where she immediately encountered racial discrimination — teachers refused to let her take AP courses despite being a high achiever.

A pivotal moment occurred after high school: she called police to report an assault on a Black woman friend, and the police arrested the friend instead. This became her entry point into thinking about systemic racism and algorithmic bias.

In 2001, entered Stanford University. Worked at Apple (2004–2013) as audio engineer after an internship — developed signal processing algorithms for the first iPad. Became more interested in computer vision during that time. Returned to Stanford in 2013 for her PhD in computer vision under [Fei-Fei Li](https://profiles.stanford.edu/fei-fei-li). PhD thesis: [Visual Computational Sociology: Computer Vision Methods and Challenges](https://searchworks.stanford.edu/view/12137902) (2017). Won the LDV Capital Vision Summit competition in 2017. During PhD, returned twice to Ethiopia to teach coding to high schoolers through [AddisCoder](http://www.addiscoder.com/).

**The Google Chapter (2018–2020):** Joined Google Brain in 2018, co-leading the Ethical AI Team with Margaret Mitchell. In 2020, co-authored the [Stochastic Parrots paper](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445922) warning about risks of large language models. Google demanded she either withdraw the paper or remove all Google employees as authors. She requested transparency about the review process; Google fired her immediately (December 2020), claiming she had resigned. ~2,700 Google employees and 4,300 academics/civil society members signed letters condemning the firing. Nine members of Congress wrote to Google for clarification. Margaret Mitchell was later fired after trying to gather evidence of Gebru's mistreatment. The incident became a landmark case in corporate AI ethics censorship.

In June 2021, announced fundraising to launch an independent research institute. [DAIR](https://www.dair-institute.org/) (Distributed AI Research Institute) launched December 2021.

## Key Works

### Foundational Papers
- **[Gender Shades: Intersectional Accuracy Disparities in Commercial Gender Classification](http://proceedings.mlr.press/v81/buolamwini18a/buolamwini18a.pdf)** (with Joy Buolamwini, FAccT 2018) — Showed Black women were 35% less likely to be correctly recognized than White men in commercial facial recognition systems. Landmark in AI bias research. Named [Project Website](http://gendershades.org/).
- **[Datasheets for Datasets](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3458723)** (with Morgenstern, Vecchione, Vaughan, Wallach, Daumé III, Crawford — CACM 2021) — 3,884+ citations. Proposed documentation standards for datasets analogous to product data sheets for electronics. Infrastructure work that shaped how the ML community thinks about data governance.
- **[On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots: Can Language Models Be Too Big? 🦜](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3442188.3445922)** (with Emily Bender, Angelina McMillan-Major, Margaret Mitchell — FAccT 2021) — 11,693+ citations. Examined environmental costs, financial costs, inscrutability, bias, and disinformation risks of very large LLMs. The paper Google tried to suppress. Among the most cited papers in AI ethics.
- **[Model Cards for Model Reporting](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.03993)** (with Margaret Mitchell et al. — FAT* 2019) — Documentation framework for ML models. Widely adopted.

### Landmark Early Research
- **[Using Deep Learning and Google Street View to Estimate the Demographic Makeup of Neighborhoods](http://www.pnas.org/content/114/50/13108)** (with Krause, Wang, Chen, Deng, Aiden, Li — PNAS 2017) — Showed voting patterns, income, race, and education can be inferred from street-level images of cars. Raised profound questions about societal inference from visual data.
- **[Fine-grained Recognition in the Wild: A Multi-Task Domain Adaptation Approach](papers/iccv.pdf)** (with Judy Hoffman, Fei-Fei Li — ICCV 2017)

- **[Statement from the listed authors of Stochastic Parrots on the "AI pause" letter](https://www.dair-institute.org/)** (with Bender, McMillan-Major, Mitchell — 2023) — Responded to the infamous "pause AI" open letter, noting it misused their work.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Documentation as intervention:** Gebru's most cited works (Datasheets for Datasets, Model Cards) aren't "findings papers" — they're **infrastructure papers** that install new practices into a field. The strategy: identify a gap in how a field thinks about accountability → propose a lightweight standard → demonstrate on cases → wait for adoption. This creates citation snowballs as everyone who adopts the standard must cite the original.

**Naming as power:** "Stochastic Parrots" is a two-word concept that perfectly captured what was wrong with LLMs before the ChatGPT era made it obvious. "TESCREAL bundle" is similar — it names and connects ideologies that were powerful precisely because they weren't seen as connected. Naming is a form of epistemological territory-claiming.

**Provocation-first framing:** The Stochastic Parrots paper didn't try to play nice — it provoked, named harms directly, and forced a response. This is a high-risk, high-reward strategy that works when the author's institutional position gives enough protection... until it doesn't (Google).

### Career Architecture

**Coalition papers as manifestos:** Gender Shades (2 authors), Datasheets (7 authors), Model Cards (9 authors), Stochastic Parrots (5 authors including external). Each paper is also a political coalition — it assembles people across institutions, making it harder to suppress and amplifying reach.

**Strategic independence:** After Google, Gebru chose to found DAIR rather than go to an elite university (she had the credentials and recognition to do so). This was a deliberate structural choice to avoid the institutional constraints that got her fired. DAIR's community-funded model is a bet that independence can be sustained at scale.

**Field declaration:** DAIR positions itself not just as a lab but as an alternative to how AI research institutions work. By writing blog posts like "Celebrating 4 Years," publishing in accessible formats, and doing policy advocacy alongside academic papers, Gebru is declaring a new kind of institution.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **2016: Founded Black in AI** — Counted the Black people at NeurIPS. Turned personal observation into collective action. Founded the community that would amplify every subsequent move. Timing: just before the AI ethics field crystallized.

2. **2018: Gender Shades at FAccT** — Chose FAccT (then FAT*) over a major ML venue. This was a signal of who she was building with. The paper was the founding myth of AI bias research. Joy Buolamwini brought the MIT Media Lab profile; Gebru brought the computer vision credibility.

3. **2019: Datasheets for Datasets** — Infrastructure move. Instead of one more bias paper, she proposed standards for the entire field. The 7-author team gave it political breadth. 3,884 citations show it worked.

4. **2020: Stochastic Parrots** — The highest-stakes paper: co-authored with only one external author (Bender), which gave it structural protection. When Google tried to suppress it, the paper's existence outside Google's servers meant it survived. The firing made the paper famous.

5. **2021: Founded DAIR** — Converting martyrdom into institution. Could have taken a prestigious university chair. Instead built infrastructure for the field she wanted to exist. DAIR's 4th anniversary (2025) means it survived the hard years.

6. **2024: TESCREAL paper** — Strategic pivot to ideology critique. Instead of documenting AI harms (reactive), she named the ideological ecosystem driving harmful AI (proactive). 258 citations in under 2 years shows the paper landed in an open field.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Infrastructure papers outperform findings papers in long-run impact.** Datasheets for Datasets (3,884 citations) outpaced most "results" papers from the same period. Amber could create documentation/governance frameworks for agent behavior — "Model Cards for Agents" or similar.

2. **Name the field before the field names itself.** Stochastic Parrots and TESCREAL both worked because they arrived before the mainstream had clear language. Amber's work on "machine death," "agent ethology," and "machine behavior" is in this position now — the naming can be the contribution.

3. **Build the coalition before you need it.** Black in AI created the network that amplified everything Gebru did afterward. When she was fired, thousands of people mobilized. Amber should be building his network now, not after something goes wrong.

4. **Choose venues as political statements.** Gebru chose FAccT over NeurIPS/ICML. This choice shaped who she became. Amber's choice of FAccT/CHI over AI-mainstream venues is similarly a political statement about what kind of researcher he is.

5. **Independence is the product.** DAIR's independence from both industry and traditional academia is not just a funding model — it's the research output itself. Amber's Reality Design Lab can make the same structural argument.
