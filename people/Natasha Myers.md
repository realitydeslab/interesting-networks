# Natasha Myers

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-02:** Stub created during Multispecies Design deep research.

Associate Professor of Anthropology at York University. Director of the [Plant Studies Collaboratory](http://plantstudies.wordpress.com) — a node gathering 25+ artists, activists, academics, and plant practitioners. She coined the term **"Planthroposcene"** (2016) — a proposed alternative to the Anthropocene that centers the intertwined histories of plants and people.

Education: BSc Biology, McGill; MA Environmental Studies, York; PhD in History, Anthropology, and Science, Technology & Society (HASTS), MIT.

## Domains
- Plant studies — plant sensing, communication, plant-human entanglements
- Multispecies anthropology and ethnography
- Science and technology studies
- Molecular biology/biosciences — her first book is an ethnography of protein crystallographers
- Ecological restoration and urban parks

## Key Works
- [*Rendering Life Molecular: Models, Modelers, and Excitable Matter*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/rendering-life-molecular) (Duke University Press, 2015) — ethnography of protein crystallographers. Won the 2016 Robert Merton Book Prize (ASA Science, Knowledge, and Technology Section).
- "Photosynthesis" (Cultural Anthropology, 2016) — coins "Planthroposcene"
- *Seeding Planthroposcenes* (ongoing project) — documenting affective ecologies in botanical gardens and ecological restoration sites
- [Becoming Sensor](http://becomingsensor.com/) — project on ecological attunement in urban parks
- "How to grow livable worlds: Ten not-so-easy steps" (2018) — manifesto for Planthroposcene
- "Conversations on Plant Sensing: Notes From the Field" (ResearchGate, 2024)

## Why Interesting
Myers coined "Planthroposcene" as an alternative epoch-label — a political move similar to Haraway's "Chthulucene." Her core contribution is taking plant sensing and communication seriously as a research object, bridging biology and anthropology. The Plant Studies Collaboratory model (gathering artists, scientists, activists, practitioners around one nonhuman) is a useful community-building template. Her training as a biologist (BSc McGill) before becoming an anthropologist gives her scientific credibility for making strong claims about plant agency.

Relevant to Amber through: the "becoming sensor" project (attunement as method), the plant-as-nonhuman-agent framing (extends to AI as nonhuman agent), and the Planthroposcene as a model for declaring alternative epochs/frameworks.
