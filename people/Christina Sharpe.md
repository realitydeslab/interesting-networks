# Christina Sharpe

Scholar, essayist, and cultural theorist. Canada Research Chair in Black Studies in the Humanities at York University, Toronto. Author of *Monstrous Intimacies: Making Post-Slavery Subjects* (2010), *In the Wake: On Blackness and Being* (2016), and *Ordinary Notes* (2023). Sharpe is arguably the most influential living scholar in Black Studies — her concept of "wake work" and "the weather" (anti-Blackness as totalizing climate) have restructured how an entire generation thinks about Black life, death, and aesthetics. *In the Wake* has been called one of the most important books published in the 21st century. Member of the **[[Practicing Refusal Collective]]**, an international Black feminist forum co-founded by Saidiya Hartman and Tina Campt.

## Background
- Grew up in Pennsylvania. BA from University of Pennsylvania in English and Africana Studies. PhD from Cornell University in English Language and Literature.
- Taught at **Hobart and William Smith College**, then for nearly **20 years at Tufts University** — eventually Director of the American Studies Program, teaching Black Feminist Theories, Queer Diasporas, Race and the Senses.
- Since 2018: **York University, Toronto** — **Canada Research Chair in Black Studies in the Humanities**. The Canada Research Chair is a prestigious national designation (federally funded, 5-7 year terms); in Sharpe's case it marks Black Studies as a field deserving of national infrastructure at one of Canada's leading research universities.
- *In the Wake* (2016) became a cultural event, traveling into prisons, community organizing groups, high school debate, artist studios. It was selected by New York Times critic Parul Sehgal as her most important discovery that year. Harper's Bazaar ran a feature in 2023 titled "Everything Comes Back to Christina Sharpe's *In the Wake*." New York Magazine noted how many contemporary artists cite it as transformative.
- Known on Twitter as **@hysterical_blkns** — "hysterical Blackness," a concept she describes as refusing the terms of diminished subjecthood, performing excess and joy.

## Domains
- **Black Studies / African Diaspora Studies** — the field's philosophical and theoretical underpinning
- **Black feminist theory** — especially the intersection of violence, care, and survival
- **Post-slavery studies** — the afterlives of slavery in contemporary life
- **Visual culture** — essays on Black visual art (Kara Walker, Simone Leigh, Jennifer Packer, Leon Golub, Jacob Lawrence, Isaac Julien)
- **Literary criticism** — Toni Morrison, Dionne Brand, Gayl Jones, Bessie Head, Adrienne Kennedy, Kazuo Ishiguro
- **Memorial and museum studies** — critique of how trauma gets institutionalized
- **Genre-bending scholarship** — refusing disciplinary containers, making form carry meaning

## Key Works

### Books
- [*Monstrous Intimacies: Making Post-Slavery Subjects*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/monstrous-intimacies) (2010, Duke University Press) — Argues that the fundamental sexual violence of slavery continues to shape Black and white subjectivities into the present. Chapters on Gayl Jones's *Corregidora*, Bessie Head's *Maru* and the history of Saartje Baartman (the "Hottentot Venus"), Isaac Julien's film *The Attendant*, and Kara Walker's black silhouettes. Coined the phrase "sadomasochism of everyday Black life." 272 pages.

- [*In the Wake: On Blackness and Being*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/in-the-wake) (2016, Duke University Press) — The book that made Sharpe's work unavoidable. Uses the multiple meanings of "wake" as its organizing structure: the wake behind a ship (history's trace), wakefulness (hypervigilance as survival), and the wake (funeral rites). Introduces **wake work** — the practice of attending to Black death while insisting on Black life. Introduces **the weather** — anti-Blackness not as a series of events but as the totalizing climate in which Black life unfolds. Genre-bending: poetry, archival images, memoir, theory. Translated into Portuguese. Travel: prisons, organizing groups, artists, high-school debaters.

- [*Ordinary Notes*](https://us.macmillan.com/books/9780374228125/ordinarynotes) (2023, FSG/US; Knopf/Canada; Daunt Books/UK) — 248 notes (some a sentence, some pages) on Black life, memory, art, theory, family. Contains "Preliminary Entries Toward a Dictionary of Untranslatable Blackness" — a lexicon co-written with artists and scholars including Phoebe Boswell, Belinda Kazeem-Kamiński, and Alexandra Bell. The book's organizing engine: "what would a *Camera Lucida* of the Black maternal look like?" Written partly as a response to Barthes, partly as a love letter to her mother. The final note reads simply: "This is a love letter to my mother." Starred reviews from Publishers Weekly and Kirkus.

- ***Black. Still. Life.*** (forthcoming, Duke University Press) — Her current book project, under contract at time of *Ordinary Notes* publication. On Black still life as a form — stasis, death, and the refusal of both.

### Key Essays
- **"Beauty as a Method"** — originally written for [Simone Leigh's Hugo Boss Prize Exhibition](https://www.guggenheim.org/press-release/hugo-boss-prize-exhibition-featuring-new-works-by-simone-leigh-opens-at-guggenheim-museum-on-april-19) at the Guggenheim Museum (2019). Argues that Sharpe's mother's practice of beauty — in the face of poverty and anti-Blackness — was not decoration but survival praxis. Central text of *Ordinary Notes*.

- **"Lose Your Kin"** — circulates as a standalone essay, referenced by scholars and poets. On kinship, loss, and the conditions under which Black mourning is permitted or foreclosed.

- **"What Could a Vessel Be?"** — essay in relation to Ursula Le Guin's "The Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction." Read on the *Between the Covers* podcast during a Lidia Yuknavitch / Ursula Le Guin episode.

- Essay in **[*Reconstructions: Architecture and Blackness in America*](https://www.moma.org/calendar/exhibitions/5086)** — MoMA exhibition catalogue (2021)

- Essay in **[*Grief and Grievance: Art and Mourning in America*](https://www.newmuseum.org/exhibitions/view/grief-and-grievance-art-and-mourning-in-america)** — New Museum exhibition catalogue (2021), co-organized posthumously by Okwui Enwezor

- Essay in **[*Jennifer Packer: The Eye Is Not Satisfied With Seeing*](https://www.sternerscheinberg.com/)** — exhibition catalogue for artist Jennifer Packer

- Essay in **[*Perceptual Drift: Black Art and an Ethics of Looking*](https://www.dartmouth.edu/)** — exhibition catalogue on Black art and looking

- **Introduction to *Nomenclature: New and Collected Poems* by Dionne Brand** (2022, Knopf Canada / Duke University Press US) — Sharpe describes writing this as a major intellectual event; she teaches Brand's work extensively and has a deep collaborative relationship with Brand as a fellow member of the Practicing Refusal Collective.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Sharpe operates across **three distinct circuits**, each reinforcing the others:

**1. Black Studies / Academic Circuit**
[Duke University Press](https://www.dukeupress.edu/) → [Small Axe journal](https://read.dukeupress.edu/small-axe) → *Journal of Visual Culture* → *Women & Performance* → [York University](https://profiles.laps.yorku.ca/profiles/cesharpe/) → Tufts (former) → Cornell (graduate training) → Columbia/Barnard (via PR Collective) → Yale (Windham-Campbell)
This is the core: she publishes with the major Black studies press, is institutionally anchored in a Canada Research Chair, and has deep ties to Barnard/Columbia (epicenter of the PR Collective).

**2. Art World / Museum Circuit**
[Guggenheim Museum](https://www.guggenheim.org/) (Simone Leigh's Hugo Boss) → [MoMA](https://www.moma.org/) (*Reconstructions*) → [New Museum](https://www.newmuseum.org/) (*Grief and Grievance*, Okwui Enwezor's posthumous show) → [Jennifer Packer exhibitions](https://www.sternerscheinberg.com/) → *Perceptual Drift* catalogue
She writes catalogue essays for major Black artists' solo and group shows. This is unusual for a literary theorist — she's embedded in contemporary visual art practice. Simone Leigh specifically invites her to contribute public talks alongside artworks, suggesting deep mutual respect and intellectual alignment.

**3. Literary-Cultural Circuit**
[FSG](https://us.macmillan.com/) (US) → [Knopf Canada](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.ca/) → [Daunt Books](https://www.dauntbooks.co.uk/) (UK) → [The Nation](https://www.thenation.com/) → Harper's Bazaar → New York Times Magazine → *Between the Covers* podcast → *Literary Hub*
Her *Ordinary Notes* launched simultaneously in three countries with three major literary presses — a mark of her crossover from academic to literary celebrity. The Harper's Bazaar feature, the NYT Magazine profile (by Jenna Wortham), and the *Between the Covers* appearance confirm she's operating in the literary-cultural mainstream, not only in Black studies or the academy.

**The Signal: Gallery TPW, Toronto**
She gave the foundational talk for *Ordinary Notes* at [Gallery TPW](https://gallerytpw.ca/) — a small, artist-run photographer's gallery in Toronto, not a major museum or university. This is the signature move of a scholar who stays rooted in the independent art scene even as she collects major prizes.

## Why Interesting

**The key methodological move: making form carry weight.**
Sharpe refuses the container of "academic writing." *In the Wake* uses ship diagrams, photographs, lyric passages, and theory in the same breath. *Ordinary Notes* is 248 numbered fragments — some one sentence, some several pages — that work through accumulation and juxtaposition, not argument. She calls this "white space doing work": the silence between notes holds meaning. This is not just an aesthetic choice; it's an epistemological claim: some things about Black life cannot be said through the apparatus of academic argumentation.

**The concept of "the weather" is the most portable idea:**
Anti-Blackness is not a series of incidents but a climate. This reframe has been adopted across art, activism, policy discussion, and philosophy. It explains why individual "fixes" (diversity programs, bias training) fail: you cannot solve weather through individual behavior change. The frame travels because it reorients analysis from event to structure.

**Her relationship with artists:**
Sharpe is one of the few humanist scholars who is genuinely *embedded* in visual art practice — not writing about it from the outside. Artists invite her to write catalogue essays, to give exhibition talks, to contribute to their public programming. Simone Leigh commissioned "Beauty as a Method" directly. This is unusual: most scholars who write about art do so with the art world at arm's length. Sharpe's position suggests she is as much an intellectual collaborator with artists as a scholar.

**Canada is a feature, not a bug:**
Moving to York University in 2018 was significant. Canada has a specific relationship to Blackness — the underground railroad mythology, the Black Loyalist communities in Nova Scotia, the Toronto Black intellectual scene (Dionne Brand, Rinaldo Walcott, M. NourbeSe Philip). Her Canada Research Chair institutionalizes Black Studies at the federal level. This is its own kind of politics.

## For Amber

1. **🔧 Professional inspiration — genre refusal as method**: Sharpe's formal choices are directly relevant to any researcher working at disciplinary borders. Her argument: form is not decoration but cognition. The white space in *Ordinary Notes*, the fragments, the juxtaposition — these are not stylistic preferences but claims about what can and can't be said. For Amber's work at the intersection of design, computing, and critical theory, this is a model: how to write scholarship that doesn't apologize for mixing forms.

2. **💡 Career inspiration — the artist-scholar position**: Sharpe has constructed a position that is neither purely academic nor purely art-world, and both scenes treat her as central. This took deliberate choice: writing catalogue essays, attending openings, forging relationships with working artists. The payoff is that her ideas travel into art practice (dozens of artists cite *In the Wake*) while remaining rigorous scholarship. Amber's work across design and computing and critical theory is a similar project — and Sharpe's path shows how to make the positioning work institutionally.

3. **🌍 Areas to know — the Practicing Refusal Collective as organizational model**: The PR Collective (16 people, multiple continents, deliberately outside normal academic conference structures, producing transnational gatherings through the Sojourner Project) is an interesting model for how to build an intellectual community that exceeds the university. Worth studying as organizational form.

4. **🌍 Areas to know — "the weather" as analytical frame**: The idea of "the weather" (a structural condition that individuals move within, not a series of events to be individually remedied) has applications far beyond Black studies. For anyone studying how systems produce conditions rather than events — including AI systems that produce environmental affordances — this is a useful frame.

5. **🌍 Areas to know — Black Canadian intellectual scene**: Dionne Brand, Rinaldo Walcott, M. NourbeSe Philip, Christina Sharpe — there is a Toronto-centered Black intellectual tradition that intersects poetics, theory, and activism in unusual ways. Brand in particular (poet, novelist, theorist, documentary filmmaker) might be interesting to Amber for her own work in combining forms.

6. **🎓 Fellowship** — The **Windham-Campbell Prize** ($165,000, Yale, no application) is a prize to be aware of — it operates by anonymous nomination. The **Guggenheim Fellowship** is similarly nomination-only for scholars. Both reward established careers. Worth knowing the pathway and who nominates.
