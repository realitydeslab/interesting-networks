# Jerome Lettvin (1920–2011)

American cognitive scientist, neuroscientist, and professor of electrical engineering and communications physiology at MIT. Lead author of **"What the Frog's Eye Tells the Frog's Brain"** (1959, with Maturana, McCulloch, Pitts) — one of the most cited papers in the Science Citation Index. Discovered feature detectors in the retina; established that the eye interprets before transmitting. Known for radical experimental approaches and fierce intellectual independence. Friend and protector of Walter Pitts; connector of the cybernetics group.

## Key Works
- **["What the Frog's Eye Tells the Frog's Brain"](https://doi.org/10.1109/JRPROC.1959.287207)** (1959, *Proceedings of the IRE*, with Maturana, McCulloch, Pitts) — discovered feature detectors in the retina; "bug detectors"; demolished passive image transmission assumption. DOI: [10.1109/JRPROC.1959.287207](https://doi.org/10.1109/JRPROC.1959.287207)
- First demonstration of "grandmother cell" concept (coined ~1969) — to illustrate the absurdity of single-neuron coding
- Work on spinal cord, unmyelinated axons, single-axon recordings

## Key Relationships
- **[[Warren McCulloch]]** — lifelong colleague; worked in same RLE group at MIT
- **[[Walter Pitts]]** — met 1938 at University of Chicago; close friends for life; Lettvin introduced Pitts to Wiener
- **[Humberto Maturana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humberto_Maturana)** — frog paper co-author; Maturana's autopoiesis theory grew from that paper

## Why Interesting
The frog paper is a prototype machine behavior study — observational, behavioral, asking what does this system DO in response to stimuli. Lettvin's methodology (find the smallest observation that contradicts a key assumption) is a useful research philosophy. His skepticism of AI and neural coding oversimplifications is historically prescient.
