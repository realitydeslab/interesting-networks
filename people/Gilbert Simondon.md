# Gilbert Simondon

French philosopher (1924–1989). The foundational figure for the entire philosophy-of-technics tradition that runs from Deleuze through Stiegler to Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics. Best known for two theses defended in 1958: *On the Mode of Existence of Technical Objects* (arguing that technical objects have their own ontological status and developmental logic) and *Individuation in Light of Notions of Form and Information* (a comprehensive philosophy of individuation across physical, biological, psychic, and collective levels). Largely ignored during his lifetime; massively rehabilitated from the 1990s onward as the continental philosophy of technology tradition caught up to ideas he'd articulated three decades earlier.

## Background

Born 2 October 1924 in **[Saint-Étienne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint-%C3%89tienne)**, a mining and manufacturing city in central France. His father, Hyppolite Simondon, was a postal worker who had been severely wounded at Verdun; his mother Nathalie Giraud came from a farming family in Puy-de-Dôme. The industrial and agricultural milieu of his childhood — he grew up among miners, machinists, and farmers — would inform his entire philosophy's groundedness in concrete technical practice.

Prepared for the ENS entrance exam at the **Lycée du Parc de Lyon** under Jean Lacroix. Admitted to the **[École Normale Supérieure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/École_normale_supérieure)** (Rue d'Ulm, the most prestigious) in 1944 (school year beginning February 1945). At the ENS, studied with **Martial Guéroult**, **Maurice Merleau-Ponty**, **Jean Hyppolite** (later his doctoral advisor), Jean-Toussaint Desanti, Georges Gusdorf, and Jean Wahl. Attended Jean Wahl's course on Heidegger alongside Lacan. Completed his diplôme d'études supérieures in philosophy on the unity of time in the Presocratics under Guéroult. Received his **agrégation** (the competitive qualification for French philosophy teaching) in 1948.

Deliberately expanded beyond philosophy into the sciences: **Licence de Psychologie** (1950), psychophysiology certificate under Alfred Fessard (Collège de France), one year of PCB (first year of medical school), three months studying social psychology at the **[University of Minnesota](https://twin-cities.umn.edu/)** (1952) — this inter-disciplinary formation was intentional and unusual for a philosophy student.

**Career trajectory:**
1. **1948–1955** — Philosophy professor at **Lycée Descartes de Tours**. Built a technology workshop in the school's basement. Simultaneously taught psychology at the Institut de Touraine (later Collège littéraire de Tours/Poitiers faculty). Gave lectures at Stanford's Tours branch. Began researching individuation (from 1952 onward).
2. **1955–1963** — Assistant then full professor, **Faculty of Letters and Human Sciences, University of Poitiers**. Established the **Laboratory of Experimental Psychology** at Poitiers (1955). Simultaneously taught at University of Lyon (1961–1963). Also taught at University of Saint-Étienne (1961–1962) and the Paris Faculty of Sciences.
3. **1958** — Defended both doctoral theses at the **University of Paris** (Sorbonne). Jury for the main thesis: Jean Hyppolite (director), Raymond Aron, Georges Canguilhem, Paul Ricoeur, Paul Fraisse. Attended by Merleau-Ponty, Jean Wahl, Mikel Dufrenne. **CNRS Bronze Medal** awarded September 1958 for *Du mode d'existence des objets techniques*. Complementary thesis published by Aubier-Montaigne immediately.
4. **1963** — Named **Associate Professor at the Sorbonne**. Founded the **Laboratory of General Psychology and Technology** (Sorbonne/Paris V, Rue Serpente, 1963–1983).
5. **1964–1970** — Participated in Georges Canguilhem's seminar on history of science and technology (Rue du Four). Taught agrégation seminars at ENS (1968).
6. **1965** — Named **full Professor, Chair B of Psychology**, later at **Paris University V (Paris Descartes)**. Colleague of Juliette Favez-Boutonnier.
7. **1970** — Added an Ethology Laboratory at Palaiseau.
8. **Mid-1970s onward** — Progressive health decline (physical, then psychological). Last completed course was on perception (1976–77). Professional life effectively ended by 1983, though he worked until early 1989 to complete publication of *L'Individuation psychique et collective*.

Married **Michelle Berger**, a Hellenist; seven children. Died 7 February 1989 at Palaiseau, age 64.

## Key Concepts

Simondon's philosophy is built around a set of interlocked concepts with no good equivalents in the existing philosophical tradition:

- **Individuation** — the ongoing process by which beings come into existence. The *individual* is an effect of individuation, not its cause or ground. This inverts the standard philosophical approach (which starts from already-formed individuals and asks about their properties). Everything is always in the midst of individuating.
- **Transduction** — the operation by which individuation propagates; a phase-shift that spreads through a metastable system (cf. how crystallization propagates through a supersaturated solution). Deleuze adopts this as a key concept.
- **Metastability** — the condition prior to individuation: rich in potential energy, on the verge of transformation. Not stable (which is poor in potential) nor unstable (which immediately collapses), but *metastable* — hovering at the edge.
- **Pre-individual** — the field from which individuations emerge. Every individuation leaves a residue of pre-individual potential, enabling further future individuations. This keeps the system open.
- **Transindividual** — the collective dimension that co-individuates with psychic individuation; neither purely individual nor purely collective, but the space between and through which both emerge.
- **Concretization** — the developmental process of technical objects: from "abstract" (where each part serves only one function) toward "concrete" (where parts serve multiple functions and integrate synergetically with the environment). The canonical example: the evolution of the vacuum tube from 1924 to 1952.
- **Technical mentality** — a mode of understanding proper to technical objects, neither purely aesthetic nor purely instrumental; requires perceiving technical objects as quasi-individuals with their own mode of existence.
- **Allagmatics** — Simondon's proposed general theory of operations and transformations (included in the 2005 complete edition of his main thesis).

## For Amber

1. **🌍 Areas to know** — Simondon is the root of the entire philosophy-of-technics tree that Amber moves in (Stiegler → Hui → cosmotechnics). Understanding his original ideas is not optional for serious engagement with [[Yuk Hui]]'s cosmotechnics or [[Bernard Stiegler]]'s pharmacology. Specifically: his critique of *hylomorphism* (the idea that form is imposed on passive matter by a maker) is the same move that posthumanism makes against humanism.

2. **📚 Learning from their path** — Simondon's strategy of grounding abstract philosophical claims in *technical concrete cases* (the vacuum tube, the electrical transformer, the steam engine governor) is precisely the methodology missing from much continental philosophy. He never loses the technical object. This is a template for how to do theory that has genuine technical content.

3. **💡 Career inspiration** — Simondon's career illustrates that profound work done outside the mainstream can eventually define the mainstream — but only if the ideas are genuinely original and there are champions. The lesson: depth and genuine novelty eventually win, even if slowly.

4. **🔧 Professional inspiration** — His approach to cybernetics: not just adopting Wiener's framework, but *critically engaging* with its limitations and proposing a more adequate alternative. This is the posture — take the technical field seriously, find where it's conceptually inadequate, propose a philosophical solution.

5. **🌍 Engagement paths** — Simondon studies is currently most active through:
   - [[Yuk Hui]]'s network at Erasmus/City University Hong Kong
   - [[Research Network for Philosophy and Technology]] conferences
   - [[Cahiers Simondon]] scholarly community (now mostly absorbed into journal literature)
   - Jean-Hugues Barthélémy's work (Paris)
   - Conferences on "philosophy of technology" and STS venues

6. **📚 Must read** — *On the Mode of Existence of Technical Objects* (MEOT) first. The *Individuation* text is longer and more abstract; read after MEOT. Deleuze's 1966 review is a perfect 6-page entry point. Yuk Hui's *On the Existence of Digital Objects* is the most direct application to contemporary media.

## Why Interesting

**Direct genealogical importance:** Every major figure in Amber's intellectual world who deals with philosophy of technology traces back to Simondon. [[Yuk Hui]]'s cosmotechnics is explicitly post-Simondonian. [[Bernard Stiegler]]'s pharmacology is built on Simondonian individuation. [[Gilles Deleuze]]'s ontology of difference borrows the key concepts. You cannot understand any of these without understanding Simondon.

**The core argument is still radical:** Technical objects have their own mode of existence — they are not slaves to human purposes but quasi-individuals that co-individuate with their users and environments. This is the philosophical basis for any serious posthumanism or more-than-human design. His claim that technophobia and technophilia are *equally mistaken* is a useful corrective.

**The concept of concretization** is one of the most useful ideas in the philosophy of technology. It explains why technologies develop the way they do (integration, environmental coupling) and why engineered artifacts that succeed look the way they do. It bridges philosophy and engineering without reducing one to the other.

**The individuation framework** offers a serious alternative to substance ontology. If you want to understand emergence, self-organization, morphogenesis, collective behavior, or AI systems that develop properties not specified by their designers — you want Simondon's process ontology. This connects to [[Active Inference]], [[Artificial Life]], and complexity science.

**The connection to Amber's cosmotechnics thread:** Simondon is the direct ancestor of [[Yuk Hui]]'s project. Reading Simondon shows you what Hui is reacting against (Western philosophy of technology as universal) and what he's building with (concretization, individuation, technical lineages).

## Research Taste Analysis

*(Historical figure — analyzing his intellectual strategies and meta-moves, not his current practice.)*

### Conceptual Strategy

**Ontological reframing as primary move:** Simondon's signature operation is to take a phenomenon that everyone thinks is understood and show that the *categories used to understand it are wrong*. Technical objects understood as instruments? Wrong — they have their own mode of existence. The individual understood as a starting point? Wrong — individuation is prior to the individual. Cybernetics as the adequate theory of machines? Wrong — it uses "established criteria and genera and species" rather than asking about machine genesis.

This move (identifying hidden presuppositions in existing frameworks) is the most powerful philosophical operation, and Simondon does it repeatedly.

**Process-before-product:** Simondon consistently privileges the *process* (individuation, concretization) over the *product* (individual, technical object). This is a methodological commitment that cuts across all his work. It makes him a process philosopher in the tradition of Whitehead and Bergson, but more technically rigorous than either.

**Two-domain synthesis:** Every major Simondon concept operates across domains simultaneously — individuation applies to crystals AND organisms AND psyches AND collectives; technical mentality relates to aesthetics AND science AND practical reason. He deliberately refuses to wall off domains. This is what makes his concepts so generative: they *propagate* (transduction again).

**Concrete case as conceptual anchor:** The vacuum tube. The brick. The steam engine governor. The electronic oscillator. Simondon never loses the technical object. His philosophy is grounded in actual engineering history. This is rare in continental philosophy and is what prevents his abstractions from floating free.

### Career Architecture

**Strategic outsider position:** Simondon was never part of the dominant intellectual movements of his era (existentialism, then structuralism, then post-structuralism). He was not a Sartrean, not a Lévi-Straussian, not a Derridean. This kept him off the main stage but also gave his work a kind of independence that allowed it to survive when those movements aged.

**Thesis as foundation:** Both his 1958 theses were so comprehensive that they functioned as *complete philosophical programs*, not just doctoral credentials. The MEOT covered a philosophy of technical evolution, culture, and aesthetics in one work. The individuation thesis covered physics, biology, psychology, and collective life. This totality meant that when thinkers later needed tools, Simondon already had them.

**Institutional patience:** He built laboratories, ran seminars, taught carefully. He didn't seek celebrity. The work was the work. This is an unusual posture in French philosophy, which tends toward public intellectual performance.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **1952–1958: Build the foundations while teaching high school.** Most remarkable: he wrote two comprehensive doctoral theses while teaching at a lycée. The discipline to do foundational conceptual work without institutional support or public attention is rare. He had no university research position; he was a secondary school teacher. Lesson: the institution is not the work.

2. **1958: Publish the MEOT immediately.** While the main thesis sat unpublished for years, the complementary thesis was published immediately as the first title in a major philosophical series (Analyse et Raisons, ed. Guéroult and Vuillemin). This was tactically correct: MEOT was more accessible and had direct cultural resonance (technology as social question); it built the audience that would eventually welcome the harder work.

3. **1964: Partial publication of main thesis + Deleuze's review.** Deleuze's 1966 review of *L'individu et sa genèse physico-biologique* was transformative — it positioned Simondon as the key thinker for the problem of difference that would dominate French philosophy through the 1970s. Simondon didn't engineer this, but his ideas were *already useful* for what Deleuze needed.

4. **Laboratory building (1955–1970).** While developing philosophy, Simondon was simultaneously building real research infrastructure: labs at Poitiers, then Paris V, then an ethology annex. This was not ceremonial — he ran actual experiments on perception, animal learning, and psychology. The philosophy and the science were genuinely co-constitutive.

5. **1989: Death + immediate posthumous rehabilitation.** The death coincided with the publication of the long-awaited *L'individuation psychique et collective*, and immediately a special issue of *Cahiers Philosophiques* and a conference were organized. The 1990s saw the "Simondon moment" — when Stiegler, Latour, and others were building frameworks that needed exactly what he'd provided.

6. **2005: Complete edition + Stiegler's championing.** The 2005 Jérôme Millon complete edition, combined with Stiegler's systematic use of Simondon in *Technics and Time* (1994ff), finally established Simondon as a canonical figure. Stiegler wrote the preface to the 2007 reprint of *L'individuation psychique et collective* — this was the canonization moment in France.

7. **2017–2020: Full English translation.** The Univocal *MEOT* (2017) and Minnesota Press *Individuation* (2020) finally made Simondon fully available in English. Expect continued growth in Anglophone scholarship.

### What Amber Can Learn

**1. Ground philosophy in technical cases.** Simondon never loses the vacuum tube. When theorizing about AI, more-than-human design, or computational creativity, Amber should follow this move: what is the *specific technical object* that the concept illuminates? What does it explain about how that thing actually works? This gives theory purchase.

**2. The process-before-product move.** When designing systems, the question "how does this individuate?" is more interesting than "what is this object?" This is design as individuation: helping systems find their form through interaction with their environment rather than imposing form from outside. This is a methodological innovation directly applicable to participatory and more-than-human design.

**3. Patience with foundations.** Simondon's most important work was done while he had no research position — just teaching, reading, thinking, doing experiments. The infrastructure follows if the ideas are genuine. Not a model to follow slavishly, but useful to know.

**4. The two-thesis strategy.** Simondon had one *accessible* work (MEOT — a concrete philosophical argument about a cultural problem everyone recognized) and one *deep* work (the individuation thesis — a complete philosophical architecture). Amber might consider maintaining both tracks: accessible interventions alongside deeper systematic work.

**5. Resist the dominant framework, use its tools.** Simondon wasn't anti-cybernetics; he used its concepts (information, signal, feedback) while showing their philosophical limitations. He wasn't anti-existentialist; he worked with phenomenological method while showing its presuppositions. This is more powerful than outright rejection — it steals the best tools and deploys them better.
