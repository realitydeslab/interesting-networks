# Adrienne Williams

Researcher, organizer, and former Amazon delivery driver who studies the exploited labor hidden inside AI systems — the "zombie trainers," the surveilled gig workers, and the data flowing from children, refugees, and prisoners into corporate models without consent or compensation. The lived experience of driving vans under algorithmic surveillance is not background: it is her method.

## Domains
- AI data labor exploitation (gig workers, refugees, children as involuntary AI trainers)
- "Zombie trainers" — the invisible, unconsenting workforce behind AI
- Amazon delivery driver surveillance (Netradyne cameras, Mentor app, wage theft)
- Race, algorithmic bias, and surveillance in Black communities
- Child data labor and ed-tech exploitation
- Transnational worker organizing as the solution to AI ethics

## Background

Williams is a former **Amazon delivery driver and labor organizer**. While driving routes she directly experienced the surveillance she now researches: Netradyne AI cameras monitoring her every move, the Mentor app assigning unpaid off-the-clock "homework" for alleged driving infractions, and algorithmic systems that set impossible quotas. She left one month before Netradyne cameras became mandatory in every Amazon-branded vehicle — a near miss that sharpened her analysis.

Before and alongside delivery driving, she worked as an **educator**, including at a school using the Chan-Zuckerberg Initiative (CZI) personalized learning platform. This school had a 99% graduation rate and a 38% literacy rate — a statistic she deploys precisely to expose the gap between ed-tech hype and learning outcomes.

As a **labor organizer**, she worked to shut down Amazon warehouses, protect pregnant workers' rights, and provide testimony to policymakers — holding Amazon accountable at the street level before doing so in academic venues.

This triple trajectory — driver, teacher, organizer — makes her one of the few critical AI researchers with genuine working-class credentials. She is not studying the exploitation from the outside; she survived it.

She holds fellowships at three institutions simultaneously:
- **Research Fellow & Employee, DAIR Institute** (Distributed AI Research Institute, founded by Timnit Gebru, 2021)
- **Just Tech Research Fellow, SSRC** (Social Science Research Council)
- **Public Voices Fellow on Technology in the Public Interest, The OpEd Project** — in partnership with The MacArthur Foundation (national fellowship to amplify underrepresented thinkers in public media)

## Key Concept: Zombie Trainers

Williams coined (or powerfully popularized) **"zombie trainers"** — people who work as data labelers, content moderators, or image data collectors *without their knowledge*. The term captures the new invisible labor force inside AI: refugees, children, prisoners, and low-wage workers who are conscripted into training AI systems without being informed, without consent, and without compensation.

The framing is deliberately provocative: these aren't just underpaid workers (bad enough), they are people who don't even know they're doing AI labor at all. Amazon delivery drivers who generate 500 million miles of 3D road-mapping data for autonomous vehicles aren't described as "AI trainers" in their job descriptions. Children using Google, Roblox, and educational apps supply behavioral data that trains the same systems that harm them. Prisoners' monitored phone calls feed Amazon's AI models.

This is **labor extraction via fraud** — creating invisible jobs that benefit employers financially while workers remain oblivious. Williams argues this falls under existing US Homeland Security definitions of forced labor.

## Key Works

### Papers & Articles
- **[The Exploited Labor Behind Artificial Intelligence](https://www.noemamag.com/the-exploited-labor-behind-artificial-intelligence/)** — *Noema Magazine*, October 2022. With [[Milagros Miceli]] and [[Timnit Gebru]]. The foundational statement: AI runs on armies of underpaid workers; supporting transnational worker organizing should be the heart of AI ethics. Adrienne wrote from direct experience as a former Amazon driver. Widely read; connects ImageNet to gig work to content moderation.

- **[Data Work and its Layers of (In)visibility](https://just-tech.ssrc.org/articles/data-work-and-its-layers-of-invisibility/)** — *Just Tech / SSRC*, 2023. With [[Milagros Miceli]]. Three-layer analysis of invisible AI labor: (1) unpaid user labor (ReCAPTCHA, platform feedback), (2) humans masquerading as AI systems (Madagascar workers behind "AI" cameras), (3) Global South exploitation. Argument: visibility alone is not enough — we need structural repair and worker organizing.

- **[Zombie Trainers and a New Era of Forced Labor](https://www.newsweek.com/zombie-trainers-new-era-forced-labor-opinion-1896624)** — *Newsweek*, May 2024. (Opinion) Full elaboration of the zombie trainer concept. Specifically analyzes Amazon drivers/Netradyne, children's ed-tech data, and prison phone call surveillance. Argues that child labor laws must be updated to cover digital data labor, and that zombie training is already illegal under forced labor statutes.

- **[What Senator Cory Booker gets wrong about Black people and AI](https://visiblemagazine.com/what-senator-cory-booker-gets-wrong-about-black-people-and-ai/)** — *Visible Magazine*, 2023. Critical intervention: Booker's call to "include Black people in AI" dangerously misunderstands that Black people are already central to AI — as the most surveilled, incarcerated, and miseducated. Simply adding Black faces to training data intensifies surveillance, not equity. Covers algorithmic bias in criminal sentencing, facial recognition in prisons, CZI ed-tech, and Florida sheriff's "future criminals" lists.

### Reports
- **[Driven Down: Worker Surveillance and Wage Theft](https://www.dair-institute.org/projects/driven-down/)** — DAIR Institute, 2024. ## Venue Choice Analysis

Williams publishes almost exclusively in **non-academic venues**: Noema Magazine, Newsweek, Visible Magazine, the SSRC Just Tech blog. This is deliberate. Her theory of change doesn't route through academic citation counts — it routes through policymakers, regulators, labor advocates, and the workers themselves. Her SSRC project description explicitly says she'll "disseminate research to policymakers, regulators, labor advocates, and organizers through public advocacy."

This is the OpEd Project model: high-profile public intellectual writing, not journal articles. Her most important work is her most accessible work. This marks her as a **public intellectual first, researcher second** — unusual in the DAIR/AI ethics space.

The absence of academic conference publications (FAccT, CHI, etc.) is notable and probably intentional. She doesn't need peer review for legitimacy; she has lived experience and access to mass media. This is a different theory of knowledge production.

## Why Interesting

Williams occupies a unique position in the AI ethics ecosystem: she's the person with **the receipts from inside**. While most AI labor scholars study precarious workers from the outside, Williams *was* the precarious worker. This gives her analysis a texture that external researchers simply cannot replicate.

Her key contribution to Amber's taste:

1. **The "zombie trainers" frame** — This is one of the sharpest conceptual moves in critical AI discourse. It transforms the question from "are workers being underpaid?" (which can be dismissed as a minimum wage argument) to "are workers even aware they're doing AI labor at all?" The framing makes visible an entirely new category of labor exploitation, implicating not just corporations but the entire architecture of surveillance capitalism.

2. **Lived experience as method** — Williams represents a strain of critical computing that treats embodied knowledge as primary. She didn't survey drivers; she *was* a driver. The Driven Down wage-theft calculator is built on interviews with people she knows from her organizing days. This is participatory research at its most authentic.

3. **Race-technology-labor nexus** — The Booker piece is particularly sharp: the call for Black "inclusion" in AI is actually a demand for more surveillance. This is the kind of structural critique (not ethics checklist) that Amber's research-taste.md signals as the good stuff.

4. **Connection to Amber's machine behavior thread** — Williams is essentially documenting machine behavior from the worker's perspective: what does algorithmic management *actually do* to people? The Netradyne / Mentor App analysis is an empirical study of how AI systems change human behavior under surveillance — which maps directly to Amber's interests.

5. **Policy pathway** — She is already giving testimony to policymakers. Unlike much academic AI ethics research, her work is explicitly designed to produce regulatory change. This is agenda-setting, not reporting.
