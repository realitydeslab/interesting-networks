# Shu Lea Cheang (鄭淑麗)

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Initial deep research. Full profile created from Wikipedia, personal site, Hyperallergic, LAS Art Foundation, transmediale, and Guggenheim sources.

Taiwanese-American artist and filmmaker. Pioneer of net.art, cyberpunk queer cinema, and bio-hacking art. Works across film, video, internet-based installation, networked performance, and bio art. Themes: racial politics, gender/sexual politics, surveillance, institutional power, viral futures, FLOSS culture, and post-internet ecology. One of the most important living artists at the intersection of technology, queerness, and radical politics.

## Background
- Born April 13, 1954 in Taiwan under martial law. Studied history at [National Taiwan University](https://www.ntu.edu.tw/) (BA, 1976), then moved to the US for [Cinema Studies at NYU](https://tisch.nyu.edu/cinema-studies) (MA, 1979).
- In 1980s New York, joined [Paper Tiger Television](https://papertiger.org/), a collective producing live weekly programmes on public-access cable — formative experience in alternative media and activist broadcasting.
- Spent 20 years in NYC (1980s–2000), where her career seeded and bloomed. Early work addressed racial stereotyping as an Asian woman in America.
- After NYC, spent a decade as a self-imposed **digital nomad** (Japan, Netherlands, UK), before settling in **Paris in 2007**, where she currently lives and works.
- Her archive is housed at [Fales Library and Special Collections, NYU](http://dlib.nyu.edu/findingaids/html/fales/mss_381/).

## Domains
- **Net.art / internet-based art** — pioneer of web-based artwork from the 1990s
- **Cyberpunk/queer science fiction cinema** — feature films creating new genre of queer sci-fi
- **Bio art / bio-hacking** — post-internet turn toward biological networks, viral love, mycelium
- **Networked installation and performance** — interactive, participatory, multi-site works
- **Surveillance and institutional critique** — panopticons, prisons, gender/sexual policing
- **FLOSS+Art** — free/libre/open source software culture as artistic practice
- **Food politics and ecology** — seed exchange, composting, garlic as currency

## Key Works

### Net Art & Digital (1990s–2000s)
- [*Color Schemes*](https://whitney.org/) (1990) — Interactive three-channel video installation at the [Whitney Museum](https://whitney.org/). People of different ethnicities reveal complex attitudes around ethnic stereotyping in American culture. Solo show at Whitney — career turning point.
- [*Bowling Alley*](https://walkerart.org/) (1995) — Commissioned by [Walker Art Center](https://walkerart.org/), funded by AT&T New Art/New Visions. Linked gallery, community bowling alley, and the World Wide Web. Her first cybernetic installation.
- [*Buy One Get One*](https://www.ntticc.or.jp/en/archive/works/buy-one-get-one/) (1997) — NTT [ICC] Biennale exhibition and collection, Tokyo.
- [**Brandon**](http://brandon.guggenheim.org/) (1998–1999) — **The first web-based artwork commissioned and collected by the [Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum](https://www.guggenheim.org/artwork/15337).** A one-year narrative project exploring gender fusion and techno-body through the story of [Brandon Teena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brandon_Teena), a trans man raped and murdered in 1993. Included live events at the Guggenheim, [De Waag Society](https://waag.org/) in Amsterdam, and Harvard. [Restored in 2017](https://www.guggenheim.org/press-release/guggenheim-restores-first-internet-artwork-acquisition-through-conserving-computer-based-art-initiative) by Guggenheim + NYU's Conserving Computer-Based Art initiative. Featured in [Rhizome's Net Art Anthology](https://anthology.rhizome.org/brandon). **Landmark work in net.art history.**
- [*Garlic=Rich Air*](https://anthology.rhizome.org/garlic-rich-air) (2002) — Challenge to the Fields, [Creative Time](https://creativetime.org/), NYC. Made garlic a commodity exchangeable for public wifi, advocating for free 802.11 wireless access.
- [*Kingdom of Piracy*](http://www.mauvaiscontact.info/kop) (2002–2006) — Curated netspace project exploring piracy as cultural practice.

### Feature Films
- [*Fresh Kill*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fresh_Kill) (1994) — Directed. Post-apocalyptic landscape of electronic detritus and toxic repercussions of mass marketing. Named after the Staten Island garbage dump.
- [*I.K.U.*](http://www.i-k-u.com) (2000) — Directed and wrote. Cyberpunk porn film inspired by *Blade Runner*. Screened at [Sundance Film Festival](https://www.sundance.org/). Nominated for International Fantasy Film Award. Landmark in queer sci-fi cinema.
- [*Fluidø*](http://www.fluidthemovie.com) (2017) — Directed, produced, wrote. Cypherpunk sci-fi. Set in post-AIDS future of 2060 where mutated AIDS viruses give birth to new organisms. Premiered at [Berlinale](https://www.berlinale.de/).
- [*UKI*](http://www.u-k-i.co) (2009–2023) — Directed. Sequel to *I.K.U.*, 14 years in the making. Digital collage of game-software animation, live action, and VFX. Premiered at [MoMA](https://www.moma.org/) New York (2023). Solo exhibition at [LAS Art Foundation](https://www.las-art.foundation/programme/uki), Berlin (July 2023). Sound design and voice by [[Sky Deep]]. Story of decommissioned replicant Reiko in toxic E-Trashville wasteland, fighting GENOM Corporation.

### Venice Biennale & Major Installations
- [**3x3x6**](http://3x3x6.com) (2019) — **Taiwan Pavilion at the [58th Venice Biennale](https://www.labiennale.org/en/art/2019)**. Cheang was the **first woman to represent Taiwan** with a solo exhibition. Installed at Palazzo delle Prigioni (former prison). Curated by philosopher [[Paul B. Preciado]]. Immersive installation with rotating inverted surveillance tower, 10 case studies of people imprisoned for gender/sexuality/race (Casanova, Marquis de Sade, "female sperm bandits," etc.) presented as trans-punk fiction films. Digital cameras watched visitors. Explored pervasive technologies of control from Bentham's panopticon to China's Skynet.
- *SLEEP1237* (2019) — Commissioned by [Performa](https://performa-arts.org/) 19 biennial.
- *Locker Baby Project* (2001–2012) — Three-part series: Baby Play (2001), Baby Love (2005), Baby Work (2012). Interactive installations proposing fictional clone baby scenarios set in 2030.
- *MovingForest* (2008–2012) — 12-hour distributed performance.

### Bio Art & Post-Net (2010s–present)
- [*UNBORN0x9*](http://unborn0x9.labomedia.org) (2016–ongoing) — With [[Ewen Chardronnet]] and Future Baby Production. Bio-hacking and reproductive futures.
- [*Mycelium Network Society*](http://mns.stwst.at) (2017–ongoing) — Post-net nature's network. Initiative with [Stadtwerkstatt](https://stwst.at/) (Linz) and [Galerija Kapelica](https://www.kapelica.org/) (Ljubljana). Fungi as medium, investigating mycelium's network capacity for molecular communication. Residency programme with open calls.
- [*HAGAY DREAMING*](http://hagaydreaming.net) (2022–ongoing) — Techno Fantasia guided theatre of Revival from Dowmong, Taiwan.
- *Composting the Net* / *Composting the City* (2012) — Biodegradable data trash; adopt a composter.
- *Seeds Underground* (2013) — Underground party for seed exchange at [[Ars Electronica]], Linz.

## Venue Circuit Analysis
Cheang's venue choices reveal a **four-decade arc across three distinct phases**:

1. **NYC activist/institutional (1980s–2000):** Paper Tiger Television → Whitney Museum → Walker Art Center → Guggenheim → Creative Time. She bridged underground activist media and the highest-tier institutional art world. The Whitney solo at age 36 and Guggenheim web commission were career-defining.

2. **European media art/festival circuit (2000–present):** After self-exile from NYC, she embedded in the European experimental art infrastructure: transmediale (Berlin), Ars Electronica/Stadtwerkstatt (Linz), Galerija Kapelica (Ljubljana), Berlinale, LAS Art Foundation (Berlin), Haus der Kunst (Munich). She chose **working spaces** (Stadtwerkstatt's INFOLAB, Kapelica's BioTehna) over prestige galleries — the signal of someone building practice infrastructure, not just showing.

3. **Global institutional recognition (2019–present):** Venice Biennale (Taiwan Pavilion), MoMA premiere, LG Guggenheim Award, Haus der Kunst solo. The institutional world caught up.

**Key pattern:** She consistently picks **venues with laboratory infrastructure** (wetlabs, media labs, network spaces) over pure white-cube galleries. Stadtwerkstatt, Kapelica, Labomedia — these are maker-spaces with philosophical ambitions. This is the venue choice of someone whose art IS the infrastructure, not something displayed within it.

Her post-2010 turn to bio-hacking and mycelium networks anticipates the current "more-than-human" wave by years.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
- **Medium-as-message radicalism:** Each new medium she adopts (public access TV → web → film → bio) isn't just a platform but becomes the content. Brandon isn't about the web; it IS the web as identity space.
- **Temporal worldbuilding:** Sets everything in fictional futures (2030, 2060) that are thinly veiled critiques of the present. Creates a persistent fictional universe she returns to across decades.
- **Genre invention:** Didn't work within existing genres — invented "cyberpunk queer sci-fi cinema" and "bio-net art" as categories.

### Career Architecture
- **Strategic institutional firsts:** First web artwork in the Guggenheim. First woman to represent Taiwan at Venice solo. These weren't accidental — she positioned herself at the frontier of what institutions didn't yet know how to handle.
- **Infrastructure building as practice:** LAPTOPSRUS (women performers' network), Mycelium Network Society (artist-scientist residency), Kingdom of Piracy (curated netspace) — she creates platforms, not just artworks.
- **Self-exile as strategy:** Leaving NYC after 20 years at the height of her institutional recognition to become a digital nomad. This RESET her context, freed her from the NYC art market, and re-embedded her in the European experimental infrastructure where bio-art and media art were better supported.

### Methodology Signature
- **Long-duration projects:** UKI (14 years), Locker Baby (11 years), MNS (9+ years). She treats projects as ecosystems that grow and mutate, not as discrete objects.
- **Participatory + networked:** From Bowling Alley (1995) linking physical and cyber space to Seeds Underground (underground seed exchange parties) — the audience IS the network.
- **Cross-media consistency:** Film, net art, installation, performance, bio art — wildly different media, but the thematic core (bodies, networks, control, resistance) never wavers.

### Network Strategy
- **Community embedding over gallery representation:** She doesn't have a mega-gallery. She embeds in maker-spaces (Stadtwerkstatt), wetlabs (Kapelica), and media art festivals (transmediale, Ars Electronica). This gives her access to resources and collaborators that galleries can't provide.
- **Cross-generational influence:** Collaborates with theorists (Paul B. Preciado, Matthew Fuller, McKenzie Wark), younger artists (Sky Deep, Jenna Sutela), and filmmakers (John Greyson). She's a hub, not a solo practitioner.

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **Joining Paper Tiger Television (1980s):** Embedded in NYC activist media. Gave her the participatory, networked, political DNA that defines everything after.
2. **Whitney solo show — Color Schemes (1990):** Institutional breakthrough at 36. Proved experimental video about race could claim major museum space.
3. **Brandon commission at Guggenheim (1998):** Historic first — web art enters the museum. Positioned her as the definitive net.art pioneer alongside JODI, Olia Lialina, etc. But she did it from WITHIN a major institution, not from the net.art underground.
4. **Self-imposed digital nomadism (2000–2007):** Left NYC, refused fixed address, traveled Japan/Netherlands/UK. This wasn't dropping out — it was repositioning. She arrived in Paris embedded in European networks.
5. **Mycelium Network Society (2017):** Post-internet turn to biological networks. Anticipated the "more-than-human" art wave. Built it as a residency programme, not just an artwork.
6. **Venice Biennale — 3x3x6 (2019):** Institutional apex. First woman for Taiwan. Chose Paul B. Preciado as curator — a queer philosopher, not an art-world insider. The work was about surveillance and prisons, IN a former prison. Everything was deliberate.
7. **UKI premiere at MoMA + LG Guggenheim Award (2023–2024):** Full-circle institutional recognition. A 14-year project premiering at MoMA, then winning the Guggenheim's tech-art prize. She waited until the work was done on her terms.

### What Amber Can Learn
- **Build long-duration projects:** Cheang's most powerful works took 10–15 years. This isn't procrastination — it's allowing the work to accumulate meaning and cultural context. Amber should consider what 10-year projects she could start now.
- **Create infrastructure, not just artworks:** LAPTOPSRUS, MNS, Kingdom of Piracy — Cheang creates platforms and networks AS her practice. Amber could build research infrastructure that IS the research.
- **Strategic self-exile:** Cheang left NYC at the peak. Moving to a different ecosystem (European media art) freed her from market pressures and connected her to labs/residencies better suited to experimental work. Amber should think about which ecosystem supports her work best, not just which is most prestigious.
- **Thematic consistency across media:** Film, net art, bio art, installation — wildly different, but always about bodies, networks, power. The consistency makes the diversity coherent. Amber can work across many media if the conceptual core is strong.
- **Choose collaborators for what they THINK, not what they make:** Preciado (philosopher), Fuller (theorist), Wark (writer) — Cheang's key collaborators are thinkers, not other visual artists. This elevates the intellectual stakes of the work.
