# Alexander R. Galloway

Media theorist, philosopher, and computer programmer who has spent two decades building one of the most systematic accounts of how digital networks operate as political formations. Best known for *Protocol* (2004), which showed that decentralized networks still enforce control — just through technical standards rather than hierarchy. Bridges continental philosophy (Deleuze, Badiou, Laruelle, Jameson) with hands-on software practice (Carnivore, Kriegspiel). One of the very few media theorists who is also actually a programmer. Close intellectual interlocutor to [[Yuk Hui]], and a foundational figure in what might be called the **New York school of media theory** (alongside [[Eugene Thacker]] and McKenzie Wark).

Professor of Media, Culture, and Communication at [NYU Steinhardt](https://steinhardt.nyu.edu/mcc/) since 2002. Author of ten books. Co-creator of [Carnivore](http://r-s-g.org/carnivore), the artistic network-surveillance tool that won the [Prix Ars Electronica Golden Nica](https://ars.electronica.art/prix/en/) in 2002. Writings translated into a dozen languages.

## Key Works

### Books
- **[*Protocol: How Control Exists After Decentralization*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/protocol)** (MIT Press, 2004) — **the foundational text**. The internet seems free and decentralized but TCP/IP, DNS, and other protocols encode a distributed form of control. Draws on Foucault → Deleuze's "control societies" to show networks are not liberation but a new topology of power. Genuinely influential across media theory, STS, political theory, and internet studies. The book that made him. ~2,000+ citations.
- **[*Gaming: Essays on Algorithmic Culture*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/gaming)** (Minnesota, 2006) — pioneered reading video games as theoretical objects; coined "gamic action"; argued games are "allegorical machines" for lived social reality. Lev Manovich: "contemporary media theory at its best."
- **[*The Exploit: A Theory of Networks*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9780816650446/the-exploit/)** (Minnesota, 2007) — co-authored with **[[Eugene Thacker]]**. Builds on *Protocol* by asking: if networks are control, how to resist? The exploit — a program exploiting a network flaw — becomes the model for counter-network practice. "Challenges the widespread assumption that networks are inherently egalitarian."
- **[*The Interface Effect*](https://www.politybooks.com/bookdetail?book_slug=the-interface-effect--9780745662527)** (Polity, 2012) — "What is an interface?" Answer: a threshold, an edge condition, a political and aesthetic phenomenon. Analyzes films, games, and software as interfaces. Argues that form is the political content of interface.
- **[*French Theory Today*](https://cultureandcommunication.org/galloway/FTT/)** (Public School NY / Erudio Editions, 2012) — five pamphlets from a seminar at The Public School New York on contemporary French thinkers (Malabou, Stiegler, Belhaj Kacem, Meillassoux, Laruelle). Contributions from Thacker, Pettman, Taeyoon Choi, David Horvitz. Risograph-printed in Brooklyn, edition of 140. A rare self-published collectible of high theoretical value.
- **[*Excommunication: Three Inquiries in Media and Mediation*](http://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/E/bo14413838.html)** (University of Chicago Press, 2013) — co-authored with **[[Eugene Thacker]]** and **McKenzie Wark**. On inmediation (Thacker), excommunication (Galloway: Hermes/Iris/the Furies as three modes of mediation), and unmediation (Wark: "furious media" as queer heresy). Thomas Bartscherer: "a founding document — has the New York school of media theory finally arrived?"
- **[*Laruelle: Against the Digital*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9780816692132/laruelle/)** (Minnesota, 2014) — introduces François Laruelle's non-philosophy to digital media theory. Reads Laruelle's "One" as a weapon against the discrete, the dialectical, and digital capitalism. Luciana Parisi: "an unmissable intervention into the thought of the digital today."
- **[*Uncomputable: Play and Politics in the Long Digital Age*](https://www.versobooks.com/products/2656-uncomputable)** (Verso, 2021) — narrates lesser-known episodes from computing history (hand looms, artificial life, war games) by rebuilding obsolete technology in modern software. What remains "uncomputable" — the analog remainder of the digital.
- **[*Digital Theory*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517920197/digital-theory/)** (Minnesota/meson press, 2026) — four-author volume with **[[M. Beatrice Fazi]]**, **Matthew Handelman**, and **Leif Weatherby**. Three essays arguing the digital is theoretical — not just consumer electronics but a mode of mediation via discrete units. Galloway's chapter connects the digital with structuralism; argues the digital is strictly *incompatible* with dialectics. Also available as [open-access PDF via meson press](https://meson.press/books/digital-theory/).

## Software / Art Projects

## Intellectual Trajectory

### Phase 1: Protocol and Network Control (2001–2007)
*Protocol* (2004) was a breakout — the first book to systematically apply post-Foucauldian theory to internet infrastructure. Argument: TCP/IP is not liberation but a *distributed* form of control. Drew on Deleuze's "Postscript on Control Societies" to show that networks have two complementary logics — decentralization (freedom) and protocol (control). *The Exploit* (with Thacker) extended this to ask what a counter-network practice would look like. The "exploit" — a program exploiting a network flaw — became the model.

### Phase 2: Interface and the Aesthetic (2008–2014)
*The Interface Effect* (2012) turned toward aesthetics. Interface is never a surface — it is always a threshold between two different kinds of media. *Laruelle* (2014) pushed further away from dialectics toward the "non-philosophical" One. Both books work out how to escape the mediation trap — neither affirming nor negating mediation but thinking from a "last philosophy."

### Phase 3: Uncomputable and Beyond (2015–present)
*Uncomputable* (Verso, 2021) pivoted toward history and the concrete — rebuilding obsolete technologies to see what they reveal. *Digital Theory* (2026) is a return to first principles: what does the digital require theoretically? Current blog work explores good infinity vs. ecological fatalism, and an emerging "ghost book" assembling Jameson's unwritten work on Lacan.

**Consistent thread throughout**: commitment to **formalism** (protocols, interfaces, algorithms, mathematics) combined with **radical political stakes** (Marxism, critique of cybernetic capitalism). Never just cultural criticism — always working at the level of the concept.

## Venue Circuit Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

Galloway operates in **three circuits simultaneously:**

### 1. Continental Theory Circuit
*e-flux Journal*, *Critical Inquiry*, *October*, *differences*, *Grey Room*, *Social Text*, *New German Critique*, *boundary 2*, *Les Temps qui restent* — flagship journals of critical theory and cultural studies. His articles here establish him as a peer of the major critical theorists, not merely a media studies professor.

**Why:** His Duke PhD placed him firmly in the continental theory tradition. He is a philosopher who uses media/technology as his object, not a media scholar who dabbles in philosophy.

### 2. Minnesota/meson Press Media Theory Circuit
University of Minnesota Press is the dominant publisher in media/cultural studies theory. *Protocol*, *Gaming*, *The Exploit*, *Laruelle*, and *Digital Theory* are all Minnesota. This is the center of gravity for serious media theory.

**Why:** Minnesota published Deleuze's translations, Massumi, Haraway, Stengers — the canon Galloway extends. This publisher relationship is itself a field-declaration: this is where serious media theory lives.

### 3. Art/Technology Circuit
Rhizome.org, e-flux, RSG, Prix Ars Electronica, Public School New York, ICA Miami — the art world's technology-engaged venues.

**Why:** The dual track (programmer + philosopher) gives Galloway something unusual: he's not just theorizing what artists do, he's doing it. This gives him credibility in both art and theory worlds simultaneously.

### Pattern: Berlin as Intellectual Incubator
Both his Berlin Prize (2018) and Cinepoetics fellowship (FU Berlin) point to Germany as where his work on digital philosophy, film theory, and continental thought gets taken seriously. The ENS Paris talk (2017) fits this too.

## For Amber

1. **🔧 Protocol is still the essential framework** — Galloway's central insight maps directly onto AI: decentralization doesn't mean freedom — it shifts control to a different layer. Who controls the protocol layer of AI (model weights, APIs, fine-tuning access, inference compute)? This is a vast open research territory with almost no one doing the kind of rigorous political-technical analysis Galloway pioneered for TCP/IP. Amber could extend protocol theory into AI sovereignty.

2. **💻 Programmer-theorist model** — Galloway built Carnivore, then wrote the theory. This is the exact combination Amber practices: doing + thinking. He's one of the few precedents for this model in critical media studies.

3. **🎮 Games as theory objects** — *Gaming* pioneered taking games seriously as thought experiments about social reality. Amber's interest in game design and simulation connects to this tradition. "Allegorical machines" is a useful vocabulary.

4. **💡 Career inspiration — the parallel track is a superpower** — The most important lesson: he was simultaneously editor/programmer at Rhizome and a PhD student at Duke. His best books are impossible to write without both tracks active. *Protocol* only works because he knows TCP/IP from the inside. Directly applicable to Amber.

5. **📚 Learning from his path — field declaration books** — He built reputation through a series of **field-defining books** on different related objects: protocols → games → interfaces → Laruelle → the uncomputable → digital theory broadly. Each book stakes out new territory while extending the core project. Compare this to scattering papers.

6. **🌍 Good Infinity** — His "Good Infinity" essay (*Les Temps qui restent* #5) connects Hegelian dialectics, Badiou's set theory, and climate politics. This kind of philosophical-political essay at the intersection of math and ecology is rare. Worth reading as a model for ambitious synthetic essays.

7. **🎓 Fellowship** — **Berlin Prize** (American Academy in Berlin) and **Guggenheim** are models worth knowing. The IAS (Princeton) is the pinnacle. His career shows these follow from an established book-based theoretical project, not conference papers.

8. **🤝 Cooperation** — *Digital Theory* (2026) with Fazi, Handelman, Weatherby shows he's interested in collaborative book-length projects across disciplines. A model for Amber's collaborative theoretical work.

## Why Interesting

Galloway is the thinker who most clearly shows that **digital infrastructure is political infrastructure** — that the TCP/IP protocol is not just a technical standard but a form of governance. His central claim: decentralization does not equal freedom; it creates new forms of control. This has not become less true as AI has developed; it has become more true.

For Amber's research taste:
- **Critical computing** — takes digital systems seriously as political and philosophical objects, not neutral tools
- **Interface theory → interaction design** — *The Interface Effect* argues every interface is an allegory — it makes some operations visible and others invisible. Directly relevant to AI system design
- **Protocol theory → AI governance** — the architecture of AI systems (APIs, fine-tuning, model weights) is to AI what TCP/IP is to the internet
- **Weird + rigorous** — his Laruelle work is the most unusual thing a media theorist could do, and he does it rigorously
- **Agenda-setting** — *Protocol* and *The Exploit* set the agenda for a generation of network scholars

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

**This section is about how Galloway builds his research — meta-strategies Amber can extract.**

### Conceptual Strategy

**Framework multiplication → specific tool per object**: Galloway consistently imports philosophical frameworks (Badiou's set theory, Deleuze's control societies, Laruelle's non-philosophy, Tiqqun's cybernetics) into media theory. Each import produces a *specific theoretical tool* for analyzing a specific media object (protocols, games, interfaces). Not eclecticism — systematic.

**Object-first approach**: Unlike many theorists who have a methodology and apply it, Galloway is driven by **objects** — the specific technical architecture of TCP/IP, the specific mechanics of video games, the specific rendering of interfaces. Theory is always in service of making the object legible.

**Provocation-first**: His books have provocative central claims that force response: "protocols are how control exists after decentralization"; "games are allegorical machines"; "networks are not egalitarian." These compress complex arguments into memorable theses that become citeable positions.

### Career Architecture

**The parallel track as core strategy**: His dual identity as programmer and philosopher is not incidental — it is the career strategy. Technical knowledge gives authority pure theorists don't have. Philosophical training gives frameworks pure technologists lack. He built both simultaneously (Rhizome + Duke PhD).

**Field declaration → book series**: Each book implicitly declares "this is now a subfield worth taking seriously" — protocol studies, game studies as philosophy, interface theory. The Minnesota Press relationship (6 of 10 books) is itself a field-declaration.

**Strategic co-authorship**: *The Exploit* (Thacker), *Excommunication* (Thacker + Wark), *Digital Theory* (Fazi, Handelman, Weatherby) — he returns to collaborative book-writing with different partners for different intellectual projects. Each collaboration produces something no solo author could write.

**The art world pipeline**: Rhizome → RSG/Carnivore/Prix Ars Electronica → e-flux, ICA Miami, Public School NY. This keeps him connected to practicing artists and gives his theory urgency that purely academic work lacks.

### Methodology Signature

**Materialism of the technical**: Core method — treat technical protocols, code, game rules, interface designs as *material facts* with political consequences. Not "technology is a social construction" but "this specific technical architecture has these specific political effects."

**Archival-historical reconstruction**: *Uncomputable* rebuilds obsolete technology in new software — literally reconstructing the past to make it legible. Media archaeology as hands-on practice, not just theoretical approach.

**The diagram as argument**: Articles frequently deploy mathematical diagrams, charts, and formal structures as philosophical arguments (Badiou articles, geometry article). Mathematics as thought, not illustration.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **1996–2001: Rhizome + Duke double track** — Worked as technical director at Rhizome while completing PhD. This dual formation is the move that makes everything else possible. He emerged from grad school with professional networks in both art and academia.

2. **2004: *Protocol*** — First book; declared a new field: "protocol studies." The claim — internet decentralization is a new form of centralized control — was counterintuitive and obviously correct once stated. This is the book that made him.

3. **2007: *The Exploit* with Thacker** — Co-authoring with a close intellectual colleague rather than writing solo. Produced something more urgent than either could write alone. Established the "New York school" template.

4. **2014: *Laruelle: Against the Digital*** — The wildest move — applying the most obscure French philosopher (Laruelle's non-philosophy) to media theory. Either career suicide or genius expansion. Secured his reputation as someone who takes genuine philosophical risks.

5. **2018: Berlin Prize + Cinepoetics fellowship** — The European turn: Berlin as intellectual home, German critical theory, film theory. Opened the continental European circuit to him.

6. **2021: *Uncomputable* (Verso)** — Move to a trade press for a more essayistic, accessible book. Demonstrates he can write for multiple audiences.

7. **2026: *Digital Theory* with four co-authors** — The collaborative "state of the field" book — four different disciplinary perspectives. This is a genre move: from individual monograph to symposium-in-print.

### What Amber Can Learn

- **The parallel track is irreplaceable**: If you want to theorize computation, be *in* computation. Working at Rhizome while doing a PhD was the condition of possibility for his theoretical work — not a distraction from it.
- **Protocol theory is unfinished**: The framework Galloway built for internet protocols applies perfectly to AI systems. Model weights, API access, fine-tuning policies, safety filters — these are all protocols in Galloway's sense. A concrete theoretical territory Amber could contribute to.
- **Object-first method**: Start with specific technical objects, not abstract theories. What *is* the architecture of this AI system? What *does* this interface do? Theory follows the object.
- **Field declaration through early book**: The move of writing a comprehensive book that declares a field early in a career is high-risk, high-reward. *Protocol* declared the field before he was well-known. It's the move that creates fields rather than merely contributing to them.
- **Collaborative books as intellectual events**: *Excommunication* and *Digital Theory* treat the co-authored book as an *event* — a happening in intellectual space. Each essay is in conversation with the others. A model for how to do collaborative theoretical work.
