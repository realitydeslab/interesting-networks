# Alexander R. Galloway

Media theorist, philosopher, and computer programmer who has spent two decades building one of the most systematic accounts of how digital networks operate as political formations. Best known for *Protocol* (2004), which showed that decentralized networks still enforce control — just through technical standards rather than hierarchy. Bridges continental philosophy (Deleuze, Badiou, Laruelle, Jameson) with hands-on software practice (Carnivore, Kriegspiel). Writing in plain, provocative prose, he is one of the very few media theorists who is also actually a programmer.

## Background
- **BA in Modern Culture and Media, [Brown University](https://www.brown.edu/)** (1996) — the MCM department was where media theory in the US was being invented; this is where he first learned to think about media critically and practically
- **PhD in Literature, [Duke University](https://duke.edu/)** (2001) — the Literature Program at Duke (Fredric Jameson's home) shaped his Marxist-inflected cultural theory approach
- **[NYU Steinhardt MCC](https://steinhardt.nyu.edu/degree/ms-media-culture-and-communication)** faculty since 2002 — one of the most important hubs for media theory in the US
- **Affiliated faculty, NYU Department of Comparative Literature**
- Was part of the **Radical Software Group ([RSG](http://r-s-g.org/))** from early career — programmed surveillance art projects that were exhibited internationally
- Writings translated into a dozen languages; a genuinely international theorist

## Domains
- **Protocol theory / network politics** — how technical standards enforce control; decentralization as a new form of power
- **Software and game studies** — pioneered critical reading of games as algorithmic culture
- **Interface theory** — interfaces as political thresholds, not endpoints
- **Continental philosophy applied to media** — Deleuze, Badiou, Laruelle, Jameson as frameworks for understanding digital media
- **Digital philosophy** — what is the digital? What theory does the digital require?
- **Non-philosophy (Laruelle)** — reading Laruelle as a weapon against the digital-dialectical nexus
- **Uncomputable / limits of the digital** — what computation cannot do; analog remainder
- **Film and aesthetics** — taught Hitchcock/Godard; connected to Cinepoetics

## Key Works

### Books
- **[*Protocol: How Control Exists After Decentralization*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/protocol)** (MIT Press, 2004) — foundational. The internet seems free and decentralized but TCP/IP, DNS, and other protocols encode a distributed form of control. Draws on Foucault → Deleuze's "control societies" to show networks are not liberation but a new topology of power. Genuinely influential across media theory, STS, political theory, and internet studies. The book that put Galloway on the map.
- **[*Gaming: Essays on Algorithmic Culture*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/gaming)** (Minnesota, 2006) — pioneered reading video games as a theoretical object; coined "gamic action"; argued games are allegorical machines for lived social reality. Quote from Lev Manovich: "contemporary media theory at its best."
- **[*The Exploit: A Theory of Networks*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9780816650446/the-exploit/)** (Minnesota, 2007) — co-authored with **[[Eugene Thacker]]**. Builds on Protocol by asking: if networks are control, how to resist? They argue the exploit — a vulnerability in a system — is the model for counter-network practice. Introduces asymmetrical counter-topologies. "Challenges the widespread assumption that networks are inherently egalitarian."
- **[*The Interface Effect*](https://www.politybooks.com/bookdetail?book_slug=the-interface-effect--9780745662527)** (Polity, 2012) — "What is an interface?" Answers: a threshold, an edge condition, a political and aesthetic phenomenon. Analyzes films, games, and software as interfaces. Argues that form is the political content of interface.
- **[*French Theory Today*](https://cultureandcommunication.org/galloway/FTT/)** (Public School NY / Erudio Editions, 2012) — five pamphlets from a seminar at The Public School New York on contemporary French thinkers (Malabou, Stiegler, Belhaj Kacem, Meillassoux, Laruelle). Risograph-printed in Brooklyn, edition of 140. A rare self-published collectible of high theoretical value.
- **[*Excommunication: Three Inquiries in Media and Mediation*](http://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/E/bo14413838.html)** (University of Chicago Press, 2013) — three essays with **[[Eugene Thacker]]** and **[[McKenzie Wark]]**. On inmediation (Thacker), excommunication (Galloway), and unmediation (Wark). Each proposes a mode of communication outside the logic of media. A tightly structured co-authored intervention.
- **[*Laruelle: Against the Digital*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9780816692132/laruelle/)** (Minnesota, 2014) — introduces François Laruelle's non-philosophy to the digital media theory world. Reads Laruelle's "One" as a weapon against the discrete, the dialectical, and digital capitalism. Quote from Luciana Parisi: "unmissable intervention into the thought of the digital today."
- **[*Uncomputable: Play and Politics in the Long Digital Age*](https://www.versobooks.com/products/2656-uncomputable)** (Verso, 2021) — narrates lesser-known episodes from computing history (hand looms, artificial life, war games) by rebuilding obsolete technology in modern software. Asks what remains "uncomputable" — the analog remainder of the digital. NYT: "conceptually sharp, visually compelling and completely attuned to the political moment."
- **[*Digital Theory*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517920197/digital-theory/)** (Minnesota/meson press, 2026) — four-author volume with **[[M. Beatrice Fazi]]**, **[[Matthew Handelman]]**, and **[[Leif Weatherby]]**. Three essays arguing the digital is theoretical — not just consumer electronics but a mode of mediation via discrete units. Galloway's chapter connects digital and structuralism; argues the digital is strictly *incompatible* with dialectics. Also available as [open-access PDF via meson press](https://meson.press/books/digital-theory/).

## Intellectual Trajectory

### Phase 1: Protocol and Network Control (2001–2007)
*Protocol* (2004) was a breakout — the first book to systematically apply post-Foucauldian theory to internet infrastructure. The argument: TCP/IP is not liberation but a *distributed* form of control. Galloway drew on Deleuze's "Postscript on Control Societies" and showed that networks have two complementary logics — decentralization (freedom) and protocol (control). *The Exploit* (with Thacker) extended this to ask what a counter-network practice would look like. The "exploit" — a program exploiting a network flaw — became the model.

### Phase 2: Interface and the Aesthetic (2008–2014)
*The Interface Effect* (2012) turned toward aesthetics and asked: what is a user interface, and what does it do politically? The answer: interface is never a surface — it is always a threshold between two different kinds of media. *Laruelle* (2014) pushed further away from dialectics toward the "non-philosophical" One. Both books work out how to escape the mediation trap — neither affirming nor negating mediation but thinking from a "last philosophy."

### Phase 3: Uncomputable and Beyond (2015–present)
*Uncomputable* (Verso, 2021) was a pivot toward history and the concrete — rebuilding obsolete technologies to see what they reveal. *Digital Theory* (2026) is a return to first principles: what does the digital require theoretically? His current blog work explores the good infinity (Hegel/Badiou) vs. ecological fatalism, AI degeneration as practice, and a forthcoming "ghost book" assembling Jameson's unwritten work on Lacan.

### Consistent Thread
Throughout: **a commitment to formalism** (protocols, interfaces, algorithms, mathematics) combined with **radical political stakes** (Marxism, critique of cybernetic capitalism). Never just cultural criticism — always working at the level of the concept.

## Why Interesting (for Amber)

1. **🔧 Protocol is still the essential framework** — If you're thinking about how AI agents get governed, how networks of agents coordinate, or what "sovereignty" means in distributed systems, *Protocol* is still the conceptual foundation. Galloway showed that decentralization doesn't mean freedom — it just shifts control to a different layer. This is exactly relevant to sovereign agent design.

2. **🎮 Games as theory objects** — *Gaming* pioneered taking games seriously as thought experiments about social reality. Amber's interest in game design and simulation maps directly onto this tradition. Galloway's reading of games as "allegories for social processes" provides vocabulary.

3. **💻 Programmer-theorist model** — Galloway built the Carnivore tool, then wrote the theory. This is the exact combination Amber practices: doing + thinking. He's one of the few precedents for this model in critical media studies.

4. **🌐 The Exploit as design principle** — The idea that counter-network practice should be asymmetrical, exploiting system vulnerabilities rather than confronting them head-on, has implications for anyone designing resistant or autonomous agents.

5. **∞ Good Infinity** — His "Good Infinity" essay connects Hegelian dialectics, Badiou's set theory, and climate politics in a way that's genuinely unusual. This kind of philosophical-political essay at the intersection of math and ecology is rare.

6. **📚 Career model** — His trajectory from Duke PhD → RSG practitioner → NYU professor → Guggenheim → IAS → Berlin Prize shows how to sustain a career combining theory and practice across 25 years without becoming merely one or the other.
