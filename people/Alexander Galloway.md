# Alexander R. Galloway

American media theorist and philosopher of technology. Professor at [NYU Steinhardt](https://as.nyu.edu/faculty/alexander-galloway.html), Department of Media, Culture and Communication. One of the most influential contemporary thinkers on **digital philosophy**, **protocol**, **interface theory**, and the politics of computation. Author of *Protocol* (MIT Press, 2004) — the foundational text on how the internet governs through decentralized technical standards. Close intellectual neighbor to [[Yuk Hui]] and the [[Research Network for Philosophy and Technology]].

## Background
Trained in literary theory and cultural studies. Developed a distinctive approach to **digital media theory** that combines continental philosophy (Badiou, Deleuze, Laruelle) with technical analysis of how digital systems actually work. His career trajectory: from protocol/network theory (*Protocol*, 2004) → game theory (*Gaming*, 2006) → interface theory (*The Interface Effect*, 2012) → Laruelle and non-philosophy (*Laruelle: Against the Digital*, 2014) → François Laruelle and the Black Universe → most recently, "a theory of everything" via digital philosophy.

Distinctive approach: uses the actual technical architecture of digital systems (TCP/IP, DNS, HTTP, game engines) as philosophical objects, not just metaphors. This makes his work genuinely hybrid — simultaneously technical and philosophical.

## Why Interesting
Galloway is the thinker who most clearly shows how digital infrastructure is political infrastructure — that the TCP/IP protocol is not just a technical standard but a form of governance. For Amber: his work connects directly to questions of **how AI platforms govern through their design** — the protocol politics of AI systems. His *Interface Effect* is directly relevant to design; his Laruelle work connects to the non-philosophy angle that intersects with Hui's cosmotechnics.

**Research taste resonance:** Galloway does what Amber values — takes technical systems seriously (not just as metaphors) and reads them philosophically. His "10 Expressions" talk is a model for how to do interdisciplinary technology philosophy in a talk setting.
