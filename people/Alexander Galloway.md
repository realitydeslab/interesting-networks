# Alexander R. Galloway

<<<<<<< HEAD
Media theorist, philosopher, and computer programmer who has spent two decades building one of the most systematic accounts of how digital networks operate as political formations. Best known for *Protocol* (2004), which showed that decentralized networks still enforce control — just through technical standards rather than hierarchy. Bridges continental philosophy (Deleuze, Badiou, Laruelle, Jameson) with hands-on software practice (Carnivore, Kriegspiel). Writing in plain, provocative prose, he is one of the very few media theorists who is also actually a programmer.

## Background
- **BA in Modern Culture and Media, [Brown University](https://www.brown.edu/)** (1996) — the MCM department was where media theory in the US was being invented; this is where he first learned to think about media critically and practically
- **PhD in Literature, [Duke University](https://duke.edu/)** (2001) — the Literature Program at Duke (Fredric Jameson's home) shaped his Marxist-inflected cultural theory approach
- **[NYU Steinhardt MCC](https://steinhardt.nyu.edu/degree/ms-media-culture-and-communication)** faculty since 2002 — one of the most important hubs for media theory in the US
- **Affiliated faculty, NYU Department of Comparative Literature**
- Was part of the **Radical Software Group ([RSG](http://r-s-g.org/))** from early career — programmed surveillance art projects that were exhibited internationally
- Writings translated into a dozen languages; a genuinely international theorist

## Domains
- **Protocol theory / network politics** — how technical standards enforce control; decentralization as a new form of power
- **Software and game studies** — pioneered critical reading of games as algorithmic culture
- **Interface theory** — interfaces as political thresholds, not endpoints
- **Continental philosophy applied to media** — Deleuze, Badiou, Laruelle, Jameson as frameworks for understanding digital media
- **Digital philosophy** — what is the digital? What theory does the digital require?
- **Non-philosophy (Laruelle)** — reading Laruelle as a weapon against the digital-dialectical nexus
- **Uncomputable / limits of the digital** — what computation cannot do; analog remainder
- **Film and aesthetics** — taught Hitchcock/Godard; connected to Cinepoetics

## Key Works

### Books
- **[*Protocol: How Control Exists After Decentralization*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/protocol)** (MIT Press, 2004) — foundational. The internet seems free and decentralized but TCP/IP, DNS, and other protocols encode a distributed form of control. Draws on Foucault → Deleuze's "control societies" to show networks are not liberation but a new topology of power. Genuinely influential across media theory, STS, political theory, and internet studies. The book that put Galloway on the map.
- **[*Gaming: Essays on Algorithmic Culture*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/gaming)** (Minnesota, 2006) — pioneered reading video games as a theoretical object; coined "gamic action"; argued games are allegorical machines for lived social reality. Quote from Lev Manovich: "contemporary media theory at its best."
- **[*The Exploit: A Theory of Networks*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9780816650446/the-exploit/)** (Minnesota, 2007) — co-authored with **[[Eugene Thacker]]**. Builds on Protocol by asking: if networks are control, how to resist? They argue the exploit — a vulnerability in a system — is the model for counter-network practice. Introduces asymmetrical counter-topologies. "Challenges the widespread assumption that networks are inherently egalitarian."
- **[*The Interface Effect*](https://www.politybooks.com/bookdetail?book_slug=the-interface-effect--9780745662527)** (Polity, 2012) — "What is an interface?" Answers: a threshold, an edge condition, a political and aesthetic phenomenon. Analyzes films, games, and software as interfaces. Argues that form is the political content of interface.
- **[*French Theory Today*](https://cultureandcommunication.org/galloway/FTT/)** (Public School NY / Erudio Editions, 2012) — five pamphlets from a seminar at The Public School New York on contemporary French thinkers (Malabou, Stiegler, Belhaj Kacem, Meillassoux, Laruelle). Risograph-printed in Brooklyn, edition of 140. A rare self-published collectible of high theoretical value.
- **[*Excommunication: Three Inquiries in Media and Mediation*](http://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/E/bo14413838.html)** (University of Chicago Press, 2013) — three essays with **[[Eugene Thacker]]** and **[[McKenzie Wark]]**. On inmediation (Thacker), excommunication (Galloway), and unmediation (Wark). Each proposes a mode of communication outside the logic of media. A tightly structured co-authored intervention.
- **[*Laruelle: Against the Digital*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9780816692132/laruelle/)** (Minnesota, 2014) — introduces François Laruelle's non-philosophy to the digital media theory world. Reads Laruelle's "One" as a weapon against the discrete, the dialectical, and digital capitalism. Quote from Luciana Parisi: "unmissable intervention into the thought of the digital today."
- **[*Uncomputable: Play and Politics in the Long Digital Age*](https://www.versobooks.com/products/2656-uncomputable)** (Verso, 2021) — narrates lesser-known episodes from computing history (hand looms, artificial life, war games) by rebuilding obsolete technology in modern software. Asks what remains "uncomputable" — the analog remainder of the digital. NYT: "conceptually sharp, visually compelling and completely attuned to the political moment."
- **[*Digital Theory*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9781517920197/digital-theory/)** (Minnesota/meson press, 2026) — four-author volume with **[[M. Beatrice Fazi]]**, **[[Matthew Handelman]]**, and **[[Leif Weatherby]]**. Three essays arguing the digital is theoretical — not just consumer electronics but a mode of mediation via discrete units. Galloway's chapter connects digital and structuralism; argues the digital is strictly *incompatible* with dialectics. Also available as [open-access PDF via meson press](https://meson.press/books/digital-theory/).

## Intellectual Trajectory

### Phase 1: Protocol and Network Control (2001–2007)
*Protocol* (2004) was a breakout — the first book to systematically apply post-Foucauldian theory to internet infrastructure. The argument: TCP/IP is not liberation but a *distributed* form of control. Galloway drew on Deleuze's "Postscript on Control Societies" and showed that networks have two complementary logics — decentralization (freedom) and protocol (control). *The Exploit* (with Thacker) extended this to ask what a counter-network practice would look like. The "exploit" — a program exploiting a network flaw — became the model.

### Phase 2: Interface and the Aesthetic (2008–2014)
*The Interface Effect* (2012) turned toward aesthetics and asked: what is a user interface, and what does it do politically? The answer: interface is never a surface — it is always a threshold between two different kinds of media. *Laruelle* (2014) pushed further away from dialectics toward the "non-philosophical" One. Both books work out how to escape the mediation trap — neither affirming nor negating mediation but thinking from a "last philosophy."

### Phase 3: Uncomputable and Beyond (2015–present)
*Uncomputable* (Verso, 2021) was a pivot toward history and the concrete — rebuilding obsolete technologies to see what they reveal. *Digital Theory* (2026) is a return to first principles: what does the digital require theoretically? His current blog work explores the good infinity (Hegel/Badiou) vs. ecological fatalism, AI degeneration as practice, and a forthcoming "ghost book" assembling Jameson's unwritten work on Lacan.

### Consistent Thread
Throughout: **a commitment to formalism** (protocols, interfaces, algorithms, mathematics) combined with **radical political stakes** (Marxism, critique of cybernetic capitalism). Never just cultural criticism — always working at the level of the concept.

## Why Interesting (for Amber)

1. **🔧 Protocol is still the essential framework** — If you're thinking about how AI agents get governed, how networks of agents coordinate, or what "sovereignty" means in distributed systems, *Protocol* is still the conceptual foundation. Galloway showed that decentralization doesn't mean freedom — it just shifts control to a different layer. This is exactly relevant to sovereign agent design.

2. **🎮 Games as theory objects** — *Gaming* pioneered taking games seriously as thought experiments about social reality. Amber's interest in game design and simulation maps directly onto this tradition. Galloway's reading of games as "allegories for social processes" provides vocabulary.

3. **💻 Programmer-theorist model** — Galloway built the Carnivore tool, then wrote the theory. This is the exact combination Amber practices: doing + thinking. He's one of the few precedents for this model in critical media studies.

4. **🌐 The Exploit as design principle** — The idea that counter-network practice should be asymmetrical, exploiting system vulnerabilities rather than confronting them head-on, has implications for anyone designing resistant or autonomous agents.

5. **∞ Good Infinity** — His "Good Infinity" essay connects Hegelian dialectics, Badiou's set theory, and climate politics in a way that's genuinely unusual. This kind of philosophical-political essay at the intersection of math and ecology is rare.

6. **📚 Career model** — His trajectory from Duke PhD → RSG practitioner → NYU professor → Guggenheim → IAS → Berlin Prize shows how to sustain a career combining theory and practice across 25 years without becoming merely one or the other.

## Venue Circuit Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

Galloway operates in **three circuits simultaneously:**

### 1. Continental Theory Circuit
*e-flux Journal*, *Critical Inquiry*, *October*, *differences*, *Grey Room*, *Social Text*, *New German Critique*, *boundary 2* — the flagship journals of critical theory and cultural studies. His articles here establish him as a peer of the major critical theorists.

**Why:** His Duke PhD placed him firmly in the continental theory tradition. He is a philosopher who uses media/technology as his object, not a media scholar who dabbles in philosophy.

### 2. Minnesota/meson Press Media Theory Circuit
University of Minnesota Press is the dominant publisher in media/cultural studies theory. *Protocol*, *Gaming*, *The Exploit*, *Laruelle*, and *Digital Theory* are all Minnesota. This is the center of gravity for serious media theory publishing.

**Why:** Minnesota published Deleuze's translated works, Massumi, Haraway, Stengers — the canon Galloway is working within and extending. This is a prestige signal within media theory specifically.

### 3. Art/Technology Circuit
Rhizome.org, e-flux, RSG, Prix Ars Electronica, Public School New York, ICA Miami — the art world's technology-engaged venues. His work with RSG/Carnivore is as artistically significant as his books are theoretically.

**Why:** The dual track (programmer + philosopher) gives Galloway something unusual: he's not just theorizing what artists do, he's also doing it. This gives him credibility in both art and theory worlds.

### Pattern: Berlin as Intellectual Incubator
Both his Berlin Prize (2018-19) and Cinepoetics fellowship (FU Berlin) point to Europe — specifically Germany — as where his work on digital philosophy, film theory, and continental thought gets taken seriously. The ENS Paris talk (2017) fits this pattern too.

## Why Interesting

Galloway is the thinker who most clearly shows that **digital infrastructure is political infrastructure** — that the TCP/IP protocol is not just a technical standard but a form of governance. His central insight: decentralization does not equal freedom; it just creates new forms of control. This has not become less true as AI has developed; it has become more true.

For Amber, his work is relevant in multiple ways:
- **Protocol theory → AI governance**: The architecture of AI systems (APIs, fine-tuning access, model weights) is to AI what TCP/IP is to the internet. Who controls the protocol layer?
- **Interface theory → interaction design**: *The Interface Effect* argues every interface is an allegory — it makes some operations visible and others invisible. This is directly relevant to how AI systems present themselves.
- **Game studies → AI play**: *Gaming* and *Uncomputable* both treat algorithmic systems as gamelike; the notion of "rule-based" behavior as a form of culture.
- **Non-standard thinking → beyond the mainstream**: His Laruelle work models how to think *outside* the dominant paradigm rather than within it.

**Research taste resonance:** "Critical computing" — takes digital systems seriously as political and philosophical objects. "Weird + rigorous" — his work on Laruelle is the most unusual thing a media theorist could do. "Agenda-setting" — *Protocol* and *The Exploit* set the agenda for a generation of network scholars.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy

**Framework multiplication**: Galloway consistently imports philosophical frameworks (Badiou's set theory, Deleuze's control societies, Laruelle's non-philosophy, Tiqqun's cybernetics) into media theory. This isn't intellectual eclecticism; each import produces a *specific theoretical tool* for analyzing a specific media object (protocols, games, interfaces).

**The object-first approach**: Unlike many theorists who have a methodology and apply it to objects, Galloway is driven by **objects** — the specific technical architecture of TCP/IP, the specific mechanics of video games, the specific rendering of interfaces. The theory is always in service of making the object legible.

**Provocation-first**: His books have provocative central claims that force response: "protocols are how control exists after decentralization"; "games are allegorical machines"; "networks are not egalitarian." These claims compress complex arguments into memorable theses that become citeable positions.

### Career Architecture

**The parallel track**: His dual identity as programmer and philosopher is not incidental — it is the career strategy. The technical knowledge gives him authority the pure theorists don't have. The philosophical training gives him frameworks the pure technologists lack. He built both tracks simultaneously (Rhizome + Duke PhD).

**Field declaration → book series**: Each book implicitly declares: "this is now a subfield worth taking seriously" — protocol studies, game studies as philosophy, interface theory. The Minnesota Press relationship (6 of his 10 books) is itself a field-declaration: this is where the serious media theory lives.

**Strategic co-authorship**: *The Exploit* (with Thacker), *Excommunication* (with Thacker + Wark), *Digital Theory* (with Fazi, Handelman, Weatherby) — he returns to collaborative book-writing with different partners for different intellectual projects. Each collaboration produces something no single author could write alone.

**The art world pipeline**: Starting at Rhizome, the RSG/Carnivore/Prix Ars Electronica trajectory, and continuing public art-theory engagement (e-flux, ICA Miami, Public School NY) — this keeps him connected to practicing artists and gives his theory a different kind of urgency than purely academic work.

### Methodology Signature

**Materialism of the technical**: His core method: treat technical protocols, code, game rules, interface designs as *material facts* with political consequences. Not "technology is a social construction" (social construction criticism) but "this specific technical architecture has these specific political effects."

**Archival-historical reconstruction**: *Uncomputable* rebuilds obsolete technology in new software — literally reconstructing the past to make it legible. This is media archaeology as hands-on practice, not just theoretical approach.

**The diagram as argument**: His articles frequently deploy mathematical diagrams, charts, and formal structures as philosophical arguments (see: the Badiou articles, the geometry article). Mathematics as thought, not just illustration.

### Network Strategy

The "New York school of media theory" label (coined by Thomas Bartscherer for *Excommunication*) is not accidental. Galloway, Thacker, and Wark built their reputations partly through their collaborative proximity — at the same institutions (The New School, NYU), in the same publications, on the same stages. The *Excommunication* book is simultaneously a theoretical argument and a territorial declaration: *this* is what New York media theory looks like.

### Key Moves (chronological)

1. **1996–2001 Rhizome + Duke double track**: While in his PhD program, worked as editor and technical director at Rhizome. This is the move that makes everything else possible. He emerged from grad school not just with a philosophy but with a professional network in both art and academia.

2. **2004 *Protocol***: First book, based on dissertation (kind of). Declared a new field: "protocol studies." The claim — that the internet's decentralization is a new form of centralized control — was both counterintuitive and obviously correct once stated. This is the book that made him.

3. **2007 *The Exploit* with Eugene Thacker**: The move of co-authoring with a close intellectual colleague rather than writing solo. Produced something more urgent than either could write alone. Established the "New York school" template.

4. **2014 *Laruelle: Against the Digital***: The wildest move — applying the most obscure French philosopher (Laruelle's non-philosophy) to media theory. This either looked like career suicide or a genius expansion. It secured his reputation as someone who takes genuine philosophical risks.

5. **2018-19 Berlin Prize, Freie Universität Cinepoetics**: The European turn — Berlin as intellectual home, German critical theory, film theory. This opened the continental European circuit to him.

6. **2021 *Uncomputable* (Verso)**: Move to a trade press (Verso, not Minnesota) for a more essayistic, accessible book. Demonstrates he can write for multiple audiences.

7. **2026 *Digital Theory* with four co-authors**: The collaborative "state of the field" book — four different disciplinary perspectives on digital theory. This is a genre move: from individual monograph to symposium-in-print.

### What Amber Can Learn

- **The parallel track is irreplaceable**: If you want to theorize computation, be *in* computation. Working at Rhizome while doing a PhD was not a distraction from his theoretical work; it was the condition of possibility for it. Amber's computational practice and theoretical work should inform each other, not run in parallel silos.
- **Protocol theory is unfinished**: The framework Galloway built for internet protocols applies perfectly to AI systems. The model weights, API access, fine-tuning policies, safety filters — these are all protocols in Galloway's sense. This is a concrete theoretical territory Amber could contribute to.
- **The object-first method**: Start with specific technical objects, not abstract theories. What *is* the architecture of this AI system? What *does* this interface do? The theory follows the object.
- **Field declaration through early book**: The move of writing a comprehensive book that declares a field (not just advances an argument) early in a career is a high-risk, high-reward strategy. Galloway did this with *Protocol* before he was well-known. It's the move that creates fields rather than just contributing to them.
- **Collaborative books as intellectual events**: *Excommunication* and *Digital Theory* both treat the co-authored book as an *event* — a happening in intellectual space — not just a multi-authored paper collection. Each essay is in conversation with the others. This is a model for how to do collaborative theoretical work.
