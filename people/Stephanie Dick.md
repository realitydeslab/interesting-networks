# Stephanie Dick

Historian of computing, mathematics, and artificial intelligence at [SFU School of Communication](https://www.sfu.ca/communication/). PhD History of Science from Harvard. Steering Committee member of the [Digital Democracies Institute](https://digitaldemocracies.org/). Co-authored "[How AI Sets Limits for Human Aspiration](https://issues.org/ai-limits-human-aspiration-dick-chun-canute/)" (with [[Wendy Chun]] and Matt Canute, *Issues in Science and Technology*, 2024). Co-editor of *[Abstractions and Embodiments: New Histories of Computing and Society](https://jhupbooks.press.jhu.edu/title/abstractions-and-embodiments)* (JHU Press) with Janet Abbate. Co-edits "Mining the Past" column at *Harvard Data Science Review*. On editorial boards of *IEEE Annals of the History of Computing* and *Cambridge Forum on AI: Culture and Society*.

## Background
PhD History of Science, Harvard → Junior Fellow, Harvard Society of Fellows → Assistant Professor, History and Sociology of Science, University of Pennsylvania → Assistant Professor, SFU School of Communication. Studies: automated theorem-proving and mathematical intelligence in 20th century; centralized computer databases in American policing (1960s); debates about the human mind at the intersection of logic, philosophy, and the occult. Currently researching: "how cultures of democracy are shaped by technology, expert knowledge, and digital media."

## Why Interesting
One of the sharpest historians of computing alive. Her work reveals what AI *was supposed to be* vs. what it actually does — providing historical receipts for present-day AI critiques. The question of automated mathematical proof is inherently linked to questions about what counts as intelligence, who gets to define it, and who gets excluded. This resonates directly with Amber's interest in AI behavior and social simulation. At SFU (Vancouver) — same institution as [[Wendy Chun]] and [[Andrew Feenberg]].
