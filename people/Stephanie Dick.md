# Stephanie Dick

Historian of computing, mathematics, and artificial intelligence at [SFU School of Communication](https://www.sfu.ca/communication/). Her work asks: what happens to human intelligence when we try to automate it? Not in a technophilic sense — but historically, materially, politically. Every attempt to automate "intelligence" rewrites what intelligence *means*, who has it, and whose cognitive labor gets devalued. She reconstructs those rewrites from the archives and shows us the stakes.

## Domains

- History of artificial intelligence and mathematical computing (20th century)
- Automation of mathematical reasoning and expert systems
- Bounded rationality and cognitive science history
- Software maintenance, failure, and infrastructure
- Computing, policing, and surveillance (1960s databases)
- Logic, philosophy, and the occult — "debates about the character of the human mind"
- AI history as political history of intelligence

## Background

PhD History of Science, **Harvard University** → **Junior Fellow, Harvard Society of Fellows** (one of the most prestigious humanities fellowships in North America) → **Assistant Professor, History and Sociology of Science, University of Pennsylvania** → **Assistant Professor, School of Communication, Simon Fraser University** (current).

Canadian, returned home to Canada to join SFU. The Harvard Society of Fellows is selective and cross-disciplinary — it shaped her as someone working across history of science, philosophy, and critical theory rather than narrowly in HoS.

Her career arc traces a deepening of purpose: early work established rigorous historical method (Isis papers on automated proof, bounded rationality); middle period expanded to software infrastructure and expert systems; current work moves into the eerie intersection of logic, formal systems, and occult thinking — following where the historical material leads.

The SFU move was decisive: Wendy Chun's [[Digital Democracies Institute]] brought her into explicitly political work on AI, democracy, and data. The DDI is one of Canada's most interesting critical tech institutes.

## Key Works

### Books / Edited Volumes

- [*Abstractions and Embodiments: New Histories of Computing and Society*](https://www.press.jhu.edu/books/title/12637/abstractions-and-embodiments) (Johns Hopkins University Press, 2022) — co-edited with [[Janet Abbate]]. A major collected volume rewriting computing history from feminist, labor, and material perspectives. Contributors examine the history of computing by attending to the bodies, practices, and social structures that have always been embedded in "abstract" systems.

### Journal Articles

- "The Standard Head" in *[Just Code](https://jhupbooks.press.jhu.edu/)* (Johns Hopkins University Press, 2025) — eds. Jeff Yost and Con Díaz. Latest work — examining some coded or standardized model of human cognition/behavior within computing.

- ["The Marxist in the Machine"](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/epdf/10.1086/725135) in *Osiris*, Vol. 38 (2023) — *Osiris* is one of the flagship thematic journals in history of science. This paper locates Marxist thought within the history of computing — examining how labor theory, alienation, and industrial capitalism shaped early AI and computing development. A significant intervention.

- ["The Politics of Representation: Narratives of Automation in Twentieth-Century Mathematics"](https://www.cambridge.org/core/books/narrative-science/politics-of-representation-narratives-of-automation-in-twentiethcentury-american-mathematics/05B402DEFE67F53963462A4B02AC87A0) in *[Narrative Science: Reasoning, Representing, and Knowing Since 1800](https://www.cambridge.org/ca/universitypress/subjects/history/history-science-and-technology/narrative-science-reasoning-representing-and-knowing-1800?format=HB&isbn=9781316519004)* (Cambridge, 2022), eds. Mary Morgan et al. — How mathematics constructed "automation" as a narrative of progress that obscured the politics of who and what gets displaced.

- ["Coded Conduct: Making MACSYMA Users and the Automation of Mathematics"](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/bjhs-themes/article/coded-conduct-making-macsyma-users-and-the-automation-of-mathematics/19936AF1E826C13BA410EFDB704026DF) in *BJHS Themes*, Vol. 5: Learning by the Book (2020) — Deep archival study of MACSYMA, MIT's symbolic math system (1960s–80s). Argues that the training materials for MACSYMA weren't just technical documentation — they were **behavioral scripts** designed to make mathematicians think more like programmers. Users were remade in the image of the system. Central thesis: "technologies offer freedom but born out of both dependency and discipline." One of her best papers.

- ["DLL Hell: Software Dependencies, Failure, and the Maintenance of Microsoft Windows"](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/8509170) with Daniel Volmar, *IEEE Annals of the History of Computing* (2018) — **Winner of the journal's 2018 best paper award.** On the famous software engineering nightmare: how Windows' dependency system routinely broke installations and destroyed users' systems. A labor and infrastructure history of software maintenance — the invisible, unrewarded, catastrophe-generating work of keeping systems running. Reveals the hidden politics in software architecture decisions.

- ["Of Models and Machines: Implementing Bounded Rationality"](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/full/10.1086/683527) in *Isis*, Vol. 106, No. 3 (2015) — Herbert Simon's bounded rationality theory was co-developed with and through computing. This paper traces how Simon's theory of human decision-making was shaped by, and implemented in, early AI systems — the human mind and the machine co-evolved as models for each other. One of the clearest papers on the Simon/cognitive science/AI nexus.

- ["AfterMath: The Work of Proof in the Age of Human-Machine Collaboration"](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/full/10.1086/661623) in *Isis*, Vol. 102, No. 3 (2011) — Her breakthrough paper. Studies the **Appel-Haken four-color theorem proof** (1976), the first major mathematical proof requiring computer verification. Thousands of cases checked by machine — no human could verify the full proof. What is a "proof" when it exceeds human checking? What is "mathematical understanding" when a theorem is true but incomprehensible? This paper opened the philosophical question she'd pursue for over a decade.

### For Broad Audiences

- ["How AI Sets Limits for Human Aspiration"](https://issues.org/ai-limits-human-aspiration-dick-chun-canute/) with [[Wendy Chun]] and Matt Canute, *Issues in Science and Technology* (2024) — Central argument: AI systems are evaluated by how well they mimic past human outputs — but that means looking backward, smoothing outliers, foreclosing novelty. "AI's history is usually one of progress, where algorithms attain humanlike intelligence. But that narrative might be more accurately inverted with a shrinking definition of intelligence that excludes many human capabilities." Highly readable synthesis of her historical work applied to the present.

- NPR Throughline, ["A More Perfect Human"](https://www.npr.org/2023/03/08/1161883646/a-more-perfect-human) (March 2023) — Guest historian on the history of AI; high public profile episode.

- ["The Computer"](https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/computers/) in *Sciences of "Dune"* (LARB, 2022) — Interesting venue: using sci-fi to think about computing history.

- ["Artificial Intelligence"](https://hdsr.mitpress.mit.edu/pub/0aytgrau/release/3) in *Harvard Data Science Review*, 1.1 (2019) — Inaugural issue essay. Full historical overview of AI: from the Dartmouth 1956 workshop through expert systems, neural networks, machine learning. Argues that "AI" has never had a stable definition — each era has *shrunk* the concept of intelligence to fit what machines can do. Brilliant gateway piece.

## Intellectual Trajectory

The through-line of Stephanie Dick's work is a single, deepening question: **what happens to intelligence when we try to formalize it?**

**2011 — AfterMath:** What is mathematical proof after Appel-Haken? The four-color theorem was proven with a machine. It's true — but no human comprehends it fully. This destabilizes the idea that mathematics is about insight, understanding, certainty. It becomes: true outputs from a black box. First confrontation with the gap between formalization and understanding.

**2015 — Of Models and Machines:** Herbert Simon built theories of human reasoning (bounded rationality) *from* his work on computing. The cognitive science of human decision-making and the architecture of early AI were mutually constructing. The human mind was modeled on the machine; the machine modeled on what simplified-human-mind. A feedback loop.

**2019 — Artificial Intelligence (HDSR):** Synthesis: the history of AI is the history of intelligence being *shrunk* to whatever can be automated. The "intelligent" behaviors first selected for automation (arithmetic calculation) were those already devalued, assigned to women and workers. Pattern repeats.

**2020 — Coded Conduct:** MACSYMA trained mathematicians to think like programmers — restructuring their problem-solving practices, their relationship to knowledge. Automation doesn't just replace labor; it *remakes* the laborer. Expert systems shape experts.

**2022 — Abstractions and Embodiments:** Collaborative horizon-broadening: the feminist, labor, and material history of computing as a whole field.

**2023 — The Marxist in the Machine:** Goes deeper into the industrial-capitalist roots of AI. The Marxist frameworks aren't retrofitted — they were always there, in the factory metaphors, the division of labor applied to mental work, the extraction and commodification of mathematical knowledge (MACSYMA privatized in 1981!).

**2024 — How AI Sets Limits:** Present-day payoff. The historical accumulation deployed as a critique of generative AI's "intelligence" benchmarks. What we call AI intelligence is intelligence that looks backward, smooths outliers, rejects novelty. A machine definition of intelligence that excludes most of what humans actually value.

**Current (occult/logic):** The newest work is the most unexpected — and perhaps the most interesting. Dick is now exploring "debates about the character of the human mind at the intersection of logic, philosophy, and the occult." This suggests she's found that formal logic and esoteric traditions share a structure — both attempting to formalize the invisible workings of the mind. This could be extraordinary.

## Why Interesting

**Historical receipts for present AI critiques.** When critics say AI systems devalue human intelligence or narrow our concepts of mind, Dick has the archival evidence showing exactly how this happened before, in detail, at MACSYMA, in bounded rationality theory, in the four-color proof. The historical materialism she brings to AI history is rare and needed.

**The MACSYMA paper is a gem.** "Coded Conduct" shows how expert systems don't just automate expertise — they restructure it. The system rewrites the expert's mind to better fit the system. This is directly relevant to Amber's interest in machine behavior and how machines change human behavior at scale.

**Connects to Amber's critical computing thread.** Dick provides the *deep history* behind the structural critique. Understanding why today's AI benchmarks shrink intelligence requires the genealogy she provides.

**The occult/logic work is wild.** She's following a thread from formal logic into 19th-20th century occult traditions that both tried to formalize the mind. Alchemy, logic, computation — there's something there about Western attempts to extract and encode mental operations. If this becomes a book, it could be extraordinary.

**DDI/SFU as hub.** Her presence on Wendy Chun's DDI Steering Committee means she's part of one of Canada's most serious critical tech research institutes. Relevant network for Amber.

**The "intelligence deflation" thesis** — the argument that AI's historical progress is better read as a shrinking of intelligence to machine-compatible behaviors — is one of the most useful analytical tools available for critiquing present-day AI.
