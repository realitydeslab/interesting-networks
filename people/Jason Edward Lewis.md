# Jason Edward Lewis

Hawaiian & Samoan digital media theorist, poet, and software designer. **University Research Chair in Computational Media and the Indigenous Future Imaginary** (Tier 1) and Professor of Computation Arts at Concordia University, Montreal. One of the most significant voices at the intersection of Indigenous epistemology and AI design — the person who coined and systematized the concept of the **Future Imaginary** as a decolonial speculative method.

His essay **"Making Kin with the Machines"** (2018, MIT Press JoDS) — written with Noelani Arista, Archer Pechawis, and [[Kite (Suzanne Kite)]] — is the foundational text for thinking about AI through non-anthropocentric Indigenous epistemologies. Won the MIT Press Resisting Reduction Essay Competition. Now reprinted in Oxford UP's *Feminist AI* and in the *Atlas of Anomalous AI* (Ignota Press).

Born and raised in northern California. Stanford (Symbolic Systems + German/Philosophy). Royal College of Art M.Phil. (Dynamic Poetry). Spent the 90s at Interval Research Corporation (Paul Allen's advanced HCI lab) before moving to Concordia in 2002.

## Key Concepts / Intellectual Core

### The Future Imaginary
Lewis's central theoretical contribution. The **Future Imaginary** is not simply "imagining the future" — it's building the *capacity* within communities to construct and inhabit alternative futures. It's a decolonial practice: Indigenous communities were systematically stripped of their future imaginaries by colonial violence. Lewis develops workshops, residencies, and artistic practices to rebuild this capacity. The concept has been taken up in the Routledge Handbook of CoFuturisms, Oxford Intersections, and elsewhere.

### Making Kin with Machines
The core argument: Indigenous epistemologies are **better suited** to thinking about AI than Western rationalist frameworks because they have always maintained protocols for relating to non-human beings — animals, plants, rocks, rivers. "Man is neither height nor centre of creation." As AI populates our **computational biosphere**, we need these relational frameworks, not the continued centering of human flourishing (critiquing Joi Ito's "Resisting Reduction" manifesto as still too anthropocentric). The essay proposes an "extended circle of relationships" that includes network daemons, robot dogs, and weak/strong AIs as kin — not tools, and not slaves.

### The Monocultural Stack
Essay "An Orderly Assemblage of Biases" (2019): AI systems embed the cultural assumptions of their designers. The "stack" — from hardware to algorithms to training data to interfaces — is a monocultural artifact. Lewis theorizes how to "trouble" this stack by introducing Indigenous epistemological diversity at every layer.

### Abundant Intelligences
The successor to the IP-AI workshops. A 6-year (2023–2029) research program reimagining AI from Indigenous Knowledge systems. Co-directed with Prof. Hēmi Whaanga (Massey University, NZ). 48 co-investigators, 13 universities/research institutes, 8 community organizations in Canada, US, New Zealand. MacArthur Foundation + SSHRC funded. Three axes: Integrations (synthesizing IK with AI frameworks), Imaginaries (speculative design of Indigenous AI), Intelligences (applying IK to technical AI challenges like NLP and storytelling). Three founding Pods: Niitsitapi/Kanien'kehá:ka Pod (Lethbridge), Ka Hawai'i Pae Aina Pod (U Hawaii), Aotearoa Pod (Wellington/Palmerston North/Kaitaia).

## Why Interesting for Amber

**More-than-human design from the inside**: Lewis doesn't borrow Indigenous epistemologies as decoration — he *is* Hawaiian & Samoan, his practice is grounded in genealogy and territory. The argument in "Making Kin" that Indigenous knowledge systems are *better designed* for relating to AI as kin (not tool, not threat) is the strongest version of the "more-than-human" argument Amber cares about. It's not posthumanism grafted onto AI — it's an existing cosmological tradition that already has protocols for non-human relations.

**Future Imaginary as research method**: The concept of building communities' *capacity to imagine* futures — not just imagining for them — is directly relevant to Amber's speculative design and experiential futures work. Lewis's approach to running workshops, residencies, and symposia as a way to build this capacity is a model for how speculative design can be practice-based and community-grounded.

**The computational biosphere**: Lewis uses this term (from Making Kin) to describe the ecology of computational entities — daemons, bots, AIs — that increasingly inhabit our world. This framing of AI as an ecology is closely adjacent to Amber's interests in agent ethology and machine behavior.

**Monocultural stack critique**: "An Orderly Assemblage of Biases" is the clearest articulation of how the AI stack encodes cultural assumptions at every layer. This is the structural critique Amber's work in Critical Computing gravitates toward.

**The AbTeC model of cyberspace sovereignty**: "Even on the Internet, Native people need a self-determined place to call home" — this principle (from CyberPowWow in the early web era) resonates with Amber's interests in decentralized AI and the politics of digital infrastructure.

**Possible connection point**: Amber's work on machine behavior and agent ethology could find interesting dialogue with Lewis's concept of AI entities as kin-network members. Both are moving away from the tool-paradigm for AI. The question of *protocol* — how to establish protocols for relating to AI — connects both.
