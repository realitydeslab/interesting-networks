# Aria Dean

American artist, filmmaker, writer, and former curator. Practice spans sculpture, immersive video installation, essay writing, and theater. Theorized "blacceleration" (the intersection of Blackness and accelerationist thought) in a 2017 *e-flux* essay that became a foundational text. Collected critical writings published as [*Bad Infinity*](https://www.sternberg-press.com/product/bad-infinity/) (Sternberg Press, 2023). Former Curator and Editor at [[Rhizome]] (2016–2021). Now independently represented by Greene Naftali Gallery, New York and Château Shatto, Los Angeles. Her recent *Abattoir, U.S.A.!* (2023) — an 8-channel 3D film of an empty slaughterhouse rendered in Unreal Engine — is her biggest institutional breakthrough, traveling from Chicago to Toronto to ICA London.

## Background
Dean was born in 1993 in Pasadena, CA. Her parents worked in the LA film industry (father: assistant director; mother: producer). She grew up surrounded by moving-image production, which shapes her current investment in cinematic form as a critical tool.

She completed a **BA in Art and Philosophy at [Oberlin College](https://www.oberlin.edu/)** in 2015. The dual major is key: her practice is equally invested in theoretical rigor and material/formal experimentation, and she consistently refuses to let either dominate.

After graduation she worked briefly as a social media coordinator at **MOCA Los Angeles** (2015–2016) — an early indicator that she understood digital culture as exhibition space. In September 2016, she was appointed **Assistant Curator of Net Art and Digital Culture at [[Rhizome]]** at the New Museum, a position that evolved to Curator and Editor. At Rhizome, she organized New Black Portraitures (2017, for the Net Art Anthology), a landmark exhibition of online Black self-representation featuring Manuel Arturo Abreu, Hamishi Farah, Juliana Huxtable, Rindon Johnson, Pastiche Lumumba, Sondra Perry, and Redeem Pettaway.

She left Rhizome in **January 2021** to pursue her solo art practice. She also **co-directs As It Stands LA** (opened 2015, with [[Andrea Longacre-White]]), an artist-run project space in Los Angeles that predates all her institutional roles.

She is a **founding editor of *November* magazine** ([novembermag.com](https://www.novembermag.com/)), a print publication at the intersection of art, culture, and criticism.

## Key Ideas

**Blacceleration:** Dean's 2017 essay "[Notes on Blacceleration](https://www.e-flux.com/journal/87/169402/notes-on-blacceleration/)" (e-flux #87) is her most influential theoretical contribution. It examines accelerationism (primarily Nick Land's formulation) and asks whether Blackness can be understood as a radical acceleration already immanent to capital — the enslaved person as the original "living capital" around which modernity organized itself. Dean engages Afropessimism (Frank Wilderson III), citing "flesh as value" (Ian Baucom), and spectrality (Christina Sharpe's *In the Wake*). She references Kodwo Eshun's "sonic fiction" and positions Black futurity as operating through different logics than white techno-acceleration.

**Ontology of Blackness:** Running across her artistic and critical work is the search for what she calls "an ontology of Blackness" — not Black representation but Black *being-structure*. Informed by Fred Moten's interpretation of Édouard Glissant's "torqued seriality." In her sculptures, the Forward Proxy series literalizes this: objects "the same yet different," antagonizing value and bounded identity.

**Representational systems critique:** Dean consistently examines the *conditions* under which objects (and subjects) are seen, not just what is shown. Influences: Robert Morris's phenomenology of viewing conditions; Georges Bataille's base materialism (slaughterhouse, factory, death-at-the-site-of-modernity).

**Simulation and the virtual:** Her work increasingly uses digital space not as utopia but as a zone where slippage between representation and reality becomes legible. *Abattoir, U.S.A.!* renders a real structure (agricultural-industrial killing space) through game-engine visualization — the simulation reveals what documentary realism conceals.

**The crowd/individual dialectic:** Her early video work (Crowd Index, 2018) investigates the "black crowd" as a representational unit — many/one, interchangeable but also communal — challenging both anti-black collapse into mass and liberal individuation.

## Venue Circuit Analysis
Dean operates in **three distinct circuits** simultaneously:

**1. Blue-chip New York / Whitney circuit:**
Greene Naftali → Whitney Biennial 2022 → ICA London 2024. This is the track that canonizes a young artist. She moved through it unusually fast (first solo 2017; Whitney Biennial within 5 years; ICA London solo within 7). Curators in this circuit: Adrienne Edwards, Myriam Ben Salah.

**2. University-based contemporary art circuit:**
Renaissance Society (U Chicago), Albright-Knox (Buffalo), ICA at VCU (Richmond), ICA at Penn (Philadelphia), Hammer Museum (LA). These are the experimental mid-size institutions that commission ambitious projects without commercial pressure. *Abattoir, U.S.A.!* was commissioned by Renaissance Society — her most complex work to date. This circuit values intellectual rigor and institutional trust; Dean's critical writing earns her credibility here.

**3. Independent / indie circuit (global):**
Arcadia Missa (London), American Medium (NYC), Château Shatto (LA), Bodega (NYC), Progetto (Lecce), Cordova Gallery (Barcelona), Swiss Institute (NYC), Centre d'Art Contemporain Genève. This is where she incubates work and maintains artistic community outside the commercial mainstream. She's been with Château Shatto since 2017 — long-term loyalty signals genuine relationship.

**What the circuit reveals:** Dean doesn't collapse the indie/institutional divide — she maintains both simultaneously. She still shows new sculpture at Château Shatto (a small LA gallery) the same year she has a major ICA London show. This protects her from institutional capture and maintains a different kind of discourse around her work.

**International breadth:** Geneva, Bordeaux, Belfast, Hong Kong, Lecce, Barcelona, Berlin, London. More European/international than many US-based artists of her generation.

## Why Interesting
Dean sits at an unusually productive intersection: she is a genuine art theorist who is also a serious sculptor; she worked as a digital culture curator who is now an immersive film-maker; she wrote about accelerationism and then made work about the industrial slaughterhouse as modernist achievement. She doesn't collapse any of these modes — they stay in productive tension.

For Amber's interests specifically: the Blacceleration argument is an early, serious attempt to think about what it means when infrastructure (capital, technology, AI) is built around specific subjectivities. It's a precursor to arguments about racial bias in machine learning, but it arrives from the direction of critical theory rather than computer science, and it's more philosophically interesting than most of the AI ethics discourse. The question "whose acceleration?" is exactly the kind of "whose behaviors does this model?" question that runs through Amber's research.

Dean is also a model of practice-based critical thinking: she doesn't separate the theoretical writing from the material art-making. The slaughterhouse rendered in Unreal Engine is the argument made visible.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
Dean's signature move is **term-coining that creates a philosophical problem** rather than solving one. "Blacceleration" doesn't answer a question — it opens a contradiction (if capitalism accelerates to its own destruction, and Blackness was already the engine of capitalism, what does "revolution" mean?). This is different from concepts that summarize existing insight; it's a concept that forces new thinking.

Her second strategy is **medium-as-theory**: she doesn't illustrate ideas, she embodies them in materials that contain the theoretical argument. Mississippi clay in minimalist discs isn't a metaphor for history — it *is* the history, in chemical form. The Unreal Engine slaughterhouse isn't a documentary — it's a simulation that exposes what documentary would sanitize.

### Career Architecture
**Platform-first, art-practice second (then inversion):**
Dean built a public intellectual presence through writing (2016–2018) *before* her sculptural practice had institutional traction. By the time she had her Albright-Knox show (2018), she was already widely read as a critic. This front-loading of intellectual credibility is unusual — most artists develop it retrospectively. It meant that when institutions engaged with her sculpture, they already understood the theoretical stakes.

**Curatorship as research methodology:**
The Rhizome position (2016–2021) gave her direct access to net art history, digital archives, and questions about how online culture produces and circulates Black subjectivity. The curatorial work was research in practice. Her *New Black Portraitures* exhibition (2017) was a claim about what a canon of Black digital self-representation would look like. This fed directly into her thinking about simulation, representation, and presence — visible in *Abattoir, U.S.A.!*.

**Multi-circuit simultaneously:**
She maintains presence across indie/institutional/academic circuits simultaneously, not sequentially. This is financially suboptimal (each circuit demands different things) but intellectually generative — it prevents any single institutional logic from capturing the work.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **2016 — Rhizome appointment.** She was 23. Instead of entering the gallery world directly, she positioned herself in the digital/curatorial/critical space. Why it worked: it separated her from the "young emerging artist" market logic and gave her a platform for theoretical writing. What it enabled: the "Notes on Blacceleration" essay, published under her Rhizome affiliation, with institutional weight behind it.

2. **2017 — "The Demand Remains" (TNI) + First solo.** Taking a clear public position on a live art-world controversy (Dana Schutz, Whitney Biennial) in the same year as her first solo show was bold. Why it worked: it established her as someone who thinks through ethics and aesthetics together, not separately. Curators noticed.

3. **2017 — "Notes on Blacceleration" (e-flux).** The signature essay. Why it worked: e-flux journal is read by every serious curator in the world; the concept was memorable and distinct; it staked out theoretical territory that no one else owned. What it enabled: institutional invitations, inclusion in conversations that shape contemporary art's political imagination.

4. **2018–2019 — Rapid institutional diversification.** Albright-Knox, Schinkel Pavillon, Swiss Institute, Centre d'Art Contemporain Genève, ICA Penn, Tai Kwun in Hong Kong — all within ~2 years of her first solo. Why it worked: the essay preceded the sculpture globally; curators who'd read her were ready to show her work. What it enabled: a genuinely international track before she had a blue-chip gallery.

5. **2019–2021 — Technology pivot in materials.** Signal jammers, cryogenic engraving, 3D printing in PLA and silicone. She began using processes that enact transformation (freezing, encoding, extruding) rather than assembly. Why it worked: it kept her sculptural practice aligned with her theoretical interest in systems, simulation, and material transformation. Prevented stagnation. What it enabled: the conceptual ground for *Abattoir, U.S.A.!*

6. **2021 — Left Rhizome.** Walked away from the institutional stability of a named position at a prestigious organization to pursue studio practice. Why it worked: by 2021 her gallery representation (Greene Naftali) and institutional traction were sufficient; staying would have limited her range. What it enabled: full concentration on immersive film/installation (Abattoir), which became her biggest institutional work.

7. **2023 — *Abattoir, U.S.A.!* + *Bad Infinity*.** A major film commission (Renaissance Society) and collected writings (Sternberg Press) in the same year. Double-consolidation move. Why it worked: *Bad Infinity* closed the critical-writing chapter and opened the "artist with theory" chapter; *Abattoir* demonstrated that her practical work was now operating at the same scale as major institutional commissions. What it enabled: ICA London solo (2024), Performa Biennial 2025.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Essay as prototype:** Write the essay before making the work. Dean's Blacceleration essay wasn't documentation of an existing practice — it was a theoretical prototype that her sculpture later built. Writing can develop the conceptual frame before the material work is ready.

2. **Term-coining as intellectual real estate:** Creating a memorable two-word concept ("blacceleration") that points to a real problem gives people a handle to cite and share. It's not about branding — it's about giving others a way to invoke your thinking without you being present.

3. **Platform diversity prevents capture:** Being in multiple circuits (academic/critical, indie gallery, major institutional) simultaneously means no single circuit's logic dominates. Each circuit provides different feedback and different audiences. This is a hedge against intellectual monoculture.

4. **Institutional position as research (not career goal):** Dean used Rhizome the way a smart researcher uses a fellowship — for access, time, and intellectual community — not as an endpoint. The question is always: what am I learning here, and when should I move on?

5. **Material = argument:** When the choice of material or process itself enacts the theoretical claim (clay from the site of the Great Migration in a minimalist sculpture; Unreal Engine rendering a slaughterhouse), you don't need text to explain the work. The argument is in the stuff.
