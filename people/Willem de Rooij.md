# Willem de Rooij

Dutch conceptual artist and educator based in Berlin. Works across installation, film, textile, and publication. His practice is forensic: he excavates colonial visual histories through meticulous formal arrangements — confronting Dutch bird paintings with Hawaiian featherwork, projecting photographs of colonial-era Suriname, filming landfills at dawn. Since 2006, Professor of Fine Arts at [Städelschule](https://www.staedelschule.de/en/) Frankfurt. Co-founded [[BPA// Berlin Program for Artists]] (2016) with [[Angela Bulloch]] and [[Simon Denny]]. Member of the [Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences (KNAW)](https://www.knaw.nl/en). His work is in MoMA, Centre Pompidou, Stedelijk, and Hamburger Bahnhof.

## Background
Born 1969 in Beverwijk, Netherlands. Studied art history at the [University of Amsterdam](https://www.uva.nl/en) (1989–1990), then fine art at the [Gerrit Rietveld Academie](https://www.gerritrietveldacademie.nl/en/) (1990–1995) and the [Rijksakademie](https://www.rijksakademie.nl/en/) (1997–1998) in Amsterdam.

From **1994 to 2006**, he worked in collaboration with **Jeroen de Rijke** (1970–2006) as **De Rijke / De Rooij** — a partnership that defined his early career with concentrated short 16mm and 35mm films probing representation, time, and institutional politics. The partnership ended with de Rijke's sudden death in Takoradi, Ghana, in 2006.

Since then, De Rooij has operated as a solo artist while building an increasingly dense institutional footprint: professorship at Städelschule, co-founding BPA//, KNAW membership, and advising at Rijksakademie. He is simultaneously an artist, educator, archivist, and infrastructure-builder — rare in the contemporary art world.

**Key intellectual move:** De Rooij uses the gallery and museum as a research instrument. His installations are not illustrative — they *are* the argument. He produces accompanying scholarly publications (catalogue raisonnés, monographs, critical essays) that go further than most academic art historians.

## Domains
- **Colonial visual history** — how Dutch art historically encoded and legitimized colonial violence and ideology
- **Politics of display** — how museums, galleries, and exhibitions shape what can be seen and known
- **Textile as cultural material** — working with textiles across multiple traditions (Azeri rugs, Dutch wax prints, Ghanaian Adinkra, Navajo patterns, handwoven German textiles)
- **Institutional politics and art education** — BPA//, Städelschule, Rijksakademie
- **Montage and appropriation** — combining existing objects and works to produce new meanings through juxtaposition
- **Transcultural exchange** — Ghana exchange program, Koyo Kouoh collaboration, Raw Material Company Dakar

## Key Works

### Solo (post-2006)
- **[Valkenburg](https://www.centraalmuseum.nl/en/now/exhibitions/valkenburg-willem-de-rooij)** (2025) — Centraal Museum Utrecht. Nearly 20 years of research. 30 works by 18th-century painter Dirk Valkenburg contextualised: hunting still lifes, portraits of Dutch elites, and idealised depictions of Indigenous and enslaved people on Surinamese sugar plantations. Accompanied by first catalogue raisonné of Valkenburg's oeuvre, co-edited with historian [Karwan Fatah-Black](https://www.universiteitleiden.nl/en/staffmembers/karwan-fatah-black). Critical essays from scholars in art history, queer studies, and postcolonial studies.
- **[Pierre Verger in Suriname](https://www.portikus.de/en/exhibitions/224_pierre_verger_in_suriname)** (2020) — Originally at Portikus Frankfurt, then sonsbeek 2021 Arnhem. Projects ethnographer [Pierre Verger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Verger)'s 1948 Suriname photographs onto a screen that briefly becomes a mirror at each transition. Viewers alternately see Suriname through colonial lens and see themselves reflected.
- **Proposal for the Memorialization of 'Asoziale' and 'Berufsverbrecher'** (2018) — Aims to give two groups underrepresented in German memorial culture a place at the former NS concentration camp in Dachau.
- **Residual** (2012–2022) — Brought Jacob van Ruisdael's *View of Bentheim Castle from the Northwest* (c.1655) into the very castle it depicts. Led to the [Ruisdael Stipendium](http://www.kunstwegen.org/index.php?id=147) (2013–2020): annual support for emerging artists with funding, publication, and exhibition.
- **Blue to Black / Black to Blue** (2012–2015) — In collaboration with curator [[Koyo Kouoh]]. Two abstract wax prints produced with industrial printers in Tema, Ghana, and batik craftspeople in Yogyakarta, Indonesia. Reframes colonial textile histories.
- **Character is Fate** (2015) — Year-long installation at [FKAWDW](https://www.fkawdw.nl/en/our_program/exhibitions/character_is_fate) Rotterdam, centered on an 1911 horoscope that Piet Mondrian commissioned for himself. Fragile document exposed to daylight for only a few minutes each day.
- **[The Impassioned No](https://www.leconsortium.fr/fr/impassioned-no)** (2015) — Le Consortium, Dijon. Included installation of his collection of 1980s sportswear by Sino-Dutch designer [Fong Leng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fong_Leng).
- **[Intolerance](http://www.intolerance-berlin.de/en/installation.html)** (2010) — Neue Nationalgalerie, Berlin. Confronted 18 works by Dutch bird-painter Melchior d'Hondecoeter (1636–1695) with 18th-century Hawaiian featherwork. Accompanied by major scholarly publication including first monograph on d'Hondecoeter and a catalogue raisonné of Hawaiian featherwork by [Adrienne Kaeppler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrienne_L._Kaeppler).

### De Rijke / De Rooij (with Jeroen de Rijke, 1994–2006)
- **Mandarin Ducks** (2005) — 16mm film. Dutch actors perform bourgeois tensions through visual clichés. Shown at Netherlands Pavilion, Venice Biennale 2005. Later installed at Stedelijk Museum with objects from the collection.
- **Bantar Gebang** (2000) — Records dawn breaking over one of the world's largest landfills, outside Jakarta, Indonesia.
- **Of Three Men** (1998) — Depicts shifting light in Amsterdam's Fatih Mosque (a former Catholic church). Responded to the rise of Islamophobia and right-wing populism.
- **The Point of Departure** (2002) — 35mm film scanning a 19th-century Shirwan rug from Azerbaijan.
- **Bouquets** (2002–2010) — Series of floral sculptures.
- **Forever and Ever** (1995) — Deconstructed narrative in four parts, filmed during academic residency at the [Film and Television Institute of India](https://www.ftii.ac.in/).

### Long-term collaboration
- **Handweberei Henni Jaensch-Zeymer** (2009–ongoing) — Long-term collaboration with a weaving workshop, producing rare abstract textile objects.

## Venue Circuit Analysis
De Rooij moves across **three primary circuits**, each serving a different function:

### 1. German/Dutch institutional art world
Neue Nationalgalerie, Städelschule, MMK Frankfurt, KW Berlin, Portikus, MAMbo, Stedelijk Museum, Rijksakademie, De Ateliers. This is his home circuit — where he has institutional authority and teaches the next generation.

### 2. Global biennials and postcolonial venues
Venice Biennale, Jakarta Biennale, Shanghai Biennale, Athens Biennale, steirischer herbst, EVA International Limerick, sonsbeek Arnhem, Raw Material Company Dakar, BDL Museum Mumbai, John Hansard Gallery Nottingham. Deliberately Southern/Eastern to test work against the communities colonial Dutch history actually affects.

### 3. US and international private museum circuit
Hammer Museum LA, The Jewish Museum NY, Petzel Gallery NY, Regen Projects LA, ICA London, Le Consortium Dijon, Secession Vienna, Kunsthalle Zürich. The established contemporary art infrastructure.

**The pattern:** Unlike most European artists who show primarily in Europe, De Rooij deliberately situates his work in the geographic contexts his research concerns — Ghana, Suriname, Indonesia, Brazil. He builds the scholarly apparatus (catalogue raisonnés, monographs) *alongside* the artwork, at a level that commands respect from academic art historians.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
De Rooij operates via what could be called **forensic formalism**: he takes objects from the past (paintings, rugs, photographs, horoscopes) and assembles them in precise juxtapositions that make visible what was previously invisible or naturalized. The method is: find the artifact that *is* the ideology, present it in the context of what it excluded or erased, let the juxtaposition do the theoretical work.

He does not caption or explain — the formal arrangement *is* the argument. This is sophisticated: it respects viewers enough to let them work, and it refuses the didactic pitfalls of "colonial art education."

**Framework multiplication:** Colonial Dutch painting × Hawaiian featherwork → *Intolerance*. Dutch masters × Surinamese photography × mirror → *Pierre Verger*. Mondrian horoscope × conservation anxiety × time → *Character is Fate*.

**Scholarly apparatus as medium:** He doesn't just make the art, he produces the first catalogue raisonné of the artist he's examining — the *Intolerance* publication was a genuine academic contribution to Melchior d'Hondecoeter scholarship. The *Valkenburg* publication is the first scholarly account of an 18th-century Dutch colonial painter. This is extraordinary: the artwork *creates* the scholarship that it needs to be understood.

### Career Architecture
**Field declaration:** De Rooij has not "declared a field" in a discursive sense, but his practice *constitutes* a field: Dutch colonial visual history as a site of contemporary artistic intervention. He owns this territory.

**Coalition papers:** His publications function like coalition papers — bringing together Adrienne Kaeppler (Smithsonian anthropologist), Karin Amatmoekrim (novelist), Gloria Wekker (gender studies), Karwan Fatah-Black (historian) — into a single scholarly/artistic object. This builds credibility across disciplines simultaneously.

**Platform building:** BPA// + Städelschule + Rijksakademie advisory + De Ateliers mentorship = he has touched every major Dutch/German art education institution. He is the infrastructure.

**Strategic patience:** 20 years on *Valkenburg*. This is not a failing — it's a strategy. No one can dispute his authority on Valkenburg because there was no other authority to dispute. He created the field and then filled it.

### Methodology Signature
**Art-science bridging:** His collaborations with Adrienne Kaeppler, Karwan Fatah-Black, Gloria Wekker etc. position his work between art and scholarship. He initiates research in global art history and visual anthropology (his Wikipedia bio literally says this).

**Archive as medium:** His primary material is archives — the colonial visual archive, the Mondrian archive, the Verger photographic archive. He doesn't just reference these; he makes them the object of the installation.

**Textile as method:** The 2009-ongoing collaboration with Handweberei Henni Jaensch-Zeymer, and the Ghana/Indonesia wax print project, are not decorative choices. Textile is the material trace of colonial trade, extraction, and cultural imposition. He works *with* the medium that is historically most implicated.

### Key Moves (Chronological)
1. **De Rijke / De Rooij (1994–2006):** Building a dual-artist practice gave him the collaborative muscle and the institutional platform (Venice Biennale) that solo career would have taken much longer to achieve.
2. **Städelschule professorship (2006):** Moved to Frankfurt the year de Rijke died. Turned grief/transition into institutional stability. Now has 20 years of students who are shaped by his approach.
3. **Intolerance (2010):** The first exhibition that synthesized art + scholarship at the level of a major academic monograph. Established his method. Got him into Neue Nationalgalerie.
4. **Ghana exchange program at Städelschule (2014):** Gave institutional weight to his postcolonial interests. Built a formal north-south pipeline in art education.
5. **BPA// co-founding (2016):** Alongside KNAW membership. Double legitimation: artist-run infrastructure + national academy. Both in the same year — strategic.
6. **Valkenburg (2025):** The culmination of 20 years of patient colonial-historical research. Definitive statement. By this point, no critic can ignore him or pigeonhole him.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **The catalogue raisonné as artistic medium.** If Amber's research involves machine behavior or agent ethology, what would be the equivalent of a *complete scholarly account* of a specific AI system or behavioral pattern, presented as both artwork and scholarship? The publication-as-medium strategy is under-explored in tech-art.

2. **Patience as competitive advantage.** In a field that rewards novelty and speed, 20-year projects become impossible to ignore. Amber is early-career — building a long-horizon project now, returning to it every few years, could create similar authority.

3. **Coalition-building through publications.** De Rooij's publications bring together scholars from art history, anthropology, postcolonial studies — not as endorsements but as genuine intellectual co-production. Amber's edited volumes, if any, should bring in similarly unexpected collaborators (biologists, philosophers, sociologists).

4. **Artist-run infrastructure as career engine.** BPA// gives De Rooij a reason to be in Berlin, access to emerging talent, and institutional visibility — at minimal cost. The Ruisdael Stipendium gave him curatorial authority. Amber already does this with HoloKit and Reality Design Lab — the lesson is to formalize it, institutionalize it, give it a name and a structure.

5. **Venue selection as argument.** De Rooij shows in Jakarta, Dakar, Mumbai, and Suriname-related contexts — not just for diversity optics but because these are the communities his work concerns. Amber's MR/HCI work could similarly choose venues in the Global South where the technology's implications are most acute.
