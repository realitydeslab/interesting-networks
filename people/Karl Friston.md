# Karl Friston

British neuroscientist and mathematical theoretician. Architect of the Free Energy Principle — the most ambitious unified theory in neuroscience claiming all living systems minimize surprise. One of the most highly cited living scientists (h-index 291). His statistical parametric mapping (SPM) software is used in over 90% of brain imaging studies worldwide. Bridges neuroscience, artificial intelligence, and fundamental questions about life itself.

## Background
- **Born 1959, York, England.** Attended Ellesmere Port Grammar School (later Whitby Comprehensive) 1970-1977.

- **Scientific advisor to numerous industry groups** — bridges academic theory and practical applications.

## Domains
### Statistical Parametric Mapping (SPM)
Revolutionary method for analyzing brain imaging data using general linear models and random field theory. Created with Keith Worsley in the 1990s. **Over 90% of neuroimaging papers use SPM** — possibly the most widely adopted methodology in neuroscience history.

### Free Energy Principle & Active Inference
**The grand unified theory.** Claims all living systems (from bacteria to brains) minimize variational free energy — a quantity that bounds the surprise of sensory observations. This connects:
- **Perception** (inferring causes of sensations)
- **Action** (changing sensations to match predictions) 
- **Learning** (updating internal models)
- **Life itself** (maintaining structural integrity against entropy)

Active Inference extends this to AI systems — agents that act to minimize expected free energy, balancing exploration and exploitation.

### Dynamic Causal Modelling (DCM)
Method for inferring causal architecture of distributed systems like the brain. Uses Bayesian model comparison to determine how brain regions interact. Invented 2003, now standard in neuroimaging.

### Computational Psychiatry
The dysconnection hypothesis: psychiatric disorders arise from abnormal synaptic plasticity leading to failures of functional integration. Applied to schizophrenia, autism, depression using variational Bayesian methods.
