# Claire Colebrook

**The philosopher who refuses to let posthumanism off the hook.** Claire Mary Colebrook (born 1965, Australia) is one of the most rigorous thinkers of extinction, posthumanism, and feminist critical theory working today. Cecile Parrish Memorial Chair of English Literature at **Monash University** (newly returned to Australia), previously the Edwin Erle Sparks Professor of English, Philosophy, and Women's, Gender, and Sexuality Studies at **Penn State** for over a decade. Her work tracks what happens when "life" becomes uncoupled from the human — and insists that this trajectory ends not in warm cyborg celebration but in *extinction* as a philosophically live category.

Co-editor (with [[Tom Cohen]]) of **Critical Climate Change** and **CCC2 Irreversibility** at [[Open Humanities Press]] — the premier academic publishing infrastructure for extinction thinking. Co-editor (with Jami Weinstein and Myra Hird) of **Critical Life Studies** at Columbia University Press. OHP editorial board member alongside Haraway, Hayles, Latour, and Harman.

Won the **inaugural Hugh J. Silverman Book Prize in Philosophy and Literature** (2023) for *Who Would You Kill to Save the World?* — her most accessible, polemical work, on postapocalyptic cinema as humanist-colonialist fantasy.

## Background

Born 1965 in Australia. **BA at the University of Melbourne** (1987), **BLett at Australian National University** (1989), then crossed to Edinburgh for her **PhD** (1993). The Edinburgh formation matters: it's where deconstruction, literary theory, and continental philosophy were practiced with unusual rigour in the British context. Her PhD supervisor position embedded her in that late-80s/early-90s moment when de Man's rhetorical reading was still live theory, before it became archival.

Taught at the **University of Edinburgh** in the 1990s–2000s — the Cambridge search results show her Edinburgh affiliation for her chapter in *Deleuze and Queer Theory* (Edinburgh UP, 2009). British Academy grants in 2004–2008 confirm UK-based institutional presence during this period: Overseas Conference Awards, Carnegie Trust, British Academy Small Grants, Huntington Library Fellowship, AHRC Leave Scheme.

Distinguished Visiting Professor at the **Friedrich Schlegel Graduate School, Free University Berlin** (2010) — the de Man–Derrida–Lacoue-Labarthe tradition, German deconstruction axis.

Moved to **Penn State** (exact date unclear, probably ca. 2010–2012), where she held the Edwin Erle Sparks Professorship — one of the university's highest endowed chairs — with joint appointment across English, Philosophy, and Women's, Gender, and Sexuality Studies. 

Most recently returned to Australia as **Cecile Parrish Memorial Chair of English Literature at Monash University** — a named chair in the English Department. A homecoming: the first major Australian philosopher of posthumanism returning from US ascendancy.

## Intellectual Trajectory

**Phase 1: Deleuzian foundations (1997–2010).** After her Edinburgh PhD on Kant and representation, she spent a decade systematically building Deleuze scholarship in English: *Gilles Deleuze* (2002), *Understanding Deleuze* (2002), *Deleuze: A Guide for the Perplexed*, *Deleuze and the Meaning of Life* (2010). These are not merely popularizations — they interpret Deleuze through irony, literary history, and feminist theory. The edited volumes with Buchanan (*Deleuze and Feminist Theory*, 2000), Weinstein (*Deleuze and Gender*, 2009), Bell (*Deleuze and History*, 2008), and Braidotti (*Deleuze and Law*, 2009) built the infrastructure for Deleuzian studies in Anglophone academe.

**Phase 2: Irony and literary history (2002–2008).** Alongside the Deleuze work: *Irony in the Work of Philosophy* (2002), *Gender* (2003), *Irony* (2004), *Milton, Evil and Literary History* (2008). This thread — irony as a mode of philosophical thought, as what exceeds any system's claim to completion — runs through all her work. Irony is not a rhetorical device for Colebrook; it is the structure of a text that knows it cannot totalize itself. This is the de Man connection (literary rhetoric as undeconstructible).

**Phase 3: Extinction and posthumanism (2011–present).** The turn comes in *Theory and the Disappearing Future* (co-authored with Cohen and J. Hillis Miller, Routledge, 2011): what does theory look like when there might not be a future to theorize *toward*? Then the two OHP "Essays on Extinction" volumes: *Death of the PostHuman* (2014) and *Sex After Life* (2014). These do something philosophically difficult: they argue that posthumanism has its own logic of exhaustion. You cannot replace "human" with "life" and be done with it — "life" itself is a normative concept that does the same work as the humanism it was supposed to overcome. *Sex After Life* extends this: sexuality and queer theory have often assumed "life" as their ground; what if life is the problem?

*Twilight of the Anthropocene Idols* (OHP, 2016, with Cohen and Miller): applying de Man's rhetorical reading to Anthropocene discourse — how the very language of climate ("we," "our future," "tipping points") is anthropomorphic machinery that conceals what it cannot face.

*Who Would You Kill to Save the World?* (Nebraska, 2023): most accessible book, most polemical. Postapocalyptic cinema — *Mad Max*, *Interstellar*, *The Road*, etc. — as Western, capitalist, colonizing, white, heteronormative ideological production. "Saving the world" means saving *us*: the privileged, the legible, the human. The book combines extinction theory, anti-colonialism, and anti-Blackness critique. Won the inaugural **Hugh J. Silverman Book Prize in Philosophy and Literature** (2023).

**The consistent thread:** Colebrook's work is against consolation. Every move that philosophy makes to find a "beyond" — posthumanism, vitalism, queer futurity, Anthropocene resilience — gets subjected to a critique asking: whose beyond? Built on whose erasure? Does this beyond reproduce the same normative logic in new vocabulary? This is irony as philosophical practice: the refusal to let any position rest satisfied with itself.

## Why Interesting

Colebrook represents the rigorous, uncomfortable wing of posthumanism — the one that refuses to let the critique of humanism become a new humanism. Where [[Donna Haraway]] offers "make kin" and [[Rosi Braidotti]] offers affirmative posthumanism, Colebrook insists: *what if there's no affirmative outside?* What if "life," "the posthuman," "the Anthropocene" are all still running on the same anthropocentric logic they claim to overcome? This is a genuinely difficult position to hold, and she holds it with unusual consistency.

**The de Man connection** is crucial and underappreciated. Her rhetorical reading approach (from irony through the climate/extinction works) inherits de Man's insistence that texts cannot totalize themselves, that language always exceeds any ideological program, that reading is a practice of encountering the text's own resistance to its stated intentions. Applied to Anthropocene discourse, this is devastating: the very language of "our future," "we humans," "the planet needs us" is the anthropomorphism that extinction discourse claims to overcome.

**For Amber's territory:** The *Who Would You Kill to Save the World?* book connects directly to speculative fiction and postapocalyptic cinema — and does something actually politically useful: it shows why the "saving the world" narrative is built on colonial exclusions, anti-Blackness, and Western privilege. This is more rigorous than most art-world critiques of apocalyptic aesthetics. Her OHP books are **free** — *Death of the PostHuman* and *Sex After Life* are essential reading, download them.

**The institutional move back to Australia** is interesting strategically: it signals either a dissatisfaction with the US university or (more likely) an opportunity to build extinction studies infrastructure in a country facing ecological crisis in real time. Australia + extinction + Colebrook is a combination to watch.

## Research Taste Analysis

### What Makes Her Career Distinctive

**The uncomfortable position**: Colebrook consistently occupies the role of critical interruptor in posthumanist discourse. When everyone moves toward affirmation (make kin, multispecies solidarity, agential matter), she asks: whose affirmation? When a new theory (OOO, new materialism, speculative realism) claims to exit anthropocentrism, she asks: did it actually exit, or did it reproduce the same normative logic in new vocabulary? This is intellectually costly — it means constant opposition to the dominant move in your field — but it's also what makes her indispensable.

**De Manian thread throughout**: The irony and close reading approach isn't incidental to her climate work. De Man's central claim — that all texts have a constitutive blindness, that the performative and constative dimensions of language never align — is her primary theoretical tool from 1999 through 2023. She is the most consistent de Manian in contemporary humanities, which makes her work unusual in an era when de Man has been largely abandoned.

**Platform building before field consolidation**: The CCC series launched in 2011-2012, when "climate humanities" was barely a term. Colebrook and Cohen built the series before the field existed in its current form. This is the right moment to build platforms — before the field becomes institutionalized and competitive. For Amber: the analogous window for "critical AI art" or "more-than-human computing" may be right now.

**The race-theory integration (late career)**: *Who Would You Kill to Save the World?* (2023) integrates anti-Blackness and colonial critique into extinction studies. This is not "add diversity and stir" but a philosophical claim: the concept of "humanity worth saving" has always been racialized. The integration happens at the level of philosophical argument, not political gesture. This is the move that keeps her relevant as extinction studies risks becoming a white-dominated field.

### Career Architecture Lessons

1. **Name a problem before it has a name**: "Extinction studies" as a field she co-constituted, not just worked in
2. **Trilogy structure**: The Cohen-Colebrook-Miller books create cross-institutional legitimacy and are harder to dismiss than solo work
3. **Free OA at the right moment**: Publishing *Death of the PostHuman* and *Sex After Life* on OHP (free) when these were unusual choices meant they became widely cited quickly
4. **Named chair to named chair**: Edwin Erle Sparks (Penn State) → Cecile Parrish (Monash) — the named chair track; sustained scholarly focus over decades is rewarded

### For Critical Computing / AI Context

Colebrook's framework is directly applicable to AI criticism:
- **"Not Now" argument**: The Anthropogenic change happening now is not on a timescale that symbiosis-thinking, slow co-evolution thinking, or "AI for the good" can address. The urgency is different. Analogously: AI development is not on a timescale that allows for gradual accommodation.
- **Who "saves the world" in AI discourse?**: The postapocalyptic cinema critique maps directly onto AI safety discourse — "saving humanity from AI" assumes a version of humanity already encoded as worth saving (wealthy, Western, technologically literate). Extinction thinking meets AI safety.
- **Deconstruction of "alignment"**: The de Manian method applied to AI alignment discourse would ask: what does alignment *occlude*? What is the performative dimension of the alignment claim? What must it not say in order to say what it says?
