# John Akomfrah

Pioneering British-Ghanaian filmmaker, installation artist, and cultural theorist. Co-founder of the Black Audio Film Collective (1982–1998) and Smoking Dogs Films (1998–present). One of Britain's most important living artists — his multi-screen video installations combine archival footage, new film, music, and text to create immersive meditations on memory, diaspora, postcolonialism, climate change, and the sublime. Represented by Lisson Gallery, London. Elected Royal Academician (RA). Knighted 2023.

## Background
Born 1957 in Accra, Ghana. Both parents deeply political — his father was a cabinet member of Kwame Nkrumah's party; his mother met Malcolm X in Accra in 1965. The family left Ghana in 1966 after a coup endangered his mother's life; his father died partly as a result of the political struggle.

Grew up in London in the late 1960s–70s, immersed in post-punk music culture, political activism (attended Althusserian study groups as a teenager), and avant-garde cinema. As a student he smuggled himself into screenings of Tarkovsky and Fassbinder at the Paris Pullman Cinema on Fulham Palace Road. Was expelled from multiple FE colleges for organizing sit-ins as a student activist.

Studied at **Portsmouth Polytechnic** (where he met the future members of the Black Audio Film Collective: Lina Gopaul, Reece Auguiste, Trevor Mathison, Edward George, Avril Johnson, Claire Joseph). After graduation, the group relocated to Hackney, east London and co-founded the **[[Black Audio Film Collective]]** in 1982.

Later undertook postgraduate study at **Goldsmiths, University of London**.

## Key Works

### Major Installations (solo)

**[Listening All Night to the Rain](https://venicebiennale.britishcouncil.org/john-akomfrah-ra)** (2024)
British Pavilion, 60th Venice Biennale. Commissioned by the British Council. A new body of work.

**[Arcadia](https://www.lissongallery.com)** (2023) — five-channel
Reflects on "The Columbian Exchange" between the Americas, Afro-Eurasia, and Europe from the 1400s onward. Premiered at Sharjah Biennial 15.

**[Becoming Wind](https://www.lissongallery.com)** (2023) — five-channel
Allegorical representation of the Garden of Eden and its disappearance. Premiered at Sharjah Biennial 15.

**[Four Nocturnes](https://www.lissongallery.com)** (2019) — three-channel
Premiered at the Ghana Pavilion, 58th Venice Biennale. Reflects on the intertwined relationship between humanity's destruction of the natural world and itself.

**[Mimesis: African Soldier](https://www.lissongallery.com)** (2018) — multi-screen
Commissioned for the 14-18 Now UK-wide WW1 arts programme. Commemorates African and colonial participants who fought, served, and perished in the Great War.

**[Purple](https://hirshhorn.si.edu/exhibitions/john-akomfrah-purple/)** (2017) — six-channel, 1 hour
Immersive symphony addressing climate change. Surveys disappearing landscapes — Alaska, Greenland, Tahiti, the Marquesas Islands — weaving archival footage of coal mines, polluted lakes, and factory labor against a hypnotic score. The carpet covering floors and walls is deep purple — the color of mourning in Ghana. Originally commissioned by the Barbican. Co-commissioned by Bildmuseet Umeå, TBA21–Academy, ICA Boston, Museu Coleção Berardo, Garage Moscow. Co-acquired by Hirshhorn and ICA Boston 2021. Major exhibition at Hirshhorn 2022–24.

**[Precarity](https://www.lissongallery.com)** (2017)
Follows the life of forgotten New Orleans jazz trumpeter Charles "Buddy" Bolden.

**[Vertigo Sea](https://www.lissongallery.com)** (2015) — three-screen
Explores the Emersonian sublime of the sea. Fuses archival material, readings from classical sources, and newly shot footage. Highlights the cruelty of the whaling industry and juxtaposes it with generations of migrants crossing oceans. One of his most celebrated works. Premiered at Venice Biennale 2015 (*All the World's Futures*).

**[Mnemosyne](https://www.lissongallery.com)** (2010)
Exposes experiences of migrants in the UK, questioning Britain as a "promised land" by revealing economic hardship and casual racism.

**[The Unfinished Conversation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unfinished_Conversation)** (2012) — three-screen, 103 min
Moving portrait of cultural theorist Stuart Hall's life and work. Developed from 800 hours of Hall's archive (audio interviews, TV recordings). Marked with the music of Miles Davis. Commissioned by Autograph ABP. Premiered at Liverpool Biennial 2012; opened at Tate Britain October 2013.

**[Peripeteia](https://www.lissongallery.com)** (2012)
Imagined drama visualising the lives of individuals depicted in two 16th-century portraits by Albrecht Dürer.

**The Nine Muses** (2011)
Multilayered film interweaving archival footage of Black and Asian people arriving in Britain with elliptical shots of an anonymous figure alone in the Alaskan wilderness. Nine chapters, each named for a Greek muse. Sprinkled with quotations from Homer to T.S. Eliot.

### BAFC-era Key Works (see [[Black Audio Film Collective]])

**[Handsworth Songs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handsworth_Songs)** (1986) — 61 min
Directed by Akomfrah; produced by Lina Gopaul; music by Trevor Mathison; cinematography by Sebastian Shah. Examines the 1985 Handsworth and Brixton riots through layered newsreel, stills, and new footage. Commissioned by Channel 4. Won seven prizes internationally including the **John Grierson Award for Best Documentary** (BFI). Described by Kodwo Eshun in 2004 as "the most important and influential art film to emerge from England in the last twenty years." 100% on Rotten Tomatoes.

**Seven Songs for Malcolm X** (1993) — 52 min
Written by Akomfrah and Edward George; produced by Lina Gopaul; cinematography by Arthur Jafa. Draws on James Van Der Zee's photography and Paradjanov's *The Color of Pomegranates* for a probing, internationalist portrait of Malcolm X, very different from Spike Lee's simultaneous biopic. Premiered Toronto Festival of Festivals 1993.

**[The Last Angel of History](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Last_Angel_of_History)** (1995/96) — 45 min
Written by Edward George; directed by Akomfrah. Hybrid documentary/fiction exploring Afrofuturism as metaphor for Black cultural displacement. Features the "Data Thief" figure (played by George). Interviews with George Clinton, Octavia Butler, Samuel R. Delany, Kodwo Eshun, Derrick May, Juan Atkins, Carl Craig, DJ Spooky, Goldie. Described as "one of the most influential video-essays of the 1990s, influencing filmmakers and inspiring conferences, novels and exhibitions." Key early text in Afrofuturism discourse.

**The Stuart Hall Project** (2013) — documentary film
Feature-length companion to *The Unfinished Conversation*. Where the installation was Akomfrah's take on Hall's legacy, the film is Hall's own take on Akomfrah and the world.

## Ideas & Theoretical Positions

**The Essay Film Tradition:** Akomfrah works in the tradition of Chris Marker, Harun Farocki, Dziga Vertov — he calls his approach a "cine-essay." He rejects both social-realist documentary and mainstream narrative. He describes his method as reading archive footage "for its ambiguity and open-endedness" — a Caribbean woman in a 1960s factory is not just "a migrant"; she is also thinking about dinner and her lover.

**Diaspora aesthetics:** BAFC's founding insight was that Black diasporic subjects deserved an avant-garde form. When the Arts Council told him "blacks can't be avant-garde film-makers," he accelerated rather than retreated. He explicitly positioned BAFC against agitprop and social realism, taking aesthetics as seriously as politics — influenced by Ritwik Ghatak (Bengal), Ousmane Sembène (Senegal), Santiago Álvarez (Cuba), as well as Tarkovsky, Fassbinder, the European avant-garde.

**Sound as primary material:** Akomfrah and Mathison pushed against high-modernist visual primacy — "The avant garde saw our emphasis on the audio as a thought crime, a heresy." The sonic is as constitutive as the image in his work. BAFC's "slide-tape texts" were explicitly audio-visual forms that resisted gallery conventions.

**Archive as political site:** Found footage is not merely illustration; it is itself contested terrain. "Migrants were often filmed in relation to debates about crime or social problems, so that's how they get fixed in official memory." His practice re-reads archival images against the grain.

**The more-than-human:** From *Vertigo Sea* onwards, his work expands from diaspora/human memory to encompass whales, wilderness, climate, geological time. Purple covers Alaska, Greenland, the South Pacific in a six-hour arc; *Four Nocturnes* holds humanity and wilderness in mutual destruction. This is not pastoral environmentalism but a form of postcolonial ecology — industrial capitalism that enslaved people also destroyed the natural world.

**Stuart Hall's influence:** Hall's concept of identity as fluid, open to recreation — never fixed, always in process — runs through all of Akomfrah's work. The BAFC was engaged with Hall's academic project since the 1970s (Hall's appearance in the BBC programme *It Ain't Half Racist, Mum*). *The Unfinished Conversation* is explicitly the heir to Hall's project of cultural studies.

**Afrofuturism:** *The Last Angel of History* (1995) is one of the founding texts of what became the Afrofuturism discourse (the term was coined by Mark Dery in 1993). The "Data Thief" figure — traveling time in search of the code that unlocks the future — became a key archetype. The film's interviews with Octavia Butler, Samuel Delany, and musicians like George Clinton, Carl Craig, Juan Atkins established the Afrofuturism canon.

## Why Interesting

Akomfrah sits at a unique intersection of several things Amber cares about deeply:

1. **Essay film as research method** — His practice demonstrates that serious intellectual work can happen through moving image; the cine-essay is as rigorous as a paper.

2. **Archive as generative material** — His entire practice is built on re-reading and re-assembling historical footage. This resonates with practices of working with inherited data, found materials, historical records.

3. **Postcolonial + more-than-human ecology** — His late work (from ~2015 onward) constructs an explicitly ecological postcolonialism: the same forces that destroyed Black and Brown lives destroyed the non-human world. This is intellectually powerful and artistically realized.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
Akomfrah operates through **thematic expansion** — each major series opens the frame wider. BAFC: Black British experience in the UK. Early solo work: global diaspora, migration. *Vertigo Sea*: diaspora + oceanic sublime + whaling. *Purple*: climate change + Anthropocene + geological time. *Arcadia*: Columbian Exchange from 1400 to present. Each work expands the temporal and spatial scope while retaining the core method (archive montage, multi-channel immersion, sonic layering).

**Conceptual move:** He consistently refuses binary frames. Diaspora is not just victimhood; the ocean is not just route of migration but site of sublime and ecological destruction; climate change is not just Western environmentalism but postcolonial reckoning.

### Career Architecture
**Three phases:**

1. **Collective infrastructure** (1982–1998): BAFC as a collective provided safety in numbers, multiple skills (sound = Mathison, writing = George, production = Gopaul), and institutional legitimacy. They operated across gallery, broadcast TV (Channel 4), and cinema — this prevented over-dependence on any one sector.

2. **Solo + Company** (1998–2010s): Smoking Dogs Films continued the collective model as a production company. Akomfrah became the named artist; Gopaul and Lawson continued as producers. The key move was the shift from collective to named artist without losing the collaborative production structure.

3. **Museum-scale + Global** (2012–present): The major solo exhibitions at Hirshhorn, Tate Britain, New Museum, etc. Position him as an international artist at the top of the institution-gallery circuit. The Venice Biennale British Pavilion (2024) is the apex.

**Key structural insight:** He is represented by Lisson Gallery (London), one of the UK's most internationally connected galleries. This provides the commercial infrastructure (art fairs, sales, press) that allows the institutional circuit (museums, biennials). This gallery-museum circuit is the standard model for career A-list artists.

### Methodology Signature
**Archive montage as epistemology:** His method is fundamentally historiographic — he reads moving image archives the way historians read documents. He learned this from his mother (who met Malcolm X), from Stuart Hall (who theorized cultural identity through history), and from the Soviet/European avant-garde filmmakers he sneaked into seeing as a teenager.

**Multi-channel as argument:** The three/six/five-channel format is not just spectacle — it creates a non-linear, simultaneous structure that cannot be reduced to a single argument or narrative. Multiple screens create contradiction, resonance, dissonance. This is his signature intellectual move: the multi-channel installation as essay form that holds multiple truths simultaneously.

**Sound design as co-equal medium:** Trevor Mathison's sonic compositions are not illustration but argument. The mesh of Arvo Pärt, spirituals, Indian music, and field recordings in *The Nine Muses* is doing intellectual work that the image alone cannot do.

### Key Moves (chronological)
1. **Portsmouth Polytechnic collective formation (1980–82):** Forming BAFC with seven friends before graduation gave him an institutional structure from day one. He never had to start as a lone artist.
2. **Handsworth Songs + Channel 4 (1986):** The breakthrough — winning major prizes, attracting Salman Rushdie's polemical response (even hostile attention raised profile), establishing the BAFC aesthetic. The film became a touchstone.
3. **The Last Angel of History (1995):** Pivoting from documentary to hybrid essay-fiction, and into Afrofuturism — this connected BAFC to a global Black intellectual conversation (the Afrofuturism discourse) well before it became mainstream.
4. **Smoking Dogs Films (1998) + Mnemosyne / Nine Muses (2010–11):** Transition from collective to named artist + production company. The shift to gallery-scale installations opened the museum circuit.
5. **The Unfinished Conversation / Stuart Hall Project (2012–13):** The Stuart Hall works positioned him as a major figure in UK cultural memory and brought Tate Britain + global attention.
6. **Vertigo Sea + Venice Biennale 2015:** Expansion to the oceanic sublime and climate themes opened the ecology conversation. The Venice placement (*All the World's Futures*, Okwui Enwezor's political biennial) put him on the international museum circuit.
7. **Purple + Artes Mundi 2017:** Six-channel format; global exhibition tour; major prize. Established him as a climate artist without abandoning diaspora.
8. **Knighthood + Venice British Pavilion 2023–24:** Official British canonization. The state apparatus acknowledged him.

### What Amber Can Learn
- **The collective model sustains individual ambition.** BAFC gave Akomfrah a community of interlocutors, a production infrastructure, and institutional legitimacy that he couldn't have built alone at 25. The lesson: founding a collective is itself a career move, not a distraction from individual work.
- **Thematic expansion as growth strategy.** Each major work opens the frame wider — starts with Black British, expands to global diaspora, then ocean, then planet, then cosmological time. This is how to avoid repetition while maintaining a coherent practice.
- **The multi-channel as intellectual form.** Rather than trying to make an argument in one channel, the multi-channel format creates space for contradiction and simultaneity. This is applicable to any practice that wants to hold complexity without resolution.
- **Archive reading as practice.** His method of re-reading archival images "for their ambiguity" — against the grain of how they were originally framed — is a transferable methodology.
- **Sound as first-class medium.** His insistence on sonic parity with the visual (against "high modernist" visual primacy) opens up the full expressive range of time-based media.
