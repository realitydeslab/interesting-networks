# Roy Ascott

British artist, theorist, and educator. The founding father of telematic art and cybernetic aesthetics — the person who, in 1961, introduced cybernetic feedback loops into art education and, by 1980, was making networked art before the internet existed. Created [[Planetary Collegium]] (1994), the world's premier transdisciplinary PhD research network for art, science, technology, and consciousness. Coined **moistmedia** (2000) — the synthesis of wet biology and dry computation. Now 90+ years old, still working from his DeTao Technoetic Arts Studio in Shanghai.

## Background
- Born 26 October 1934, Bath, Somerset, England.
- **Royal Air Force, 1953–1955** — radar surveillance officer. This military exposure to telematic sensing/control systems gave early form to his obsession with feedback, connectivity, and consciousness.
- **BA Fine Art (Hons), King's College, University of Durham** (now Newcastle), 1955–1959 — studied under **[[Victor Pasmore]]** and **[[Richard Hamilton]]** (both major British constructivists), art history under Lawrence Gowing and Quentin Bell. Victor Pasmore's "favourite student."
- **Studio Demonstrator, University of Durham, 1959–1961** — working with Pasmore immediately post-graduation.
- Pasmore arranged for him to establish the **Groundcourse** at Ealing School of Art, London (1961) — the move that launched everything.
- Has lived and worked across UK, Canada, USA, Austria, and now Shanghai. **Currently based in Shanghai** at the DeTao Technoetic Arts Studio, Shanghai Institute of Visual Arts.

## Key Works

### Interactive/Cybernetic Art (1959–1979)
- **Change Paintings** (1959–1961) — Sliding Plexiglas panels that viewers could rearrange, creating new compositions. First realization of the cybernetic principle in art: feedback, participation, indeterminacy. Influenced by Japanese calligraphy and Bergson's "duration." Presaged interactive digital art by decades.
- **Video-Roget** (1962) — Conceptual matrix of abstract forms in a grid; viewer could mentally recombine them. In the **Tate collection** (acquired 2014). Shown at Molton Gallery solo exhibition "Diagram Boxes & Analogue Structures" (Feb 1963).
- **Transactional Set** (1971) — Standard objects (rings, clothespins, funnels) on a grid table; altered by viewers like I Ching hexagrams.
- **Plastic Transactions** (1971) — Chairs, table, plastic objects; dimensions variable.

### Telematic Art (1980–1995)
- **Terminal Art** (1980) — **The world's first telematic artwork.** Linked artists between US and UK over Jacques Vallee's Infomedia NOTEPAD conferencing system. Mailed portable terminals to artists in California, New York, and Wales; they collectively generated ideas from their studios.
- **"World in 24 Hours" (I Ching contribution)** (1982) — Robert Adrian X's Ars Electronica networking event. Ascott organized the first planetary throw of the I Ching via connected computer terminals worldwide.
- **La Plissure du Texte** (1983) — **His most radical piece.** Commissioned for "Electra: Electricity and Electronics in the Art of the Twentieth Century" (Musée d'Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris, curated by Frank Popper). A collective global fairy tale: 11 nodes on the ARTEX network (pre-web artist internet) each embodied a fairy tale character (wicked witch, magician, etc.) and contributed to a distributed narrative over 5–6 weeks. Paris / San Francisco / Pittsburgh / Toronto / Alma QC / Sydney / Vienna / Amsterdam / Honolulu / Tokyo / Vancouver (Dec 11–23, 1983). Story visible on-screen at the Musée d'Art Moderne; visitors could type in suggestions. Title references Roland Barthes ("Le Plaisir du Texte") — "the pleating of the text."
- **Planetary Network** (1986) — Venice Biennale, XLII edition. Telematic networking project.
- **Aspects of Gaia: Digital Pathways Across the Whole Earth** (1989) — Ars Electronica, Linz. Interactive installation: an underground railway built around the Brucknerhaus, with stops showing video/slides sent by remote communities in Africa, Japan, and elsewhere responding to "What aspects of Gaia are beautiful/important to you?" Upstairs: interactive displays for visitor contributions. [[Peter Weibel]] helped fund and realize it.

### Post-1990: Technoetics & Moistmedia
- **"Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?"** (1990) — Landmark essay, *Art Journal* 49:3. Defined telematics, argued for passionate attraction (via Fourier) through networked art, theorized love and consciousness in telematic systems. The theoretical culmination of his 1980s practice.
- **"Moist Manifesto"** (2000) — Coined **moistmedia**: the synthesis of dry (digital/computational) and wet (biological/plant/neural) systems. Argued artists could work with organic elements exhibiting storage/construction behaviors beyond digital computation. Opened art practice to bioart, neuroscience, nanotechnology.
- **DeTao Technoetic Arts Studio** (2014–present) — Active studio at Shanghai Institute of Visual Arts / DeTao Masters Academy. Current primary working base.

## Groundcourse (1961–1964)

The most influential art pedagogy of the 20th century that most people haven't heard of.

Ascott designed the **Groundcourse** at Ealing School of Art (1961–1964), then continued it at Ipswich Civic College (1964–1967). A radically cybernetic 2-year foundation curriculum:
- Identity–Behavior–Environment as the core triad
- Students built **calibrators** — wearable or structural analog devices that altered personality/behavior based on environmental inputs
- Groups of 6 students functioned as integrated self-regulating systems
- Inverted logic, chance operations, behavioral psychology
- Performance, role-play, environmental interventions

**Famous students**: **[[Brian Eno]]** (Ipswich), **[[Pete Townshend]]** (Ealing, of The Who — who was once instructed to navigate a corridor without using his legs, so built a trolley), **[[Stephen Willats]]** (conceptual artist), **[[Michael English]]** (psychedelic poster artist). Students went into music, engineering, politics — not just art. Eno has said it shaped everything he later did with generative music.

The Groundcourse anticipated by decades: design thinking's "prototyping," performance studies' "identity in flux," and interactive art's "behavioral feedback."

## Theoretical Concepts

### Cybernetics & Behavior (1961–1980)
- Introduced first-order cybernetics (Norbert Wiener) then second-order cybernetics (**[[Heinz von Foerster]]**) into art practice
- "The Construction of Change" (1964) essay — art as process/system, behavior over object
- "Behaviourables and Futuribles" (1967) manifesto — "When art is a form of behaviour, software predominates over hardware"
- "Behaviourist Art and the Cybernetic Vision" (1966/67) — *CYBERNETICA* journal — proposed a "cybernetic art matrix" with computers as "intelligence amplifiers"
- **[[Gordon Pask]]** — crucial friendship with the British cybernetician. Pask proposed Ascott as Associate Member of the Institution of Computer Science (1968). Shared deep interest in system theory, conversation theory, and art-behavior.

### Telematics (1980–1995)
- "Art and Telematics: Towards a Network Consciousness" (1984) — in Heidi Grundmann ed., *Art + Telecommunication*
- Telematics defined as: "computer-mediated communications networking between geographically dispersed individuals and institutions... and between the human mind and artificial systems of intelligence and perception"
- Influenced by: Henri Bergson (duration), Teilhard de Chardin (noosphere), Peter Russell (global brain), I Ching, Duchamp's Large Glass

### Technoetics (1994–present)
- Coined **technoetics** — art practice exploring consciousness and connectivity through digital, telematic, chemical, and spiritual means
- Embraces interactive and psychoactive technologies + moist media
- **"Is There Love in the Telematic Embrace?"** (1990) — the key manifesto text
- *Telematic Embrace: Visionary Theories of Art, Technology, and Consciousness* (UC Press, 2003) — collected writings with comprehensive introduction by **[[Edward Shanken]]**. Definitive book.

### Moistmedia (2000–present)
- **"Moist Manifesto"** (2000) — coined the term
- Moistmedia = wet (biological/plant/animal/neural) + dry (digital/silicon) media in synthesis
- Opened path from telematic to bio art, nano art, brain-computer interfaces
- Thesis: biological systems can exhibit computational behaviors (storage, construction, programming); artists should work with living matter

## Institutional Creations

### [[Planetary Collegium]] (1994–present)
- **CAiiA** (Centre for Advanced Inquiry in the Interactive Arts) — founded 1994 at University of Wales, Newport
- **CAiiA-STAR** (Science Technology and Art Research) — joint platform with University of Plymouth, 1997
- **Planetary Collegium** — renamed & relocated to Plymouth, 2003. Faculty of Arts, Humanities and Business.
- Nodes: **DeTao-Node Shanghai** (Director: Ascott); **T-Node Trento** (Fondazione Ahref; Director: Francesco Monico); **NGL-Node Lucerne** (SAA; Director: René Stettler)
- Over 80 PhD graduates; more than 70 conferences in Europe, Americas, Japan, China, Australia
- Advisory Board: [[Pierre Lévy]], [[Roger Malina]], [[James Gimzewski]], Barbara Maria Stafford, Marilyn Schlitz, Luis Eduardo Luna, Thomas S. Ray
- Won **World Universities Forum Award for Best Practice in Higher Education 2011**
- No longer recruiting as of publication date; legacy through i-DAT, Plymouth
- **Select PhD graduates**: Eduardo Kac (2003, bio art), Natasha Vita-More (transhumanism), Elisa Giaccardi (metadesign), Diane Gromala (interactive arts), Thecla Schiphorst (somatics × HCI)

### Technoetic Arts (journal, 2003–present)

- **[[Gordon Pask]]** ⭐⭐ — British cybernetician, close friend and intellectual ally. Proposed Ascott for Associate Membership of Institution of Computer Science (1968). Conversation Theory, adaptive systems. Their friendship was foundational for the cybernetic aesthetics project.

## Venue Circuit Analysis
Ascott operates across three circuits:
1. **British cybernetics/fine art**: King's College Durham → Ealing → Ipswich → Ontario → SFAI → Vienna — the postwar British avant-garde education network
2. **European media art**: Ars Electronica (1982, 1989, 2014), Venice Biennale, Milan Triennale, Musée d'Art Moderne Paris — the founding circuit of media art as an institutional field
3. **Shanghai/Asia**: DeTao Masters Academy, Shanghai Institute of Visual Arts, Shanghai Biennale — his current base and the bridge to the Chinese art-tech ecosystem

The Plymouth node connects the UK institutional establishment to the global network. The Shanghai node is his actual working home now — DeTao is where the new work lives.

## Why Interesting
**The Originary Node**: Ascott is the single person most responsible for the theoretical and institutional framework within which art+technology+consciousness research operates. Before there was "media art" as a field, Ascott had already done it, named it, institutionalized it, and trained two generations. His 1964 essay anticipates Joseph Kosuth's "Art After Philosophy" by 5 years. His 1966 cybernetic art matrix proposal anticipates the internet by 30 years.

**The Amber Connection**: The Planetary Collegium is the institutional ancestor of the entire Chinese art-tech scene that Amber moves in. Eduardo Kac, Elisa Giaccardi, and dozens of others passed through; the Shanghai node directly connects to DeTao, SIVA, and China's art-tech infrastructure. If Amber is navigating the consciousness-technology-art space, she is walking paths Ascott built.

**Groundcourse Pedagogy**: His approach — identity/behavior/environment as the core triad, students as nodes in a self-regulating system, art as process not product — is the missing theoretical foundation for thinking about how AI agents and human artists might collaborate. Extremely relevant for Amber's research on behavioral creativity and agent-environment interaction.

**Moistmedia Futures**: The wet/dry synthesis is now the central problem in biocomputation, wet AI, wetware. He named it in 2000. Still ahead of the field.

**Still Active at 90**: The Artforum Obrist interview (2025) shows him intellectually alive and thinking clearly about AI ("AI will find its way in the world. Like interactivity, it will grow"). Not a historical figure — a living interlocutor.
