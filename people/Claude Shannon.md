# Claude Shannon

Father of information theory and a founding figure of AI. Published *A Mathematical Theory of Communication* (1948) — the "Magna Carta of the Information Age." Also built chess-playing machines, maze-solving robots, and unicycle-juggling contraptions at Bell Labs — a playful, curiosity-driven scientist whose "side projects" were proto-ALife research. Directly relevant to Amber's interests in information theory, machine behavior, protocol studies, open-endedness, and computational creativity.

## Why Interesting
Shannon's *Mathematical Theory of Communication* underlies all of: information theory, protocol design, network theory, cryptography, compression, and digital communication. His "Theseus" maze-solving robot (1950) is one of the first machine learning demonstrations. His chess program (1950) is foundational AI. His juggling theorems are a serious mathematical contribution wrapped in playfulness. Shannon is the rare figure who was rigorous AND weird AND playful AND civilization-defining — a model for the kind of science Amber admires.

## Key Works
- [*A Mathematical Theory of Communication*](https://people.math.harvard.edu/~ctm/home/text/others/shannon/entropy/entropy.pdf) (1948) — Bell System Technical Journal
- [*Communication Theory of Secrecy Systems*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communication_Theory_of_Secrecy_Systems) (1949)
- Theseus mouse (1950) — first machine learning demonstration (maze-solving robot)
- *Programming a Computer for Playing Chess* (1950) — foundational AI paper
- Juggling theorems — mathematical analysis of juggling patterns
