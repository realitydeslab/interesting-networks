# Ezekiel Dixon-Román

Co-founding member of [[Critical Computation Bureau (CCB)]]. Director of the Master of Science in Social Policy Program and chair of the Data Analytics for Social Policy Certificate at the **University of Pennsylvania**. Theorist of quantification, algorithmic governance, and racialization in computational systems.

## Background
- Director, MS Social Policy Program, UPenn
- Chair, Data Analytics for Social Policy Certificate, UPenn

## Key Works
- **[Inheriting Possibility: Social Reproduction & Quantification in Education](https://www.upress.umn.edu/9780816698271)** (University of Minnesota Press, 2017) — foundational text on how quantification operates as social reproduction
- Co-edited: *Thinking Comprehensively About Education: Spaces of Educative Possibility* (Routledge, 2012)
- Co-guest edited: "Alternative Ontologies of Number: Rethinking the Quantitative in Computational Culture" (2016, *Cultural Studies–Critical Methodologies*)
- Co-guest edited: "The computational turn in education research: Critical and creative perspectives on the digital data deluge" (2017, *Research in Education*)
- **[Parisi & Dixon-Román, "Recursive Colonialism & Cosmo-Computation"](https://socialtextjournal.org)** — *Social Text Online* (forthcoming)
- "Algo-Ritmo: More-Than-Human Performative Acts and the Racializing Assemblages of Algorithmic Architectures" (2016, *Cultural Studies Critical Methodologies*)
- "Toward a Hauntology on Data: On the Sociopolitical Forces of Data Assemblages" (2017, *Research in Education*)
- Current book project: algorithms and race as racialized forces in algorithmic governance, sociopolitical reconfiguration

## Key Concepts
- **Racialized algorithms** — data and algorithms as active agencies in racial capitalism, not neutral tools
- **Hauntology on data** — data assemblages haunted by historical violence and inequity (after Derrida/Fisher)
- **Recursive colonialism** (with Parisi) — computational epistemologies as colonial loops
- **Quantification as social reproduction** — how metrics reproduce inequality
