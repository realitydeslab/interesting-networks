# Thecla Schiphorst

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-27:** Initial deep research. Created full profile from stub. Publications mapped via Semantic Scholar. 120+ papers spanning 1990-2025.

The intellectual godmother of body-centered computing. Schiphorst has spent 35+ years building the conceptual and practical foundations for computing that takes the dancing, breathing, sensing, touching body as its primary object of concern — not as input device, but as site of knowledge. She co-developed **Life Forms** with Merce Cunningham (1990), coined the phrase "the somatic turn in HCI" (with Lian Loke, 2018), and co-founded the **MOCO** (Movement and Computing) conference. Her work is the linchpin between dance knowledge, Laban Movement Analysis, somaesthetics, and HCI design methodology.

---

## Background

Schiphorst is a Dutch-Canadian researcher — her practice begins in dance, not computing. She came to computers through choreographic necessity: how do you compose with movement? How do you capture, transmit, analyze the knowledge in a dancer's body? This origin story shapes everything — she never became a computer scientist who studied dance; she remained a dancer who used computers.

Her breakthrough came in 1990 when she joined the team at Simon Fraser University building **Life Forms**, the landmark computer choreographic software that would become Merce Cunningham's compositional tool for the last two decades of his career. This placed her at the center of one of the most significant art-technology collaborations of the twentieth century — and gave her firsthand knowledge that computation could genuinely transform how artists create.

She stayed at SFU, completing her MA there in 1993 ("under special arrangements" — the interdisciplinary nature of her work didn't fit existing degree programs), then eventually her PhD at the University of Plymouth in 2008. She is now a full Professor at SFU's School of Interactive Arts and Technology (SIAT), the institution that has been her intellectual home for 35 years.

Her research trajectory has three phases:
1. **Choreographic computing (1990–2002)**: Life Forms, Merce Cunningham, interfaces for choreographers, body visualization
2. **Touch, breath, skin (2002–2012)**: Wearable art, somaesthetics of touch, intimate computing, the ethics and aesthetics of proximity
3. **Somatic HCI methodology (2012–present)**: Laban Movement Analysis applied to interaction design, kinesthetic empathy, somatic turn, MOCO, breath-based interaction

## Domains

- **Laban Movement Analysis (LMA) in HCI** — bringing Laban's notation system into interface design; Effort qualities (Weight, Space, Time, Flow) as design dimensions
- **Somaesthetics of touch** — the aesthetics and ethics of computational touch; skin as interface
- **Breath-based interaction** — using breath as both input modality and design material
- **Choreographic computation** — software tools for dance composition; movement notation systems
- **Wearable art** — garments that compute; e-textiles as intimate interface
- **Kinesthetic empathy** — designing interfaces that transmit and cultivate movement knowledge across bodies
- **First-person somatic research** — using the designer's own bodily experience as design method
- **Movement visualization** — EMVIZ, computational aesthetics of movement quality representation
- **VR and embodiment** — flying locomotion, lucid dreaming, breath awareness in virtual environments

## Key Works

### Software & Systems
- **[Life Forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DanceForms)** (1990) — Co-developer. Landmark computer choreographic tool used by Merce Cunningham from 1990 until his death in 2009. Revolutionized how dance could be composed with computers. Now called DanceForms; still in use. This is the most consequential software project in the history of dance-computing.
- **[EMVIZ](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2030475.2030482)** (2011/2013) — Emotional/Movement Visualization system; translates Laban Effort qualities into aesthetic visual representations of dance movement; real-time poetics of movement quality
- **[POEME](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2839462.2839494)** (2016) — Poetry Engine Powered by Your Movement; TEI installation; movement generates poetry in real-time
- **m+m** (2016, with Tom Mitchell) — novel middleware for distributed, movement-based interactive multimedia systems; open-source infrastructure for MOCO community

### Artworks & Installations
- **[Bodymaps: artifacts of touch](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/259956.259960)** (1997, SIGGRAPH) — Exploring the sensuality and anarchy of touch; interactive installation mapping body surfaces; early major statement on computing and skin
- **[The electric body project](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/259956.259961)** (1997, SIGGRAPH) — Body as electric field; interactive performance installation
- **[FlowField](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/566570.566605)** (2002, SIGGRAPH) — Investigating the semantics of caress; interactive installation translating touch gestures into flow visualization
- **[exhale: (breath between bodies)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1198555.1198685)** (2005, SIGGRAPH) — Networked wearable installation; breath as shared medium between distributed bodies; sound responds to breath across network; landmark in somatic wearable art
- **[tendrils](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1709886.1709926)** (2010, TEI) — Exploring the poetics of collective touch in wearable art; networked garments that sense and transmit touch between wearers
- **[Never Alone](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3027063.3052567)** (2017, CHI Art) — Video Agents Based Generative Audio-Visual Installation; AI agents respond to human presence
- **[Hybrid Dance Xplorations](https://doi.org/10.1145/3736539.3737497)** (2025, SIGGRAPH Frontiers) — Artist-centric XR/AI Sandbox for co-creation and performance; contemporary dance + salsa; latest major work

- [**"Virtual Transcendent Dream: Empowering People through Embodied Flying in Virtual Reality"**](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3491102.3517528) (CHI 2022) — Embodied VR locomotion research.
- [**"Art, Activism, and AI: Generating Visual Narratives of Injustice through Research-Creation with a Feminist Lens"**](https://computationalcreativity.net/iccc23) (ICCC 2023, with Amineh Ahmadi Nejad and Steve DiPaola) — AI art + feminist activism turn.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Schiphorst occupies a **body-centered HCI + dance-computing** circuit. Unlike many media artists who move through gallery/festival circuits (Ars Electronica, Transmediale), her primary venues are **academic conferences with art shows**:

**Core circuit:**
- **CHI** — HCI mothership; where somatic turn papers get widest HCI audience; Art.CHI for installations
- **SIGGRAPH** — original home for Life Forms era; Art Gallery for major installations; now SIGGRAPH Frontiers for dance-AI
- **TEI** — Tangible, Embedded, Embodied Interaction; natural venue for wearables and touch work
- **MOCO** — the community she co-built; home ground
- **DIS** — Designing Interactive Systems; methodology and theory work

**Significant secondary venues:**
- **C&C (Creativity and Cognition)** — computational creativity, performance, design cognition
- **ICCC** — International Conference on Computational Creativity; choreographic generation, AI art
- **Interactions (ACM magazine)** — high-visibility practitioner-facing venue for position pieces
- **Leonardo (MIT Press)** — prestige art-science journal; theory papers
- **EVA (Electronic Visualisation and the Arts)** — UK-based digital arts; European presence

**Conspicuously absent:**
- Art gallery circuits (no Ars Electronica, Transmediale, ZKM presence visible)
- Social science venues (no CSCW, FAccT)
- European festival circuits

Her circuit is **academic HCI with artistic ambitions** — not artist-first. This contrasts with practitioners like [[Monika Bielskyte]] or [[Daisy Ginsberg]] who are festival/gallery-first. Schiphorst's power comes from institutional credibility (SFU full professor, ACM publications) combined with artistic practice — a rare combination.

## Why Interesting

Schiphorst is **evidence that the somatic turn has deep roots** — she was doing body-centered computing in 1990, a decade before Dourish's *Where the Action Is* (2001) and two decades before Höök's soma design manifesto. She represents the practitioner strand of this tradition: not theory-first but **body-first, practice-first**.

The most interesting thing about Schiphorst is that she **never needed the somatic turn to happen** because she was already on the other side of it. When the academic HCI world "turned somatic" in the 2010s, she was the person they were turning toward.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**Somatic grounding first, then computation** — Schiphorst never derives her questions from computing. She starts with the question: what does the body know that computing currently ignores? Then she asks: can computing be redesigned to honor that knowledge? This is the inversion of most HCI research, which starts with computing capabilities and asks how bodies can be fitted to them.

**Two-word concept coining**: "somatic turn," "somaesthetics of touch," "kinesthetic empathy," "somatic connoisseurship" — she repeatedly coins precise, citable two-or-three-word terms that name previously unnamed phenomena in the body-computing relationship.

**Laban as master framework**: The Laban Movement Analysis system is her perpetual reference point. LMA gives her a vocabulary that is both grounded in body practice (Laban trained thousands of movers) and formalizable enough for computing. She has spent 30+ years working out the mapping: LMA Effort qualities → design dimensions → interaction modalities.

### Career Architecture

**Institutional loyalty pays off**: 35 years at SFU gave Schiphorst depth and longevity that institutional hopping never could. She built SIAT's body-computing tradition by staying. The institution became her.

**Conference co-founding as field declaration**: Creating MOCO (2014) was a territorial claim — this community exists, it has a name, it meets. It made the body-computing field legible in a way that scattered publications could not.

**Art + academia dual identity**: By maintaining both a gallery practice (SIGGRAPH Art Gallery, TEI) and an academic publication record (CHI, DIS, Leonardo), she claims authority in both worlds. Neither art critics nor computer scientists can dismiss her.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **Join the Life Forms team (1990)** — Positioned herself at the center of the most consequential dance-computing collaboration of the century. Put her name on the map in both dance and computing communities simultaneously.

2. **Persist at SFU through the 1990s** — While the HCI field was dominated by usability and cognitive approaches, Schiphorst kept doing body-centered work that had no mainstream home. The persistence paid when the field caught up.

3. **SIGGRAPH Art Gallery series (1997-2005)** — SIGGRAPH was the premier venue for art-technology in the 1990s-2000s. By placing major works there, she established art credentials with a technical audience that takes artists seriously.

4. **Introduce somaesthetics explicitly (2009)** — The CHI 2009 "soft(n)" paper named the philosophical framework (Shusterman's somaesthetics) for the first time in her HCI work. This created a citable theoretical anchor for the whole body of work.

5. **Co-found MOCO (2014)** — Created the conference the field needed. MOCO is now in its 10th year and growing. This is community infrastructure that permanently establishes her as a founder of a field.

6. **Name "the somatic turn" (2018)** — The Interactions paper with Lian Loke is the gesture that consolidated and named the movement. "The somatic turn in HCI" is now a citable concept — and Schiphorst's name is attached to it. This is field-declaration through naming.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Name your moves** — Amber is already building somaesthetic design practice. He needs to name it precisely and publish those names in accessible venues (Interactions, Leonardo, not just CHI proceedings). "Somaesthetic Realities" is a good start; what are the sub-concepts?

2. **Build the conference you need** — MOCO didn't exist until Schiphorst and colleagues created it. If the venue circuit for Amber's work doesn't exist perfectly, create or co-create it. ALIFE + somaesthetics workshop? More-than-human design + CHI? The act of creating a venue is a field declaration.

3. **Hold the philosophical anchor** — Schiphorst kept Laban as her reference point for 30 years. Amber should identify the equivalent: what is the master framework that grounds his somaesthetic/more-than-human work? Shusterman? Yuk Hui's technics? Something more synthetic?

4. **Dual-channel credibility** — Schiphorst's gallery practice gives her credibility with artists that pure academics lack. Amber's HoloKit + China Academy of Art work functions similarly. Maintain both channels deliberately.

5. **The body of work is the argument** — Schiphorst never wrote a book-length argument for body-centered computing. She didn't need to — 120+ papers over 35 years IS the argument. Consistency of commitment over time IS the contribution.
