# Jane Bennett

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-27:** Stub created during Graham Harman deep research.

American political theorist. Professor in the [Department of Political Science at Johns Hopkins University](https://politicalscience.jhu.edu/). Influential for *Vibrant Matter: A Political Ecology of Things* (2010) — a landmark text in **new materialism** arguing that nonhuman "thing-power" is real and politically consequential. Adjacent to but distinct from OOO.

## Domains
- **New materialism / vibrant matter** — the agency and efficacy of nonhuman materials
- **Political theory** — politics of nonhuman materiality; ecological democracy
- **Spinozism** — power, affect, assemblage; draws on Spinoza and Deleuze
- **Enchantment** — *The Enchantment of Modern Life* (2001): wonder and ethics

## Key Works
- *[The Enchantment of Modern Life: Attachments, Crossings, and Ethics](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691088136/the-enchantment-of-modern-life)* (Princeton UP, 2001)
- *[Vibrant Matter: A Political Ecology of Things](https://www.dukeupress.edu/vibrant-matter)* (Duke UP, 2010) — the key new materialism text; argues that materiality is always already active, that "thing-power" operates in political life
- *[Influx and Efflux: Writing Up with Walt Whitman](https://www.dukeupress.edu/influx-and-efflux)* (Duke UP, 2020)

## Key Relationships
- **[[Graham Harman]]** — intellectual adjacency. Both argue for the autonomy and power of nonhuman entities, but differently: Harman via "withdrawal," Bennett via "thing-power" and assemblage. Bennett draws on Spinoza/Deleuze; Harman on Heidegger/phenomenology.
- **[William Connolly](https://politicalscience.jhu.edu/directory/william-e-connolly/)** — Johns Hopkins colleague; related new materialism work
- **[[Karen Barad]]** — both in the new materialist orbit; Barad more feminist/physics-grounded
- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — shared feminist-materialist concerns

## Why Interesting
Bennett's *Vibrant Matter* is the political theory text that opened new materialism to political science. The contrast with Harman is productive: both decentre humans, but Bennett sees things as relational/affecting while Harman sees them as withdrawing. For any work on nonhuman agencies in political or design contexts, Bennett and Harman together offer a useful conceptual landscape.
