# Alexander Kluge

German filmmaker, author, philosopher, and TV producer. Founding figure of [New German Cinema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_German_Cinema); co-signatory of the [Oberhausen Manifesto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oberhausen_Manifesto) (1962); student of Theodor Adorno and legal counsel to the Frankfurt School; founder and driving force of [DCTP](https://www.dctp.tv/) (1987–present), which produces late-night independent television slots on RTL, Sat.1, and VOX. At 94, he is Germany's most significant living polymath — spanning narrative fiction, critical theory, cinema, and media infrastructure. Winner of virtually every major German cultural prize, including the Georg Büchner Prize (2003, Germany's highest literary honour) and the Theodor W. Adorno Award (2009).

## Background
Born 14 February 1932 in Halberstadt (then Province of Saxony, now Saxony-Anhalt), Kluge grew up through World War II — an experience that has shaped everything he has ever made. Halberstadt was heavily bombed in April 1945; the trauma of German amnesia about this history became his lifelong artistic subject.

He studied history, law, and music at the University of Marburg, then at Goethe University Frankfurt, where he completed a **doctorate in law (Dr. jur.) in 1956**. Frankfurt changed everything: there he befriended **Theodor W. Adorno**, then teaching at the [Institute for Social Research (Frankfurt School)](https://www.ifs.uni-frankfurt.de/en.html). Kluge served as **legal counsel to the Institute**, absorbing Frankfurt School critical theory at its source — not as a student reader but as a practitioner inside the institution.

At Adorno's suggestion, Kluge began investigating filmmaking. In 1958 Adorno introduced him to Fritz Lang, for whom Kluge worked as an assistant on [*The Tiger of Eschnapur*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tiger_of_Eschnapur_(1959_film)) — the unlikely encounter between the Frankfurt critic and the classic Hollywood director that gave Kluge his cinematic apprenticeship.

His 1960 debut film *Brutality in Stone* premiered at Oberhausen. In February 1962 he was one of **26 signatories to the [Oberhausen Manifesto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oberhausen_Manifesto)** — and, crucially, **moderated the press conference** at which it was read. The Manifesto declared "Der alte Film ist tot. Wir glauben an den neuen" ("The old cinema is dead. We believe in the new cinema"), founding the [New German Cinema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_German_Cinema) as a movement.

That same year, with Edgar Reitz and Detlev Schleiermacher, he co-founded the [Ulm Institut für Filmgestaltung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hochschule_f%C3%BCr_Gestaltung_Ulm) at the legendary [Hochschule für Gestaltung Ulm (HfG Ulm)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hochschule_f%C3%BCr_Gestaltung_Ulm) — the Bauhaus successor school that was the most intellectually ambitious design institution of postwar Europe.

In 1987, Kluge founded **[DCTP](https://www.dctp.tv/)** (Development Company for Television Program mbH), his media production company. DCTP operates independent television slots on RTL, Sat.1, and VOX, producing late-night documentary and interview programming that runs counter to commercial television logic. DCTP also maintains a web archive at [dctp.tv](https://www.dctp.tv/) — thousands of interview segments with the world's foremost intellectuals, now publicly accessible.

His sister **[Alexandra Kluge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandra_Kluge)** was a film actress who starred in his early features.

## Key Works

### Films (chronological)
- **[*Brutality in Stone*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brutality_in_Stone)** (1960/61) — 12-minute black-and-white montage confronting Nazi architectural amnesia. Premiered Oberhausen 1961.
- **[*Yesterday Girl* (*Abschied von gestern*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yesterday_Girl)** (1966) — debut feature; young East German migrating to West Germany. **Silver Lion, Venice Film Festival.** Four German Film Awards. Stars Alexandra Kluge.
- **[*Artists Under the Big Top: Perplexed* (*Die Artisten in der Zirkuskuppel: Ratlos*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artists_Under_the_Big_Top:_Perplexed)** (1968) — **Golden Lion, Venice Film Festival.** (A political scandal; no Golden Lions awarded 1969–1979.)
- **[*The Big Mess* (*Der große Verhau*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Big_Mess)** (1971)
- **[*Gelegenheitsarbeit einer Sklavin*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gelegenheitsarbeit_einer_Sklavin)** (1973) — *Part-Time Work of a Domestic Slave*
- **[*In Gefahr und größter Not bringt der Mittelweg den Tod*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_Gefahr_und_gr%C3%B6%C3%9Fter_Not_bringt_der_Mittelweg_den_Tod)** (1974) — co-directed with Edgar Reitz
- **[*Strongman Ferdinand* (*Der starke Ferdinand*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strongman_Ferdinand)** (1976)
- **[*Germany in Autumn* (*Deutschland im Herbst*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany_in_Autumn)** (1978) — collaborative portmanteau film with Fassbinder, Herzog, Schlöndorff, Reitz, and others, responding to the German Autumn (RAF terrorism)
- **[*The Patriotic Woman* (*Die Patriotin*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Patriotic_Woman)** (1979)
- **[*The Candidate* (*Der Kandidat*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Candidate_(1980_film))** (1980)

- **[*The Power of Feelings* (*Die Macht der Gefühle*)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Power_of_Feelings)** (1983)
- **[*The Assault of the Present on the Rest of Time*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Assault_of_the_Present_on_the_Rest_of_Time)** (1985)
- **[*Nachrichten aus der ideologischen Antike: Marx – Eisenstein – Das Kapital*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nachrichten_aus_der_ideologischen_Antike)** (2008) — 570-minute film essay on Eisenstein's unrealized project to film Marx's *Capital*. Monumental rethinking of historical materialism through cinema.
- **[*Primitive Diversity*](https://iffr.com/)** (2025) — new feature, Harbour section, IFFR Rotterdam.

### Books (selected)
- **[*Lebensläufe*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Kluge)** (1962) — *Life Stories*. Includes "Anita G.", which became *Yesterday Girl*.
- **[*Schlachtbeschreibung*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schlachtbeschreibung)** (1964) — *The Battle*. Documentary-style reconstruction of Stalingrad.
- **[*Learning Processes with a Deadly Outcome*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Kluge)** (1973) — science fiction.
- **[*Öffentlichkeit und Erfahrung*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/)** (1972, with [[Oskar Negt]]) — *Public Sphere and Experience*. Revisits Habermas; calls for a "proletarian public sphere". Translated into English by University of Minnesota Press.
- **[*Geschichte und Eigensinn*](https://www.zone-books.com/)** (1981, with [[Oskar Negt]]) — *History and Obstinacy*. Rethinks proletarian experience and "living labour" via Marx. English translation (2014) by [Zone Books](https://www.zone-books.com/).
- **[*Chronik der Gefühle*](https://www.suhrkamp.de/)** (2000) — *Chronicle of Feelings*. Two-volume "modern epic", 2,000 pages.
- **[*Cinema Stories*](https://www.ndpublishing.com/)** — translated by Martin Brady and Helen Hughes; published by [New Directions](https://www.ndpublishing.com/).
- **[*The Snows of Venice*](https://www.spectorbooks.com/)** (2018, with Ben Lerner) — [Spector Books](https://www.spectorbooks.com/). Collaboration begun 2016.

## Key Relationships & Collaborators

- **[[Theodor W. Adorno]]** — mentor, friend, intellectual father. Adorno introduced Kluge to filmmaking (1958) and to Fritz Lang. The entire critical-theory orientation of Kluge's work flows from this relationship. Kluge is one of very few people who knew Adorno personally and carried his thinking into new media.
- **[[Oskar Negt]]** (⭐⭐⭐) — lifelong co-theorist; co-authored *Public Sphere and Experience* (1972) and *History and Obstinacy* (1981). Together these books are the most systematic attempt to extend Frankfurt School critical theory into a Marxist theory of experience and media. Negt died February 2, 2024, aged 89.
- **[[Edgar Reitz]]** (⭐⭐) — co-signatory Oberhausen Manifesto; co-founder Ulm Institut für Filmgestaltung; co-directed *In Gefahr und größter Not bringt der Mittelweg den Tod* (1974). Reitz went on to make *Heimat* (1984–2004).
- **[[Rainer Werner Fassbinder]]** — co-creator *Germany in Autumn* (1978); central New German Cinema figure.
- **[[Werner Herzog]]** — co-creator *Germany in Autumn* (1978); New German Cinema peer.
- **[[Volker Schlöndorff]]** — co-creator *Germany in Autumn* (1978); New German Cinema peer.
- **[[Ben Lerner]]** (⭐⭐) — American poet/novelist (MacArthur Fellow, Guggenheim, b. 1979). Collaboration begun 2016; *The Snows of Venice* (Spector Books, 2018). Teaches at Brooklyn College.
- **[[Sarah Morris]]** — artist who featured Kluge in her film *Finite and Infinite Games* (2017), which focused on the Elbphilharmonie and included conversation on architecture, music, and James P. Carse's philosophy.
- **[[Fritz Lang]]** — Kluge's cinematic mentor; worked as Lang's assistant on *The Tiger of Eschnapur* (1958/59). The Frankfurt School critic apprenticed to the Hollywood classicist.
- **[[Alexandra Kluge]]** — sister, film actress; starred in *Yesterday Girl* (1966).
- **[[Helge Schneider]]** and **[[Peter Berling]]** — comic actors who play fictitious or parodied characters in DCTP productions (including Hitler, Napoleon's advisors, Michael Jackson's lawyer).

## Venue Circuit Analysis
Kluge's circuit is fundamentally **German/European and intellectual-institutional** — which is part of what makes him unusual and important. He is a globally significant thinker who has largely *resisted* the Anglo-American academic-conference circuit in favor of building his own infrastructure.

**Primary circuits:**
1. **German cultural prize circuit** — Büchner Prize, Kleist Prize, Böll Prize, Schiller Prize, Adorno Award, Pour le Mérite. The full canon of German cultural honour. This is the circuit of Günter Grass, Heinrich Böll, and Peter Handke — the postwar German literary-intellectual establishment.
2. **European cinema** — Venice Film Festival (2× major prizes: Silver Lion 1966, Golden Lion 1968), Berlinale (jury 1965), IFFR Rotterdam (2025). Notably European art cinema rather than Hollywood or global film festival mainstream.
3. **Frankfurt School institutional network** — Institut für Sozialforschung, Goethe University Frankfurt, Zone Books, Suhrkamp Verlag. The infrastructure of critical theory publishing and institutional affiliation.
4. **German commercial/public TV (DCTP model)** — RTL, Sat.1, VOX. Genuinely unusual: a major intellectual figure operating inside commercial TV, using its infrastructure for counter-commercial ends. 37 years running.
5. **International art-theory collaborations** — Ben Lerner (US poetry), Sarah Morris (contemporary art), dctp.tv archive spans philosophy, science, politics, arts globally.

**Signal small venues:** The dctp.tv archive itself is the "small venue" — an independent platform operating since 1987 outside mainstream media distribution. This is the signal.

**What this tells you:** Kluge has spent 60+ years building *infrastructure*, not just making work. HfG Ulm was infrastructure. The Oberhausen Manifesto was infrastructure. DCTP is infrastructure. *Public Sphere and Experience* with Negt *is* infrastructure thinking. He is a builder of conditions of possibility.

## Why Interesting
Alexander Kluge is interesting to Amber's research for three reasons:

**1. The counter-public sphere as computational critique avant la lettre.** Kluge and Negt's 1972 theory of the proletarian public sphere — a space of experience and expression outside bourgeois media control — is essentially a theoretical framework for what platform studies, critical AI, and decentralized tech communities are building now. The analysis of commercial media as structurally suppressing working-class experience maps directly onto the analysis of algorithmic systems as structurally suppressing certain kinds of knowledge and agency.

**2. The media-infrastructure-as-practice model.** Kluge didn't just theorize the counter-public sphere — he built DCTP to actualize it, using commercial TV's infrastructure against itself. This is the exact move that work on decentralized AI and sovereign agents is attempting: using dominant infrastructure to create conditions for different kinds of agency. DCTP has run for 37 years. That's infrastructure thinking at its most durable.

**3. The Frankfurt School / critical theory tradition as missing genealogy.** The Adorno → Kluge/Negt → Habermas lineage developed the most rigorous account of how technological systems become instruments of domination and how counter-institutions can resist this. Kluge is the one figure who also *made art* and *built media infrastructure*, rather than writing only philosophical texts. He is the embodied argument that critical theory and practice are the same project.
