# Joy Buolamwini

Canadian-American computer scientist, digital activist, poet, and artist. Founder of the [Algorithmic Justice League](https://www.ajl.org/) — the organization that brought AI bias from academic research into global policy. Her landmark **Gender Shades** paper (2018, with Timnit Gebru) showed that commercial facial recognition failed dramatically on darker-skinned women (error rates up to 47% vs. 0.8% for lighter-skinned men). Calls herself "poet of code." Author of *[Unmasking AI](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/670356/unmasking-ai-by-dr-joy-buolamwini/)* (2023, Random House, national bestseller). Testified before US Congress (2019). Advisor to President Biden on Executive Order 14110 on AI (2023). Featured in Netflix documentary *Coded Bias* (2021).

## Background

Born January 23, 1990 in Edmonton, Alberta, Canada to Ghanaian immigrant parents (father = academic, mother = artist). Grew up in Mississippi; attended Cordova High School in Tennessee. As a competitive pole vaulter and basketball player, she balanced athletics with academics (completed AP Physics homework between basketball breaks). Self-taught XHTML, JavaScript, and PHP at age nine after being inspired by Kismet, the MIT robot.

**Undergraduate:** BS Computer Science at **[Georgia Institute of Technology](https://www.gatech.edu/)** (2012). Stamps President's Scholar. Youngest finalist of the Georgia Tech InVenture Prize (2009). Researched health informatics; worked with the trachoma programme at the Carter Center (developing an Android-based assessment system in Ethiopia, 2011).

**Rhodes Scholar:** MS at **[Jesus College, Oxford](https://www.jesus.ox.ac.uk/)** on Learning and Technology. Took part in the first formal Rhodes Service Year (community-focused projects). Also: Fulbright Fellow (worked with Zambian youth as technology creators, 2013), Stamps Scholar, Astronaut Scholar, Anita Borg Institute Scholar.

**MIT Media Lab:** MS (2017) and PhD (2022) in Media Arts & Sciences. PhD supervisor: [Ethan Zuckerman](https://ethanzuckerman.com/). Member of Zuckerman's Center for Civic Media group. Her MIT journey began with her artwork "Aspire Mirror" — a device that lets users see a reflection of someone who inspires them. When the facial recognition tech couldn't detect her darker face, she put on a white mask and it worked. This became the founding moment of the Algorithmic Justice League and her academic trajectory.

## Key Works

### Papers
- [**Gender Shades: Intersectional Accuracy Disparities in Commercial Gender Classification**](https://proceedings.mlr.press/v81/buolamwini18a.html) (Buolamwini & Gebru, FAccT 2018) — The landmark paper. Evaluated IBM, Microsoft, and Face++ facial recognition. Error rates: 0.8% for lighter-skinned men vs. 34.7% for darker-skinned women. Introduced the **Pilot Parliaments Benchmark** (PPB) — diverse dataset that set new standards for AI evaluation. Prompted IBM and Microsoft to revamp their algorithms.
- **Actionable Auditing: Investigating the Impact of Publicly Naming Biased Performance Results of Commercial AI Products** (Buolamwini & Deb Raji) — Follow-up showing the impact of public audits. Led Amazon to confront their Rekognition product.
- [**Gender shades: Intersectional phenotypic and demographic evaluation of face datasets and gender classifiers**](http://hdl.handle.net/1721.1/114068) — MS thesis (2017)
- [**Facing the Coded Gaze with Evocative Audits and Algorithmic Audits**](https://hdl.handle.net/1721.1/143396) — PhD thesis (2022)

### Book
- [*Unmasking AI: My Mission to Protect What Is Human in a World of Machines*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/670356/unmasking-ai-by-dr-joy-buolamwini/) (Random House, 2023) — National bestseller. Chronicles her journey from MIT grad student discovering AI bias to founding the AJL and testifying before Congress. Explains how algorithmic harms affect healthcare, employment, and criminal justice.

### Art & Film
- **[The Coded Gaze](https://medium.com/mit-media-lab/the-algorithmic-justice-league-3cc4131c5148)** (mini-documentary, 2016) — Debuted at Museum of Fine Arts, Boston. Shows her discovering she must wear a white mask to be "seen" by facial recognition.
- **["AI, Ain't I A Woman?"](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=QxuyfWoVV98)** — Award-winning visual spoken word poem. Shows facial recognition systems misidentifying iconic Black women (Sojourner Truth, Shirley Chisholm, Serena Williams). Exhibited globally.
- **[Voicing Erasure](https://www.ajl.org/voicing-erasure)** — Collaborative spoken word piece on bias in voice recognition. Performed with Allison Koenecke, Safiya Noble, Ruha Benjamin, Kimberlé Crenshaw, Megan Smith, Sasha Costanza-Chock.
- **[Aspire Mirror](http://www.aspiremirror.com/)** — Interactive device that reflects a role model's face onto yours.
- **[Coded Bias](https://www.pbs.org/independentlens/documentaries/coded-bias/)** (documentary, dir. Shalini Kantayya, 2020/Netflix 2021) — Official Sundance Selection 2020. Features her Gender Shades research and the broader AI bias landscape.
- **Code4Rights** (film) — Advocacy organization using technology for human rights awareness.

## Venue Circuit Analysis
Joy operates in **three distinct circuits**:
1. **Academic/research circuit**: FAccT (where she's foundational), MIT, Harvard, Congressional testimony, White House summits — legitimizing the field scientifically and politically
2. **Art/culture circuit**: Barbican, Ars Electronica, Ford Foundation Gallery, Museum of Fine Arts Boston, Apexart, Nave Gallery — making AI bias visible through exhibition and performance
3. **Policy/media circuit**: TED, Netflix (Coded Bias), TIME Magazine, Fortune, BBC — converting academic findings into cultural and political pressure

**Key pattern:** She moves *upward through legitimacy channels* — from academic paper → Congressional testimony → Presidential advisory role. The art is not peripheral; it's what makes the scientific findings comprehensible to non-specialists and builds the moral urgency that moves policy.

## Why Interesting

Joy represents a **third way** between pure technical AI research and pure activist criticism: she does both simultaneously, using each to strengthen the other. Her concept of the **"coded gaze"** — the way AI systems encode the perspectives of their creators and miss those who weren't in the room — is directly relevant to Amber's interest in machine behavior and agent ethology.

She also embodies the poet-scientist archetype that Amber's research taste celebrates: technically rigorous (MIT PhD, landmark papers) but expressing findings through spoken word poetry, art installations, and documentary film. She shows that critical computing doesn't have to sacrifice technical credibility to be publicly impactful.

**For Amber's specific research:** Joy's work asks "whose gaze is embedded in this machine?" Amber's work on agent ethology asks "what behavioral patterns does this machine express?" These are complementary questions about machine perspective and behavior. As AI systems become more agentic, Joy's accountability frameworks will need to grapple with emergent behaviors — exactly what Amber studies.

**Oxford connection:** Joy was a Rhodes Scholar at Jesus College, Oxford — the same institution Amber is PhD-ing at (Human Centred Computing). This is a genuine proximity point in academic networks.
