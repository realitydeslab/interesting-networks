# Heinz von Foerster (1911–2002)

Austrian-American physicist and cybernetician. The founder of **second-order cybernetics** — the radical proposition that *the observer cannot be excluded from the system they observe*. Secretary and editor of the Macy Cybernetics Conferences (Vols. 6–10). Founder and director of the **Biological Computer Laboratory** (BCL) at UIUC (1958–1975), one of the most influential interdisciplinary research environments of the 20th century. Author of the Ethical Imperative: *"Act always so as to increase the number of choices."* Called "one of the most consequential thinkers in the history of cybernetics."

## Background

### Origins: Vienna Circle and Viennese Intellectual Networks
Von Foerster was born November 13, 1911, in Vienna, Austria-Hungary — into extraordinary cultural proximity. His relatives included **Ludwig Wittgenstein** (cousin) and **Hugo von Hofmannsthal** (the poet). His paternal grandfather was Emil von Förster, a prominent Austrian architect. His maternal grandmother was **Marie Lang** — Austrian feminist, theosophist, and publisher — an early signal of his lifelong interest in anti-authoritarian epistemology. He was shaped by the intellectual ferment of **Vienna Circle** positivism and Wittgenstein's language philosophy, but ultimately went further toward radical constructivism.

He studied physics at the **Technical University of Vienna** and the **University of Breslau**, receiving his **PhD in Physics in 1944**. During the Nazi era, he worked in radar laboratories; his Jewish roots "did not cause him much trouble" because an employer "chose not to press him for documents." He survived by strategic invisibility.

### Immigration and the McCulloch Encounter
He arrived in the **United States in 1949** with almost nothing, speaking little English. His critical break: he managed to get a copy of Norbert Wiener's *Cybernetics* (1948) and wrote a short monograph, *Das Gedächtnis* (Memory, 1948), on quantum mechanics of memory — which he sent to **Warren Sturgis McCulloch**. McCulloch was astonished and invited him to present at the **Macy Conferences** on Cybernetics. Von Foerster arrived as the youngest member of the core group, and was immediately asked to become **secretary and editor** of the conference proceedings.

He joined the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign (UIUC) in 1949, initially at the electron tube laboratory of the Electrical Engineering Department. He became **Professor of Electrical Engineering (1951–1975)** and **Professor of Biophysics (1962–1975)**.

### The BCL Years (1958–1975)
On January 1, 1958, von Foerster founded the **Biological Computer Laboratory** — an entirely unique research environment at UIUC. He directed BCL until his retirement in 1975, when lack of military research funding (the political climate had shifted) caused it to close in 1974.

### Later Life
After retirement, he and his wife Mai moved to a farm in **Pescadero, California** — the "Rattlesnake Farm." He continued writing, giving talks worldwide, and remained intellectually active into his 80s. He died October 2, 2002, age 90.

## The Macy Cybernetics Conferences — His Role

The Macy Conferences on Cybernetics ran from 1946 to 1953 (ten conferences in total). Von Foerster:
- Was **the youngest member** of the core group
- **Suggested the name "Cybernetics"** for the conference series (previously called "Circular Causal and Feedback Mechanisms in Biological and Social Systems" — following Wiener's book)
- Served as **secretary and editor** of the published Transactions
- **Edited Volumes 6–10** (1949–1953) of the Cybernetics proceedings — alongside Hans-Lukas Teuber and Margaret Mead — the primary historical record of foundational cybernetics conversations

The Transactions, as von Foerster edited them, were unusually faithful verbatim records that captured cross-disciplinary thinking-in-process. This editorial work brought him into deep contact with the entire first generation of cyberneticians: **Norbert Wiener**, **John von Neumann**, **Gregory Bateson**, **Margaret Mead**, **Claude Shannon**, **Warren McCulloch**.

## The Biological Computer Laboratory (BCL)

BCL was one of the most unusual research environments of the 20th century. Founded 1958, closed 1974.

**Focus:** Self-organizing systems, bionics, bio-inspired computing — analyzing and implementing biological processes using computational models.

**Visiting researchers (partial list, before 1965):**
- **[W. Ross Ashby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._Ross_Ashby)** (England) — design for a brain, homeostasis; received regular professorship 1961. Original Ratio Club member.
- **[Gordon Pask](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gordon_Pask)** (England) — conversation theory, cybernetics of education; stayed in constant contact after visiting professorship
- **[Gotthard Günther](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gotthard_G%C3%BCnther)** (USA/Germany) — polycontexturality, non-Aristotelian logic; received regular professorship 1967
- **Lars Löfgren** (Sweden) — formal systems, self-reference
- **[Humberto Maturana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humberto_Maturana)** (Chile) — developed autopoiesis partly at BCL
- **[Francisco Varela](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francisco_Varela)** (Chile) — co-developed autopoiesis, embodied cognition
- **[Ernst von Glasersfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_von_Glasersfeld)** (Austria) — radical constructivism
- **[Stafford Beer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stafford_Beer)** (England) — management cybernetics, syntegration
- **[John C. Lilly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_C._Lilly)** (USA) — dolphin communication, mind expansion

**Technical work:** BCL built the **Numa-Rete**, claimed to be the first parallel computer. It was financed by US Air Force, US Navy, NIH, NSF, NASA, Wenner-Gren Foundation, and eventually the Point Foundation in San Francisco. When military funding shifted to requiring militarily useful results in the early 1970s, von Foerster refused to redirect research accordingly, and BCL closed.

**Legacy:** BCL alumni reshaped multiple fields — autopoiesis, constructivism, conversation theory, management cybernetics, embodied cognition, complexity science. The BCL was ahead of its time in treating biology, computation, and epistemology as a unified field.

## Second-Order Cybernetics — The Theory

**First-order cybernetics** (Wiener): the cybernetics of *observed* systems — feedback, homeostasis, control, communication. The observer is outside, objective.

**Second-order cybernetics** (von Foerster): the cybernetics of *observing* systems — the observer enters the domain of observation. You cannot exclude yourself from the system you are studying. "The control of control and the communication of communication."

This was developed between the late 1960s and mid-1970s, with key inspiration from **Margaret Mead's 1967 ASC keynote** (which von Foerster titled "Cybernetics of Cybernetics" when publishing it, slightly emphasizing his own concerns).

Key implications:
- **Objectivity is impossible** — every description is self-referential
- **Ethics enters science** — if you cannot be outside, you are responsible for what you observe
- **Circularity is fundamental** — not a vicious circle but a generative one
- **Eigenvalues and eigen-forms** — stable recursive forms that emerge from circular processes

Second-order cybernetics was consolidated by the mid-1970s through:
1. Von Foerster's 1974 BCL course book *Cybernetics of Cybernetics*
2. Maturana & Varela's **autopoiesis** theory
3. Gordon Pask's **conversation theory**
4. Ernst von Glasersfeld's **radical constructivism**

## Key Ideas

### The Ethical Imperative
Von Foerster's most famous aphorism: **"Act always so as to increase the number of choices."** (Also formulated as: *"Act so that you increase the number of choices open to others."*)

This is his constructivist ethics: since reality is constructed through observation, the ethical act is to preserve the possibility of alternative constructions — to keep open the space of possible worlds. Closing down options, foreclosing perspectives, forcing single-frame reality = the ethical violation.

### Radical Constructivism
Reality is not discovered but *constructed* by the observer. The environment does not "inform" the observer — the observer constructs the environment through their cognitive processes. Knowledge is not a representation of an independent reality but an instrument for coping with experience. This position, developed with Glasersfeld, became foundational for educational theory, psychotherapy (systemic therapy), family therapy, and complexity science.

### Eigen-forms
Mathematical objects that are invariant under a recursive operation — the observer's "stable constructions." A way to formalize how circular cognitive processes stabilize into objects of perception and knowledge.

### The Von Foerster Equation (1959)
A population dynamics model (now standard in mathematical biology) describing how a population ages over time. His 1960 "Doomsday Equation" in *Science* — predicting infinite population growth by November 13, 2026 (his 115th birthday) — was a provocation designed to illustrate the limits of linear extrapolation. Characteristically, he used the exact date of his birthday, and the equation predicted Doomsday on *his* birthday — a self-referential joke with a serious point.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Von Foerster was primarily an **institution builder and community organizer**, not a conference-circuit figure in the modern sense. His venues:

1. **The Macy Foundation** — New York; where cybernetics was born (1946–1953). He was the secretary who made it legible.
2. **UIUC / BCL** — Urbana-Champaign; his 25-year base and the lab he built from nothing.
3. **American Society for Cybernetics (ASC)** — key venue; he was a central figure; published in its journal.
4. **Austrian/German academic circuit** — lectured extensively in German-speaking Europe after retirement; published *KybernEthik* with Merve Verlag (a Berlin leftist theory press).
5. **Constructivism conferences** — participated in the constructivism/radical constructivism network associated with Glasersfeld.
6. **The "Rattlesnake Farm" salon** — after retiring to Pescadero CA, continued to host and correspond. Not a public venue, but an intellectual salon at a distance.

His publication venues:
- *Science* (mainstream)
- *Cybernetica* (cybernetics journal)
- *Systems Research* 
- Springer (books)
- Fordham University Press (books)
- Suhrkamp / Merve (German theory publishers)

Signal: He chose Merve Verlag for German publication — the same press that published Deleuze, Guattari, Baudrillard, Foucault in German. This positioned cybernetics within continental critical theory, not just engineering.

Von Foerster is the ancestor of **machine behavior as ecology**. His core move — *you cannot study a system from outside it, the observer is always entangled* — is precisely the epistemological move Amber is making with agent ethology. He was saying this in 1968.

Key connections to Amber's work:

He also matters to Amber's **Vienna → CA intellectual lineage** interest: von Foerster, like Wittgenstein, was a Viennese Jewish intellectual who escaped to the Anglo-American world and transformed it from within. His intellectual lineage goes: Vienna Circle → cybernetics → constructivism → autopoiesis → embodied cognition → complexity science → ALife.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy

**Observer-inclusion as field-declaring move**: Von Foerster's core intellectual strategy was to take the *embarrassing problem* of the observer in science (the fact that you can't fully exclude yourself from what you study) and instead of treating it as a limitation to be minimized, **declare it as the foundational phenomenon**. First-order cybernetics tried to be objective; second-order cybernetics made reflexivity the subject matter. This is a classic "problem inversion" move — he turned a bug into a feature, and named a whole new field.

**Two-word concept coining**: "Second-order cybernetics," "eigen-forms," "observing systems," "computing in the semantic domain." Each phrase does work: it is memorable, citable, and contains a complete epistemological claim. The Ethical Imperative ("increase the number of choices") is a complete ethical system in six words.

**Provocation-first**: The Doomsday Equation (1960) was deliberate provocation — a scientific paper with a ridiculous conclusion (infinite population on his birthday) designed to make people think about extrapolation, models, and the nature of prediction. He was using science as a thinking tool, not just a knowledge-production machine.

### Career Architecture

**Secretary as power move**: Von Foerster arrived as an unknown refugee and made himself *indispensable* as secretary/editor of the Macy Conferences. The secretary is often underestimated, but editorial control over the proceedings is genuine intellectual power — you shape how the conversations are remembered, what gets emphasized, whose voices persist. This is platform-building through service.

**Institution as intellectual environment**: BCL was not organized around von Foerster's research agenda — it was organized around *encounters*. He brought Maturana, Pask, Ashby, Beer, Glasersfeld together and let the ideas emerge. His genius was curation, not control. The BCL is a model of **intellectual hospitality as research strategy**.

**Strategic refusal**: When military funding demanded militarily useful results (early 1970s), von Foerster closed BCL rather than redirect it. This was a career-shortening move that preserved intellectual integrity. It also meant that BCL's closure was clean — it didn't slowly degrade into instrumentalized research. **Knowing when to end something** is a rare strategic skill.

### Methodology Signature

**Physics intuition in philosophy**: He consistently used mathematical/physical concepts (eigen-values, wave equations, population dynamics) to make philosophical arguments concrete and computable. He could move between formal and discursive registers — his papers are simultaneously rigorous and literary.

**Dialogue as method**: Both *The Beginning of Heaven and Earth Has No Name* and *Part of the World* are structured as extended dialogues. He believed conversation was epistemically primary — you don't think alone, you think in encounter. This is itself a second-order cybernetic methodology.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **1948 — Write the monograph that earns the invitation**: Before he could speak English well, he produced *Das Gedächtnis* (Memory) — a short theoretical text applying quantum mechanics to memory. Sent it cold to McCulloch. This one move got him into the Macy Conferences as the youngest core member. **Lesson: produce one sharp, original text that demonstrates your framework; address it to the right person.**

2. **1949–1953 — Become the secretary**: Rather than trying to be the loudest voice in the room, he made himself indispensable as editor of the Transactions. He had access to every conversation, shaped how it was preserved, and built relationships with everyone. **Lesson: infrastructure and curation are forms of intellectual power.**

3. **1958 — Found BCL**: At age 47, having been at UIUC for nearly a decade, he created his own research environment rather than fitting into existing department structures. BCL had its own funding (military + foundation), its own visitors, its own agenda. **Lesson: when you have enough credibility, build the environment you want rather than optimizing within existing ones.**

4. **1967–1974 — Declare second-order cybernetics**: Taking Margaret Mead's 1967 ASC address and titling it "Cybernetics of Cybernetics," then building BCL's 1974 course book around this frame, he crystallized a new paradigm. **Lesson: sometimes naming and framing someone else's move is as powerful as making the move yourself.**

5. **1975 — Retire to Pescadero rather than compromise**: BCL closed; he moved to a farm. He continued writing and lecturing, but on his own terms. His intellectual output after retirement — including *Understanding Understanding* (posthumous, 2003) — remains the core of his legacy. **Lesson: protecting the quality of the project sometimes means ending the institution.**

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **The observer-inclusion move**: In Amber's work on machine behavior and agent ethology, there's an opportunity to make the explicit epistemological claim that *researchers who study AI behavior are entangled with what they study* — this is the second-order cybernetics move applied to AI research. It's a powerful framing that connects to a serious intellectual tradition.

2. **BCL model for lab-building**: Amber's research group at Oxford could be organized more like BCL than a conventional HCI lab — curate encounters, bring diverse people together, let ideas emerge from collision. The interesting-networks knowledge graph is itself a move in this direction.

3. **The Ethical Imperative as design principle**: "Act so as to increase the number of choices" is a concrete design principle for AI systems — more tractable than "be ethical" or "reduce harm." It connects to open-endedness research (maximize future optionality) and more-than-human design (whose choices count?).

4. **Build your own Macy Conference**: The Macy Conferences were designed to create productive cross-disciplinary collision — deliberate, curated interdisciplinarity. ALIFE workshops, small symposia, working groups are opportunities to build such spaces. Von Foerster's lesson: control the format and you shape the intellectual output.

5. **Use provocation for traction**: The Doomsday Equation got into *Science* not because it was a serious population prediction but because it was a *provocation* that made people think. Amber's papers (machine death, terrified agents) use a similar move — provocative framing that forces engagement with the underlying question.
