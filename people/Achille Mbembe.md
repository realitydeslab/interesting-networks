# Achille Mbembe

Cameroonian-South African philosopher, historian, and political theorist. Research professor at the [Wits Institute for Social and Economic Research (WISER)](https://wiser.wits.ac.za/), University of the Witwatersrand, Johannesburg. One of the most consequential living critical theorists — the inventor of **necropolitics**, author of *On the Postcolony*, *Critique of Black Reason*, and *Necropolitics*. A defining intellectual force in African philosophy, postcolonial thought, and the emerging field of African AI ethics and digital futures. Married to [[Sarah Nuttall]], co-director and professor at WISER.

## Domains
- **Necropolitics & biopolitics** — the thanatopolitical extension of Foucault: power as the right to expose populations to death, "social death," or "the zone of non-being" (Fanon)
- **African political philosophy** — subjectivity, time, temporality in the postcolony; African self-writing
- **Postcolonial theory** — though he rejects the label: his project is acceptance + transcendence of difference, not return to pre-colonial origins
- **Afropolitanism** — rethinking African cosmopolitan identity beyond nativism, nationalism, and ethnocentrism
- **Critique of Black Reason** — philosophy of race, capitalism, and the ontology of Blackness across modernity
- **Decolonisation of knowledge** — epistemological critique of Western universalism; African knowledge systems
- **Technology and the digital condition** — growing body of work on AI, surveillance, digital capitalism, and the posthuman from African/African diasporic perspectives
- **The commons** — ethics of shared humanity, universal rights, and the "right to breathe"

## Writing & Ideas — Conceptual Architecture

### 1. Necropolitics (2003 → 2019)
The foundational concept. Extends Foucault's **biopower** — the power to make live and let die — into **necropower**: the right to expose populations to death, slavery, colonial violence, and "social death." The **death-world**: a "new and unique form of social existence in which vast populations are subjected to conditions of life conferring upon them the status of living dead."

Key move: colonialism was never just an economic or political project — it was fundamentally a **thanatopolitical** project that Foucault's biopower framework cannot capture.

Judith Butler: "tracks the lethal afterlife of sovereign power as it subjects whole populations to what Fanon called 'the zone of non-being.'"

**Why this matters for AI/tech:** Algorithmic systems that expose certain populations to death (predictive policing, insurance redlining, drone targeting, healthcare rationing) are necropolitical systems. Mbembe gives this a philosophical foundation.

### 2. Afropolitanism
Against nativism ("Africa for Africans, return to pre-colonial tradition") and against uncritical Westernisation, Mbembe proposes **Afropolitanism**: an African subjectivity that is cosmopolitan, hybrid, and world-facing — at home in multiple cultures while rooted in African experience. Africa as **resource for universal thought**, not as exception or victim.

### 3. Critique of Black Reason
The major philosophical synthesis. Traces how **"Black reason"** — the Western construction of Blackness as an ontological category for exploitation — operates across: the slave trade, colonialism, apartheid, and now digital capitalism. Race is not a natural category but a **technology of power**. In the digital era: biometric surveillance, facial recognition, platform capitalism continue this logic.

### 4. The Digital Condition & African AI Futures
Recent work (post-2020) explicitly addresses:
- **Surveillance capitalism** as an extension of colonial extraction logics
- **AI and the African body** — facial recognition trained on non-African faces; biometric data extraction from Global South populations
- **Digital colonialism** — the infrastructure of the internet as a new colonial layer
- The possibility of African-centered AI ethics grounded in ubuntu philosophy and relational ontology

### 5. Universal Right to Breathe (2020)
The COVID-19 essay became a major document of the pandemic moment. Connects:
- Respiratory justice (breathing as the commons)
- George Floyd ("I can't breathe")
- COVID-19 as revealing our failure to share planetary commons
- The need to think together across species, generations, and geographies

"We have never learned to live with all living species, have never really worried about the damage we as humans wreak on the lungs of the earth."

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy
**Concept amplification:** Mbembe takes an existing concept (Foucault's biopower) and extends it to cover the terrain that Foucault's Eurocentrism prevented him from seeing. This is a signature move: find the gap, fill it with African experience. *Critique of Black Reason* does the same to Kant's "reason."

**Two-word concept coining:** "necropolitics," "Afropolitanism," "death-world," "living dead" — all memorable, all became citeable. This is how you enter the citation economy.

**Provocation-first:** His work is deliberately confrontational — it names Western liberalism as complicit in death. This generates debate, which generates citations.

### Career Architecture
**Institution bridging:** He moved through Anglophone institutions (Columbia, Penn, Brookings, Harvard, Duke) while remaining based in a French intellectual tradition AND in an African institution (WISER, CODESRIA). This triangulation made him irreplaceable — no one else occupied that precise intersection.

**Platform building:** Co-founding Les Ateliers de la Pensée de Dakar was not just an intellectual event — it was **building the infrastructure for African critical thought**. This is a 25-year investment that now positions him as the center of a network.

**Honorary degrees as signal:** Two European honorary doctorates signal that he's not only read in the Global South but that European institutions recognize his authority. This validates him for audiences that require European institutional endorsement.

### Key Moves (Chronological)
1. **1988-2000: Anglophone credentialing** — Columbia, Penn, Brookings, CODESRIA. Builds CV in US institutions while maintaining African base. Creates the institutional legitimacy needed for his later theoretical work to travel.
2. **2000: *On the Postcolony*** — positions him as the African postcolonial theorist (even though he'll later reject the label). The book makes Africa legible to Western theory on its own terms.
3. **2003: "Necropolitics" essay in *Public Culture*** — the concept launch. Hits the major interdisciplinary journal at the right moment (post-9/11, War on Terror). Becomes one of the most cited papers in critical theory.
4. **2006-2011: Duke residency** — enters the heart of the American university system as visiting professor at the institution that publishes him. Consolidates the US network.
5. **2013: *Critique de la raison nègre* (French original)** — the synthesis work, showing that the necropolitics framework connects to a larger philosophy of race and reason.
6. **2015 onwards: Les Ateliers de la Pensée + German awards** — builds the African intellectual infrastructure while getting European recognition simultaneously.
7. **2020: "Universal Right to Breathe"** — moves into the media/essay space during a global crisis. Shows how to translate theory into public language without losing the philosophy.

### What Amber Can Learn
- **The gap move:** Find where a famous theorist's Eurocentrism created a blind spot. Fill it with non-Western experience. The new concept you generate is yours.
- **Infrastructure building:** Create the forum, the event, the institute — not just the paper. Les Ateliers de la Pensée is Mbembe's greatest contribution to African thought, more than any single book.
- **Triangulation:** Be legible to multiple audiences simultaneously (Francophone theory, Anglophone academy, African humanities, European prizes). Don't choose.
- **Crisis essay:** When a global event happens (COVID, AI winter, AGI moment), be ready with the framework you've built for years. The "right to breathe" essay worked because he'd been building necropolitics for 17 years.
- **Translation investment:** His books translate into 13 languages because the concepts travel. Invest in concepts that are translatable, not just arguments.
