# Saodat Ismailova

Uzbek filmmaker and video artist working at the intersection of cinema, sound and visual art. Central figure of the first post-Soviet generation of Central Asian artists. Her practice excavates the spiritual, ecological and feminine dimensions of Central Asian culture — Sufi mysticism, animist traditions, oral memory, colonial erasure — through immersive multi-channel video installations, films, sculpture and performance. Founded [[DAVRA]] collective (2021), the first pan-Central Asian contemporary art research group.

## Background

Ismailova was born in 1981 in Tashkent, then part of the Soviet Union. Her passion for cinema was instilled by her father, an Uzbek cinematographer, and her grandmother, a storyteller — two genealogical strands (visual/technical, oral/mythic) that define her entire practice. She was drawn early to Soviet filmmakers who broke from socialist realism: [Sergei Parajanov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Parajanov) and [Andrei Tarkovsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrei_Tarkovsky) — filmmakers who used cinema as spiritual, not ideological, space.

After graduating from the [Uzbekistan State Institute of Arts and Culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uzbekistan_State_Institute_of_Arts_and_Culture) in film (winning the Grand Prix at the Tashkent Student Film Festival in 1999), she moved to Italy for a residency at [Fabrica](https://www.fabrica.it/), Benetton's research and communication center in Treviso — a hub for experimental creative work. While there she directed documentary films about Central Asian music for [Smithsonian Folkways](https://folkways.si.edu/), suggesting an early ethnographic/archival instinct.

Her debut documentary *Aral: Fishing in an Invisible Sea* (2004, co-directed with [Carlos Casas](https://www.carloscasas.net/)) won Best Documentary at the Turin Film Festival — an ecological tragedy, the desiccation of the Aral Sea, witnessed through Kazakh and Uzbek fishermen. The film marked her lifelong concern: **landscape as archive of colonial destruction**.

In 2005 she received a place on the prestigious [DAAD Artists-in-Berlin Program](https://www.daad.de/en/the-daad/artists-in-berlin-programme/), where she developed her feature film project. In 2008 she founded **MAP**, a Central Asian production company. The feature *40 Days of Silence* (shot in Tajikistan) premiered at Berlinale Forum in 2014, nominated for Best Debut Film.

The decisive turn came in 2013 with her first video installation *Zukhra* at the Venice Biennale (Central Asian Pavilion), moving her from cinema into the gallery. She then studied at [Le Fresnoy](https://www.lefresnoy.net/en/) — France's National Studio of Contemporary Arts in Tourcoing — from 2015 to 2018, where she developed *Stains of Oxus*, *Two Horizons*, and *The Haunted*. Le Fresnoy gave her the technical infrastructure for large-scale multi-channel installation work and embedded her in French contemporary art circuits.

Since 2017 she has been fully established in the international contemporary art world while remaining "deeply rooted in the region as a source of creative inspiration" (documenta fifteen).

In 2021 she founded [[DAVRA]] — the first collective dedicated to researching, documenting and disseminating Central Asian culture — bringing together 18 artists from Kazakhstan, Kyrgyzstan, Tajikistan and Uzbekistan. DAVRA participated in documenta fifteen as lumbung members, running a 40-day programme.

## Domains

- **Central Asian spiritual heritage** — Sufi traditions, chilltan spirit beings (shapeshifters of no fixed gender), oral knowledge systems passed mother to daughter, ritual healing
- **Post-Soviet ecological memory** — the Aral Sea catastrophe, Caspian/Turan tiger extinction, destruction of landscapes and languages under Soviet and post-Soviet regimes
- **Women's identities and emancipation** — women as custodians of cultural and spiritual heritage; veil as site of contested autonomy; female genealogies
- **Archival and found footage practice** — Soviet cinema archive, early Uzbek films, ethnographic materials, textile traditions
- **Multi-channel video installation** — immersive, multi-sensory, music-forward environments
- **Sculpture and material practice** — horsehair, neon, resin, gold — material culture as carrier of memory
- **Performance and collective practice** — DAVRA as live research group; *Qyrq Qyz* as interdisciplinary performance

## Key Works

### Films
- **[*Aral: Fishing in an Invisible Sea*](https://vimeo.com/817181088/ec1de7138d)** (2004, with Carlos Casas) — documentary on the desiccation of the Aral Sea. Best Documentary, Turin Film Festival.
- **[*40 Days of Silence*](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt2788236/)** (2014) — debut feature. A young woman's retreat into silence in a Tajik village. Berlin International Film Festival, Forum section, nominated Best Debut Film.
- **[*Her Five Lives*](https://www.asianfilmarchive.org/)** (2020) — commissioned by the [Asian Film Archive](https://www.asianfilmarchive.org/). Charted the history of women in Uzbekistan across five life-stages.

### Video Installations
- **[*Zukhra*](https://www.saodatismailova.com/)** (2013) — first video installation. A woman narrates Uzbek history while lying in bed. *Zukhra* is the Uzbek name for Venus/the Evening Star. Venice Biennale, Central Asian Pavilion.
- **[*Stains of Oxus*](https://www.saodatismailova.com/)** (2016) — three-channel HD video installation, 23 min, 4.1 surround. The Amu Darya (Oxus) river and Soviet-era environmental destruction. Developed at Le Fresnoy.
- **[*The Haunted*](https://www.saodatismailova.com/)** (2017) — HD video, 24 min. About the Caspian/Turan tiger, extinct since the 1970s. Made during OCA Norway residency. Premiered at Tromsø Kunstforening.
- **[*Two Horizons*](https://www.saodatismailova.com/)** (2017) — two-channel HD video, 24 min, 4.1 surround. Developed at Le Fresnoy.
- **[*Qyrq Qyz*](https://www.saodatismailova.com/)** (2018) — multimedia performance/installation. Retells the Central Asian legend of 40 warrior women. BAM Brooklyn, Musée du quai Branly Paris.
- **[*Her Right*](https://www.saodatismailova.com/)** (2020) — video collage, 15 min, b&w. Women's religious autonomy and the veil.
- **[*What was my name?*](https://aspangallery.com/en/artists/saodat-ismailova)** (2021) — installation with horsehair and neon. Solo show at Aspan Gallery, Almaty.
- **[*Chilltan*](https://documenta-fifteen.de/en/lumbung-members-artists/saodat-ismailova/)** (2022) — documenta fifteen. Labyrinthine work across basement rooms of the Fridericianum. Combines film, performance, environment, silk cloth, artisanal Uzbek mattresses, shamanic texts. DAVRA ran concurrent 40-day programme.
- **[*Chillahona*](https://www.labiennale.org/)** (2022) — three-channel video. Venice Biennale *The Milk of Dreams*. Reimagines Tashkent traditional embroidery; set in perestroika-era Uzbekistan.
- **[*Arslanbob*](https://pirellihangarbicocca.org/en/exhibition/saodat-ismailova/)** (2023–24) — newly edited film about the Arslanbob forest in Kyrgyzstan, a sacred walnut grove. Pirelli HangarBicocca.
- **[*Amanat*](https://pirellihangarbicocca.org/en/exhibition/saodat-ismailova/)** (2024) — golden seed sculpture. The seed as vessel of transmission across generations.
- **[*The Mountain Our Bodies Emptied*](https://pirellihangarbicocca.org/en/exhibition/saodat-ismailova/)** (2024) — resin cast of a cave interior. Pirelli HangarBicocca.

### Retrospective Exhibition
- **[*18,000 Worlds*](https://www.eyefilm.nl/en/programme/saodat-ismailova/841392)** (Jan–June 2023) — first major retrospective at [[EYE Filmmuseum]], Amsterdam. Title from Islamic cosmology: 18,000 worlds exist in the universe. Included film, video, photography, textiles, and new sculptures. Accompanying publication by nai010 publishers (€24.95, available online). Essays by Dina Akhmadeeva, Erica Moukarzel, Yuliya Sorokina, Filipa Ramos.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Ismailova moves in three interconnected circuits, distinct from the dominant commercial art world:

### 1. European Art-Film Biennials (the prestige circuit)
Venice Biennale, documenta fifteen, Sharjah Biennial. These are the top of the institutional art world — not commercial, but reputationally defining. Being selected for Venice *and* documenta in the same year (2022) is exceptionally rare.

### 2. European Institutional Solo Shows
EYE Filmmuseum Amsterdam, Pirelli HangarBicocca Milan, Le Fresnoy Tourcoing, Tromsø Centre for Contemporary Art. These are mid-size European institutions known for serious art-film intersection programming. Not MoMA or Tate — she operates at the level that values rigour over marketability. EYE in particular is a perfect institutional home for her work: it sits between cinema and contemporary art.

### 3. Performing Arts & Colonial Archives (bridging circuit)
BAM Brooklyn, Musée du quai Branly Paris, Asian Film Archive Singapore. These are venues that sit at a different intersection: ethnography, performance, world music/cinema. The quai Branly is France's museum of non-Western cultures (a complicated post-colonial institution). BAM is US's leading performing arts venue for international work. This circuit positions her as someone who transcends the visual art world into performance and film.

### 4. Central Asian Infrastructure (the base)
CCAT Tashkent, Aspan Gallery Almaty, Ilkhom Theatre Tashkent. She consciously maintains this base — not just for authenticity but as part of a structural project to build Central Asian contemporary art infrastructure from within. DAVRA is the institutional vehicle for this.

**The pattern:** She chose venues that have institutional legitimacy *without* being commercial, that are genuinely interested in non-Western perspectives, and that operate at the intersection of cinema/art/ecology/performance. She avoids the blockbuster commercial circuit and the London/NY-centred art market. This is a clear strategic choice.

## Why Interesting

**Amber's emerging interest in "AI × theology × mysticism × erotics" (via [[Bogna Konior]]) finds a living practice in Ismailova.** Her work is not about theology — it IS theology, in non-Western form. Sufi mysticism, chilltan spirit beings (shapeshifters), animist practices, oral transmission across generations — this is the exact terrain that Konior maps theoretically, but Ismailova embodies it as practice.

**The cosmotechnics angle is precise.** Ismailova represents what philosopher [Yuk Hui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yuk_Hui) calls cosmotechnics: a non-Western conception of technology (here: cinema, video, installation as spiritual technology) that operates according to different cosmological assumptions than the Western tradition. She uses film the way a shaman uses ritual — as a vessel that "carries and remembers everything." This is exactly the framework Amber needs to understand for his research on non-Western AI futures.

**The ecological × spiritual fusion** is rare and important. Most ecological art is secularized; most spiritual art is apolitical. Ismailova holds both: the Aral Sea catastrophe, the Turan tiger extinction, and the destruction of Uzbek oral traditions under Soviet rule are all one continuous catastrophe — colonial, environmental, spiritual. This is more-than-human thinking without the Western posthumanist framework.

**The DAVRA model** is extremely interesting as a research infrastructure pattern. She didn't just make art about Central Asian culture; she built the institution (a research collective) that could sustain that practice. This parallels the "communities as research infrastructure" pattern Amber is interested in (Future Observatory model).

**She is not in London or Cambridge, but Paris ↔ Tashkent.** Distance from Amber means encounter would require planning but also represents a genuinely different network to access.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

Ismailova operates through **cosmological inversion**: she takes dominant narratives (Soviet modernity, ecological rationalism, Western art history) and inverts them by surfacing the cosmological substrate they erased. The chilltan are not metaphors — they are the actual framework through which she organizes the work. The title *18,000 Worlds* is not poetic decoration; it is a literal cosmological claim: Islamic cosmology says there are 18,000 worlds, and she is giving us one.

She uses **number as cosmological anchor**: 40 (chilltan = 40 bodies; *40 Days of Silence*; DAVRA's 40-day programme at documenta), 18,000 (worlds in Islamic cosmology), 7 (seven generations). This is not mystical rhetoric — it's a structural use of non-Western numerical systems as compositional logic.

Her **material logic** is also cosmological: horsehair (animist), neon (industrial/spiritual), gold seed (generational transmission), resin cave (body as landscape). Each material carries a specific theory of transmission and memory.

### Career Architecture

**Phase 1 (1999–2012): Building the technical base.** Film school → Fabrica → Smithsonian Folkways → DAAD Berlin → MAP production company. She built a complete production infrastructure (both technical skill and institutional relationships) before attempting the gallery world.

**Phase 2 (2013–2018): The gateway works.** *Zukhra* at Venice (2013) and *40 Days of Silence* at Berlinale (2014) established her simultaneously in art world AND film world. Then Le Fresnoy gave her the infrastructure to make large-scale installations. This dual legitimation (film + art) is unusual and powerful.

**Phase 3 (2018–2022): Coalition building.** *Qyrq Qyz* (BAM + quai Branly), DAVRA (2021), *Chilltan* at documenta (DAVRA 40-day programme). She shifted from individual artist to cultural institution builder — creating the collective that then becomes her most powerful work.

**Phase 4 (2022–present): Retrospective institutionalization.** EYE Prize, Venice main exhibition, documenta, Pirelli HangarBicocca solo survey, comprehensive monograph. The institutional validation phase — but she's still making new work and new conceptual moves.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **2004 — *Aral* with Carlos Casas**: Established ecological concern and international cred with first documentary. Best Documentary Turin = immediate legitimacy.
2. **2005 — DAAD Berlin**: Got into the European art infrastructure early, before the gallery world was interested in Central Asian artists.
3. **2013 — *Zukhra* at Venice**: First gallery work at the world's most prestigious art event. The "star turn" move — maximum visibility for debut work.
4. **2014 — *40 Days of Silence* at Berlinale**: Maintained dual presence in film and art worlds. This is very unusual — most artists choose one lane. She refused to.
5. **2015–2018 — Le Fresnoy**: Got the technical training to make large-scale immersive work. The institutional training program as capability building.
6. **2021 — Founded DAVRA**: Transformed from individual artist to infrastructure builder. Created the institution that Central Asian contemporary art needed. Now she "owns" this entire territory.
7. **2022 — Venice + documenta in same year**: Extremely rare. Established her as one of the most significant artists of her generation, full stop.
8. **2024 — Pirelli HangarBicocca survey + monograph**: Locked in the retrospective narrative — this is the move that creates art historical significance.

### What Amber Can Learn

- **Build the institution you need.** DAVRA didn't exist; she created it. If the infrastructure for your research area doesn't exist, create it. This is not a side project — it becomes central to the practice.
- **Dual legitimation.** Operating in two fields simultaneously (film + art; in Amber's case: research + practice-based design) creates unique positioning. Don't choose one lane.
- **Cosmological anchoring.** She doesn't use Western theoretical frameworks as scaffolding; she uses indigenous Central Asian cosmological structures. For Amber: use Chinese, Buddhist, or Islamic cosmological frameworks as conceptual anchors rather than always defaulting to Western theory.
- **Transmissional frame.** "We are responsible for the seven generations before us and the seven generations to come after us" is a research methodology, not just ethics. It asks: what are we transmitting, and how?
- **Strategic venue selection.** She chose EYE (film + art), BAM (performance + world culture), quai Branly (ethnography + anti-colonial) rather than Tate or MoMA. She positioned herself in the venue interstices, where she could define the terms.
- **Technical training matters.** Le Fresnoy gave her the skills for large-scale installation. Don't try to make ambitious technical work without first getting the proper infrastructure support.
