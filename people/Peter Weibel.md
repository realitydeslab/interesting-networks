# Peter Weibel

(5 March 1944, Odessa – 1 March 2023, Karlsruhe) Polymath: artist, media theorist, curator, museum director. The intellectual architect of media art as a discipline. Raised in Ried, Upper Austria. Nomad between art and science — from Viennese Actionism body performance in the 1960s to running the world's largest media art institution for 24 years. Coined **BioMedia** — the concept that 21st-century media exhibit life-like behavior. Co-edited *Iconoclash* and *Making Things Public* with Bruno Latour. Artistic director of [[Ars Electronica]] 1986–1995. Chairman of [[ZKM | Center for Art and Media Karlsruhe]] 1999–2023. Over 70 landmark exhibitions. His final show: *BioMedia* (2022–2023) — his theoretical summa.

## Background

Born March 5, 1944 in **Odessa, Ukrainian SSR** (now Ukraine, then Soviet Union). Raised in **Ried im Innkreis, Upper Austria**. Studied French language and cinematography in Paris, then began medical studies in Vienna in 1964, quickly pivoting to mathematics with emphasis on logic. He never completed a traditional doctoral degree — instead assembled a cross-disciplinary formation in literature, philosophy, logic, film theory, and semiotics through an autodidactic trajectory across Paris and Vienna in the 1960s.

This unconventional formation is the KEY to his work: the blend of analytical rigor (logic, mathematics, semiotics — Peirce, Wittgenstein, Jakobson) with artistic transgression (Viennese Actionism, Fluxus), with institutional ambition (building ZKM into the world's most significant media art institution), with theoretical scope (60+ exhibition catalogs, major collaborations with Latour, Zielinski, Hershman Leeson). He was impossible to categorize — and used that impossibility strategically.

**Career arc in phases:**
1. **1964–1975:** Vienna avant-garde — visual poetry → performance → Expanded Cinema → video art
2. **1976–1989:** Academic/institutional positioning — professor at SUNY Buffalo, Universität für Angewandte Kunst Vienna, Städelschule Frankfurt; international exhibition circuit
3. **1986–1995:** Ars Electronica — defining the digital arts festival paradigm
4. **1993–1999:** Graz/Venice — chief curator Neue Galerie Graz; Austrian pavilion curator at Venice Biennale
5. **1999–2023:** ZKM — 24-year reign as Chairman; the defining global media art institution

## Domains

- **Media Art Theory** — the comprehensive theoretical framework for understanding computational, video, interactive, net, and "post-media" art
- **BioMedia** — inorganic media exhibiting life-like (organic, adaptive, generative) behavior: his conceptual name for the 21st-century paradigm
- **Post-Media Condition** — after mass media, after broadcast, after cinema: what is medium now?
- **Expanded Cinema** — dissolving the film frame into live performance, television, installation (1967+)
- **Constructivist Epistemology in Art** — art as cognition, not representation; observer theory; the art object as model of reality-construction
- **Art History + Technology History** — his argument: art history must include history of technology and science, or it is incomplete
- **Performance & Body Art** — early career; direct connections to Viennese Actionism

## Key Artworks

Weibel was simultaneously artist, curator, theorist — these roles are inseparable. His artworks:

- **Visual Poetry** (1964+) — concrete and visual poetry; precursor to conceptual art
- **Expanded Cinema performances** (1967–1972) — with [[VALIE EXPORT]], Ernst Schmidt Jr., Hans Scheugl; dissolving the cinematic apparatus into live action, mirrors, multiple screens, feedback loops
- **tv und vt works** (1972) — television action broadcast on Austrian national television ORF; querying video technology as mass medium; transcending the gallery space
- **Computer-aided video processing** (mid-1980s) — among the first European artists to explore computational video
- **Interactive computer-based installations** (from early 1990s) — onward into VR, network-based, participatory environments
- **Hotel Morphila Orchester** (1978–1980s) — band co-founded with Loys Egg; Weibel as musician/composer; experimental/rock

- **[*Iconoclash: Beyond Image Wars in Science, Religion and Art*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262523127/)** (MIT Press, 2002) — with Bruno Latour. Landmark. ## Viennese Actionism & Early Career (1964–1975)

Peter Weibel was not a "core" Viennese Actionist (that was Günter Brus, Otto Mühl, Hermann Nitsch, Rudolf Schwarzkogler) but was a fellow traveler, collaborator, and crucial connector:

- **Destruction in Art Symposium (DIAS), London, 1966** — Weibel participated alongside Gustav Metzger, Otto Mühl, Wolf Vostell, Hermann Nitsch. This was the first international encounter between Fluxus and the Actionists — a formative networking moment.
- **Happenings and Actions (1966–1968)** — participated in Viennese Actionist events; body art, transgression, performance in Vienna
- **Expanded Cinema (1967+)** — developed WITH [[VALIE EXPORT]], Ernst Schmidt Jr., Hans Scheugl. Inspired by American expanded cinema (Stan Brakhage, Ken Jacobs, etc.) but reflected ideological/technological conditions of cinematic representation as they existed in 1960s Vienna. This is the direct feminist / media-theory collaboration.
- **tv und vt works** (1972) — ORF broadcast; the moment he moved from gallery space to television as art medium, questioning video technology itself

His trajectory DIVERGED from the Actionists: while Nitsch/Brus/Mühl went toward ritual, transgression, materiality of the body, Weibel moved toward **media systems, technology, theory** — using the Actionist impulse (break the frame, question the medium) but applying it to information technology, television, then computers.

**Key Actionist relationships:**
- [[VALIE EXPORT]] — extended collaboration through Expanded Cinema and beyond (1967+). One of the great art partnerships of the era. VALIE EXPORT remains alive (b. 1940); Weibel was her partner in the late 1960s and co-developed the Expanded Cinema concept together.
- [Hermann Nitsch](https://www.nitsch.org/) — shared DIAS participation; Weibel later organized exhibitions engaging with Actionism's legacy (e.g., Bruseum, 2011 at Neue Galerie Graz with Anke Orgel)
- [Günter Brus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%BCnter_Brus) — Weibel curated the Bruseum at Neue Galerie Graz (2011)
- [Kurt Kren](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Kren) — Actionist filmmaker who documented performances; parallel trajectory to Weibel's film/video work
- [Wolf Vostell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_Vostell) — DIAS participant; Fluxus/decollage; broader avant-garde overlap

## Ars Electronica (1986–1995)

Weibel's role at Ars Electronica is his most underappreciated contribution:

- **1986:** Joined Ars Electronica as artistic advisor when it was still largely a technology showcase
- **1992–1995:** Became **Artistic Director** — the defining intellectual leadership period
- Under his direction, the festival transformed from a techno-art fair into a critically engaged platform asking fundamental questions about digital culture, cybernetics, society, and machine consciousness
- The **Prix Ars Electronica** (founded 1987) became the global benchmark for media art under this period
- Weibel introduced theoretical depth, performance, and critical discourse alongside the technology demonstrations
- He left in 1995 to take the ZKM chairmanship (1999) and the Neue Galerie Graz chief curator role (1993)

See: [[Ars Electronica]] for the full institutional history

## BioMedia Concept

Weibel coined **BioMedia** — his most important theoretical contribution to 21st-century art theory.

**The core claim:** 21st-century media will exhibit *life-like behavior* — not Bio Art (which uses living organisms as medium) but **inorganic media that develop organic features**:
- Adaptive behavior
- Generative / evolutionary processes
- Self-organization
- Responsiveness to environment
- Emergent complexity

This is NOT the same as "art about biology." It is the claim that the media themselves — code, algorithms, machines, networks — **act like life**. Software evolves. Generative systems adapt. This is the conceptual bridge between:
- Artificial Life (ALIFE) → BioMedia
- Computational Creativity → BioMedia
- Posthumanism → BioMedia

The *BioMedia* exhibition (ZKM, 2022–2023) was his theoretical summa — 60+ artists, including [Christa Sommerer & Laurent Mignonneau](https://www.interface.ufg.ac.at/christa-laurent/) (artificial life), [Stelarc](https://stelarc.org/), adaptive robots, generative AI works.

Key essays articulating the concept:
- Weibel, "BioMedia: The Age of Media with Life-like Behavior" (in the exhibition catalog, Spector Books 2023)
- Samuel Bianchini and Emanuele Quinz also contributed key essays in the catalog

## ZKM: The 24-Year Reign (1999–2023)

ZKM was founded by [Heinrich Klotz](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Klotz) (1989–1999) on a Bauhaus-inspired model. When Klotz died in 1999, Weibel took over — and his 24-year tenure defined the institution's global significance.

**What Weibel built at ZKM:**
1. **The theory** — positioned ZKM not just as an exhibition space but as the intellectual center of media art's self-understanding. Exhibition catalogs became major theoretical texts.
2. **The collection** — over 12,000 works, the world's largest media art collection
3. **The archive** — pioneered digital preservation; Laboratory for Antiquated Video Systems
4. **The international network** — brought Bruno Latour, Jeffrey Shaw, Siegfried Zielinski, major theorists and artists into ZKM's orbit
5. **The Bauhaus analogy** — consistently framed ZKM's mission as the Bauhaus of the digital age: where art and technology must compete and enrich each other
6. **Post-Western scope** — in later years (esp. *The Global Contemporary* 2011), consciously expanded beyond the Euro-American art world

**Key intellectual partnerships at ZKM:**
- [[Bruno Latour]]: three exhibition-books (*Iconoclash*, *Making Things Public*, *Critical Zones*) — the most productive collaboration of his career; connected media art to science studies, philosophy of politics, Gaia theory
- [[Jeffrey Shaw]]: *Future Cinema* (2002), *Net_condition* (2000), long collaboration; Shaw was ZKM's founding Institute for Visual Media director
- [Siegfried Zielinski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siegfried_Zielinski): several exhibitions (*DIA-LOGOS*, *Kunst in Bewegung*, *Bodenlos*); deep media archaeology connection
- [Margit Rosen](https://zkm.de/en/persons/margit-rosen): head of ZKM collections/archives; co-curated *Writing the History of the Future* (2019)
- [Christiane Riedel](https://zkm.de/en/persons/christiane-riedel): COO since 2002 — the operational partner who enabled the Weibel era

**The transition:** Weibel died March 1, 2023, days before his 79th birthday. [Alistair Hudson](https://zkm.de/en/persons/alistair-hudson) was appointed Chairman from April 2023 — a major ideological pivot from Weibel's media theory toward "useful museum" participatory practices.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Weibel operated on **three overlapping circuits** — unusually for an institution builder, he maintained an active curatorial practice beyond ZKM:

### 1. Central European Media Art Circuit
[[ZKM | Center for Art and Media Karlsruhe]] (his own) → [[Ars Electronica]] Linz → [[Neue Galerie Graz]] → [[Transmediale]] Berlin → [[ISEA]] → [[Stedelijk Museum]] Amsterdam → [[Centre Pompidou]] Paris

This was his primary operating territory: the European media art "triangle" of Karlsruhe-Linz-Graz, extending to the major European museum circuit.

### 2. Atlantic Theoretical Circuit
MIT Press (co-publications with Latour) → [[Venice Biennale]] → Seville Biennial BIACS3 → Moscow Biennial → international museum lending (ZKM collection travels widely)

This was his theoretical-curatorial circuit: connecting European media art theory to global contemporary art biennials, mediated through MIT Press as publisher.

### 3. American Academic Circuit
SUNY Buffalo → Nova Scotia College of Art and Design → Gesamthochschule Kassel → Städelschule Frankfurt

His early academic trajectory through North American and German institutions, teaching at centers of media art education.

**Pattern:** Weibel used ZKM as a **base of operations** rather than a retreat — he kept curating, advising, co-editing internationally throughout the ZKM years. The venue circuit reflects his model: build the institution, but don't let it become a bureaucratic cage. Maintain relationships across the field through co-curations, publications, and biennials.

### 💡 Career Inspiration
Weibel is perhaps the single best model for Amber's career trajectory:
- Combined **artistic practice + theoretical writing + institutional building** — the full stack
- Built his institution (**ZKM**) into the global reference point for his field
- Used **co-edited volumes** (with Latour, Shaw) as the primary mode of intellectual positioning — not solo monographs but multi-author landmark texts
- Moved between **practitioner and theorist** roles fluidly — never got trapped in one box
- Internationalized early: Paris, Vienna, Buffalo, Halifax, Frankfurt, before Karlsruhe
- The **BioMedia** concept is the model: one powerful concept that unifies practice, exhibition, archive, publication, and theory

### 🔧 Professional Inspiration
**Exhibition-as-research method:** Weibel's exhibitions were not just shows — they were **research programs with material form**. *Iconoclash* was 6 years of collaboration with Latour and 50+ scholars. *BioMedia* was a decades-long theoretical project materialized. Each exhibition generated a major publication (MIT Press or Spector Books), which then became required reading. Amber should think about exhibitions this way.

**The concept coinage strategy:** Weibel coined **BioMedia** at exactly the right moment (2000s–2020s) to name something that was emerging. Amber should identify what she wants to name — the two-word concept that will anchor her theoretical contribution.

**The Latour alliance:** Weibel found in Latour a peer from another discipline who shared his core intuition (images are not representations but actants; media construct reality, not reflect it). The Weibel-Latour collaboration produced work neither could have done alone. Amber should look for her Latour — a theorist from an adjacent field who shares her core intuitions.

### 🌍 Areas to Know
- **BioMedia / Artificial Life** lineage: Weibel → Christa Sommerer & Laurent Mignonneau → current AI-art generation. Understanding this genealogy positions Amber within the tradition rather than outside it.
- **Media constructivism** (Weibel's philosophical framework) — the argument that media don't represent reality but construct it. This is the philosophical foundation for taking AI behavior seriously as a research object (not just a tool).
- **Post-media condition** — Weibel's concept of what happens after the era of mass media. Now we are in "post-media" — everyone produces, distributes, consumes simultaneously. Understanding his analysis of this condition helps Amber theorize the computational creativity environment she works in.
- **European media art theory** (as distinct from American new media studies): ZKM vs. MIT Media Lab. Different epistemologies, different institutional models. Amber should know both traditions.

### 📚 Learning from Their Path
1. **Build before you write:** Weibel didn't just write about media art — he built the institution where media art happened. The theory had institutional force because ZKM had material force.
2. **Publish with major press:** MIT Press co-publications gave Weibel's curatorial work academic legitimacy. The choice of publisher matters.
3. **Find the right collaborator early:** The Latour collaboration began with a shared intuition. Don't wait for the perfect conditions — find the person who thinks in a complementary way and start making things together.
4. **Name things:** "BioMedia" is a powerful concept because Weibel named it. He could have described the phenomenon without naming it. He didn't. Name your territory.
5. **The CV as trajectory:** Weibel's career looks chaotic from outside — artist, curator, teacher, director. But it follows a clear internal logic: always moving toward greater institutional capacity to ask his questions. Amber should think similarly: what institution gives you the capacity to ask your questions?
6. **The importance of dying in place:** Weibel died at ZKM, at his desk, still working. He was ZKM and ZKM was him for 24 years. That kind of identification with an institution is only possible if you're building it, not just working in it.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

**This is about the meta-strategies Amber can learn from Weibel's career.**

### Conceptual Strategy

**Framework declaration at maximum scope:** Weibel didn't just observe trends in media art — he declared frameworks that organized the entire field. "BioMedia" is the canonical example: instead of describing individual works about biology, he declared a concept that made 60+ artists legible as part of one theoretical project. Then he built an institution (ZKM) and a publication series (MIT Press) to materialize that framework.

**Latent concept surfacing:** Weibel repeatedly noticed things that practitioners were doing without being able to name. His role was to NAME them — and the name gave the practitioners a common identity, made them citable, made them powerful. This is the "two-word concept strategy" from the RULES.md at maximum effectiveness.

**Cross-domain translation:** He translated between art history and technology history (his argument that you can't understand art without understanding the technology of its time); between performance and media theory; between semiotics and curatorial practice. Each translation created a new synthesis.

### Career Architecture

**Field declaration:** Perhaps the most important career move. Weibel didn't join an existing field (contemporary art, film studies, computer science) — he DECLARED a new field (media art theory) and built its institutional home (ZKM). The distinction between "working in a field" and "declaring a field" is Weibel's central lesson.

**Platform building at maximum scale:** ZKM is not a gallery or a lab — it's a museum + production studio + archive + library + academic publisher + residency program + conference organizer, all under one roof. Weibel understood that a single platform type is insufficient. The platform must combine multiple institutional modes to sustain a field.

**Coalition publications:** The MIT Press volumes (*Iconoclash*, *Making Things Public*, *Net_condition*, *Future Cinema*, *The Global Contemporary*) are coalition papers — they declare territory, bring collaborators under one banner, create institutional legitimacy across disciplines. Weibel was a master of the edited volume as territorial claim.

**Strategic longevity:** 24 years at ZKM is not tenure-capture — it's a deliberate strategy of building institutional depth. You can't build something like ZKM in 5 years. Weibel understood that the scale of what he wanted to do required sustained presence. The lesson: sometimes you should stay.

### Methodology Signature

**Exhibition as research method:** Every Weibel exhibition was a research program — years in development, with scholars and artists co-producing knowledge, concluded with a major publication. The exhibition was not the final product; the PROCESS (including the exhibition) was the research. This is fundamentally different from most curators who treat the exhibition as end-product.

**Art-science bridging:** Weibel didn't treat art and science as metaphorically similar — he argued they USE THE SAME EPISTEMIC OPERATIONS. Both construct models of reality. This is the philosophical foundation for all his institutional work and makes his approach unusual: not "art inspired by science" but "art as cognition" (hence the posthumous monograph's title).

**Archive as research:** ZKM's digital preservation work (Laboratory for Antiquated Video Systems) is not just conservation — it's research into the material conditions of past media. Weibel understood that archives are not passive repositories but active research tools. His media archaeology collaborations with Zielinski express this.

### Network Strategy

**The perfect complement:** Weibel found in Bruno Latour the ideal intellectual complement. Latour was a philosopher-sociologist studying how scientists make images, how objects have agency, how democracy is materialized. Weibel was studying how media construct reality, how images act, how art institutions make knowledge. They shared a core intuition from different directions. The collaboration was productive because it was NON-REDUNDANT — they each did what the other couldn't.

**The German federal model:** Weibel mastered the German public arts funding system — ZKM is funded by the state of Baden-Württemberg AND the city of Karlsruhe. Securing dual public funding insulates the institution from market pressures. This is a specifically European institutional model that Amber should understand (it doesn't exist in the UK/US in the same form).

**International circuit maintenance:** Even as ZKM director, Weibel kept accepting invitations to curate elsewhere (Seville, Moscow, Bad Rothenfelde). This wasn't distraction — it was deliberate network maintenance. The ZKM network was sustained by Weibel's personal presence in multiple circuits.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **1967: Expanded Cinema with VALIE EXPORT** — established Weibel as part of the avant-garde while connecting to feminist art politics. Timing: exactly when film theory was emerging as academic discipline. Effect: positioned him at the intersection of artistic practice and theoretical discourse from the start.

2. **1984: Double appointment (SUNY Buffalo + Vienna Applied Arts)** — simultaneously transatlantic. Established credibility in both North American and European art-media scenes at a moment (early 1980s) when the Atlantic divide was still real. Effect: enabled later MIT Press co-publications (American distribution for European theoretical work).

3. **1986: Ars Electronica artistic directorship** — transformed a technology festival into a critically engaged platform. Timing: just as personal computers were becoming cultural objects. Effect: established his reputation as the person who could bring intellectual rigor to digital culture without losing the practitioners.

4. **1989: Städelschule Institute for New Media** — founded an academic home for media art within an established art school context (Frankfurt). Timing: just as net art, CD-ROM art, etc. were emerging. Effect: generated a generation of European media artists trained under his theoretical framework.

5. **1999: ZKM chairmanship** — took over from Klotz's death and spent 24 years building it into the world's major media art institution. Timing: the turn of the millennium, post-web, pre-social media — the moment when digital culture needed a museum. Effect: ZKM became the institutional anchor for everything that followed in media art.

6. **2002: Iconoclash with Latour** — the single most important collaboration of his career. Connected media art theory to the most important philosopher of science of the era. Timing: post-9/11, when image wars were viscerally real. Effect: elevated ZKM's curatorial project to philosophical significance; made media art legible to non-art audiences.

7. **2022: BioMedia** — his theoretical summa, produced at 78, dying. Connected his early insights (media constructs reality) to the current AI moment. Timing: exactly as AI art was exploding and lacking theoretical frameworks. Effect: provided a lineage and a conceptual home for AI art within media art history. His final gift.

### What Amber Can Learn

1. **Name your territory.** "BioMedia" = a two-word concept that organized 60 years of Weibel's thinking AND an entire exhibition program. What is Amber's two-word concept? Not describing her interests — NAMING them.

2. **Exhibition as research method.** Don't separate your practice from your scholarship. The exhibition can be the research. The catalog can be the paper. The residency can generate the theory. Weibel never separated making from thinking.

3. **Find your Latour.** Identify the theorist from an adjacent field who shares your core intuitions. The Weibel-Latour collaboration was productive because they were non-redundant. Don't collaborate with people who think like you — collaborate with people who think in complementary ways.

4. **The edited volume as territorial claim.** Weibel didn't try to write all the theory himself — he curated intellectual coalitions through edited volumes. *Iconoclash* has 50+ contributors. It declares territory more powerfully than any solo paper could.

5. **Build the institution.** If no existing institution can host your research program, you may need to build one. ZKM was built because no existing museum could do what Weibel needed. This is a 10-20 year project. Start thinking about it early.

6. **Sustained presence beats strategic migration.** Weibel stayed at ZKM for 24 years. This is the opposite of the "strategic migration" pattern. It worked because he was BUILDING, not just inhabiting. The lesson: if you're building, stay. If you're just inhabiting, migrate.

7. **The art-science bridge must be philosophical, not metaphorical.** Weibel's claim was not that art and science are "similar" or "inspirational to each other" — his claim was that they perform the SAME EPISTEMIC OPERATIONS (constructing models of reality). This philosophical specificity is why the claim is powerful. Amber's art-science bridging should have this philosophical specificity.
