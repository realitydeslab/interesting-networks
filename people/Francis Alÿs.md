# Francis Alÿs

Belgian conceptual artist permanently based in Mexico City since 1986. Trained as an architect and urbanist, he abandoned practice after encountering the social upheaval of Mexico City's late 1980s. Over four decades he has built a sui generis body of work spanning walking actions, video, painting, drawing, animation, and documentary film — all unified by a single method: translating geopolitical, social, and historical tensions into *poetic physical acts* that become parables. His own description: "a sort of discursive argument composed of episodes, metaphors, or parables." He is most famous for *When Faith Moves Mountains* (Lima, 2002), in which 500 volunteers with shovels moved a sand dune 10cm — an action that crystallized his concept of the "social sublime" (coined with [[Cuauhtémoc Medina]]). Since 1999 he has maintained the long-running *Children's Games* documentary series — filming children at play in conflict zones and ordinary streets around the world, up to number 18 or more as of 2024.

## Background

**Origin:** Born 1959 in Antwerp, Belgium, in a small town called Herfelingen. He describes a childhood shaped by Bruegel the Elder (saw a major retrospective as a child in Belgium — a direct influence on *Children's Games*) and pre-Renaissance Italian painting (Lorenzetti, Fra Angelico), encountered during his student years in Venice.

**Architectural education:** Trained as an architect and urbanist at the Institut Supérieur d'Architecture de la Ville de Liège in Tournai, Belgium, then went to Venice for postgraduate study at the Istituto Universitario di Architettura di Venezia (IUAV). At this stage he was immersed in the Italian *Transvanguardia* (the highly figurative movement of the 1980s), Beat poetry, and Latin American literature, which he discovered through Mexican poet friends.

**Move to Mexico:** In 1986, Alÿs moved to Mexico City — initially as an architect, working for NGOs. The confrontation with extreme urbanization, social unrest, and political crisis in late-1980s Mexico City (the aftermath of the 1985 earthquake, the political crisis of the single-party state, poverty, and the chaotic energy of the megalopolis) prompted him to abandon architecture and become a visual artist. He has never left: Mexico City has been his permanent home for nearly 40 years.

**Becoming an artist:** Through the early 1990s he developed his method. First experiments were *The Collector* (1990–92) — walking Mexico City's Centro Histórico with a toy made of magnets on wheels, gathering metal fragments and generating a growing urban legend about the "crazy gringo" with the magnetic dog. He worked as a draftsman between projects to support himself, worked in charrettes (intensive paid architectural drafting sessions), and lived frugally while developing his practice.

**Intellectual formation:** Deep roots in Latin American literature (Julio Cortázar, Augusto Monterroso — the latter's flash fiction about faith and mountains directly validated *When Faith Moves Mountains*), fable as method (urban intervention through narrative rather than physical matter), crime fiction (daily reading), semiotics, art history, and contemporary theory. He stopped reading theory once he began producing and switched to newspapers and crime fiction — "to manage a production phase and absorption phase." His notebooks are 2/3 written text and 1/3 drawings — the script reduced to its fewest possible words is the proof of arriving at essence.

**Core method:** The script. Alÿs reduces each work to its essential script — ideally a sentence or two. The success of the method is that you don't need to witness the action to imagine it; the script circulates freely. "Sometimes doing something leads to nothing" — the paradox is already in the title, the action is already complete as text. Then: almost architecture, almost sculpture, almost painting — his practice refuses to settle in any category.

## Domains

- **Conceptual/performance art & walking practice** — durational urban actions as political metaphor
- **Documentary film & video** — long-running *Children's Games* series (1999–), conflict-zone documentary (*Sandlines*, *Reel-Unreel*, Iraqi Pavilion work)
- **Political allegory** — geopolitics made physical: borders (The Green Line, Gibraltar), postcolonial displacement, migration, historical trauma
- **Painting & drawing** — ongoing parallel practice; *Les Temps Du Sommeil* (small paintings on wood, 1996–present); Mexican sign-painting aesthetic
- **Animation** — short 2D animations (*Bolero*, *Song for Lupita*)
- **Children's games as ethnographic/political research** — cross-cultural documentation of play as a mirror of society and history
- **Conflict-zone art practice** — embedded war artist approach: Afghanistan, Iraq (Mosul front line 2016, Yazidi refugee camps), Israel/Palestine

## Key Works

All works on artist website: <https://francisalys.com/>

### Major Actions & Videos

- **[The Collector](https://francisalys.com/the-collector/)** (1990–92, Mexico City) — walking Mexico City's Centro Histórico pulling a magnetic toy on wheels, gathering metal fragments over weeks. Generated an urban legend about the "crazy gringo." His first experiment with narrative as urban intervention.
- **[Bolero](https://francisalys.com/bolero/)** (1996–2007) — short animation harnessing the rhythms of a shoeshine, with 500+ preparatory drawings. Combines the bodily rhythm of informal street labor with musical/aesthetic form.
- **Song for Lupita** (1998) — animation with the recurring phrase "mañana, mañana…" — temporal procrastination as cultural condition.
- **[Patriotic Tales](https://francisalys.com/patriotic-tales/)** (1997, Mexico City) — sheep herded in a circle around the giant flagpole at the Zócalo. With [[Cuauhtémoc Medina]] and [[Rafael Ortega]]. The ceremony of the absurd: the Mexican state's monument met by its actual flock.
- **[Paradox of Praxis 1 (Sometimes Making Something Leads to Nothing)](https://francisalys.com/paradox-of-praxis-1/)** (1997, Mexico City) — Alÿs pushes a large block of melting ice through Mexico City for nine hours until it becomes a puddle. The paradigmatic action: maximum physical effort, zero lasting result. The script as complete statement.
- **[Sleepers](https://francisalys.com/sleepers/)** (1999–present, Mexico City) — slide projection of people and animals sleeping in the streets. Documentary of informal rest in public space.
- **[Rehearsal I (Tijuana)](https://francisalys.com/rehearsal-i/)** (1999–2001, Tijuana) — a Volkswagen Beetle repeatedly attempts to climb a steep hill; a brass band plays a bolero faster and slower to correspond to the car's efforts. With [[Rafael Ortega]]. The political as Sisyphean loop. Latin American developmentalism as eternal rehearsal.
- **[When Faith Moves Mountains / Cuando la fe mueve montañas](https://francisalys.com/when-faith-moves-mountains/)** (2002, Lima, Peru) — 500 volunteers with shovels formed a single human line at the foot of a giant sand dune in Ventanilla (a shantytown outside Lima) and moved the dune approximately 4 inches. With [[Cuauhtémoc Medina]] and [[Rafael Ortega]]. The moment of the "social sublime": reaching the summit, seeing the sea, collective hallucination. Produced for the 3rd Bienal Iberoamericana de Lima. The work addresses the Lima of the post-Fujimori political crisis: "an epic response, a beau geste at once futile and heroic, absurd and urgent." Widely considered one of the most important works of early 21st century contemporary art.
- **[The Green Line (Sometimes Doing Something Poetic Can Become Political and Sometimes Doing Something Political Can Become Poetic)](https://francisalys.com/the-green-line/)** (2004, Jerusalem) — Alÿs walked the line of the 1948 armistice boundary through Jerusalem with a leaking can of green paint (24km, 58 liters). With Philippe Bellaiche, Rachel Leah Jones, and [[Julien Devaux]]. Previously performed in São Paulo (1995) with blue paint as a poetic gesture; the re-staging in Jerusalem flips the register: what was poetic becomes political. A filmed documentation was presented to a range of people who responded spontaneously.
- **[Seven Walks](https://www.artangel.org.uk/project/seven-walks/)** (2005, London) — A series of walks and actions across London, produced by [Artangel](https://www.artangel.org.uk/). Includes *Nightwatch* (fox in the National Portrait Gallery), *Guards* (a circuit of guards), and *Railings* (a stick dragged along iron railings). Accompanied by a rumour Artangel helped spread across the city, and a collaboration with composer Orlando Gough. Wall-based works from Seven Walks are in the Tate Collection; films are in the Artangel Collection.
- **[Politics of Rehearsal](https://francisalys.com/politics-of-rehearsal/)** (2005–07) — 30-minute video combining footage of a Truman speech, narration by [[Cuauhtémoc Medina]], and a striptease rehearsal. Parallels the sociopolitical promises of Latin American liberalism with the stripper's perennial delay — "always leaving something to be desired."
- **[Tornado](https://francisalys.com/tornado/)** (2000–2010, Milpa Alta, near Mexico City) — over ten years Alÿs entered the path of tornados in Tlaxcala/Milpa Alta, running into their funnels with a camera. With [[Julien Devaux]]. Duration as practice; elemental force as medium.
- **[Fabiola](https://francisalys.com/fabiola/)** (1994–ongoing) — Alÿs collected over 300 amateur reproductions of the portrait of early Christian martyr Fabiola (the original by Jean-Jacques Henner, 1885, is lost; only copies remain). A meditation on reproducibility, devotion, and the minor image.
- **[REEL-UNREEL](https://francisalys.com/reel-unreel/)** (2012, Kabul, Afghanistan) — produced for documenta 13. A game played by local Kabul children: they roll a film reel through the streets, as if the reel of Afghan cinema history itself is being unspooled and rewound. Screened at a satellite documenta venue in Kabul; paintings shown in Kassel.
- **[Paradox of Praxis 5 (Sometimes Doing Something Leads Somewhere)](https://francisalys.com/)** (2013) — presented at Cleveland Museum of Art (2023).
- **[Don't Cross the Bridge Before You Get to the River](https://francisalys.com/dont-cross-the-bridge-before-you-get-to-the-river/)** (The Gibraltar Projects, 2008–ongoing) — children on either side of the Strait of Gibraltar attempt to walk toward each other across the water on boats. The border as play; children enacting the geopolitics their parents live.
- **[Sandlines, the Story of History](https://francisalys.com/sandlines/)** (Iraq, 2018–2020; 61 min) — children of a mountain village near Mosul reenact a century of Iraqi history — from the Sykes-Picot agreement (1916) to the Islamic State (2016) — through children's sand drawings. Written and directed by Alÿs; co-directed and filmed by [[Julien Devaux]]; sound design by Felix Blume; produced with the [Ruya Foundation](https://ruyafoundation.org/). Premiered January 25, 2020. Screened at 16 festivals in 10 countries; won Best Feature Documentary Award (children's jury). The work distills his Iraq research — begun with a 2016 workshop in Baghdad and visits to refugee camps and the Mosul front line.
- **[Children's Games](https://francisalys.com/childrens-games/)** (1999–present) — ongoing series of short videos documenting games played by children around the world, in cities, villages, and conflict zones. Up to number 18+ at time of research. Locations include: Mexico, Iraq, Hong Kong, Afghanistan, Morocco (Tangier), Venezuela, Nepal (Kathmandu), Belgium, and many more. The series references Bruegel the Elder's *Children's Games* (1560). The games mirror each society's rituals, symbols, and political conditions. The most recent major exhibitions presented 25+ videos simultaneously as immersive installation.
- **Les Temps Du Sommeil** (1996–present) — ongoing series of small oil, encaustic and crayon paintings on wood (4⅓ × 5⅞ inches). Now exceeding 111 paintings. An intimate parallel practice running underneath all the public work — personal, dream-like imagery.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Alÿs operates in a distinctly non-commercial, geopolitically oriented circuit. His venue choices reveal a strategic logic:

### 1. Global South / Conflict Zone Sites
**Mexico City → Lima, Peru → Jerusalem → Kabul → Mosul/Iraq → Morocco → Hong Kong → Nepal → Venezuela**
The primary circuit is not institutional at all — it is geographic/political. Alÿs travels to sites of political tension, informal urbanization, postcolonial dislocation, and conflict. The venue is the dune, the green line, the street, the refugee camp. This makes his practice inherently site-specific in the deepest sense — not "installed in an interesting location" but *generated* by that location.

### 2. European Institutional Circuit (survey/retrospective)
**Tate Modern → MoMA/PS1 → Barbican → Wiels Brussels → Museum Ludwig → Copenhagen Contemporary → Musée cantonal des Beaux-Arts Lausanne → Eye Filmmuseum → IMMA Dublin → Portikus Frankfurt → Hirshhorn Washington**
His European/North American institutional circuit is firmly in the major museums and serious non-commercial venues. He does not appear in Frieze Art Fair booths or commercial biennales; his museum-circuit work is survey-based, retrospective, and focused on the documentary and action works rather than the paintings.

### 3. Latin American Institutional Circuit
**MUAC Mexico City → MALBA Buenos Aires → Museo Nacional de Bellas Artes Havana → MAC Montreal → AGO Toronto → Museo Tamayo Mexico City → MUAC/Zapopan**
The Latin American circuit is defined by serious public/university museums. [[MUAC]] is the gravitational center.

### 4. David Zwirner Gallery Circuit
**David Zwirner New York → David Zwirner London → David Zwirner Paris**
The gallery relationship (since 2004) provides commercial infrastructure without defining his practice. His gallery shows focus on the finished video/film works and paintings, positioned as the marketable distillation of actions that mostly happened elsewhere.

### 5. Biennale / Documenta Circuit
**Venice Biennale (Belgian Pavilion 2022, Iraqi Pavilion 2017) → documenta 13 (2012) → Bienal Iberoamericana de Lima (2002) → MOMENTA Montreal**
He appears at the major non-commercial international exhibitions — Venice, documenta — in the context of specific geopolitical projects (Iraq, Belgium). This is not a careerist biennial strategy but an opportunity-matching one: when a specific geopolitical framing aligns with existing work.

**Summary**: Alÿs chooses venues that serve the work's politics. He moves between Mexico City as permanent base → conflict zones as generating sites → major Western museums for retrospective survey → Latin American public institutions for regional dialogue. His gallery appears last in this logic, not first.

## For Amber

1. **🌍 Architecture of refusal** — Alÿs was trained as an architect, then abandoned practice to become an artist. But his architectural thinking — urbanist's attention to the city as social text, attention to movement and circulation, site-specificity — never left. Every work is architecturally conceived: the dune, the line, the border. This trained-but-refused professional identity is a productive model: what you trained to do shapes you most when you've left it.

2. **💡 The script as methodology** — His reduction of each work to a sentence or two ("someone pushes a block of ice through Mexico City until it melts") is one of the most useful conceptual tools in contemporary art. The script is: executable, non-media-specific, self-explanatory without documentation, and immediately imageable. This is directly relevant to designing research-based art projects: can you write the work in a sentence? If not, it's not finished.

3. **🔧 The "social sublime" concept** — Coined with Cuauhtémoc Medina for *When Faith Moves Mountains*: the moment of collective action that produces a sensation of the sublime in participants — a temporary break in ordinary social reality. This is a precise and useful concept for thinking about participatory art that aims for more than documentation. It's the opposite of interactive media art's user-testing logic: not "did it work?" but "did something sublime happen?" Worth deploying in Amber's participatory/computational work.

4. **🔮 Play as political intelligence** — The *Children's Games* series — now 25+ years of documentation — is one of the most sustained arguments that play is a mirror of political and social conditions. Children in Kabul play film reel as a game; children near Mosul reenact a century of history in sand. This is a research methodology: find the game that is already happening, film it, don't intervene. Amber's interest in machine behavior and embodied interaction connects here: what are the "games" machines already play?

5. **🤝 Conflict-zone embedded practice** — Alÿs has developed a consistent practice of embedded art-making in conflict zones (Afghanistan, Iraq, Israel/Palestine) that is not humanitarian tourism or "trauma tourism" but sustained research leading to works of genuine poetic power. The *Sandlines* collaboration with the Ruya Foundation (children reenacting history in sand near Mosul) is a model for how to work in extremely difficult contexts without appropriating suffering.

6. **🌐 Belgium → Mexico as identity architecture** — He is Belgian by birth, Mexican by choice, and globally significant by practice. He never naturalized (officially) in Mexico but his entire practice is generated from it. This migrant/expatriate position — insider-outsider — gives him a particular angle on place: he sees what locals can't see, and he brings what locals can use. This is a version of the "productive foreigner" dynamic that applies to many research-in-context practices.

7. **📚 Collaboration as method** — His major works are almost all collaborative: Cuauhtémoc Medina (critical frame), Rafael Ortega (camera/film), Julien Devaux (camera/edit), Tamara Chalabi (access/facilitation). The collaborators are permanent, not project-based: Medina for 25+ years, Ortega and Devaux for 20+ years. This is not the star artist with a team; it's a practice held together by sustained mutual trust. A model for research practice.

8. **🎭 The paradox of (in)effectiveness** — His key conceptual move is holding the tension between efficacy and futility. *Sometimes making something leads to nothing.* The sand dune moved 4 inches. The ice became a puddle. These are not failures — they are precise. The paradox is the content. For Amber's work on AI and power: what does it mean to make something that doesn't lead anywhere? What's the value of the poetic gesture when faced with structural conditions that don't move?

9. **🏛️ Institutional relationship** — His gallery representation by David Zwirner (one of the most powerful commercial galleries) and his working relationship with major public museums coexist with an almost anti-commercial practice logic. He's figured out how to be inside and outside the market simultaneously: the gallery handles the paintings and films; the major works happen in Lima, Jerusalem, Mosul.

10. **🎬 Documentary as ethical commitment** — *Children's Games* is documentary at its most patient and its most political. He follows the children "patiently, moving with their movements, but never getting involved in their games." This observational ethics — presence without intervention — is also a research methodology Amber could use for studying emergent behaviors in computational or hybrid systems.

## Why Interesting

Francis Alÿs is interesting to Amber for a cluster of reasons that converge on a single core:

**The political poetic action as a research method.** His practice demonstrates that you can do rigorous research — ethnographic, historical, geopolitical — entirely through actions that are also artworks. The *Sandlines* film (children near Mosul reenacting a century of Iraqi history in sand drawings) is both a historical document, a political artwork, and a research outcome. *Children's Games* is both a 25-year ethnographic archive and an aesthetic practice. This dual register — the work *is* the research, not the illustration of it — is precisely what Amber is developing.

**Scale of action / scale of system**: Alÿs consistently creates micro-actions (walk across a line, push a block of ice, move a dune 4 inches) that are *isomorphic* with macro-systems (the Middle East conflict, colonialism, postcolonial politics). The 4-inch dune shift doesn't "solve" Lima's social crisis — but for a moment, the absurdity of the situation is made visible. This is a model for working with AI as a medium: not "fix society" but "make visible a structure." 

**Children's games as a lens**: His bet — that children's play is a mirror of political and social conditions — has proven correct over 25 years. It's also directly relevant to machine behavior research: if you want to understand a system's values, watch what it does when not optimizing. What are the "games" AI systems play?

**The migrant/expatriate practice**: His positioning — Belgian, living in Mexico City for 40 years, making work about the Global South — is a study in the productive use of permanent outsider-insider status. This connects to Amber's own cross-cultural position (Chinese-British, AI researcher working in multiple institutional contexts).

## Research Taste Analysis

### The Method

Alÿs's practice embodies several principles that resonate with Amber's research taste profile:

**Script-first development**: Every work begins with a sentence. "Someone pushes a block of ice through Mexico City until it melts." This is how good research proposals work too — can you describe your intervention in a sentence? If not, the intervention isn't finished.

**Action > documentation**: The work is the action; the video/photograph/painting is the evidence, not the work. This priority is increasingly rare in an era of relational aesthetics where the documentation is the primary artifact. For Amber: what is the *action* in her research? Not the paper, not the installation — the act.

**Patience as methodology**: *Children's Games* is 25 years long. *Les Temps Du Sommeil* is 30 years long. He doesn't finish series; he maintains them. This is research-time, not project-time.

**The collaborators are the method**: Medina writes the critical frame; Ortega and Devaux film it; Alÿs conceives and performs it. The collaboration is not auxiliary — it's constitutive. The work wouldn't be the work without the specific relationship.

**The political is always specific**: He never makes "political art" in general. He makes work about the Mosul liberation of 2016, the Green Line of 1948, the Ventanilla shantytown outside Lima in 2002. The politics are always attached to a specific place, a specific historical moment. This prevents the work from becoming abstract commentary.
