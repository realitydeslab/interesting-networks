# Paul Dourish

Chancellor's Professor and Steckler Endowed Chair in Information and Computer Science at UC Irvine. Foundational theorist of embodied interaction. Author of *[Where the Action Is](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262541787/)* (MIT Press, 2001/2004) — the most-cited book in the TEI community and the canonical text translating phenomenology (Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty, Wittgenstein) into HCI vocabulary. Moved from embodied computing through STS/ethnography toward postcolonial computing, data politics, and responsible AI. Directs the [Steckler Center for Responsible, Ethical, and Accessible Technology (CREATE)](https://create.ics.uci.edu) at UCI.

## Background

Paul Dourish grew up in Britain, absorbed in the microcomputers of the late 1970s–80s (BBC Micro, Commodore PET) — a nerd-self that never left him (he builds his own 65C02 single-board computers as a hobby). He trained in AI and computer science at Edinburgh, then took a PhD in CS at University College London, which put him in the orbit of British CSCW and ethnomethodology — a formative encounter.

His career in research began at **[Rank Xerox EuroPARC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EuroPARC)** in Cambridge (early 1990s) — the legendary European sibling of Xerox PARC that was doing foundational work in media spaces, awareness, and cooperative work. He then moved to **Apple** (briefly), then to **Xerox PARC** in California as Senior Member of Research Staff — the center of HCI's industrial-academic world in the 1990s. He joined UCI's faculty in the late 1990s, where he has been ever since, accumulating titles: Chancellor's Professor, Steckler Endowed Chair, and now director of CREATE.

He holds appointments in **both Informatics and Anthropology** at UCI — a signal of the disciplinary crossing that defines his work. He also holds an Honorary Professorial Fellowship in Computing and Information Systems at the **[University of Melbourne](https://www.unimelb.edu.au)**, reflecting deep ties to Australian HCI.

Visiting positions at Stanford, MIT, IT University of Copenhagen, Intel, and Microsoft Research trace his network through the Anglo-American HCI world.

The intellectual biography: started in CSCW and awareness systems at EuroPARC → phenomenological HCI (the "embodied interaction" move) → STS-influenced ethnographic computing → postcolonial computing → materialities of data → responsible/ethical tech. Each move was a genuine discipline-level intervention, not just a topic shift.

## Key Works

### Books
- [*Where the Action Is: The Foundations of Embodied Interaction*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262541787/) (MIT Press, 2001/2004, paperback 2004) — The foundational text of the TEI community. Translates Heidegger's "ready-to-hand/present-at-hand" distinction, Merleau-Ponty's body phenomenology, and Wittgenstein's language game philosophy into a framework for designing interactive technologies. Argues that computing should engage with the whole embodied, situated person, not a disembodied mind operating a GUI. 35,000+ citations.

- [*Divining a Digital Future: Mess and Mythology in Ubiquitous Computing*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262528955/) (MIT Press, 2011, with [[Genevieve Bell]]) — A critical archaeology of ubicomp's myths, tropes, and ideologies. Collaborating with an Intel Research anthropologist, they examine how assumptions about "the home," "the city," and "the future" are embedded in ubicomp research. A postcolonial critique before postcolonial computing became a named field.

- [*The Stuff of Bits: An Essay on the Materialities of Information*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262546522/the-stuff-of-bits/) (MIT Press, 2017) — Examines how digital information is always material: stored in physical media, encoded in formats, governed by protocols, embedded in institutions. Draws on media studies, STS, and organizational sociology to argue against the idea of "pure" or "ethereal" digital data.

### Key Papers
- [**"Implications for Design"**](http://dl.acm.org/authorize?893743) (CHI 2006) — Critiques how ethnographic findings are deployed in HCI: typically extracted as design "implications" that strip out the critical and contextual content. One of the most-cited and most-debated papers in HCI methodological discourse.

- [**"Postcolonial Computing: A Lens on Design and Development"**](http://dl.acm.org/authorize?242188) (CHI 2010, with Irani, Vertesi, Philip, Grinter) — Introduced postcolonial theory into HCI. Argues that design practices embed colonial knowledge hierarchies. The paper that opened up a decade of critical computing work.

- [**"Ubicomp's Colonial Impulse"**](https://www.dourish.com/publications/2012/ubicomp2012-colonial.pdf) (Ubicomp 2012, with Mainwaring) — Examines Victorian rubber thieves as a lens on ubiquitous computing's extractive logic. Characteristically eccentric, deeply serious.

- [**"Awareness and Coordination in Shared Workspaces"**](https://www.dourish.com/publications/1992/cscw92-awareness.pdf) (CSCW 1992, with Bellotti) — **CSCW Lasting Impact Award.** Foundational paper on "awareness" as a design concept in CSCW. From the EuroPARC era.

- [**"Re-Place-ing Space: The Roles of Place and Space in Collaborative Systems"**](https://www.dourish.com/publications/1996/cscw96-place.pdf) (CSCW 1996, with Harrison) — **CSCW Lasting Impact Award.** Distinguishes "space" (abstract geometry) from "place" (socially constituted) — a framework that shaped subsequent location-aware computing, urban informatics, and HCI.

- [**"Algorithms and their Others: Algorithmic Culture in Context"**](https://www.dourish.com/publications/2017/AlgorithmsOthers-BDS-onlinefirst.pdf) (Big Data & Society, 2016) — Critical analysis of algorithmic culture. Argues that "the algorithm" is always embedded in social, institutional, and cultural contexts that shape its effects. Pre-dates the algorithmic accountability field.

- [**"Not The Internet, but This Internet"**](https://www.dourish.com/publications/2015/NotTheInternet.pdf) (Aarhus 2015) — "Digital feudalism" analysis of internet infrastructure. One of those papers that names something you knew but couldn't articulate.

- [**"On Being Iterated"**](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3313831.3376545) (CHI 2020, with Lawrence, Leong, Wadley) — **Best Paper Award.** Indigenous Australian collaborative design. Examines the "affective demands" on communities who participate in design processes.

- [**"Postcolonial Computing: A Tactical Survey"**](https://www.dourish.com/publications/2012/PostcolonialComputingTactical-STHV.pdf) (Science, Technology, and Human Values, 2012, with Philip and Irani) — Systematic scholarly treatment of postcolonial theory × computing.

- [**"NoSQL: The Shifting Materialities of Database Technology"**](http://computationalculture.net/article/no-sql-the-shifting-materialities-of-database-technology) (Computational Culture, 2014) — Technical databases as cultural artifacts and institutions.

- [**"Datafication and Data Fiction"**](http://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/2053951718784083) (Big Data & Society, 2018, with Gomez Cruz) — Narrating data and narrating with data; how data becomes story.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy

**The Phenomenology Translation Move**
Dourish's signature conceptual strategy is **translation from philosophy into design vocabulary**. The move: take a rich philosophical tradition (Heidegger's ready-to-hand/present-at-hand, Merleau-Ponty's body phenomenology) that CS practitioners would never read, distill it into actionable principles, and give it a memorable label ("embodied interaction"). This is "framework translation" — not original philosophy, but enormously valuable bridge-building.

**The Reframe-the-Question Move**
His second signature move: reframe what seems like a technical problem as a social/cultural one. Ubicomp is presented as a technical challenge → actually it's a cultural imagination (with Bell). Algorithms are presented as neutral computation → actually they're cultural actors embedded in institutional practices. Databases are presented as neutral storage → actually they're material and political. Each paper/book takes a technical object and shows its cultural underbelly.

**The Eccentric Case Study Move**
Dourish uses surprising empirical subjects (zombies, Victorian rubber thieves, spacecraft, parole officers) to defamiliarize familiar theoretical questions. The zombie paper isn't about zombies — it's about digital persistence and death. The Victorian rubber paper isn't about thieves — it's about ubicomp's colonial extractive logic. This is a rhetorical strategy: the weird case makes the theory vivid and memorable.

### Career Architecture

**Field Declaration via Canonical Text**
*Where the Action Is* (2001) is the paradigm case. The TEI community was forming; it needed a theoretical vocabulary; Dourish provided it. This is "field declaration through text" — a single book that founds a community's self-understanding. The timing was perfect: TEI was institutionalizing (first TEI conference 2007) and needed its canonical reference.

**The Coalition Paper**
The postcolonial computing papers (CHI 2010, STVH 2012) are coalition papers: multi-author manifestos that establish a collective position and territory. By co-authoring with Irani, Vertesi, Philip, Lindtner, Grinter, he built a network of scholars who all now cite each other and constitute a recognizable sub-community.

**Building Institutional Infrastructure**
CREATE (2020) is not just a lab — it's a center with fellowships, seed funds, and public programming. Dourish used the Steckler gift to build something durable. The center amplifies his research agenda beyond his own papers.

**Strategic Student Placement**
His former students now occupy key positions: Irani (UCSD), Lindtner (Michigan), Ames (Berkeley), Raval (UCLA), Cohn (ITU Copenhagen), Shklovski (Copenhagen). These aren't random placements — they're a distributed network of scholars all working in critical/social computing. As a group, they have sustained his intellectual agenda across institutions.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **EuroPARC → PARC (1989-1999)** — Positioned himself in the most productive industrial HCI research environment of the era. Co-authored foundational CSCW papers (Portholes 1992, Re-Place-ing Space 1996) that would win Lasting Impact Awards 20 years later. The PARC network gave him the institutional prestige to make bold theoretical claims later.

2. **"Where the Action Is" (2001)** — Translated phenomenology into HCI. Gave the TEI community its vocabulary. Made him a citation superstar (~35,000 citations) and an internationally recognized intellectual, not just a conference presenter.

3. **"Implications for Design" (CHI 2006)** — A provocation-first paper that challenged HCI's most cherished practice (ethnography-to-design). Positioned him as a critical voice within HCI, not just a designer of new methods.

4. **The Postcolonial Turn (2010-2012)** — With Irani, Vertesi, Philip, he imported postcolonial theory into HCI. A coalition-paper strategy that established a new critical subdiscipline. Timed perfectly: critical theory was entering computing just as HCI was diversifying geographically.

5. **"The Stuff of Bits" (2017)** — The materialities of information book extended his critical framework to data and infrastructure. Engages media studies, STS, and organizational sociology. This is the "second foundational text" move — consolidating a research programme, not opening a new one.

6. **CREATE (2020)** — Institutional capstone. The Steckler gift allowed him to build a center that will outlast him and amplify his research agenda across UCI and the field.

7. **SIGCHI Lifetime Achievement Award (2025)** — Formal field recognition that his career-long agenda has been accepted into the canon.
