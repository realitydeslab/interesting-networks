## Background

Born February 1, 1984 in California. Son of **[Jef Raskin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jef_Raskin)** — the HCI legend who initiated the Macintosh project at Apple and coined the term "humane" for interface design. This lineage is not incidental: it is the foundation of everything Aza has done.

At age 10, Aza gave a talk on user interfaces alongside his father at a [San Francisco SIGCHI (Special Interest Group on Computer-Human Interaction)](https://sigchi.org/) meeting. He later toured with the San Francisco Youth Symphony and studied physics and abstract mathematics at the **[University of Chicago](https://www.uchicago.edu/)**, earning a double BS in Mathematics and Physics (with dark matter research). He was trained to find the hidden rules beneath apparent complexity — a sensibility that runs through all his work.

In 2004, while his father was still alive, Aza worked with him at the Raskin Center for Humane Interfaces developing [Archy software](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archy_(software)). In 2005, Jef Raskin died of pancreatic cancer. This was the shaping event. Aza carried on — founding Humanized to develop Enso software (a linguistic command interface), then in 2008 joining Mozilla as Head of User Experience at Mozilla Labs, where he worked on Firefox for mobile, wrote the original geolocation API spec, invented Tab Candy (later Firefox Panorama), and introduced Ubiquity. He left Mozilla in late 2010 to co-found Massive Health (applying design principles to health behavior), which was acquired by Jawbone in 2013.

In 2017, he co-founded the **[Center for Humane Technology](https://www.humanetech.com)** with Tristan Harris and in 2017 launched **[Earth Species Project](https://www.earthspecies.org)**.

### The Jef Raskin Connection — Why It Matters

Jef Raskin (1943–2005): mathematician, physicist, musician, computer scientist. He conceived and started the Macintosh project at Apple in the late 1970s. His philosophy: technology should be "humane" — meaning it should work with human cognitive limits, not exploit them. He authored *[The Humane Interface](https://www.nngroup.com/books/humane-interface/)* (2000), which is the founding text of human-centered computing as a discipline. He also developed Archy and the Canon Cat — zooming user interfaces designed around human cognition.

Aza explicitly sees his work as a continuation: Center for Humane Technology ("humane" is Jef's word applied at systemic/societal scale), and Earth Species Project (extending the definition of "humane" to include non-human animals). The HCI lineage is: Jef Raskin → Aza Raskin → (now) researchers like Amber at Oxford HCC.

## The Infinite Scroll — Cultural Impact

In approximately 2006, while working at Humanized, Aza Raskin invented infinite scroll — the mechanism by which feeds, timelines, and content streams load new content automatically as you approach the bottom, eliminating page breaks. The design goal was seamlessness and reduced friction. The effect, at social media scale with algorithmic amplification, was something else entirely.

Raskin has publicly stated he estimates infinite scroll wastes roughly **200,000 human lifetimes per day** across the internet. He has expressed regret and traced it as a case study in unintended design consequences — perfectly aligned with his father Jef's philosophy about humane design (which sought the opposite). This became a central narrative in *The Social Dilemma*.

The irony is genuinely significant: the son of the man who coined "humane" interface design invented one of the most inhumane features of the attention economy. The recognition of this — and the decade-long attempt to reckon with it — is what drives his work.

## AI Safety Views

Aza is among the most prominent non-academic AI safety voices. His position:

1. **Catastrophic risk is NOW, not science fiction.** In "The AI Dilemma" (2023), he argues that existing AI capabilities — not AGI, not the theoretical stuff — already pose catastrophic risks to democratic societies, functional information ecosystems, and human sense-making. The risk is misuse, manipulation, and race dynamics, not robot uprisings.

2. **Race dynamics are the core problem.** He draws the parallel to social media: individual platform engineers didn't intend harm, but competitive dynamics (engagement metrics, growth targets) produced harmful outcomes at scale. The same dynamics now apply to AI development. Safety cannot be an afterthought.

3. **Institutions are not ready.** "What it would mean to upgrade our institutions to a post-AI world" — his framing is that existing governance structures (governments, international treaties, regulatory frameworks) were designed for slower-moving threats. AI moves faster than institutions.

4. **He advises heads of state and government officials** (as stated on azaza.org). He has appeared at the Vatican on ethics panels and at WEF. His AI safety work is explicitly policy-oriented.

**Compared to ERA fellowship circle:** Aza is NOT a traditional AI alignment researcher (no MIRI/OpenAI/DeepMind background). He's closer to the "AI governance and societal harm" camp than the "technical alignment" camp. He would resonate with:
- Joel Lehman's interest in open-endedness and unintended consequences of optimization
- The ERA fellowship's focus on transformative/existential risk from near-term AI
- The "race dynamics" framing common in AI governance circles

He is NOT primarily focused on RLHF, interpretability, or formal alignment — his concern is social/institutional/civilizational, not technical/mathematical.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
**Moral amplification of technical work:** Aza doesn't just invent things — he coins frames that moralize his inventions. "Infinite scroll" → "200,000 wasted lifetimes per day." "Freedom of speech is not freedom of reach." "The race to the bottom of the brain stem." These are memorable phrases that make abstract technical phenomena feel ethically legible. The move: take a design choice or platform mechanic, quantify its human cost, then give it a name that travels. This is how design critique becomes policy.

**"Hinge moment" positioning:** Both CHT and ESP were founded at moments of institutional crisis — CHT when the attention economy critique was crystallizing (2017: Cambridge Analytica era), ESP when AI was becoming powerful enough to plausibly decode animal communication. Aza has a strong instinct for when a field is about to become important.

**Empathy expansion as research frame:** His deepest conceptual move: continuously expand the circle of moral consideration. CHT: from attention to human flourishing. ESP: from human flourishing to all living communication. This is an elegant research program that generates new projects at each expansion step.

### Career Architecture
**Founder, not professor.** Aza has consistently chosen founding institutions over joining them. This gives him independence, agenda-setting power, and the ability to define new fields rather than publish within existing ones.

**Public intellectual as leverage.** He writes articles, gives talks, appears in documentaries, hosts podcasts. Each public-facing act expands the influence of the institutional work. The Social Dilemma has been seen by tens of millions. No academic paper can do that.

**The regret narrative as credibility.** His willingness to publicly regret infinite scroll is unusual and powerful. It signals: this is someone with enough moral seriousness to critique his own work. This is what distinguishes him from standard Silicon Valley tech ethics washing.
