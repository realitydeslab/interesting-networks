# Mario Tronti

<<<<<<< HEAD
Italian political philosopher (24 July 1931 – 7 August 2023). Co-founder of **operaismo** (workerism) with [[Antonio Negri]], [[Raniero Panzieri]], Romano Alquati, and Sergio Bologna. A towering figure in Italian Marxist theory, whose central contribution was the **"Copernican inversion"**: capital does not develop according to its own laws; rather, *working-class struggle drives capital*. 

Co-founded *Quaderni Rossi* (1961–1965) and then *Classe Operaia* (1964–1967) with Negri. Author of *Operai e capitale* (Workers and Capital, 1966) — the foundational text of operaismo, still untranslated into English in its entirety. His phrase "We too have seen capitalism, and it is irreversible" became the rallying cry of a generation.

Diverged politically from Negri in the late 1960s: where Negri moved toward extra-parliamentary autonomy, Tronti developed the "autonomy of the political" thesis — that the political sphere is not reducible to economic struggle and that the Communist Party remains a necessary mediator. He later made a controversial (to many) accommodation with the PCI, then with Italian Senate positions. Died August 2023, same year as Negri's death in December.

## Why Interesting
Understanding the Tronti/Negri divergence is essential for understanding the theoretical landscape of autonomism. Tronti represents the operaismo tradition's more state-engaged, party-politics branch; Negri represents its anti-state, autonomous branch. This split has echoes in every subsequent debate about whether radical movements should engage with existing institutions or build outside them. Relevant to AI governance debates (reform vs. exit strategies).

Italian Marxist philosopher and politician (1931–2023). Co-founder of *operaismo* alongside [[Antonio Negri]] — the two defining figures of Italian workerism. Founded *Quaderni Rossi* (with Negri, Panzieri, Alquati) in 1961 and then *Classe Operaia* in 1964 after splitting. His landmark *Operai e Capitale* (Workers and Capital, 1966) formulated the "workers' strategy" thesis that became the cornerstone of operaismo. Later diverged from Negri's autonomism by insisting on the "autonomy of the political" and returned to the Italian Communist Party (PCI). Served in the Italian Senate 2013–2018. Died August 2023 — three months before Negri, ending the generation that founded operaismo.

## Domains

## Key Work
- *Operai e Capitale* (Workers and Capital, 1966) — the key text. Formulated the central operaismo thesis: workers are not just objects of capital's power but its active subjects; the workers' struggle is the motor of capitalist development; the party must start from the workers, not from capital.
- *Il politico* (1979)
- *Sull'autonomia del politico* (1977)

## Why Interesting
The other pole of operaismo relative to Negri. Where Negri moved toward autonomism, anti-parliamentary politics, and eventually the global Empire framework, Tronti insisted on working within political institutions and the party form. Their divergence maps onto a fundamental question: can radical change come through existing political institutions, or must it come from autonomous social movements outside them? This debate is alive in every discussion of AI governance and tech regulation.

## Key Relationship
Diverged from [[Antonio Negri]] in 1964: Tronti → Classe Operaia and eventually back to the PCI; Negri → Autonomia and eventually exile. They died within 4 months of each other in 2023, ending the founding generation of operaismo.
