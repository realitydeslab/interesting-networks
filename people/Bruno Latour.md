# Bruno Latour

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-02-26:** Initial deep research. Full profile created from stub.
- **2024:** *How to Inhabit the Earth* (posthumous) published by Polity — interviews with Nicolas Truong translated by Julie Rose.
- **2022-10-09:** Died of pancreatic cancer in Paris, age 75.
- **2022:** *On the Emergence of an Ecological Class: A Memo* (with Nikolaj Schultz, Polity) — final completed book.
- **2021:** [Kyoto Prize](https://www.kyotoprize.org/en/laureates/bruno_latour/) in "Thought and Ethics" category.

---

French philosopher, anthropologist, and sociologist. Co-founder of **Actor-Network Theory (ANT)** — the idea that objects, microbes, machines, and institutions are actors equal to humans in shaping social life. Author of ~25 books translated into dozens of languages, one of the most cited social scientists alive. Held positions at École des Mines de Paris (1982–2006) and Sciences Po Paris (2006–2017). Co-curated four landmark "thought exhibitions" at [[ZKM Center for Art and Media]] Karlsruhe with [[Peter Weibel]]. His late environmental work — the *Facing Gaia* lectures, *Down to Earth*, *Critical Zones* — became the theoretical armature for a generation of climate-engaged artists and scholars. Died October 9, 2022.

## Background

Born June 22, 1947, [Beaune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beaune), Côte-d'Or, Burgundy, France — into a family related to the Burgundy wine dynasty [Maison Louis Latour](https://www.louislatour.com/) (not affiliated with Château Latour, Bordeaux). Raised Catholic; remained devoutly religious throughout his life, reading the Bible "devotedly."

**Education:**
- **Agrégation de philosophie** — ranked 2nd then 1st nationally in the 1971–72 French competitive exam (CAPES de philosophie). The agrégation is France's most competitive academic exam; top ranking signals elite formation.
- **PhD in Philosophical Theology**, [University of Tours (François Rabelais University)](https://www.univ-tours.fr/), 1975. Dissertation: *Exégèse et ontologie: une analyse des textes de résurrection* ("Exegesis and Ontology: An Analysis of the Texts of Resurrection"). Philosophy of religion → philosophy of science: the migration is telling.

**Career trajectory:**
- **Late 1960s–70s:** Developed interest in anthropology. Undertook fieldwork in **Ivory Coast** on behalf of [ORSTOM](https://www.ird.fr/) (now IRD, Institut de recherche pour le développement) — resulting in a monograph on decolonisation, race, and industrial relations. This anthropological fieldwork formed his methodological sensibility.
- **1975–1977:** Postdoctoral research at the [Salk Institute for Biological Studies](https://www.salk.edu/), La Jolla, California — where he conducted the famous ethnographic study of the neuroendocrinology lab that became *Laboratory Life* (1979). Studied how scientific "facts" are socially constructed in practice.
- **1982–2006 (24 years):** Professor, [Centre de Sociologie de l'Innovation (CSI)](https://www.csi.mines-paristech.fr/), [École des Mines de Paris](https://www.minesparis.psl.eu/) (now Mines ParisTech / PSL University). This is where ANT was developed with Michel Callon and Madeleine Akrich.
- **2005:** [Spinoza Chair of Philosophy](https://www.uva.nl/en/disciplines/philosophy/home/components-centrecolumn/the-spinoza-chair.html), [University of Amsterdam](https://www.uva.nl/).
- **2006–2017:** Professor, [Sciences Po Paris](https://www.sciencespo.fr/). First occupant of the **Gabriel Tarde Chair**. Scientific Director of the **[Sciences Po Medialab](https://medialab.sciencespo.fr/)** — digital methods laboratory for the mapping of controversies (Forccast pedagogy).
- **Also:** Centennial Professor, [London School of Economics](https://www.lse.ac.uk/).
- **2017:** Retired from regular academic duties.
- **2020–2022:** Critical Zones exhibition running at ZKM; continued writing on ecology and the "Terrestrial" condition.
- **2022-10-09:** Died of pancreatic cancer, Paris.

**Archives:** His papers were donated to the French [National Archives](https://www.archives-nationales.culture.gouv.fr/en/) and the Municipal Archives of [Beaune](https://www.beaune.fr/).

## Key Works

### Books (chronological)
- **[*Laboratory Life: The Construction of Scientific Facts*](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691028187/laboratory-life)** (with [Steve Woolgar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steve_Woolgar), 1979 / 2nd ed. 1986, Princeton UP) — Ethnographic study of a neuroendocrinology lab at the Salk Institute. Foundational for STS. Argues that scientific facts are socially constructed through network-building, not just discovered.
- **[*Science in Action: How to Follow Scientists and Engineers Through Society*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674792913)** (Harvard UP, 1987) — The key text of the sociology of scientific knowledge. Introduces the "black box" and the principle that technology is "society made durable."
- **[*The Pasteurization of France*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674657618)** (Harvard UP, 1988; fr. *Les Microbes: guerre et paix*, 1984) — Political biography of Louis Pasteur and the microbe. How social forces shape scientific acceptance.
- **[*Aramis, or The Love of Technology*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674043237)** (Harvard UP, 1996) — Semi-fictional investigation of a failed French transit project. Delineates the core tenets of ANT through storytelling.
- **[*We Have Never Been Modern*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674948396)** (Harvard UP, 1993; fr. 1991) — The modernity critique. The separation of nature/culture/society is a modern fiction — and it has never held in practice. We are all "nonmodern." Possibly his most influential single text.
- **[*Pandora's Hope: Essays on the Reality of Science Studies*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674653368)** (Harvard UP, 1999) — Essays using case studies (Amazon pedologists, Pasteur, atomic science) to develop his "empirical metaphysics."
- **[*Politics of Nature: How to Bring the Sciences into Democracy*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674013476)** (Harvard UP, 2004) — Political philosophy of nature; the "parliament of things." Non-humans must be included in political assemblies.
- **[*Making Things Public: Atmospheres of Democracy*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262122795/)** (with [[Peter Weibel]], MIT Press/ZKM, 2005) — Catalog of the 2005 ZKM thought exhibition. Politics as the assembly of things.
- **[*Reassembling the Social: An Introduction to Actor-Network-Theory*](https://academic.oup.com/book/37015)** (Oxford UP, 2005) — The definitive ANT textbook. Introduces the distinction between intermediaries and mediators.
- **["Why Has Critique Run Out of Steam? From Matters of Fact to Matters of Concern"](http://www.bruno-latour.fr/sites/default/files/89-CRITICAL-INQUIRY-GB.pdf)** (*Critical Inquiry*, 2004) — Landmark self-critique. Social criticism has been weaponized by conspiracy theorists; we need to move from "matters of fact" to "matters of concern."
- **[*On the Modern Cult of the Factish Gods*](https://archive.org/details/onthemodernc_lato_2010_000_10375375)** (Duke UP, 2010)
- **[*The Making of Law: An Ethnography of the Conseil d'État*](https://www.politybooks.com/bookdetail?book_slug=the-making-of-law-an-ethnography-of-the-conseil-d-etat--9780745639857)** (Polity, 2010)
- **[*An Inquiry into Modes of Existence (AIME)*](https://www.hup.harvard.edu/catalog.php?isbn=9780674724990)** (Harvard UP, 2013) — His most ambitious philosophical work. 15 different modes of existence (religion, science, law, fiction, politics, etc.), each with their own truth conditions. Accompanied by a [digital humanities platform](https://www.modesofexistence.org/).
- **[*Facing Gaia: Eight Lectures on the New Climatic Regime*](https://www.wiley.com/en-gb/Facing+Gaia%3A+Eight+Lectures+on+the+New+Climatic+Regime-p-9780745684338)** (Polity, 2017) — His late career environmental masterwork. Originally the 2013 Gifford Lectures at the University of Edinburgh. Gaia as a geopolitical actor. The "new climatic regime" as a rupture in human history.
- **[*Down to Earth: Politics in the New Climatic Regime*](https://www.wiley.com/en-gb/Down+to+Earth%3A+Politics+in+the+New+Climatic+Regime-p-9781509530557)** (Polity, 2018) — Short, sharp manifesto. The political implications of "landing on Earth." Earthbound vs. Humans.
- **[*After Lockdown: A Metamorphosis*](https://www.wiley.com/en-gb/After+Lockdown%3A+A+Metamorphosis-p-9781509550029)** (Polity, 2021) — COVID-19 as an occasion to rethink the relation between organisms and their milieu.
- **[*On the Emergence of an Ecological Class: A Memo*](https://www.wiley.com/en-gb/On+the+Emergence+of+an+Ecological+Class%3A+A+Memo-p-9781509555079)** (with [Nikolaj Schultz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikolaj_Schultz), Polity, 2022) — Final completed book. Argues for an ecological class as the political force capable of responding to the climate crisis.
- **[*How to Inhabit the Earth*](https://www.wiley.com/en-gb/How+to+Inhabit+the+Earth-p-9781509559466)** (Polity, 2024, posthumous) — Interviews with Nicolas Truong, translated by Julie Rose.

### Key Concepts
- **Actant** — any entity that acts or causes action, regardless of whether human or nonhuman
- **Blackboxing** — when a network stabilizes and its internal complexity becomes invisible
- **Translation** — how actors build networks by aligning interests
- **Inscription** — how technologies "write" programs of action into objects
- **Nonmodern/Amodern** — the alternative to modern/postmodern (we never separated nature and culture)
- **Parliament of Things** — political assembly of humans and nonhumans
- **Matters of Concern** — vs. "matters of fact"; toward a more engaged empiricism
- **Critical Zone** — the thin living skin of Earth (from geoscience), extended to political philosophy
- **Terrestrials** — Latour's term for those who choose to inhabit Earth rather than escape it

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Latour operated in two distinct circuits that he deliberately bridged:

**1. French Academic Establishment:**
Sciences Po → École des Mines → national education system. He was both an insider (Légion d'Honneur, top agrégation) and a permanent provocateur (deconstructing scientific objectivity).

**2. European Art-Philosophy Circuit:**
[[ZKM Center for Art and Media]] Karlsruhe is the central node. The "thought exhibitions" genre he developed with Weibel (1982–2022) is a uniquely European format — large-scale exhibitions as philosophical argument, in the tradition of documenta but more explicitly theoretical. His exhibitions drew artists, scientists, architects, and philosophers simultaneously.

**3. Anglo-American Academic Recognition:**
LSE Centennial Professor, Harvard UP as primary publisher, Oxford UP, Gifford Lectures at Edinburgh. He was deeply embedded in American and British humanities despite his French base.

**4. Prize Circuits:**
Holberg (Norway), Kyoto (Japan), Spinozalens (Netherlands) — deliberately non-French prizes, reflecting his international reach beyond la Republique.

**The key strategic choice:** ZKM. Latour chose to work with art institutions, not just academic publishers, to develop philosophical ideas. The exhibition format let him assemble artifacts, images, testimonies, and arguments that no single text could contain. This is the move that most clearly separated him from his STS peers (Callon, Law, Woolgar) — he became a curator-philosopher.

He read the field correctly and moved before it settled. This pattern of deliberate field-migration is a career strategy Amber can study.

5. **🌍 Areas to know** — **Sciences Po Medialab** and **controversy mapping** — a method for visualizing and teaching about complex sociotechnical controversies using digital methods. 7. **📚 Learning from their path** — Latour remained Catholic while developing one of the most radical critiques of modernity. The tension between religious sensibility and scientific critique was productive for him. He never resolved it — he held it. This is a model for living with intellectual contradiction.

## Research Taste Analysis ⭐⭐⭐

**This is about HOW Latour built his research empire — what Amber can learn from the meta-strategy.**

### Conceptual Strategy

**Framework multiplication × field declaration × two-word concept coining:**
Latour's entire career is a machine for producing new conceptual frameworks at the moment existing ones become saturated. The sequence:
1. STS / constructivism (1979–1987): *Laboratory Life*, *Science in Action*
2. ANT / symmetry (1987–1999): the actor-network framework as a generalization of STS
3. Onto-politics / "matters of concern" (1999–2013): *Pandora's Hope*, *Politics of Nature*, *Reassembling the Social*, AIME
4. Ecological / Gaia / Terrestrials (2013–2022): *Facing Gaia*, *Down to Earth*, *Critical Zones*

Each phase is enabled by the previous one but is a genuine conceptual rupture. **He never stayed in the same register long enough for critics to fully catch up.**

**Two-word concept coining as a discipline:** "actor-network," "black box," "matters of concern," "critical zones," "ecological class," "parliament of things," "obligatory passage point" — every phase produces memorable, citeable vocabulary. This is not incidental. Short conceptual monikers that travel across disciplines are a competitive advantage.

### Career Architecture

**Three key architectural moves:**

**1. The CSI years (1982–2006): coalition-building in a niche.**
The Centre de Sociologie de l'Innovation was not a prestigious institution. Latour and Callon built STS as a field *from a peripheral position* — using it as a laboratory where they could develop ideas without the constraints of established disciplines. This is the "Medici strategy" — build your own institution rather than fight for position in existing ones.

**2. The ZKM move (2002–2022): art-world amplification.**
Starting with *Iconoclash* (2002), Latour chose to develop philosophical ideas in collaboration with an art institution rather than exclusively through academic publishing. The thought exhibitions reached a global audience of artists, curators, architects, and scientists simultaneously — and generated massive catalogs (MIT Press) that functioned as philosophical anthologies. **The art world gave him access to publics and formats unavailable in academic philosophy.**

**3. The Sciences Po Medialab (2006–2017): digital methods infrastructure.**
Rather than just publishing about controversy mapping, he built institutional infrastructure — a laboratory, a pedagogy (Forccast), tools (digital controversy mapping). This transformed his ideas into a teachable method, ensuring they survived him.

### Methodology Signature

**Exhibition as empirical argument:**
Latour's most distinctive methodological innovation is the "thought exhibition" (Gedankenausstellung). Unlike a conference or a book, an exhibition can present *heterogeneous actors simultaneously* — which is exactly what ANT theory demands. The form enacts the content.

**Ethnographic empiricism as the ground for ontological claims:**
Latour never makes purely abstract claims. Every theoretical point is grounded in a case: the Salk Institute neuroendocrinology lab, Pasteur and the microbe, the failed Aramis transit system, pedologists in the Amazon rainforest. The method: go to where things are being made, observe, describe, then generalize. This gives his philosophy an unusually grounded quality.

**Self-critique as renewal:**
"Why Has Critique Run Out of Steam?" (2004) was a public act of self-sabotage: critiquing the field he had helped create. The timing was precise — he published this when social constructivism was becoming vulnerable to climate denial weaponization. By getting ahead of the critique, he preserved his intellectual authority and enabled his ecological turn.

### Network Strategy

**Weibel as co-conspirator:**
The Latour-Weibel collaboration at ZKM is a template for philosopher-institution partnership. Weibel provided venue, resources, audiences, and artistic networks; Latour provided conceptual framing and prestige. Each amplified the other. **Finding institutional partners who provide what you can't provide for yourself** is a key strategy.

**The CSI as a theory-lab:**
At École des Mines, Callon, Akrich, Law, and Latour were a productive cluster — each contributed different angles to ANT. Coalition papers (especially Callon's scallops paper, 1984, and Latour-Akrich's semiotics of technology) established ANT as a multi-author, multi-national enterprise rather than a single-author claim. This made it harder to attack.

### Key Moves (Chronological)

1. **1975–1977: The Salk Institute bet.** Go to the heart of Big Science — a Nobel Prize factory — and conduct ethnography as a philosopher. Risk: being dismissed as an outsider. Payoff: *Laboratory Life* became canonical because it had empirical authority no philosopher had before.

2. **1984–1987: Naming the method.** "Actor-network theory" gives the approach a portable name that travels across disciplines. Without the name, it's just a collection of case studies. With it, it's a field.

3. **2002: The ZKM turn.** *Iconoclash* — first thought exhibition. At 54, pivoting into the art world. Risk: being seen as a media philosopher rather than a rigorous academic. Payoff: exponentially larger audience and a format that matched his thinking.

4. **2004: The self-critique.** "Why Has Critique Run Out of Steam?" published in *Critical Inquiry*. This is the move that enabled the ecological turn — by publicly questioning social constructivism's limits, he created space for a more realist, "matters of concern" approach.

5. **2013: The Gifford Lectures.** Choosing the Gifford Lectures (one of the world's most prestigious lecture series, with William James, Hannah Arendt as precedents) to launch his ecological project. **Framing** matters: he didn't publish in an ecology journal; he delivered philosophical lectures in the tradition of natural theology.

6. **2020: Critical Zones during COVID.** The exhibition opened May 2020 — just as COVID began. The planetary dimension of Critical Zones suddenly became immediately legible to audiences who might otherwise have found it abstract. **Historical timing** rewarded the long preparation.

### What Amber Can Learn

**1. The exhibition as a research method.** Amber doesn't need to wait for a publication to make a philosophical argument. An exhibition that assembles the right objects, testimonies, and images can do philosophy. ZKM's model (large, multi-contributor, catalog as anthology) is replicable at smaller scale.

**2. Coalition work at the level of concept.** ANT succeeded because it was a multi-author enterprise. Build concepts with a close cluster of colleagues rather than defending solo positions. The coalition paper as a territorial claim.

**3. Time your field migrations.** Latour moved from STS to ANT to ecological thought at precisely the moments when each field became vulnerable to critique or saturation. Reading the field's trajectory — when is it getting stale? when is it peaking? — and moving before it settles is a career strategy.

**4. The Weibel template: find an institutional co-conspirator.** Identify an institution (art, media, design) that wants the credibility your philosophical framing provides, and that can offer resources and audiences you can't access alone. This is not opportunism — it's a strategic alliance.

**5. Empiricism as armor.** In every debate (Science Wars, climate, critique), Latour's empirical case studies protected him. Having been there — in the lab, in the field — gave his abstract claims a grounding that pure theorists lacked. Research practice as both a method and a credibility source.
