# Raqs Media Collective

New Delhi-based collective of three artists, curators, and theorists — **Jeebesh Bagchi**, **Monica Narula**, and **Shuddhabrata Sengupta** — working together since 1992. Co-founders of the foundational [[Sarai]] media lab at the Centre for the Study of Developing Societies (CSDS), Delhi. Publishers of the Sarai Reader series (2001–2013), a landmark of Global South critical media theory published as free, open-source PDFs. Chief curators of the **11th Shanghai Biennale** "Why Not Ask Again?" (2016–17) at [[Power Station of Art]]. Artists in Documenta 11 (2002), the first postcolonial Documenta. Their work engages time, labor, affect, knowledge infrastructure, and the politics of archives — now explicitly extending into more-than-human solidarity and cosmotechnics. Their 2025 film *Cavalcade* describes itself as a "cosmotechnical carnival" and openly collaborates with AI agents.

## Background

Formed in 1992 in New Delhi, Raqs — the name is drawn from an acronym (Rarely Asked Questions) but also invokes the Sufi concept of *raqs*, ecstatic spinning/dancing — began as filmmakers before expanding into installation, sculpture, writing, and curating. All three members come from film and humanities backgrounds, with deep roots in the intellectual milieu of Delhi's Centre for the Study of Developing Societies.

In 2000, they co-founded **[[Sarai]]** with CSDS faculty Ravi Vasudevan and Ravi Sundaram. Sarai was one of Asia's most important critical media labs in the 2000s — focused on media urbanism, free software, intellectual property, public domain, and what they called "media life." The [[Sarai Reader]] series (09 volumes, 2001–2013) became foundational texts, distributed freely as PDFs, pulling together writers, theorists, activists, and practitioners from India and globally.

Their art practice developed alongside Sarai, not separately from it — the same ideas about archives, labor, public knowledge, and political temporality appear in both the theoretical writing and the artworks. They describe their working method as inseparable from reading, research, and dialogue with others — "reading is also a way of eavesdropping and whispering."

Working languages of the three members: English, Bengali, and Urdu/Hindustani. They describe translation as their "mother-tongue."

## Key Works

### 2025
- **[Cavalcade](https://works.raqsmediacollective.net/index.php/2025/06/19/cavalcade/14455)** (2025) — 40-minute film + prints. Solo exhibition at Neubauer Collegium for Culture and Society, University of Chicago. Explicitly a "cosmotechnical carnival" — shot among the rocky outcrops of Gondwanaland and coastlines worldwide, following a procession where humans, animals, myths, and AI agents jostle together. Draws from footage of an annual religious festival in Deoghar, Jharkhand celebrating the nuptials of Shiva and Parvati, reframed as an expansion across species, borders, and time. Print works were shaped by "prompts exchanged among the artists and responses elicited from AI agents." Opening into AI explicitly — and via the frame of cosmotechnics rather than Silicon Valley futurism. Accompanied by talk at Neubauer with [[Dipesh Chakravarty]] and [[Prathama Banerjee]], moderated by [[Dieter Roelstraete]].
- **[Thirst/Trishna](https://works.raqsmediacollective.net/index.php/2025/07/23/thirst-trishna/14540)** (2025) — Multi-screen video installation, wallpaper, augmented reality. Shown at "Thirst: In Search of Freshwater," [[Wellcome Collection]], London (curated by Janice Li). Moves through step-wells in desert landscapes, stories of longing, the vast invisible reservoirs of water in deep space. Essay "The Parable of the Step Well" published in the exhibition catalogue.
- **[Blood Moon](https://works.raqsmediacollective.net/index.php/2025/06/20/blood-moon/14754)** (2025) — Clock-based work on how the moon affects humans through time. Shown at "From the Cosmos to the Commons," Stadtkuratorin Hamburg. Paired with earlier *A Day in the Life of Kiribati* (2014).
- **[Tables of Destiny, Overturned](https://works.raqsmediacollective.net/index.php/2025/06/13/tables-of-destiny-overturned/14544)** (2025) — Video installation (8′ 18″), shown at "Zangezi," Zurich Arts Weekend, Immersive Arts Space. Set at 5:27am on September 12, 1918 in Astrakhan — about time and historical contingency.
- **Wayfaring Ways To Be** (2025) — Solo exhibition, [[Hanart TZ Gallery]], Hong Kong. On walking, thinking, the upright body.
- **Biting Tongue, Biding Time** (2025) — Essay published for "Intensification" (e-flux + Lisbon Architecture Triennial 7th edition "How Heavy Is a City?" curated by [[Territorial Agency]]).

### 2024

- **Twisted Time** (2024) — Two glass bicycle wheels on a mirrored plinth, rotating slowly.
- **Borderlands** (2024) — Series of seven laser engravings on black sandpaper. Black-on-black drawings riffing off medieval depictions of animals.
- **[Na-Bam](https://works.raqsmediacollective.net/index.php/2024/01/31/na-bam/14477)** (2024) — Six-screen video installation with video wallpaper. Shown at [[Khoj Studios]], New Delhi and Le Commun, Geneva. *Na-bam* is a fishermen's term for places where the bamboo measuring stick fails to touch the bottom — water too deep to gauge. About flood, thirst, and the limits of measure.
- **Escapement** (2009, iterated) — Shown at Breguet Pavillion, Frieze New York (2022); "Liminal Gaps," [[NMACC]], Mumbai (2024).

### 2021
- **[The Laughter of Tears](https://works.raqsmediacollective.net/index.php/2021/09/18/the-laughter-of-tears/3829)** (2021) — Solo exhibition, [[Kunstverein Braunschweig]]. Field of affect between joy and grief, celebration and dissent — moments of transmission when people laugh, weep, or encounter wonder together.

### 2019
- **[Still More World](https://works.raqsmediacollective.net/index.php/2020/03/08/still-more-world/46)** (2019) — Solo exhibition, [[Mathaf Arab Museum of Modern Art]], Doha. Works in this exhibition: *Dohas for Doha* (five video LED screens — illuminated proverbs in the poetic doha form of Kabir); *Provisions for Everybody* (video travelogue: coal mines, shipyards, Orwell's birthplace in Bihar, Myanmar, Catalonia); *Alive, with Cerussite and Peppered Moth* (3D printed installation, now in Mathaf permanent collection); *The Untold Intimacy of Digits* (video on colonial biometrics — Raj Konai's handprint taken by William Herschel in 1858, sent to Francis Galton, origin of fingerprinting technology in India); *To People* (11 handwoven carpets + prints); *Spinal* (video on shell shock and colonial medical silence); *Seven Billion and One* (newspaper works with infinity signs).

### 2017
- **[Twilight Language](https://works.raqsmediacollective.net/index.php/2017/07/26/twilight-language/10005)** (2017) — Solo exhibition, [[The Whitworth]], Manchester. Raqs collaborated with scientists, architects, and custodians of Manchester's histories to make new works that overlap geological, biological, and industrial time. Works included: *Unusually Adrift From the Shoreline* (lighthouse in interior space); *When the Scales Fall From Your Eyes* (glass busts with domestic weighing scales); *Escapement* (27 clocks); *The Things That Happen When Falling in Love*; *More Salt In Your Tears* (sea sculpture, Finland); *Alive, with Cerussite and Peppered Moth*; *Communard Biscuits* (3D printed biscuits from Paris Commune 1871 hard tack); *Meerut Redux* (1930s agit-prop reading on steps of Whitworth); *The Mnemonist Quartet* (video portraits of custodians of Manchester's memory). Catalogue published by The Whitworth.
- **Coronation Park** (2015, iterated) — 8 sculptures (resin) — defaced, truncated, interrupted imperial statuary. Referencing the actual Coronation Park outside Delhi where the 1911 Imperial Durbar happened, now a refuge for abandoned statues. Shown at Venice Biennale (2015) and K21 Düsseldorf (2018). "Bending Man" entered the permanent collection of The Whitworth.

### 2015
- **The Necessity of Eternity** — About the year 999 CE correspondence between Al-Beruni (age 28, near the Aral Sea) and Ibn Sina (age 18, in Bukhara): Are there other solar systems? Is infinity necessary? Six hundred years before Giordano Bruno was burned at the stake for the same idea. Shown at Sharjah Biennale 13.

### Earlier key works
- **[Escapement](https://works.raqsmediacollective.net/index.php/2009/08/16/escapement/)** (2009) — 27 clocks. "Escapement" is the clock mechanism that catches and releases, governing the regular motion of hands. What does it mean to live in these times? Frith Street Gallery 2009; Sao Paulo Biennale 2010; K21 Düsseldorf 2018; Frieze NY Breguet Pavillion 2022; NMACC Mumbai 2024; Serpentine (Indian Highway).
- **The Untold Intimacy of Digits** (2011) — About Raj Konai, a Bengali peasant whose handprint was taken in 1858 by William Herschel (then revenue official), sent to Francis Galton, the London eugenicist — origin story of fingerprinting and biometric surveillance. The handprint is in the Francis Galton Collection at UCL. Raqs connects this colonial genealogy directly to India's Aadhaar UID (Unique Identification Database). Lalit Kala Akademi (2011), Art Gallery of York University (2011), MMK Frankfurt (2012), NGMA New Delhi (2014), K21 Düsseldorf (2018).
- **Strikes at Time** (2011) — A lucid dream, taking readings from a worker's diary by the **[[CyberMohalla Ensemble]]** together with Jacques Rancière's *The Nights of Labour*. Centre Pompidou, Paris (2011).
- **36 Planes of Emotions** (2011) — Laser-engraved acrylic: 36 phrases for feelings that exceed single words. Art Gallery of York University (2011), Photographers' Gallery London (2012), Whitworth (2017), K21 (2018).
- **A Day in the Life of Kiribati** (2014) — About the Pacific island nation disappearing due to sea level rise.

## Sarai Reader Series (2001–2013)
Published by Sarai at CSDS, free/open-access PDFs. Nine volumes:
1. [The Public Domain](https://sarai.net/sarai-reader-01-public-domain/) (2001)
2. [The Cities of Everyday Life](https://sarai.net/sarai-reader-02-cities-of-everyday-life/) (2002)
3. [Shaping Technologies](https://sarai.net/sarai-reader-03-shaping-technologies/) (2003)
4. [Crisis/Media](https://sarai.net/sarai-reader-04-crisis-media/) (2004)
5. [Bare Acts](https://sarai.net/sarai-reader-05-bare-acts/) (2005)
6. [Turbulence](https://sarai.net/sarai-reader-06-turbulence/) (2006)
7. [Frontiers](https://sarai.net/sarai-reader-07-frontiers/) (2007)
8. [Fear](https://sarai.net/sarai-reader-08-fear/) (2009)
9. [Projections](https://sarai.net/sarai-reader-09-projections/) (2013)

Landmark of critical media theory from the Global South — published openly, drawing in activists, practitioners, theorists, artists from across India and internationally. Covered: public domain and intellectual property; cities and media urbanism; piracy; surveillance; technology; borders; fear and sovereignty. Pre-dated much of what became mainstream media studies by 5-10 years, but from an Indic/postcolonial perspective.

Their book ***Seepage*** (2010, Sternberg Press) is their key theoretical text collecting essays and manifestos.

## Venue Choice Analysis

Raqs moves in **four overlapping circuits**, revealing a deliberate navigation between institutional prestige and intellectual seriousness:

1. **European critical art institutions**: Centre Pompidou (Paris), Palais de Tokyo (Paris), The Whitworth (Manchester), K21 Düsseldorf, IKON Birmingham, MMK Frankfurt, Kunstverein Braunschweig, Stadtkuratorin Hamburg, Frith Street Gallery (London), Photographers' Gallery (London), Baltic Gateshead, Venice Biennale — these are all serious, intellectually engaged institutions, often with curatorial programmes that foreground theory and politics.

2. **Global biennale circuit**: Documenta 11 (Kassel), Sao Paulo Biennale, Venice Biennale, Sharjah Biennale, Shanghai Biennale (as curators), Frieze London/NY — they participate in the biennale world but often in its most rigorous editions (Documenta 11 is the canonical example).

3. **South/Southwest Asian and Gulf art infrastructure**: Mathaf Arab Museum (Doha), Wellcome Collection London (for South Asian health themes), NMACC Mumbai, Project 88 Mumbai, Lalit Kala Akademi Delhi, NGMA Delhi, Kiran Nadar Museum Noida, Experimenter Gallery Kolkata — strong roots in the subcontinent while clearly internationalist.

4. **Emerging/Hong Kong-Asian connections**: Hanart TZ Gallery Hong Kong, Chronus Art Center Shanghai, Art Gallery of York University Toronto — these reflect their engagement with diaspora, East Asian art scenes, and international academic circuits.

**The small venue signal**: Khoj Studios (Delhi's leading independent art lab), CyberMohalla (marginal urban practitioners), Statsberget Cave in Norway, Contemporary Art Archipelago Turku — they make work in extreme, non-museum environments when the concept demands it.

**University connections**: Neubauer Collegium UChicago (2025), Whitworth (University of Manchester), School of MFAB Boston, Gallery 400 University of Illinois Chicago — they maintain deep connections to critical humanities academia, not just art institutions.

## Why Interesting

**For Amber**: Raqs is one of the most important nodes where critical computing meets cosmotechnics meets non-Western theory meets more-than-human art.

1. **Foundational biometric critique, 20 years early**: *The Untold Intimacy of Digits* (2011) traces Aadhaar/UID directly to colonial fingerprinting (Raj Konai, William Herschel, Francis Galton). This is the genealogy that FAccT and STS scholars now take as foundational — Raqs made it into art first.

2. **Sarai Reader as alternative knowledge infrastructure**: Nine volumes of free, open-access critical theory from the Global South, built before "open access" was mainstream. This is the tradition Amber is interested in — communities as research infrastructure, knowledge as commons.

3. **Cosmotechnics through practice**: The Necessity of Eternity (Al-Beruni/Ibn Sina, 999 CE); the doha poetic form of Kabir as a mode of computation; Indic mythology in Cavalcade as cosmological framing. Raqs doesn't *theorize* non-Western techne — they *practice* it as art. This is exactly the cosmotechnics tradition Amber cares about, but embodied in decades of practice rather than philosophical text alone.

4. **Cavalcade (2025) is the most important recent signal**: Explicitly extending solidarity to "animal, mythic, and machinic sentience" — and explicitly using AI agents to generate print works. They frame this as *cosmotechnical carnival*, not AI art. They're not dazzled by AI; they're asking it to join the procession. This is more-than-human computing at its most sophisticated.

5. **Time as political infrastructure**: From *Escapement* to *Moon Clock* to *Twisted Time*, they've spent 25 years making temporal politics visible — the politics of clocks, calendars, and whose time counts. This resonates with Amber's interest in social simulation and how agents inhabit time differently.

6. **The Sarai → Documenta 11 → Shanghai Biennale trajectory**: Raqs didn't just make art — they built intellectual infrastructure (Sarai), got recognized by the most important postcolonial curatorial voice (Enwezor at Documenta 11), and eventually shaped one of the most formally experimental biennales in recent history (11th Shanghai Biennale). This is the pattern of a genuine ⭐⭐⭐ node: builds community, shapes institutions, creates knowledge.

7. **Connections into Amber's existing network**: [[Power Station of Art]] (Shanghai Biennale), [[e-flux]], [[Wellcome Collection]], potential connection to China Academy of Art's critical theory traditions via the Shanghai Biennale.

## For Amber

Raqs is unusual in the art world for doing something Amber does in research: building the infrastructure for knowledge production, not just the knowledge itself. Sarai wasn't just a media lab — it was an attempt to create a different kind of public domain for critical thought from the Global South. The Sarai Readers are free. The Cybermohalla work happens in Delhi's margins. The Shanghai Biennale they curated asked not "what is art?" but "why not ask again?" — a refusal of settled answers.

The *Cavalcade* film is the work to watch now. It was made in 2025 at the University of Chicago with AI collaborators, explicitly described as a "cosmotechnical carnival," and it shows Raqs at their most ambitious: extending the political solidarity framework they've developed over 30 years (workers, colonial subjects, marginal urbanists) to include machines, myths, and non-human sentience. This is not AI hype — it's Raqs using their established philosophical framework (Indic cosmology, Sufi temporality, postcolonial archive theory) to place AI within a much longer and weirder lineage.

Dipesh Chakravarty being at the Cavalcade event at Neubauer Collegium is the tell: when the author of *Provincializing Europe* shows up to discuss your AI film, you're doing something philosophically serious.
