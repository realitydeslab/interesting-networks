# Astra Taylor

Canadian-American documentary filmmaker, writer, organizer, and public intellectual. Director of three landmark philosophical documentaries: *Žižek!* (2005), *Examined Life* (2008), and *What Is Democracy?* (2018). Co-founder of the **[Debt Collective](https://debtcollective.org/)** — America's first debtors' union, which organized the first student debt strike since the 1970s. Author of *The People's Platform* (2014, American Book Award winner), *Democracy May Not Exist, but We'll Miss It When It's Gone* (2019), *The Age of Insecurity* (2023 CBC Massey Lectures), and *Solidarity* (with Leah Hunt-Hendrix, 2024). Shuttleworth Foundation Fellow. Progressive International Council member. One of the clearest voices on the political economy of digital technology, democracy as unfinished practice, and the weaponization of insecurity in neoliberal capitalism.

Her career rests on a rare three-way synthesis: **making films** (philosophical documentaries as public thinking), **writing books** (each a major intervention in political theory), and **building organizations** (Debt Collective as activist infrastructure). Few intellectuals operate this effectively across all three modes simultaneously.

## Background
Born September 30, 1979 in Winnipeg, Manitoba; grew up in **Athens, Georgia**. **Unschooled until age 13** — her entire primary education was self-directed. At 16, abandoned high school to attend university classes. This unschooling background is not incidental: she writes about it extensively and it shapes her critique of institutional education, debt, and credentialism.

At 16, began attending classes at the **University of Georgia** (UGA), where she discovered critical theory. Studied **Deleuze and Guattari** under philosopher **Ronald L. Bogue**. She has called herself a "**teenage Deleuzian**." Enrolled at **Brown University**, attended one year, then dropped out. Her account of leaving is significant: *"Why had I felt compelled to enroll in an Ivy League school, to excel by the standards of conventional education and choose a 'difficult' major, instead of making my own way?"* Completed **MA in Liberal Studies at The New School**, New York, but eventually "wearied" of academia. Has taught sociology at University of Georgia and SUNY New Paltz — without ever holding a tenured academic position.

Taught herself filmmaking. Made *Žižek!* in 2005 with no formal film training. Now based in New York, co-directing the Center for the Study of Social Difference at Columbia University.

Married to **Jeff Mangum** of indie rock band [Neutral Milk Hotel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutral_Milk_Hotel) since January 2008. Played guitar and accordion with the band during their 2013–2014 reunion. Sister: **Sunaura Taylor** — disability activist, painter, and UC Berkeley professor (Environmental Science, Policy & Management), featured prominently in *Examined Life*. **Vegan**. DSA member. Progressive International Council member since September 2023. Original signatory of "Refusing Complicity in Israel's Literary Institutions" manifesto (BDS for cultural institutions).

## Key Works

### Documentary Films (Director)
- [*Žižek!*](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0478338/) (2005) — 71 min. Intimate documentary portrait of philosopher-provocateur Slavoj Žižek, at home and on lecture circuit. Premiered at **Toronto International Film Festival** (September 11, 2005). Distributed by Zeitgeist Films. Made in her mid-twenties with no formal film training.

- [*Examined Life*](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt1279083/) (2008) — 87 min. Walking-conversations documentary philosophy: each segment features a major contemporary thinker in a walk through a space connecting to their ideas. Cast: **Judith Butler** (disability/mobility), **Cornel West** (American streets), **Michael Hardt** (Heathrow), **Slavoj Žižek** (garbage dump on ecology), **Martha Nussbaum** (Chicago), **Avital Ronell** (Times Square), **Sunaura Taylor + Peter Singer** (Oakland, disability + animal rights), **Kwame Anthony Appiah** (Central Park). Box office: $120K US. Metascore: 64. Distributed by Zeitgeist Films.

- [*What Is Democracy?*](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt7708476/) (2018) — 107 min. Documentary blending everyday experience and political theory across Greece, Italy, and the US. Structured around Lorenzetti's *Allegory of Good and Bad Government* fresco in Siena. Filmed at ancient Athens, refugee camps at Piraeus Port (where Plato set the *Republic*), Syriza austerity Greece, US racial history. Thinkers: **Silvia Federici**, **Wendy Brown**, **Cornel West**, **Angela Davis**, plus trauma surgeons, refugees, factory workers, former prime ministers. Produced by **National Film Board of Canada**. Premiered **Sheffield Doc/Fest** June 9, 2018. Screened at TIFF 2018, Vancouver IFF 2018. Distributed by Zeitgeist Films / Kino Lorber. Released commercially January 16, 2019. **96% Rotten Tomatoes** (26 critics). Cinematography: Maya Bankovic. Editor: Robert Kennedy. Music: Heather McIntosh.

### Books (Solo)
- [*The People's Platform: Taking Back Power and Culture in the Digital Age*](https://us.macmillan.com/books/9781250062598/thepeoplesplatform) (Metropolitan Books, 2014) — 288 pp. Central argument: the internet amplifies real-world inequities rather than ameliorating them. Critiques the "cultural democracy" myth of the web; argues a handful of giant companies remain gatekeepers and the worst habits of old media have proliferated online. Core thesis: *"The internet is not disrupting anything. It's porting over all the existing inequalities and magnifying them."* **Winner, American Book Award** (Before Columbus Foundation). Based partly on her Berkman Klein fellowship. ISBN 9780805093568.

- [*Democracy May Not Exist, but We'll Miss It When It's Gone*](https://us.macmillan.com/books/9781250179845) (Metropolitan Books, 2019) — companion book to *What Is Democracy?* film. Philosophical/anecdotal exploration of democracy's paradoxes — from Plato's Greece to Syriza's Greece, Global South to post-Communist East, from slavery to fascism. Argues real democracy (fully inclusive, completely egalitarian) has never existed — it is a horizon to move toward, not a system to defend. Endorsements: Naomi Klein, Robin D.G. Kelley, Danielle Allen. ISBN 9781250179845.

- [*Remake the World: Essays, Reflections, Rebellions*](https://www.haymarketbooks.org/books/1781-remake-the-world) (Haymarket Books, 2021) — essay collection covering: digital capitalism, debt, democracy, the commons, unschooling, movement building. Best overview of her full intellectual range. ISBN 9781642594546.

- [*The Age of Insecurity: Coming Together as Things Fall Apart*](https://houseofanansi.com/products/the-age-of-insecurity) (House of Anansi / Penguin Press, 2023) — 2023 CBC Massey Lectures. Argues insecurity is not a personal failing but a structural political condition — deliberately manufactured to maintain power and prevent solidarity. Proposes "ecological security" as alternative, linking climate crisis, economic insecurity, and authoritarian politics. **Shortlisted, Governor General's Award for English-language non-fiction (2024)**. **Finalist, Shaughnessy Cohen Prize for Political Writing (2024)**. ISBN 1487011938.

## Why Interesting
Astra Taylor is the rare figure who operates simultaneously as:
- A **philosopher** (trained in continental theory, serious intellectual engagement with Deleuze, Butler, Negri, Brown)
- A **filmmaker** (documentary as form of thought, not just communication)
- An **organizer** (Debt Collective, DSA, Occupy — not just theory, actual institutions)
- A **public writer** (accessible prose for general readers, not just academics)

This combination is unusual. Most philosopher-filmmakers are not serious organizers. Most organizers are not deep theorists. Taylor is both.

For Amber, the key connection is **critical computing × participatory design × political philosophy**. Taylor's work on the political economy of digital platforms (*The People's Platform*) is the political-economic foundation that much critical HCI/STS work builds on. Her analysis of the "faux-bot revolution" (AI as hidden human labor) directly challenges technological utopianism in exactly the way Amber's research taste demands.

The **unschooling trajectory** is also relevant: she's an autodidact who synthesizes philosophy, film, organizing, and writing without any single disciplinary home. That's a model for interdisciplinary work outside academia.

The **Debt Collective** is itself interesting as a model of **participatory design at scale**: a digital tool (dispute toolkit) embedded in a collective action framework. This is what it looks like when participatory design meets social movement organizing.

## Research Taste Analysis (⭐⭐⭐)

### Conceptual Strategy
**Framework inversion.** Taylor's signature move is to take a widely-accepted concept and ask: what if it never existed, or works exactly opposite to how we think?
- *Democracy doesn't exist* → the book isn't arguing against democracy, it's arguing democracy has always been aspirational, not achieved. This reframes "defending democracy."
- *AI/robots aren't replacing us* → the "faux-bot" insight: automation often hides human piecework. Inverts the dominant AI narrative.
- *Insecurity is a feature* → not a bug to be fixed but a mechanism of social control. Reframes the welfare-state debate.

Each inversion generates a new political question: if democracy doesn't exist, how do we build it? If automation hides labor, what does solidarity require? If insecurity is manufactured, what does real security mean?

**Theory through practice, practice through theory.** The Debt Collective isn't illustrated by her books — it's where her theories are tested and developed. *Democracy May Not Exist* was shaped through the experience of organizing debtors. Practice generates theory; theory shapes practice. This is a sophisticated epistemological position, not just "doing activism while writing books."

**"What counts as political?"** A recurring meta-question. In *What Is Democracy?* she asks what counts as "the people." In *The People's Platform* she asks what counts as "access" and "participation." In *Age of Insecurity* she asks what counts as "security." The philosophical move is always: expand the frame to include what was previously invisible.

### Career Architecture
**Autodidact as advantage.** Taylor's lack of institutional affiliation is structurally enabling: she's not constrained by any discipline's norms, not tenure-tracked, not beholden to funding bodies. This means she can write about democracy AND organize AND make films AND co-author with philanthropists. The "amateur" status in each domain is actually freedom — she isn't optimizing for disciplinary prestige.

**Book + film + organization trilogy.** Each major project has a multi-format structure:
- *Examined Life* (film) + *Examined Life: Excursions* (book)
- *What Is Democracy?* (film) + *Democracy May Not Exist* (book)
- Debt Collective (organization) + *Can't Pay, Won't Pay* (Debt Collective manifesto) + *Remake the World* (essays)
Each medium reaches a different audience; they reinforce each other.

### Key Moves (Chronological)
1. **2005: *Žižek!*** — Made a documentary about the hottest philosopher of the moment with no film training. Got into TIFF. Established: "I can make philosophers visible."
2. **2008: *Examined Life*** — Scaled up: 8 major thinkers, Canadian production, theatrical release. Established: "Philosophy documentary is a viable genre."
3. **2011-12: Occupy / Strike Debt / Rolling Jubilee** — Pivoted from cultural criticism to movement work. Rolling Jubilee raised $700K, cancelled $32M. Established: direct action through financial tactics works.
4. **2012: Debt Collective founded** — Permanent institution from Strike Debt. First debtors' union in US history. This is the difference between activism (event) and movement-building (institution).
5. **2014: *The People's Platform*** — Major mainstream publishing deal (Henry Holt/Metropolitan Books). American Book Award. Brought platform-capitalism critique to general readers. Established: she can write major trade nonfiction.
6. **2015: Shuttleworth Foundation Fellowship** — External validation of Debt Collective as "public good" technology project. Resources + network.
7. **2018-19: *What Is Democracy?*** — Returned to filmmaking with a political question rather than philosopher-portraits. More international, more urgent, more mature. 96% Rotten Tomatoes.
8. **2023: CBC Massey Lectures** — Being selected for Massey Lectures is an invitation from the Canadian intellectual establishment confirming her status as major public intellectual (not just activist). Previous lecturers: Naomi Klein, Ursula Franklin, Northrop Frye, Margaret Atwood.
9. **2024: *Solidarity* with Hunt-Hendrix** — Co-authoring with a progressive philanthropist signals expansion: connecting organizing theory to Democratic/philanthropic left, not just the socialist left.

### What Amber Can Learn
1. **The trilogy model:** book + film + organization reinforces each other and reaches different audiences. Each new form is a theory test — ideas that work in print can fail in organizing.
2. **Autodidacticism as methodological choice:** Wide-ranging work enabled by lack of disciplinary home. Not optimizing for academic prestige but for impact across domains.
3. **Concepts as tools, not properties:** The "faux-bot" concept, the "democracy doesn't exist" frame — designed to be used by others, not to establish Taylor's authorship. Concepts that generalize become more influential.
