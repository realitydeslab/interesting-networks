# Astra Taylor

Canadian-American documentary filmmaker, writer, organizer, and public intellectual. Director of three landmark philosophical documentaries: *Žižek!* (2005), *Examined Life* (2008), and *What Is Democracy?* (2018). Co-founder of the **Debt Collective** — America's first debtors' union. Author of *The People's Platform* (2014, American Book Award winner), *Democracy May Not Exist, but We'll Miss It When It's Gone* (2019), and *The Age of Insecurity* (2023 CBC Massey Lectures). Shuttleworth Foundation Fellow. Progressive International Council member. One of the clearest voices on the political economy of digital technology, democracy as unfinished practice, and the weaponization of insecurity in neoliberal capitalism.

Her career is built on a rare three-way synthesis: **making films** (philosophical documentaries as public thinking), **writing books** (each one a major intervention in political theory), and **building organizations** (Debt Collective as activist infrastructure). Few intellectuals operate this effectively across all three modes.

## Background

- Born September 30, 1979, Winnipeg, Manitoba, Canada. Grew up in **Athens, Georgia**.
- **Unschooled** until age 13 — her entire primary education was self-directed. At 16, abandoned high school to attend university classes. This unschooling background is not incidental: she writes about it extensively and it shapes her critique of institutional education, debt, and credentialism.
- At 16, began attending classes at the **University of Georgia** (UGA), where she discovered critical theory. Studied **Deleuze and Guattari** under philosopher **Ronald L. Bogue**. She has called herself a "teenage Deleuzian."
- Enrolled at **Brown University** (Ivy League), attended one year, then dropped out. Her account of leaving is significant: "Why had I felt compelled to enroll in an Ivy League school, to excel by the standards of conventional education and choose a 'difficult' major, instead of making my own way?"
- Completed **MA in Liberal Studies at The New School**, New York — the left-academic institution associated with critical theory, sociology, and continental philosophy. Eventually "wearied" of academia.
- Has taught sociology at University of Georgia and **SUNY New Paltz**.
- Now based in New York. Co-director, Center for the Study of Social Difference at Columbia University.
- Married to **Jeff Mangum** of indie rock band Neutral Milk Hotel (since 2008). She played guitar and accordion with the band during their 2013-2014 reunion.
- Sister: **Sunaura Taylor** — disability activist, painter, assistant professor at UC Berkeley (Environmental Science, Policy & Management). Featured in *Examined Life*.
- Vegan.

## Key Works

### Documentary Films

- **[*Žižek!*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zizek!)** (2005, dir.) — 71 min. Intimate documentary portrait of philosopher-provocateur Slavoj Žižek at home, in public, across multiple countries. Premiered at the **Toronto International Film Festival** (September 11, 2005). Distributed by **Zeitgeist Films**. Her first major film, made when she was in her mid-twenties, following Žižek as he lectures, shops, watches films, explains his theories. Cinematography: Jesse Epstein, Martina Radwan. Editor: Laura Hanna.

- **[*Examined Life*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Examined_Life)** (2008, dir.) — 87 min. Walking-conversations documentary philosophy film. Each segment features a contemporary thinker in a walk through a space that connects to their ideas. Features **Judith Butler** (disability), **Cornel West** (American streets), **Michael Hardt** (Heathrow), **Slavoj Žižek** (a garbage dump on ecology), **Martha Nussbaum** (Chicago), **Avital Ronell** (Times Square), **Sunaura Taylor** + **Peter Singer** (Oakland, disability and animal rights), and **Kwame Anthony Appiah** (Central Park). Theatrical release. Published companion book (see below). Distributed by Zeitgeist Films.

- **[*What Is Democracy?*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/What_Is_Democracy%3F)** (2018, dir.) — 107 min. Documentary blending everyday experience and political theory across Greece, Italy, and the US. Filmed in sites including Siena (Lorenzetti's *Allegory of Good and Bad Government* as structuring device), ancient Athens, and a refugee camp at Piraeus Port where Plato set the *Republic*. Features **Silvia Federici** (on the fresco), **Wendy Brown** (on Hobbes vs. Rousseau), **Cornel West**, refugees, trauma surgeons, former prime ministers. Produced by **National Film Board of Canada**. Premiered **Sheffield Doc/Fest** June 9, 2018. Screened at **2018 Toronto International Film Festival** and **2018 Vancouver International Film Festival**. Distributed by **Zeitgeist Films** and **Kino Lorber**. Released commercially January 16, 2019. **96% on Rotten Tomatoes** (26 critics). Cinematography: Maya Bankovic. Editor: Robert Kennedy. Music: Heather McIntosh.

ISBN 1487011938.

## Intellectual Framework & Key Ideas

### 1. The Internet as Amplifier of Inequality
*The People's Platform* (2014) dismantles techno-utopianism: the internet does not create cultural democracy. It amplifies existing inequities. Attention accrues to those who already have it. Platforms are gatekeepers disguised as liberation. The "middle class of culture" has been wiped out; replaced by "cultural plantations ruled over by West Coast aggregators." Written from experience as an independent filmmaker navigating a media landscape that was destroying the conditions for independent work.

### 2. Democracy as Practice, Not Given
*What Is Democracy?* and *Democracy May Not Exist* argue that democracy has never existed in fully inclusive, egalitarian form — and that's the point. Democracy is not a system to be preserved but a **promise to be fought for**. The film uses Lorenzetti's 14th-century *Allegory of Good and Bad Government* as a structuring device: the fresco is about the choice between good governance (which benefits the common people) and bad governance (which concentrates power). Placing this alongside the Greek refugee crisis creates a direct confrontation: who counts as the people?

### 3. Debt as Political Technology
The Debt Collective's theory (developed with Graeber's *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* as backdrop): debt is not a natural obligation but a **political relationship** — and a tool of control. Student debt disciplines young people; medical debt punishes the sick; the entire system targets vulnerable and minority populations. The Rolling Jubilee demonstrated that "bad debt" trades at pennies on the dollar — exposing how fictitious the moral weight of debt really is.

### 4. Insecurity as Structural
*The Age of Insecurity* (2023): insecurity is not a personal condition but a **manufactured political one**. Neoliberalism deliberately creates insecurity — economic, physical, psychological — to prevent collective action and maintain the status quo. "Coming together as things fall apart" is the response: solidarity as the antidote to manufactured insecurity. The Massey Lectures context elevates this to a Canada-wide public conversation.

### 5. Unschooling as Politics
Her own unschooled childhood shapes her critique of credentialism and educational debt. She sees institutionalized education as a technology of discipline — and her n+1 essay "Unschooling" is one of the best accounts of what happens when you raise a child outside that system. This connects to her Debt Collective work: student loans are the price of credentials that the economy demands, regardless of their actual value.

## Why Interesting (For Amber)

Astra Taylor is interesting at the intersection of **Critical Computing**, **Participatory & Democratic Design**, and **Political Philosophy**:

1. **Critical Computing avant la lettre**: *The People's Platform* (2014) is what critical computing scholarship aspired to be — a structural critique of who benefits from digital platforms. Written before "surveillance capitalism" (Zuboff, 2019) or "AI ethics" became mainstream, it makes the same argument: the internet is not neutral, it's political. From outside tech academia, she said what FAccT papers try to say.

2. **Democracy as design problem**: *What Is Democracy?* asks what governance actually looks like — not as an abstract ideal but as a practical design question. Who counts? Who is excluded? What institutions enable or prevent participation? This is participatory design philosophy applied to democracy itself.

3. **Organizing as research infrastructure**: The Debt Collective is not just activism — it's a data collection and mobilization infrastructure. It builds collective knowledge about the debt system and creates leverage through collective action. In DEEP-RESEARCH-PROTOCOL terms: this is "communities as research infrastructure" (Amber's emerging interest in Future Observatory model).

4. **The film-book-organization trilogy**: Taylor's method is unusual — she makes a film to *think* (walking conversations in *Examined Life*), then writes a book to *argue*, then builds an organization to *act*. This tripartite research-practice loop is a model worth studying.

5. **Unschooling + outsider epistemology**: Her "teenage Deleuzian" origin story and her Brown dropout narrative position her as an intellectual outsider who is more rigorous than most insiders. Her critique of credentialism is lived experience, not theory. This connects to dark forest theory of the internet (Amber's emerging interest) — she understands that institutional gatekeeping is always political.

## Research Taste Analysis

**Rating justification: ⭐⭐⭐**

Astra Taylor is a rare case: a public intellectual who is simultaneously a filmmaker, an organizer, and a theorist — and good at all three. Most people who try all three are mediocre at one. She is not.

**Why she's rare:**
- Documentary filmmakers rarely also write serious political theory books
- Political theorists rarely also build activist organizations
- Organizers rarely also make films that premiere at TIFF
- All three modes inform each other: the Debt Collective informs the *Democracy May Not Exist* analysis; the film *What Is Democracy?* informs the book; the book informs the CBC lectures

**The unschooling thread:** Her entire career is an argument against institutionalized credentialism. She dropped out of Brown, wearied of The New School, and built a career on her own terms. Her best work — *Examined Life*, *The People's Platform*, *What Is Democracy?* — was done outside academia. The Debt Collective is literally a campaign against the coercive financial apparatus that enforces credentialism. This is internally coherent and politically serious.

**Networked position:** She sits at an unusual intersection of:
- Continental philosophy (Deleuze, Butler, Brown, Hardt)
- Left journalism (n+1, Dissent, The Nation)
- Debt/economic justice activism (Debt Collective, DSA)
- Documentary cinema (TIFF, Sheffield, Zeitgeist)
- Canadian intellectual life (Massey Lectures, Anansi Press, CBC)
- Tech critique (The People's Platform, Berkman Klein)

Almost no one else spans all these simultaneously. She is a **cross-circuit connector** whose work is legible in multiple worlds.

**For Amber specifically:** The question Taylor's work poses — "what if democracy was actually a design problem?" — is directly relevant to participatory design, critical computing, and questions about who gets to shape institutions. Her critique of platform capitalism in *The People's Platform* is what FAccT (Amber's field) was built to address. Her move from theory to organization (Debt Collective) is a model of how intellectual work can become infrastructure.
