# Anna Cox

Professor of Human-Computer Interaction at [[UCL Interaction Centre]] (UCLIC). HCI MSc Deputy Programme Director. The person who coined *design frictions* as an HCI concept — the idea that intentional friction (microboundaries) in interfaces can support mindful use, self-regulation, and wellbeing rather than hinder it. h-index 45, 258+ papers on Semantic Scholar. One of the most cited HCI researchers in the UK on the intersection of technology design and human psychology. Central figure in establishing "digital wellbeing" as a legitimate research area with policy weight.

## Background

Anna Cox built her career at the intersection of cognitive psychology and HCI. Her foundational work was on procedural errors and patient safety — how clinicians interact with complex medical devices, and how poor interface design leads to medication errors. This empirical, consequentialist approach to interface design became the lens she applied to consumer technology: what are the real effects on real people?

The pivot to digital wellbeing came through a sequence of observations. Smartwatches create "always on" dynamics. Crowdworkers lose temporal flexibility. Smartphone users form compulsive loops. Her 2016 CHI paper on *design frictions* named the countermove: rather than frictionless design that maximizes engagement, you can deliberately introduce friction (microboundaries — brief interruptions, confirmation steps, friction-based transitions) to restore user agency and mindful choice. This became one of the most cited ideas in digital wellbeing HCI.

She followed the design frictions insight empirically: TimeToFocus (feedback on interruption durations), studies of work-home boundary management, anxiety app functionality analysis, crowdwork temporal flexibility, and now cognitive personal informatics (what happens when wearables measure your cognition?) and neurodivergence in higher education technology.

Her methodological signature is **mixed methods with a behavioral economics flavor** — diary studies, field deployments, surveys, and interviews combined with quantitative behavioral measurement. She doesn't just describe; she builds things (apps, probes) and measures effects.

## Key Works

### Landmark Papers

- **[Design Frictions for Mindful Interactions: The Case for Microboundaries](https://doi.org/10.1145/2851581.2892410)** (CHI EA 2016, **188 cites**) — The paper. Co-authored with Sandy Gould, Marta Cecchinato, Ioanna Iacovides, Ian Renfree. Argues that *design friction* (introduced as microboundaries) can have positive effects by prompting reflection, supporting self-regulation, and reducing mindless technology use. Launched a subfield.

- **[A Special Interest Group on Designed and Engineered Friction in Interaction](https://doi.org/10.1145/3411763.3451553)** (CHI EA 2021, **13 cites**) — Followed up with a formal SIG to build community around the concept. Authors: Sandy Gould, L. Chuang, Ioanna Iacovides, Diego Garaialde, Marta Cecchinato, Anna Cox. Marks maturation from idea → field.

- **[Ecological Momentary Interventions for Mental Health: A Scoping Review](https://doi.org/10.1371/journal.pone.0248811)** (PLoS ONE 2021, **144 cites**) — Co-authored with Andreas Balaskas, Stephen Schueller, Gavin Doherty. Systematic review establishing the evidence base for in-the-moment digital interventions.

- **[Quantifying the Body and Caring for the Mind: Self-Tracking in Multiple Sclerosis](https://doi.org/10.1145/3025453.3026084)** (CHI 2017, **144 cites**) — With Amid Ayobi, Paul Marshall. How people with MS use self-tracking differently — not for optimization but for care, narrative, and emotional self-management. Shifts how personal informatics thinks about disability.

- **[Flexible and Mindful Self-Tracking: Design Implications from Paper Bullet Journals](https://doi.org/10.1145/3173574.3173650)** (CHI 2018, **139 cites**) — With Amid Ayobi, Anja Thieme, Paul Marshall. Paper bullet journals as self-tracking systems: what their design affords (flexibility, mindfulness, narrative). Design implications for digital tools.

- **[Trackly: A Customisable and Pictorial Self-Tracking App to Support Agency in Multiple Sclerosis Self-Care](https://doi.org/10.1145/3313831.3376768)** (CHI 2020, **96 cites**) — With Amid Ayobi, Paul Marshall. Building on self-tracking in MS work; deployed a customisable app designed from the ground up with the MS community.

- **[TimeToFocus: Feedback on Interruption Durations Discourages Distractions and Shortens Interruptions](https://doi.org/10.1145/3392835)** (TOCHI 2020, ~25 cites) — With Sandy Gould, Duncan Brumby, Marta Cecchinato. Empirical test of a design friction intervention: showing people how long their distractions last reduces them. The friction paper turned into a deployed system.

- **[Always On(line)?: User Experience of Smartwatches and their Role within Multi-Device Ecologies](https://doi.org/10.1145/3025453.3025504)** (CHI 2017, **78 cites**) — With Jonas Oppenlaender, Niels van Berkel, Anssi Lautala, Jorge Gonçalves, Vassilis Kostakos, Anu Reeves. Smartwatches and "digital handcuffs" — always-on availability as constraint, not feature.

- **[Digital Games and Mindfulness Apps: Comparison of Effects on Post Work Recovery](https://doi.org/10.2196/mental.9606)** (JMIR Mental Health 2019, **48 cites**) — With Emily Collins, Caroline Wilcock, Glyn Sethu-Jones. Games can be equally or more effective than mindfulness apps for recovery from work.

- **[HawkEye – Deploying a Design Fiction Probe](https://doi.org/10.1145/3290605.3300823)** (CHI 2019, **66 cites**) — With Renee Noortman, Britta Schulte, Paul Marshall, Saskia Bakker. Design fiction deployed as a probe — speculative surveillance device to elicit responses about data, privacy, public space.

- **[Monotasking or Multitasking: Designing for Crowdworkers' Preferences](https://doi.org/10.1145/3290605.3300701)** (CHI 2019, **55 cites**) — With Laura Lascau, Sandy Gould, Elena Karmannaya, Duncan Brumby. Crowdworkers — how do they want to work? Design implications for platforms.

- **["Sometimes It's Like Putting the Track in Front of the Rushing Train": Having to Be 'On Call' for Work Limits the Temporal Flexibility of Crowdworkers](https://doi.org/10.1145/3635145)** (TOCHI 2024, recent) — With Laura Lascau, Sandy Gould, Duncan Brumby. Platform mechanisms that covertly constrain crowdworkers' time. Critical platform analysis.

## Venue Circuit Analysis

Anna Cox moves across three interconnected circuits:

**1. CHI ecosystem (core)**
CHI is her home. She has been TPC Chair (2019), runs workshops, organizes SIGs, and publishes regularly. CHI EA (extended abstracts/notes) is where she floats conceptual ideas (design frictions, cognitive PI) before developing them into full papers. CHI is also where she builds community around digital wellbeing as a field. TOCHI (Transactions on CHI) is for mature empirical work.

**2. Interdisciplinary health/mental health venues**
JMIR Mental Health, PLoS ONE, PLoS Digital Health, BMC Medical Education, Frontiers in Digital Health, IJHCS — she publishes in these to reach clinical/public health audiences beyond HCI. This is strategic: it legitimizes digital wellbeing as a medical/public health concern, not just a design nicety. This is how "HCI research" becomes "policy-relevant research."

**3. Specialist HCI venues for niche topics**
MobileHCI (smartphones, mobile wellbeing), TVX (video/streaming experiences), CHI PLAY (games), CHIWORK (work & HCI), UbiComp/ISWC (ubicomp, ecological momentary methods). Each one is where a specific Anna Cox research thread finds its specialist community.

**What she avoids:** Purely speculative/design venues (RTD, DIS design tracks, art-science venues). Her work is empirical and consequentialist, not speculative. She wants to know *what actually happens*, not just imagine what might.

## Research Taste Analysis

### Conceptual Strategy
Anna's conceptual breakthrough was **concept coining**: she named *design frictions* and *microboundaries* at the right moment (2016, just as the "frictionless design" gospel was at its peak in UX circles). The paper explicitly argues *against* the dominant doctrine ("remove all friction!") and argues *for* intentional friction. This contrarian framing — naming the thing, disagreeing with the consensus — is a powerful academic strategy. Compare: "dark patterns" (Brignull, 2010), "persuasive technology" (Fogg, 2003). The concept did the work.

Her broader strategy is **empirical gap filling**: when a concept exists but no one has measured it properly, she measures it. Design frictions → TimeToFocus (measuring friction effects). Digital wellbeing → multiple diary studies. Crowdwork wellbeing → temporal flexibility studies. She converts speculative claims into empirical evidence.

### Career Architecture
She built her empirical base through **patient safety** (unambiguously important, methodologically rigorous, gives credibility) and then migrated to **consumer technology** (higher public profile, more policy relevance, more citation impact). This is a smart institutional trajectory: start where rigor is unquestioned, migrate to where impact is largest.

She is a **platform builder**: HCI MSc Deputy Programme Director, CHI TPC Chair 2019, CHIWORK regular organizer, multiple SIGs at CHI. This is deliberate — building institutional positions that funnel students, papers, and community energy into her research agenda.

### Methodology Signature
**Mixed methods + deployment**: she doesn't just survey people about their technology use; she builds systems (TimeToFocus, Trackly), deploys them, and studies what happens. This is "research in the wild" applied to digital wellbeing — the UCLIC house style. The probe/deployment approach (HawkEye design fiction probe) extends this to speculative methods.

**Longitudinal studies**: diary studies, experience sampling, longitudinal interviews. She resists one-shot lab studies because technology use is contextual and habitual — you have to study it over time.

### Key Moves
1. **(~2010-2015) Patient safety empiricism** — built methodological credibility through rigorous mixed-methods work on IV infusion errors and clinical decision support. Published in BMJ Quality & Safety, BMC Health Services Research. Gave her the empirical heft that later consumer tech work benefited from.

2. **(2016) Design Frictions** — coined the concept, published at CHI EA. One paper, one concept, 188 cites. Timing was perfect: industry was evangelizing frictionless design; she provided the counterargument before the backlash became mainstream.

3. **(2017-2018) Self-tracking in MS & Bullet Journals** — moved into personal informatics through disability lens (MS). Two high-citation papers (144, 139 cites) that changed how personal informatics thought about non-optimizing use cases. Built a productive collaboration with Amid Ayobi and Paul Marshall.

4. **(2019-2020) Crowdwork + Digital Wellbeing convergence** — started studying crowdworkers' temporal flexibility. Connected "frictionless design" critique to platform labor: gig platforms use subtle mechanisms to remove workers' temporal autonomy. This is design frictions at the systems level.

5. **(2021) Frictions SIG at CHI** — formalized the design frictions community, built institutional home for it. Research agenda → community → institution.

6. **(2022-present) Cognitive PI + Neurodivergence** — following the cognitive tracking wave (wearables measuring stress, focus, cognitive load). Running CPI workshops at CHI 2024, CHI 2026. Also exploring how digital systems burden neurodivergent students — disability × HCI from a structural critique angle.

2. **Be the empiricist in a speculative field.** Anna works in a field full of speculative claims about technology and wellbeing — and she measures them. In Amber's field (AI agent behavior, machine welfare), there is a lot of theory and not much measurement. Being the person who actually deploys and studies AI agents in the wild is a distinctive position.

3. **Build across venues simultaneously.** CHI + JMIR + PLoS ONE = same research reaching three different communities. Amber's work should similarly target HCI + policy venues + philosophy/theory venues.

4. **The SIG as community-building tool.** SIGs at CHI cost almost nothing to organize but can nucleate a community. Amber should use this for AI agent ethics or more-than-human HCI.

5. **Collaborate through students.** Most of Anna's major papers involve PhD students (Amid Ayobi, Andreas Balaskas, Laura Lascau, Yıldız Ahmetoğlu). They do the empirical legwork; she provides the conceptual framing and institutional backing. This is standard UCLIC style and it's how you scale output.
