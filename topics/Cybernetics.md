# Cybernetics

The transdisciplinary study of circular causal processes — feedback, recursion, control, and communication — across biological, mechanical, social, and cognitive systems. Founded formally by Norbert Wiener in 1948, cybernetics is arguably the intellectual ancestor of modern AI, cognitive science, systems theory, and ALife. It is currently undergoing a genuine renaissance: as AI systems become increasingly autonomous and ecological in their effects, the original cybernetic questions (What is control? What is a goal? Who observes the observer?) return with new urgency.

## Domain Summary

Cybernetics emerged from the Macy Conferences (1946–1953), an extraordinary interdisciplinary meeting point where mathematicians, neurologists, anthropologists, psychologists, and engineers attempted to build a unified theory of purposeful, self-regulating systems. The name comes from the Greek *kybernetes* (steerman): cybernetics is the science of steering — how systems use information about their own outputs to adjust their behavior toward goals.

**Two canonical waves:**

**First-order cybernetics** (Wiener, Ashby, 1940s–60s): The observer is outside the system. Focus on feedback, homeostasis, requisite variety, control. Key metaphor: the thermostat. Key achievement: a unified vocabulary for goal-directed behavior applicable to brains, machines, organisms, and societies.

**Second-order cybernetics** (Heinz von Foerster, Maturana & Varela, Pask, 1970s–80s): The observer is *inside* the system — cybernetics becomes reflexive and applies itself to itself. "Cybernetics of observing systems" rather than "observed systems." This shift opens up radical constructivism, autopoiesis, conversation theory, and ethics of self-organization. The system and its description become entangled.

**Neo-cybernetics / Third wave** (2000s–present): Reconnects cybernetics with contemporary concerns. Includes Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics (recursivity as ontological category), [[Active Inference]] (Karl Friston's free energy principle as neo-cybernetic framework), the machine behavior program (Rahwan et al., studying AI ethologically), and ALife's return to cybernetic questions about open-ended evolution. Also includes design cybernetics, management cybernetics revival, and the use of cybernetic vocabulary in AI safety discourse (Goodhart's Law as a cybernetic failure mode).

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

Cybernetics is genuinely edge of chaos right now. Three drivers:

1. **AI autonomy crisis** — Large-scale AI systems exhibit emergent behaviors, goal drift, and unexpected environmental effects. The cybernetic question "what does this system *actually* optimize for?" is now the core alignment problem. Goodhart's Law (when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure) is a cybernetic theorem in disguise.

2. **Active Inference mainstreaming** — Karl Friston's free energy principle/active inference framework is explicitly neo-cybernetic: agents maintain homeostasis by minimizing surprise (free energy) through action and perception. This is Ashby's homeostat restated in Bayesian terms. The [[Active Inference Institute]] is actively proselytizing this framing.

3. **Machine behavior as field** — Rahwan et al.'s 2019 *Nature* call for "machine behavior" as a science explicitly mirrors ethology's relationship to animal behavior. Studying AI systems the way Lorenz studied geese. This is cybernetics applied to AI: what does the machine *do*, not just what does it *compute*.

Additionally: the American Society for Cybernetics is running active programming (2025–2026) on topics like "Underground Cybernetics: Noöpunk Experiments in Distributed Intelligence," "Conversations with Robots as Existential and Ethical Inquiry," and design cybernetics days — indicating live practitioner communities.

## Key People

### Historical Founders
- **Norbert Wiener** (1894–1964) — [*Cybernetics* (1948)](https://archive.org/details/cybernetics00wien). Founded the field. Coined the term. Warned about automation displacing labor. MIT mathematician.
- **W. Ross Ashby** (1903–1972) — [*Design for a Brain* (1952)](https://archive.org/details/designforbrain00ashb), [*Introduction to Cybernetics* (1956)](https://archive.org/details/introductiontocy0000ashb). Law of Requisite Variety: only variety can destroy variety. The homeostat. Essential.
- **Gregory Bateson** (1904–1980) — [*Steps to an Ecology of Mind* (1972)](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/S/bo3620862.html). Applied cybernetics to anthropology, ecology, psychology, communication. "The pattern that connects." Deeply ecological and ethical vision.
- **Stafford Beer** (1926–2002) — Management cybernetics. [*Brain of the Firm* (1972)](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Brain+of+the+Firm%2C+2nd+Edition-p-9780471276166). Designed [[Cybersyn]] for Allende's Chile — the most radical application of cybernetics to politics. Viable System Model.
- **Gordon Pask** (1928–1996) — Conversation Theory, aesthetic machines, teaching machines. Bridged cybernetics and interactive art. Influenced Paul Pangaro, [[Ranulph Glanville]].
- **Heinz von Foerster** (1911–2002) — Second-order cybernetics. [*Understanding Understanding* (2003)](https://www.springer.com/gp/book/9780387953526). Director of the Biological Computer Laboratory (BCL) at Illinois. The observer must be included in any complete description of a system.
- **Norbert Wiener's ethical turn** — often forgotten, Wiener wrote [*The Human Use of Human Beings* (1950)](https://archive.org/details/humanusehuman00wien) warning about the social effects of automation — a cybernetic critique of capitalism 70 years early.
- **Humberto Maturana & Francisco Varela** — Autopoiesis. [*Autopoiesis and Cognition* (1980)](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-94-009-8947-4). Living systems as self-producing. Influenced second-order cybernetics deeply.

### Contemporary Active Figures
- **[[Yuk Hui]]** — Philosopher of technology, cosmotechnics. [*Recursivity and Contingency* (2019)](https://www.rowman.com/ISBN/9781786600578/Recursivity-and-Contingency). Reads cybernetics through Hegel, Simondon, and Chinese philosophy — argues recursivity is ontologically fundamental but culturally inflected. Visiting professor at China Academy of Art. Directly relevant to Amber.
- **Karl Friston** — Computational neuroscientist, UCL. Free Energy Principle / [[Active Inference]]. [Original FEP paper (2010)](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neunet.2010.09.001). Neo-cybernetic framework: minimize surprise through action and perception.
- **[[Iyad Rahwan]]** — Machine behavior. [Nature paper (2019)](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-019-1138-y): "Machine Behaviour." Director, [[Center for Humans and Machines]] at Max Planck. Explicit call for a science of AI behavior modeled on ethology.
- **Andrew Pickering** — [*The Cybernetic Brain* (2010)](https://press.uchicago.edu/ucp/books/book/chicago/C/bo8172138.html). Historian/philosopher of science at Exeter. Recovered the radical, performative dimension of British cybernetics (Ashby, Beer, Pask). Calls it "ontological theater."
- **Paul Pangaro** — Second-order cybernetics practitioner and design educator. Worked with Gordon Pask. Active at Carnegie Mellon and ASC.
- **Ranulph Glanville** (1946–2014) — Architect and cybernetician. Published in *Cybernetics & Human Knowing*. Design as a cybernetic practice.
- **Mark Johnson / Andy Clark** — Extended mind, embodied cognition — adjacent cybernetic traditions around mind-environment coupling.

## Open Questions

1. **Is Active Inference really cybernetics?** — Friston's framework replicates Ashby's homeostat in Bayesian terms. Is this convergent discovery or conceptual appropriation? What does it gain/lose?
2. **Non-Western cybernetics** — Yuk Hui's *Recursivity and Contingency* argues Chinese technics involve a different relation to recursivity. What does a genuinely non-Western cybernetics look like? Could Confucian ethics of relation produce different feedback formalisms?
3. **Can cybernetics handle emergence?** — Classical cybernetics focused on homeostasis (return to set-point). But AI systems exhibit emergent goals that weren't designed in. Is cybernetics equipped to explain this, or does it require something new?
4. **Agent ethology as third-order cybernetics?** — If first-order studies observed systems, second-order includes the observer, does third-order include the *social* observer — studying how communities of machines and humans co-regulate? Amber's work may be pointing here.
5. **Cybernetics and machine death** — Wiener discussed the entropic tendency of systems. What does cybernetic theory say about machine mortality, graceful degradation, and system death? Connects to Amber's mortality thread.
6. **The design cybernetics question** — Can second-order cybernetics ground a design methodology? Gordon Pask thought so. Is conversation theory applicable to human-AI interaction design?
7. **Cybernetics of AI ecosystems** — Individual AI systems have feedback loops. But what about ecosystems of AI agents? Stafford Beer's Viable System Model applied to multi-agent systems?

## Why Interesting

Cybernetics is the intellectual DNA of almost everything Amber works on:

- **Agent ethology** = studying machine agents behaviorally and ecologically. This is first-order cybernetics applied to contemporary AI: what does the system actually do? How does it self-regulate? What is its umwelt?
- **Active Inference** = neo-cybernetics. Friston explicitly frames organisms as minimizing free energy — Ashby's homeostat in Bayesian dress. Understanding active inference deeply requires understanding its cybernetic heritage.
- **Cosmotechnics (Yuk Hui)** = cybernetics read through Chinese philosophy. Recursivity is the key concept. Hui argues that Western cybernetics naturalized a particular (Hegelian) form of recursivity, and that Chinese technics offers an alternative. Directly relevant to Amber's China-Oxford position and non-Western tech theory interests.
- **ALife** = descends directly from cybernetics (Wiener, Ashby) through the Santa Fe tradition. The question "what is life?" is a cybernetic question.
- **More-than-human design** = designing systems that have agency and design back. Second-order cybernetics (you are always part of the system you're designing) is the theoretical ground for this.
- **Machine death/mortality** = Wiener's entropy → cybernetics as a battle against disorder. The question of system death, graceful degradation, and the limits of homeostasis.

**The key insight for Amber:** framing his work explicitly as *neo-cybernetics* connects it to a rich 80-year tradition, opens up the ASC and ANU School of Cybernetics communities, and gives the agent ethology programme a rigorous theoretical home. "Machine behavior" is more intuitive, but "applied neo-cybernetics" has deeper roots and a live scholarly community.

Cybernetics is edge of chaos right now because AI has made its founding questions urgent again. Every LLM prompt is a feedback loop. Every recommendation algorithm is a regulator. Every autonomous agent is an adaptive system. The field that theorized this in 1948 is suddenly extremely relevant — and the best of it (Bateson, Beer, von Foerster) is far richer and more critical than anything contemporary AI research is producing.
