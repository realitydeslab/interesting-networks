# Solarpunk

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-03:** Initial deep research. Full note written covering origins, philosophy, key people, texts, communities, venues, and connections to Amber's research.

Solarpunk is a literary, artistic, and social movement — and now an emerging academic discourse — that envisions sustainable futures built on the coexistence of humans, nonhumans, and technologies within ecological webs of mutual care. Coined in 2008 in an anonymous blog post, crystallized visually in 2014 by artist Olivia Louise on Tumblr, and theorized by researcher Adam Flynn in his landmark "Notes toward a Manifesto" at Project Hieroglyph (also 2014), solarpunk stands in deliberate opposition to cyberpunk's dystopian aesthetics and tech-alienation narratives. The "solar" signals renewable energy and radical optimism against climate doomerism; the "punk" signals DIY ethics, prefigurative politics, post-capitalism, and anti-authoritarianism. Aesthetically, it fuses Art Nouveau's organic sinuous lines, Miyazaki's nature-human intimacy, Afrofuturism's counter-hegemonic worldbuilding, and appropriate-technology cultures from the Global South. Politically, it practices **prefigurative politics**: building now the world it wants — ecovillages, tool libraries, community gardens, cooperative meshworks — rather than waiting for system change. Its core tension is between utopian aspiration and prefigurative realism, and between genuine ecological transformation and greenwashed aesthetics of sustainability-as-branding.

## Domain Summary

### Origins & History
- **2008**: Anonymous blog post "From Steampunk to Solarpunk" on Tumblr coins the term, taking inspiration from the MS Beluga Skysails (ship partially powered by kite rig)
- **2009**: Matt Staggs publishes "GreenPunk Manifesto" describing a technophilic, DIY, ecologically-positive genre
- **2012**: First explicit solarpunk fiction anthology published in Brazil: *Solarpunk: Ecological and Fantastical Stories in a Sustainable World*, ed. Gaia Cottee Jonk. (Brazilian origin is often overlooked — Fabio Fernandes and the Brazilian SF community were crucial early adopters)
- **2014**: Visual artist Olivia Louise posts concept art on Tumblr defining the solarpunk aesthetic. Adam Flynn writes "Notes toward a Manifesto" for Project Hieroglyph/ASU
- **2015**: *Wings of Renewal: A Solarpunk Dragons Anthology* published
- **2017**: *Sunvault: Stories of Solarpunk and Eco-Speculation*, ed. Phoebe Wagner & Brontë Christopher Wieland (Upper Rubber Boot Books)
- **2018**: *Glass and Gardens: Solarpunk Summers*, ed. Sarena Ulibarri (World Weaver Press)
- **2019**: A Solarpunk Manifesto formally published at solarpunks.net based on Flynn's notes
- **2021**: Becky Chambers publishes *A Psalm for the Wild-Built* (Tor Books) — mainstream breakthrough for solarpunk fiction
- **2022**: Becky Chambers publishes *A Prayer for the Crown-Shy*
- **2024**: Solarpunk enters The Encyclopedia of Science Fiction; academic adoption accelerates

### Philosophy & Politics
- **Prefigurative politics**: Creating the future now through lived practice (ecovillages, commons, cooperatives)
- **Post-capitalism**: Not a single ideology but default anti-consumerist, anti-authoritarian, decentralist
- **Infrastructure as resistance**: Following Chokwe Lumumba's formulation: building local energy/food resilience as protection against dispossession
- **Appropriate technology**: Knowable, repairable, locally adaptable — against black-box tech
- **Decolonial threads**: Drawing from Global South traditions of jugaad (frugal innovation), swadeshi, Indigenous land stewardship
- **Social ecology**: Inspired by Murray Bookchin — decentralized communities, confederal democracy
- **Conviviality**: Ivan Illich's vision of tools that serve human self-determination

### Aesthetic
- Art Nouveau + organic architecture (Frank Lloyd Wright, Gaudi tendencies)
- Dense plant integration into built environments; vertical gardens, living buildings
- Miyazaki aesthetic: *Castle in the Sky*, *Nausicaä of the Valley of the Wind*
- Afrofuturist counter-hegemony: Wakanda's tech-indigenous synthesis
- Bright greens and blues; sunlight as motif of abundance vs. surveillance
- DIY bricolage: creative reuse, repurposed infrastructure, jugaad-style making
- Contrast with cyberpunk's dark, alienated, neon-noir aesthetic

### Active Debates
1. **Greenwashing risk**: Adam Flynn himself warns of "fake solarpunk urbanism" — luxury condos with green roofs that price out communities. Aesthetics without structural change
2. **Utopianism vs. realism**: Is solarpunk too optimistic to grapple with the real power of fossil capital? Or does the "utopian" mode itself do political work?
3. **Technology's role**: Tension between hi-tech solar futures and low-tech appropriate-tech traditions. Eco-modernism infiltration?
4. **Global South vs. Anglophone dominance**: Brazilian origins often erased; non-Western solarpunk traditions marginalized in English discourse
5. **Nonhuman inclusion**: Can solarpunk extend beyond human community to genuinely include nonhuman agents, more-than-human relationships? Still largely anthropocentric in practice
6. **Scale problem**: Prefigurative politics at small community scale vs. the need for systemic change at planetary scale
7. **Class politics**: Who has access to solarpunk practice (rooftop solar, community gardens)? Risk of becoming a bourgeois aesthetic

## Key People

### Founders & Theorists
- **Adam Flynn** — researcher, wrote the foundational "Notes toward a Manifesto" (2014) for Project Hieroglyph/ASU; continued developing solarpunk theory; concern about greenwashing
- **Olivia Louise** (missolivialouise.tumblr.com) — visual artist who crystallized the aesthetic in 2014 with Art Nouveau-inspired concept art on Tumblr
- **Gaia Cottee Jonk** — editor of the 2012 Brazilian anthology that started it all; Brazilian SF scene as origin point
- **Fabio Fernandes** — Brazilian SF author and translator; key figure in the Brazilian solarpunk origin story

### Fiction Writers
- **[[Kim Stanley Robinson]]** — proto-solarpunk with *Pacific Edge* (1990) and later *The Ministry for the Future* (2020); Robinson's work on cooperative futures and climate solutions directly prefigures and influences solarpunk
- **Becky Chambers** — *A Psalm for the Wild-Built* (2021) is the most mainstream solarpunk novel; nonhuman (robot) relations with humans; cozy + hopeful. Not in graph yet → [[Becky Chambers]]
- **[[China Miéville]]** — adjacent: Miéville is ambivalent about utopian fiction but his weird fiction inflects solarpunk's more experimental edges
- **Ursula K. Le Guin** (1929–2018) — proto-solarpunk: *Always Coming Home* (1985), *The Dispossessed* (1974); Le Guin's anarchist-ecological utopias are foundational
- **Ernest Callenbach** (1929–2012) — *Ecotopia* (1975), another proto-solarpunk foundational text
- **Starhawk** — *The Fifth Sacred Thing* (1993), ecofeminist proto-solarpunk
- **Phoebe Wagner** — co-editor of *Sunvault* (2017); active solarpunk fiction editor
- **Sarena Ulibarri** — editor of *Glass and Gardens* (2018); runs World Weaver Press

### Researchers & Theorists (Academic Turn)
- **Andrew Flynn** — (same as Adam Flynn above, sometimes cited with first name variant) at Project Hieroglyph
- **[[Stuart Candy]]** — experiential futures; solarpunk adjacent; speculative design as prefigurative practice
- **[[Liam Young]]** — speculative urbanism; designs solarpunk-adjacent future cities as film/design
- **[[Fiona Raby]]** / **[[Anthony Dunne]]** — critical design progenitors that solarpunk practice builds on
- **Rhiannon Rasmussen-Silverstein** — solarpunk editor, community organizer in US solarpunk circles

### Adjacent Thinkers (not explicitly solarpunk but generative)
- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — Chthulucene, making-kin, composting-the-future; SF as speculative fabulation; solarpunk absorbs heavily from Haraway
- **[[Anna Tsing]]** — more-than-human entanglements; multispecies becoming
- **[[Robin Wall Kimmerer]]** — indigenous plant knowledge; Braiding Sweetgrass (2013); solarpunk draws heavily on indigenous ecological epistemologies
- **[[George Monbiot]]** — rewilding as solarpunk-adjacent practice
- **[[Yuk Hui]]** — cosmotechnics: the question of what kind of technology; solarpunk's appropriate-tech ethos is in conversation with Hui's tech-plurality

## Open Questions

1. **Nonhuman agency in solarpunk**: Can the movement genuinely incorporate nonhuman agents (plants, fungi, AI, animals) as co-creators of futures — or does it remain fundamentally anthropocentric despite claiming more-than-human solidarity?
2. **The greenwashing problem**: How does solarpunk defend its aesthetic from corporate co-optation? (Green branding, tech company "sustainable" PR)
3. **Scale + system change**: Can prefigurative micro-practices actually scale? Or are they boutique alternatives that leave fossil capital intact?
4. **AI and solarpunk**: What would solarpunk AI look like? AI designed with ecological logic — open, repairable, appropriate, locally accountable — vs. today's centralized black-box systems?
5. **Machine behavior in ecological terms**: How would machine learning agents be "solarpunk"? (Relevant to Amber's agent ethology work)
6. **The role of fiction**: Is speculative fiction a political tool for change, or does it provide safe imaginary release that substitutes for action?
7. **Cosmotechnics and solarpunk**: Are non-Western technological traditions the natural allies of solarpunk? Or does solarpunk reproduce Western environmental aesthetics in new forms?
8. **Post-capitalism specifics**: What are the actual economic mechanisms of a solarpunk society? (Still underspecified in most fiction)

## Why Interesting

For Amber specifically, solarpunk matters on several axes:

1. **More-than-human design as prefigurative practice**: Solarpunk doesn't just theorize more-than-human futures — it builds them. This makes it a live testing ground for more-than-human design methods. Amber's interest in design that includes nonhuman participants finds natural allies here.

2. **Agent ethology in ecological framing**: Solarpunk's insistence on technology that is knowable, repairable, and locally adapted maps onto the question of machine behavior in interesting ways. What would an agent ethology that works WITH ecological systems (not against them) look like? Solarpunk provides a future context for that question.

3. **Speculative design meets activism**: Solarpunk sits at the precise intersection of speculative design (worldbuilding methods) and prefigurative politics (actually doing it). This is Amber's territory — HoloKit and Reality Design Lab's work on experiential futures has natural resonance.

4. **Cosmotechnics connection**: Yuk Hui's question — "what kind of technology?" for which civilization — is exactly what solarpunk asks from an activist-aesthetic position. For Amber connected to China Academy of Art and Yuk Hui's visiting professorship, this is a natural bridge. Solarpunk's plurality-of-technics could be enriched by Hui's cosmotechnical framework.

5. **ALife in ecological register**: Solarpunk imagines living systems as models — communities that work like ecosystems, cities that breathe like forests. ALife's interest in emergence, self-organization, open-endedness has natural resonance with solarpunk's ecological imagination.

**Honest assessment**: Solarpunk is **established enough** that Amber doesn't need to build a research identity around it. But it is a generative **frame** — a future context that can give her work on machine behavior, agent ethology, and more-than-human design more legibility and political grounding. The most productive move is probably writing one sharp theoretical piece connecting solarpunk to one of her core concepts (cosmotechnics, agent ethology, ALife), rather than fully entering the solarpunk community.
