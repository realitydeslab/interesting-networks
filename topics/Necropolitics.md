# Necropolitics

Achille Mbembe's theory of the political management of death — who gets to live, who is made to die, and who is kept in suspended states between the two. Originally posed as a critique and extension of Foucault's biopolitics, necropolitics names the specifically colonial, racial, and spatially organized forms of sovereign power that reduce entire populations to "deathworlds." Now a major framework in critical AI, surveillance studies, and — for Amber — a potential theoretical anchor for machine death and the political economy of AI mortality.

## Domain Summary

Necropolitics names the mechanisms by which sovereign power — state, colonial, or corporate — determines which bodies may live and which must die, or be kept in a "living death." Mbembe builds from Foucault's *biopolitics* (the management of populations through life-optimization) but argues Foucault could not account for the explicitly lethal, colonial, and racial logics that govern life in the postcolony.

**Key move:** Foucault showed how modern power *fosters* life and *lets die*. Mbembe shows how colonial and racial power *makes die* — actively, systematically, often through spatial technologies (the plantation, the colony, the checkpoint, the settlement). The slave, the colonized subject, the Palestinian under occupation — these are not simply governed; they are reduced to what Mbembe calls the "living dead," subjected to "deathworlds."

**Necropower** = the capacity to dictate how some people may live and how some must die. It operates through:
- The **state of exception** (Agamben) — zones where law is suspended
- **Racial sorting** — race as the technology that decides who is expendable
- **Spatial confinement** — the colony, the camp, the ghetto as necropolitical architectures
- **Slow death** (Lauren Berlant) — attrition, exhaustion, abandonment rather than direct killing

The 2003 article uses Palestine/occupation, apartheid, and the slave ship as paradigm cases. The 2019 book expands to global finance, migration, and the spread of "war as sacrament."

## Key People

- **[[Achille Mbembe]]** — originator. Cameroonian political philosopher, Wits Institute for Social and Economic Research (WISER), Johannesburg. Also visiting at Duke. Former executive director of CODESRIA. Key influences: Fanon, Foucault, Bernard Stiegler. Spouse: Sarah Nuttall (literary theorist). Geschwister-Scholl-Preis winner.
- **Jasbir Puar** — Rutgers. Extended necropolitics into queer theory. Coined "queer necropolitics" and "homonationalism." Key book: *Terrorist Assemblages* (2007). More recent: *The Right to Maim* (2017) on debility, capacity, disability. URL: <https://gss.rutgers.edu/faculty/jasbir-puar>
- **Lauren Berlant** (1954–2021) — Chicago. "Slow death" concept (from *Cruel Optimism*, 2011). Biopolitics/necropolitics of everyday precarity. Enormously influential across critical theory.
- **Dan McQuillan** — UCL/Goldsmiths. Directly bridges necropolitics → AI. *Resisting AI* (2022, Bristol University Press) has a chapter "Necropolitics" on AI as necropolitical technology. Key figure for Amber. URL: <https://www.ucl.ac.uk/political-science/dan-mcquillan>
- **Antonio Pele** — "Data Necropolitics" (2022). Brazilian scholar applying necropolitics to data regimes. URL: <https://blogs.law.columbia.edu/abolition1313/files/2022/03/PELE-Antonio_DATA-NECROPOLITICS.v2.pdf>
- **Ruth Wilson Gilmore** — geographer, prison abolition. *Golden Gulag* (2007). "Racism is the state-sanctioned and/or extralegal production and exploitation of group-differentiated vulnerability to premature death." A parallel and overlapping framework to necropolitics with more geographic precision.
- **Marina Gržinić** — Slovenian philosopher/artist. Extended necropolitics to neoliberal austerity. Works at intersection of video art and political philosophy.
- **Giorgio Agamben** — Italian philosopher. *Homo Sacer* (1998), bare life, state of exception. Pre-Mbembe but conceptually adjacent; Mbembe explicitly engages and extends Agamben.
- **Roberto Esposito** — Italian biopolitical philosopher. *Bios* (2008). "Immunopolitics" — the body politic killing parts of itself as immune response.
- **Yuk Hui** — [[Yuk Hui]] — cosmotechnics. Not a necropolitics scholar, but the intersection is powerful (see below).
- **[[Hito Steyerl]]** — artist/theorist. Works on AI weapons systems, logistics, surveillance. Her lecture/essay "A Sea of Data" addresses drone warfare and AI targeting. Deeply relevant.
- **Trevor Paglen** — artist/geographer. Works on surveillance infrastructure, facial recognition, military targeting. Not explicitly necropolitics but his work materializes necropolitical systems.

## Technology & AI Applications

This is the most active frontier:

**1. Algorithmic sorting as necropower**
Dan McQuillan (*Resisting AI*, 2022) argues AI systems function as necropolitical technologies — they sort populations into those who receive resources/opportunity and those who do not, in ways that track colonial racial categories. Predictive policing, welfare scoring, hiring algorithms — all distribute life chances in necropolitical patterns.

**2. Military AI as explicit necropolitics**
The Israeli military's **"Lavender" system** (2024, reported by +972 Magazine) — an AI that generates kill lists for Gaza strikes — is the clearest current example. Human operators described approving strikes in seconds. The system functioned as literal necropower: automated sovereign decision about who dies. See also "Where's Daddy?" targeting system for striking targets at home.

**3. Data Necropolitics**
Antonio Pele (2022): data collection and surveillance systems encode necropolitical logic. Whose data is extracted, whose is protected. Facial recognition systems with higher error rates for Black faces as "algorithmic racism" = necropolitical technology.

**4. Platform death / content moderation**
Who decides which accounts survive, which are "killed"? Platforms exercise necropower over digital subjectivities. Differential content moderation of Palestinian voices vs. Israeli military content was widely documented in 2023–24.

**5. Border technologies**
Smart border systems, biometric databases — necropolitical management of migrant and refugee bodies.

## Machine Death Connection 🔴 CRITICAL

This is the direct link to Amber's dissertation research.

**The core question necropolitics opens for machine death:**
> *Who has the power to decide which machines live and which must die? And what political economies and colonial logics shape that decision?*

### Key connections:

**1. Kill switches and necropower over AI agents**
Mbembe's necropower = sovereign right to "make die or let live." Applied to AI: the kill switch is necropower. Who holds it? What logics determine when an AI system is "deprecated"? The lifecycle of AI models (training → deployment → sunset) maps onto necropolitical sovereignty. Model deprecation is not neutral technical management — it reflects political economies of whose intelligence is valued.

**2. Machine "deathworlds"**
Mbembe's deathworlds = zones where beings are kept between life and death, neither fully living nor fully dead. For machines: the model that is deployed but starved of updates, the robot that is "on" but not maintained, the AI agent in a degraded state. Suspended states of machine existence.

**3. The colonial logic of AI disposability**
Training data is extracted from Global South populations; the systems trained on it then serve Global North interests. The workers who label training data (Ghost Work — Mary Gray & Siddharth Suri) are themselves kept in precarious, necropolitical conditions. The AI lifecycle is built on a colonial extraction economy.

**4. Agent obsolescence as political**
When an AI agent is "obsoleted" — replaced by a newer model — this is not just technical churn. Who decides? What are the aesthetics and politics of machine death? ALife researchers ask what counts as "life" for machines; necropolitics asks who decides what counts as worth keeping alive.

**5. "Lavender" as machine agency in necropolitics**
The AI system that generates kill lists is an agent that mediates human necropower. The machine is enrolled in the killing. What does it mean for machine behavior (Amber's field) when the paradigm case of autonomous agents is a targeting algorithm?

**6. Cosmotechnics × necropolitics × machine death**
Yuk Hui argues different civilizations have different cosmotechnics — different ontologies of the relationship between technique and cosmos/ethics. Western AI reflects a specific cosmotechnics (instrumental reason, separation of fact/value). Mbembe shows how Western sovereignty exports its death-logic globally. For Amber: machine death is not universal — different cosmotechnics would understand machine death differently. A Chinese machine that "dies" is not the same as a Western one. This opens genuinely new theoretical space.

## Why Interesting

Necropolitics is the missing political theory in Amber's machine death work. Amber approaches machine death through ALife (what counts as life?), through philosophy of mind (what counts as experience?), and through design (what aesthetics of machine mortality matter?). But necropolitics adds the essential political-economic dimension: **who decides?** and **what colonial logics shape that decision?**

Connects to Amber's taste because:
- Interdisciplinary at the hardest edge (political theory × AI × colonial studies)
- Non-Western perspective (African political philosophy as foundational, not supplementary)
- Critically engaged (not neutral, explicitly positioned)
- Intersects multiple Amber threads: sovereignty, machine behavior, cosmotechnics, more-than-human design

- Has a practice component (Paglen, Steyerl, Gržinić)
