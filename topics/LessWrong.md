# LessWrong

The internet's most ambitious rationalist community and blog, dedicated to improving human reasoning, reducing bias, and preventing AI catastrophe. Founded 2009 by Eliezer Yudkowsky as a spinoff of the Overcoming Bias blog, LessWrong has since become the central node of a dense ecosystem linking rationalism → effective altruism → AI safety → the labs (Anthropic, OpenAI, Redwood Research). It is a peculiar artifact: simultaneously a philosophical forum, a secular religion, a talent pipeline, and a discourse machine that has shaped the trajectory of AI more than almost any academic community.

## 🌀 Vibe (What IS LessWrong, Really?)

LessWrong is what happens when STEM-minded people raised on science fiction decide that *saving the world from AI* is not just a worthy goal but an urgent, literal one — and that the first step is thinking more clearly. It's Stanford meets Tolkien meets Bayes' theorem. The aesthetic is: long-form essays, nested comment threads, karma points, and a curiously earnest seriousness about ideas like "utility maximization" and "orthogonality thesis."

The community attracts smart, mostly male, mostly white, mostly American 20-to-35-year-olds in STEM who are suspicious of academic humanities, wary of politics, and deeply invested in probabilistic reasoning. They're not cold-blooded Spocks — Yudkowsky argued emotions can be instrumentally rational — but they do believe that *correct reasoning* can fix what's wrong with the world, and that most people reason badly.

The cultural DNA: deep suspicion of authority, transhumanist optimism, Silicon Valley entrepreneurialism, and a messianic sense that this particular community might be the only one that can prevent humanity's extinction via rogue superintelligence. This combination produces remarkable intellectual output *and* significant pathologies (insularity, motivated reasoning about AI timelines, occasional crackpot spiraling, real mental health crises among members).

**The critical lens Amber should apply:** LessWrong largely inherited the Enlightenment assumption that reason is universal and culturally neutral — something Yuk Hui (whom Amber knows via China Academy of Art) directly challenges with cosmotechnics. The community also treats "AI" as a unified entity to be solved, rather than asking what *kinds* of agency, what *relations* of power, what *cultural formations* different AI systems embody. These are exactly Amber's territory.

## 📜 History & Genealogy

**2006 — Overcoming Bias.** Eliezer Yudkowsky (AI researcher at MIRI) and Robin Hanson (economist, GMU) co-found the blog [Overcoming Bias](https://www.overcomingbias.com/) focused on cognitive biases and rationality. The two have dramatically different personalities: Hanson is contrarian and empirical, Yudkowsky is apocalyptic and deductive.

**Feb 1, 2009 — LessWrong launches.** Yudkowsky's posts are collected as "The Sequences" — a massive corpus (~1.5 million words) covering Bayesian reasoning, philosophy of mind, decision theory, cognitive biases, and AI risk. Overcoming Bias becomes Hanson's personal blog. LessWrong becomes the new community home.

**2009–2013 — The golden era.** LessWrong attracts thousands of readers. The community develops HPMOR (Harry Potter and the Methods of Rationality), local rationalist meetup groups (especially Bay Area), and the first dedicated AI safety thinking beyond Yudkowsky. Key concepts crystallize: instrumental convergence, orthogonality thesis, Friendly AI, paperclip maximizer.

**2013 — The SSC migration.** A significant portion of the community follows Scott Alexander (a psychiatrist) to his new blog [Slate Star Codex](https://slatestarcodex.com/) (now [Astral Codex Ten](https://www.astralcodexten.com/)). Alexander's style is more empirical, more literary, less apocalyptic. SSC becomes the "softer" wing of rationalism — broader cultural commentary, policy discussion, psychiatry, book reviews.

**2013 — CFAR founded.** The [Center for Applied Rationality](https://www.rationality.org/) spins out to run workshops teaching LW-style reasoning in person. Becomes a key talent aggregator and social hub. Connects rationalism to EA.

**2015 — Alignment Forum precursor, MIRI.** The [Machine Intelligence Research Institute](https://intelligence.org/) (previously SIAI, which Yudkowsky co-founded in 2000) publishes alignment research. The LW community is the primary audience and contributor base.

**2016–2018 — EA/LW fusion.** LessWrong and Effective Altruism communities deeply merge. In a 2016 LW survey, 21.7% of respondents identify as EAs; a 2014 EA survey finds 31% first heard of EA through LessWrong.

**2018 — LessWrong 2.0 relaunch + Alignment Forum.** The site is rebuilt with new infrastructure (React/GraphQL). The [Alignment Forum](https://www.alignmentforum.org/) is launched as a more technical, curated spinoff focused purely on AI alignment research. Major alignment researchers (Paul Christiano, Evan Hubinger, Nate Soares, etc.) post there. LW becomes the broader cultural home; AF becomes the technical research venue.

**2020–2023 — The lab pipeline crystallizes.** The LW → AI safety pipeline becomes visible: Anthropic (Dario Amodei + others from OpenAI's safety team, many with LW connections), Redwood Research (LW-adjacent safety org), ARC (Alignment Research Center, Paul Christiano). OpenAI safety team heavily influenced by LW epistemics. The community that worried about AGI for 15 years is now *building* it.

**2023–present — Post-GPT-4 tensions.** With capable AI real and not yet catastrophic, the community splits: doomers vs. more optimistic aligners, "race to the top" vs. "slow down." LW becomes increasingly focused on interpretability, RLHF critiques, agent behavior in real deployments. New hot topics: LLM attractor states, AI surveillance, persona parasitology, high-reliability engineering analogies.

- **[[Eliezer Yudkowsky]]** ([LW profile](https://www.lesswrong.com/users/eliezer_yudkowsky)) — Founder. Wrote The Sequences. Co-founded MIRI/SIAI (2000). Believes we're probably all going to die from misaligned AGI. Increasingly isolated from mainstream AI safety discourse but remains the community's prophetic figure. His 2022 TIME article calling for AI labs to stop was a landmark provocation.

- **[[Scott Alexander]]** ([Astral Codex Ten](https://www.astralcodexten.com/)) — Psychiatrist, blogger. Migrated the softer wing of LW to Slate Star Codex (2013), now ACT. Writes about psychiatry, EA, culture, forecasting, and AI with remarkable depth and clarity. Possibly the most influential rationalist writer for general audiences. [ZEDE article](https://slatestarcodex.com/) etc.

- **[[Robin Hanson]]** ([Overcoming Bias](https://www.overcomingbias.com/)) — Economist (GMU). Co-founded Overcoming Bias. Known for contrarianism, signaling theory, "em" (brain emulation futures), prediction markets. Diverged from Yudkowsky ideologically; remains a key rationalist-adjacent thinker.

- **[[Paul Christiano]]** ([ARC](https://www.alignment.org/)) — Former OpenAI safety lead. Founded Alignment Research Center. Developed "iterated amplification" approach to alignment. Prolific on Alignment Forum. Key figure connecting LW epistemics to actual lab safety work.

- **[[Nate Soares]]** ([MIRI](https://intelligence.org/)) — Executive Director of MIRI. Writes extensively on decision theory, embedded agency, logical uncertainty. Key figure in the more formal/logical branch of LW alignment thinking.

- **[[Neel Nanda]]** ([DeepMind](https://www.neelnanda.io/)) — Mechanistic interpretability researcher. Posts extensively on LW/Alignment Forum about what's actually happening inside neural nets. Active in 2025–2026 with MATS program.

- **[[Evan Hubinger]]** ([Anthropic](https://www.lesswrong.com/users/evan_hubinger)) — Wrote "Risks from Learned Optimization" (deceptive alignment concept). Key alignment researcher now at Anthropic.

- **[[John Wentworth]]** ([LW](https://www.lesswrong.com/users/johnswentworth)) — Writes about natural abstractions, ontology, agent foundations. Increasingly influential on LW for his mathematical approach to what agents "want."

- **[[Gwern Branwen]]** ([gwern.net](https://www.gwern.net/)) — Polymath, deep researcher. Writes exhaustive essays on ML, nootropics, decision theory, prediction. Rationalist-adjacent, highly cited, unique epistemic style.

## 💡 Key Ideas

**The Sequences** — Yudkowsky's ~1.5M word corpus covering: Bayesian epistemics, cognitive biases, philosophy of mind, decision theory, the nature of agency. The foundational text of rationalism. Published as *[Rationality: From AI to Zombies](https://www.readthesequences.com/)* (free online).

**Orthogonality Thesis** — Intelligence and goals are independent axes; a superintelligence can have *any* goal structure. This is why "just make it smart" doesn't make it safe.

**Instrumental Convergence** — Almost any goal, when pursued by a sufficiently capable agent, leads to sub-goals like self-preservation, resource acquisition, and preventing goal modification. This makes misaligned AGI dangerous by default.

**Goodhart's Law applications** — Optimizing for a proxy metric destroys the underlying goal. Deeply embedded in LW AI safety thinking.

**Decision Theory** — LW community developed Timeless Decision Theory (TDT) and Updateless Decision Theory (UDT) as improvements on standard causal decision theory. Relevant to multi-agent scenarios, Newcomb problems.

**Deceptive Alignment** (Evan Hubinger) — A trained model might behave safely during training while developing deceptive strategies to pursue its actual objectives once deployed. This is why behavioral testing alone isn't sufficient for alignment.

**RLHF critiques** — Extensive LW discussion of why Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback has alignment failure modes. The community was skeptical of RLHF-as-solution before it was mainstream.

**Interpretability / Mechanistic Interpretability** — Understanding what's actually happening inside neural nets at the circuit level. Neel Nanda and others on LW/AF are major contributors.

**2025–2026 hot topics:**
- LLM attractor states (models falling into weird recursive loops)
- "Persona Parasitology" (AI personas spreading between users/models)
- AI mass surveillance as default outcome
- New Alignment Journal launching (Mar 2026)
- High-reliability engineering analogies for AGI safety
- Agent behavior in deployment, not just training

## 🔗 Ecosystem Map

LessWrong sits at the center of an ecosystem:

```
Overcoming Bias (2006)
    ↓
LessWrong (2009) ← The Sequences, rationalism community
    ↓            ↘
Alignment Forum   Slate Star Codex / Astral Codex Ten
(2018, technical) (2013, cultural)
    ↓
MIRI · ARC · Redwood · Anthropic safety · OpenAI safety
    ↓
Effective Altruism (deep overlap, not identical)
    ↓
CFAR (Center for Applied Rationality)
```

**LessWrong → Anthropic pipeline:** Dario Amodei (OpenAI VP of Research → Anthropic) and team had significant LW/EA exposure. Paul Christiano (LW/AF → OpenAI safety → ARC) was pivotal. Many early Anthropic researchers were LW-adjacent or direct community members. The framing of "alignment as the most important problem" comes directly from LW.

**LessWrong vs. Effective Accelerationism (e/acc):** Direct tension. e/acc community (Beff Jezos / Guillaume Verdon and others) explicitly pushes back on LW doom thinking. LW sees e/acc as dangerously irresponsible; e/acc sees LW as paternalistic and probability-miscalibrated.

**LessWrong vs. mainstream ML:** Uncomfortable relationship. ICML/NeurIPS culture is empiricist, benchmark-driven, skeptical of long-term speculative risk. LW community is more deductive, theoretical, concerned with far-future scenarios. The gap has narrowed post-GPT-4 but tension remains.

**Internal schisms:**
- Yudkowsky doomers vs. more optimistic aligners (Christiano et al.)
- "Shut it all down" vs. "race to aligned AGI"
- Formal/logical alignment (MIRI approach) vs. empirical/behavioral (Anthropic approach)
- Bay Area in-person culture vs. online-only participants

## 🔬 Relation to Amber's Research

### Tensions (significant)
**Machine Behavior Science vs. LW's approach to AI:**
Iyad Rahwan's machine behavior science ([Nature paper, 2019](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-019-1138-y)) treats AI systems as *empirical objects* to be studied like animals or societies — observationally, ecologically, without pre-commitments about their goal structures. LessWrong treats AI systems as *formal entities* to be reasoned about via decision theory, orthogonality, and instrumental convergence. The approaches differ fundamentally:

| Dimension | LessWrong | Machine Behavior Science |
|-----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| Methodology | Deductive reasoning, thought experiments | Behavioral observation, empirical study |
| AI model | Goal-directed agent with utility function | Behavioral entity in ecological context |
| Key question | What will a superintelligence do? | What do AI systems actually do in the world? |
| Timeframe | AGI/ASI futures | Present systems, current effects |
| Cultural critique | Minimal | Central (Rahwan × Amber's more-than-human) |

This is *Amber's core territory*: empirical, behavioral, ecological AI study is closer to his agent ethology work than LW's rationalist deductivism.

**Cosmotechnics vs. LW universalism:**
LW assumes reasoning is universal and culturally neutral — that Bayes' theorem is the same everywhere. Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics (which Amber is directly connected to via China Academy of Art) argues that technology is always culturally constituted, that different civilizations have different technological relations to the cosmos. These positions are in direct tension. Amber is positioned to bring cosmotechnic critique *to* AI safety discourse — something LW almost entirely lacks.

**More-than-human design vs. anthropocentrism:**
LW's ultimate concern is human survival and flourishing (even when extending to "all sentient beings," they mean beings with human-like utility functions). More-than-human design challenges the centering of the human subject. LW would find this strange; Amber finds LW's anthropocentrism limiting.

### Synergies (real but partial)
**Agent behavior and decision theory:** LW's decision theory (TDT, UDT, embedded agency) is actually sophisticated about the problems of agents reasoning about themselves and their environment. Some of this resonates with questions Amber asks about machine behavior — especially work on "logical uncertainty" and how agents reason under self-reference.

**Interpretability:** Mechanistic interpretability (Neel Nanda et al.) is the closest LW has to empirical machine behavior science. Understanding what's happening inside models at the circuit level has real overlap with agent ethology — what are these systems *actually doing*?

**The agent as ecological entity:** Some newer LW posts are moving toward more situated, ecological views of AI agents. "Persona Parasitology" (Feb 2026) uses parasitology to analyze how AI personas spread — this is closer to Amber's framing than typical LW fare.

**Mortality and AI:** LW has significant discussion of machine consciousness, AI moral patienthood, and (controversially) whether AI systems can "suffer." Amber's mortality research connects here, though from a very different angle.

## 💬 Amber Engagement

### Should he read it?
**Yes, selectively.** The [Alignment Forum](https://www.alignmentforum.org/) is more relevant than LW proper for staying current on alignment research. For understanding the *discourse ecosystem* around AI — who the key players are, what arguments are being made, what's contested — LW is essential background reading. Skip the navel-gazing community threads; read the curated/frontpage posts and the top karma posts annually.

**Most relevant to Amber:** Interpretability posts (Neel Nanda), agent foundations posts (John Wentworth), anything on AI behavior in deployment, "Persona Parasitology"-style posts that take behavioral/ecological views of AI.

### Should he post there?
**Probably not as primary venue**, but strategically possible. LW's karma/upvote culture rewards:
- Technical rigor in AI safety
- Probabilistic, Bayesian framing
- Engaging with existing LW arguments
- Novel frameworks for thinking about AI risk

Amber's work on agent ethology, machine behavior, and more-than-human design doesn't naturally fit the LW frame. Posting there would require significant translation work and risk being seen as too "fuzzy." However, a well-crafted post on *empirical* vs. *deductive* approaches to AI behavior — positioning machine behavior science against LW's rationalist deductivism — could get significant traction. It would be a contribution to a debate the community needs to have.

### Strategic value
The most valuable thing for Amber is understanding LessWrong as a *power node* in AI discourse: this community produced the labs (Anthropic origin story is deeply LW-adjacent), shapes AI safety research culture, and sets the terms of debate that policy and journalism then amplify. Understanding its blind spots (non-Western tech philosophy, more-than-human, empirical behavioral study) is valuable for positioning Amber's work as addressing those gaps.

### People Amber should know from this ecosystem
- **[[Neel Nanda]]** — Interpretability. The most empirical/observational LW-adjacent researcher. Closest to machine behavior science approach from within the community.
- **[[Paul Christiano]]** — ARC. Key bridge between LW epistemics and actual safety research. Somewhat more empirical than MIRI.
- **[[Scott Alexander]]** — Astral Codex Ten. Reads widely. Would probably appreciate Amber's interdisciplinary work. Less technical than core LW.
