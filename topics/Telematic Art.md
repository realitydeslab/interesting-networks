# Telematic Art

Telematic art is art that uses computer-mediated telecommunications networks as its primary medium — transforming the viewer from passive spectator into active co-author of a distributed, emergent work. Coined and theorized by [[Roy Ascott]] from 1978 onwards (the term "telematics" itself comes from Nora & Minc's 1980 *The Computerization of Society*), telematic art emerged from cybernetics and second-order systems theory: it treats artworks not as objects but as processes — open, indeterminate, and co-constituted by geographically dispersed participants. Its key theoretical move is **distributed authorship**: no single author, no fixed text, no stable meaning, only an ongoing system of becoming. From Ascott's *Terminal Art* (1980) to *La Plissure du Texte* (1983), from [[Kit Galloway & Sherrie Rabinowitz]]'s *Hole-in-Space* (1980) to Norman White's *Hearsay* (1985), telematic art constituted one of the most radical proposals in 20th century aesthetics — that connectivity itself is the medium and that consciousness is fundamentally networked. Today, this field sits as a historical precursor to internet art and networked media, but its theoretical framework resonates powerfully with distributed AI agent systems, multi-agent creativity, and questions of collective machine behavior.

## Domain Summary

**Origin:** Telematic art emerged from two genealogies. First, the cybernetics tradition (Wiener, Ashby, Pask, von Foerster) that reframed art as behavior, process, and feedback system. Second, the telecommunications infrastructure of the late 1970s–1980s (ARPANET, early conferencing systems like ARTEX and Infomedia NOTEPAD, satellite uplinks) that made remote creative collaboration technically possible for the first time.

**Ascott's framing:** He distinguishes telematics from mere "teleconferencing" — telematics, for Ascott, involves the full interpenetration of human consciousness with digital networks, informed by Teilhard de Chardin's noosphere, Henri Bergson's duration, and I Ching's dynamic systems. The telematic embrace is not communication; it's co-evolution of mind and network.

**Theoretical pillars:**
- **Distributed authorship** — no one author; meaning emerges from collective, asynchronous contribution
- **Behavioral aesthetics** — art as process not product; Ascott's "Behaviourables and Futuribles" (1967) anticipates this
- **Moistmedia** (Ascott, 2000) — synthesis of wet (biological/neural) and dry (computational/digital) media, extending telematic art into bioart and consciousness research
- **Connectivity as consciousness** — the network is not a tool but an extension of mind (Ascott reads McLuhan through Bergson and Teilhard)

**Scope:** The field spans from Ascott's 1980 *Terminal Art* through the mid-1990s explosion of internet art, encompassing satellite art, slow-scan television experiments, fax art, ISDN video, and early internet projects. It institutionalized around Ars Electronica (from 1979), [[Planetary Collegium]] (1994), and the journal *Leonardo*.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Verdict: Established (not edge of chaos now).** The core telematic art period was 1978–1995. By the mid-1990s, the World Wide Web absorbed much of its energy, spawning "internet art" / "net.art" as a successor field. Telematic art is now **historicized** — studied via Ascott's *Telematic Embrace*, Edward Shanken's scholarship, Walker Art Center's archive. It is not currently fast-evolving.

**However:** Its theoretical framework is having a *second life* in distributed AI research. The questions Ascott asked — what does it mean for authorship to be distributed across a network? what kinds of collective intelligence can emerge? is connectivity itself generative? — are now being asked of multi-agent AI systems. This convergence is the intellectually live edge, but it's more of a historical revisitation than a fast-evolving new field.

**Relevance trajectory:** Rising for Amber's purposes precisely because AI agent research (including agent ethology) needs historical grounding. Telematic art is the art world's 40-year experiment in distributed, networked agency. Not edge of chaos itself, but generative for adjacent work that is.

## Key People

- **[[Roy Ascott]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — The founding father. Coined the term, built the theory, organized the key works, founded the Planetary Collegium. *Telematic Embrace* (2003) is the primary text. Still active in Shanghai.
- **[[Kit Galloway & Sherrie Rabinowitz]]** ⭐⭐ — Pioneered satellite art with *Satellite Arts Project* (1977, NASA) and *Hole-in-Space* (1980). Founded [[Electronic Café International]], Santa Monica. First to achieve live telematic performance across geographic distances.
- **[[Edward Shanken]]** ⭐⭐ — Primary historian and archivist of telematic art. Edited *Telematic Embrace*. Professor at UC Santa Cruz. For anyone studying this field, Shanken is the essential secondary source.
- **Norman White** — Canadian artist; *Hearsay* (1985), *Telephonic Arm Wrestling* (1986). Pioneer of machine-to-machine telematic interaction. Less theorized than Ascott but important practitioner.
- **Robert Adrian X** (Robert Adrian) — Vienna-based Canadian artist; organized *The World in 24 Hours* (Ars Electronica, 1982) — 16 cities connected via slow-scan TV, telephone, and early computer networks. Crucial early node.
- **Don Foresta** — Paris-based artist, key figure in French Minitel telematic art experiments and ISDN video networking.
- **Fred Forest** — French artist and sociologist; pioneered sociological art via Minitel networks in France; conceptually extended telematic art into participatory media sociology.
- **Karen O'Rourke** — Paris; telematic mapping projects; artist-researcher connecting geography and networked image.
- **[[Peter Weibel]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — Ran Ars Electronica (1986–1995); helped fund Ascott's *Aspects of Gaia* (1989); then ZKM. Institutional backbone of European telematic art.
- **Heidi Grundmann** — Editor of *Art + Telecommunication* (1984), the first major anthology documenting the field.

## Key Works

- **Terminal Art** (Roy Ascott, 1980) — World's first networked telematic artwork. US/UK computer conferencing via Infomedia NOTEPAD. Artists in California, New York, Wales.
- **Satellite Arts Project** (Kit Galloway & Sherrie Rabinowitz, 1977) — East/West coast US connected via NASA satellite; live composited dance performance. First satellite telematic artwork.
- **The World in 24 Hours** (Robert Adrian X, Ars Electronica 1982) — 16 global cities connected simultaneously via slow-scan TV, telephone, early computer networks. 24-hour real-time global artwork.
- **La Plissure du Texte** (Roy Ascott, 1983) — 11 nodes on ARTEX network collectively write a distributed fairy tale over 5–6 weeks; shown at Musée d'Art Moderne de la Ville de Paris ("Electra" exhibition). Title riffs on Barthes's "Le Plaisir du Texte." Defining work of distributed authorship.
- **Hole-in-Space** (Kit Galloway & Sherrie Rabinowitz, 1980) — Real-time video link between pedestrians in New York and Los Angeles; unannounced public installation. Landmark public telematic encounter.
- **Hearsay** (Norman White, 1985) — Early machine-to-machine telematic loop.
- **Aspects of Gaia: Digital Pathways Across the Whole Earth** (Ascott, Ars Electronica 1989) — Interactive installation connecting global communities around planetary consciousness questions.
- **Planetary Network** (Ascott, Venice Biennale 1986, XLII edition) — Telematic networking across venues and artists.

## Open Questions

1. **What does distributed authorship mean for AI agents?** If Ascott's telematic networks created works with no single author, what is the status of text/image/sound generated by multi-agent AI systems? Is there a meaningful continuity?
2. **Can "moistmedia" theorize AI+bio hybrids?** Ascott's wet/dry synthesis concept predates current biocomputation discussions — is this framework still useful?
3. **What gets lost in the transition from telematics to internet?** Net.art (jodi.org, etoy) had a very different aesthetics than telematic art — more ironic, less utopian. What happened to the consciousness-connectivity project?
4. **How does the non-Western context matter?** Ascott's Shanghai node and connections to Chinese art-tech infrastructure raise cosmotechnics questions: are there distinct Chinese or non-Western telematic aesthetics?
5. **Is collective AI output "distributed authorship"?** ChatGPT, Midjourney, collaborative AI tools — are these the telematic dream realized or a betrayal of it?

## Why Interesting

Telematic art is the art world's 40-year experiment in exactly the questions that now define AI agent systems: what emerges from distributed, asynchronous, networked co-creation? What is authorship when no individual is responsible for the whole? What kinds of consciousness or intelligence can arise from connectivity itself? Ascott was asking these questions in 1980 with artist networks; they're now being asked of GPT-4, multi-agent frameworks, and swarm intelligence systems. For Amber, who works on agent ethology and distributed AI, this is not just art history — it's a theoretical reservoir for conceptualizing what AI agent collectives *do* and what we should expect of them. The moistmedia concept specifically bridges wet (biological) and dry (computational) in a way that anticipates current biocomputation debates. And unlike most AI discourse, telematic art brings a critical, aesthetic, and humanistic framework to these questions — exactly the kind of bridge Amber builds.
