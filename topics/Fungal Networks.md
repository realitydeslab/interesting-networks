# Fungal Networks

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-03:** Full deep research. Upgraded to ⭐⭐⭐ — direct relevance to agent ethology, more-than-human design, distributed cognition, and ALife. Note covers science, key people, ALife connections, design/art practice, and actionable paths for Amber.
- **2026-02-26:** Stub created.

Fungal networks — most famously the mycorrhizal networks connecting tree roots underground — are one of the most generative intellectual objects of our moment. They challenge almost every assumption we bring from computing and AI about what intelligence, agency, and information processing require: a central processor, neurons, a body. Fungi have none of these, yet they solve optimization problems, respond to signals, cooperate and defect, and sustain ecosystems across millions of years. For researchers in agent ethology, more-than-human design, and artificial life, fungal networks are not just a biological curiosity but a **model system and a provocation**.

---

## Domain Summary

### What Mycorrhizal Networks Are
A **mycorrhizal network** (also: common mycorrhizal network, CMN) is an underground web formed by fungal hyphae (thread-like filaments) that interweave with plant roots. Approximately 90% of land plant species form mycorrhizal symbioses. The network physically connects individual trees and plants, enabling flows of carbon, water, phosphorus, nitrogen, and chemical signals across the forest floor.

The term **"Wood Wide Web"** was coined in a 1997 *Nature* paper by Suzanne Simard et al., demonstrating carbon transfer between Douglas fir and paper birch through shared fungal networks. The analogy to the internet is apt but also somewhat misleading — the network is not a neutral infrastructure but a living, evolving, economically structured system of exchange between agents with competing interests.

### The Science
Key mechanisms and findings:
- **Nutrient transfer**: Carbon (from photosynthesis) flows from trees to fungi in exchange for phosphorus and nitrogen. Ratios can shift with seasonal demand, drought, or herbivory.
- **Preferential allocation**: Both fungi and plants allocate more resources to better-performing partners — a form of market-like selection discovered by Kiers et al.
- **Chemical signaling**: Simard's lab documented warning signals (defensive enzyme induction) traveling tree-to-tree via fungal networks — though the evidence is contested.
- **Hub trees ("Mother Trees")**: Older, larger trees appear to act as highly connected hubs, disproportionately supporting seedlings of their own and other species.
- **Contestation**: A 2023 *Nature Ecology & Evolution* review significantly challenged "Wood Wide Web" claims — especially about directed resource transfer and network-level solidarity. The science is still live and contentious, which is intellectually interesting.

### Slime Molds as Related Model Systems
**Physarum polycephalum** (a slime mold, not a fungus but often discussed together) became famous for the 2010 *Science* paper by Tero et al. showing it can solve the Tokyo rail network optimization problem. This sparked enormous interest in fungal/slime-mold computation as a model for distributed, non-neural intelligence — directly relevant to ALife and decentralized computing.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Not quite edge of chaos, but productively contested.** The mycorrhizal "Wood Wide Web" narrative peaked around 2020 (Sheldrake's book) and is now being empirically stress-tested. A 2023 pushback in *Nature Ecology & Evolution* challenged the "solidarity" framing. This creates a valuable intellectual moment: the pop narrative is too simple, but the underlying science — distributed biochemical computation, non-neural agency, multispecies mutualism — remains deeply interesting and under-theorized from a design/computing perspective.

What IS fast-evolving:
- The design/art community engaging with mycelium as material (Ecovative, Officina Corpuscoli)
- Computational models of fungal network behavior in ALife
- Critical posthumanism using fungi as case study (Haraway, Tsing, Sheldrake lineage)

## Connections to Amber's Research

### Agent Ethology
Fungi are perhaps the most interesting case for **agent ethology without agents** — a distributed network that exhibits goal-directed behavior (nutrient seeking, route optimization, partner preference) without any identifiable agent to observe. This inverts the usual problem: instead of studying agents, we study emergent agency from a network. *What would an ethogram of a mycorrhizal network look like?* This is a genuine open question and publishable framing.

### Distributed Cognition / Stigmergy
Fungal networks are a physical instantiation of stigmergy — the environment itself is the computational medium. Hyphae modify the substrate as they grow; the network topology encodes past experience. This connects directly to decentralized AI architectures, swarm intelligence, and the broader distributed cognition literature. The parallel to blockchain/mesh networks is explicit in some computing literature.

### More-than-Human Design
The strongest design angle is not **designing for fungi** but **designing with fungal networks** — treating the network as an active design partner with its own agenda. This echoes Wakkary's more-than-human design agenda. Mycelium design studios (Officina Corpuscoli, Ecovative) are doing this practically; Amber could contribute the theoretical framing.

### ALife
Fungal network growth is studied as an ALife system: hyphal tip extension, anastomosis (network fusion), resource-driven branching, network pruning. There are agent-based models and L-system models of mycelial growth. The slime mold / Physarum work (Tero 2010) is the most famous computational result from this space.

### Cosmotechnics / East Asian Relations to Fungi
This is an underexplored angle. Japan has the richest cultural relationship to fungi — matsutake in particular (Tsing's *Mushroom at the End of the World* centers Japan's satoyama forests). Chinese medical traditions also use fungi extensively (Reishi/Lingzhi, Cordyceps). The question of **different technics of relating to fungal networks** — Japanese satoyama stewardship vs. Western industrial forestry vs. mycoremediation engineering — is a cosmotechnics question that bridges Yuk Hui's framework with more-than-human ecology. This is highly original territory.

### Machine Metaphors: "Machine Fungus"
What if AI architectures were designed mycelially — no central node, distributed sensing, adaptive routing, cooperative resource allocation with other networks, dying when resources are absent? The fungal network is an existence proof that decentralized, non-neural, non-directed computation can sustain complex ecosystems. This is a productive provocation for machine behavior and AI architecture critique.
