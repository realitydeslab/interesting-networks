# Symbiogenesis

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-03:** Deep research complete. Upgraded from ⭐⭐ to ⭐⭐⭐ — directly bridges ALife, more-than-human design, and agent ethology. ALIFE 2026 deadline March 30.

---

Symbiogenesis (also called the endosymbiotic theory or serial endosymbiotic theory) is the leading evolutionary account of how eukaryotic cells arose from mergers of prokaryotic organisms — not through mutation and selection alone, but through *cooperative incorporation*. Organisms became organisms by swallowing each other and living together. The mitochondria in every one of your cells were once free-living bacteria. So were chloroplasts. Life complexifies not only by competition but by **radical merger**. This is the theory Lynn Margulis spent her career defending against a resistant establishment, and it is now accepted as foundational biology. Its implications — for how we think about individuality, cooperation, emergence, and the boundaries of organisms — are far from exhausted.

---

## Domain Summary

**What it is:** Symbiogenesis holds that eukaryotic cells (cells with nuclei — the basis for all complex life: animals, plants, fungi, protists) arose through a series of endosymbiotic events in which one prokaryote was engulfed by another and, rather than being digested, became a permanent resident. Mitochondria evolved from proteobacteria (related to *Rickettsiales*); chloroplasts from cyanobacteria. The evidence is overwhelming: mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own circular DNA, replicate independently by binary fission, have their own ribosomes, and share membrane proteins (porins, cardiolipin) with bacteria.

**History:**
- **1883:** [Andreas Schimper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andreas_Franz_Wilhelm_Schimper) notes chloroplast division resembles free-living cyanobacteria — cautiously suggests symbiotic origin.
- **1905–1910:** [Konstantin Mereschkowski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konstantin_Mereschkowski), Russian botanist, formalizes the theory: *The Theory of Two Plasms as the Basis of Symbiogenesis* (1910). Proposes two episodes of symbiogenesis for nuclei and chloroplasts.
- **1924:** [Boris Kozo-Polyansky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boris_Kozo-Polyansky) frames symbiogenesis in Darwinian terms: "The theory of symbiogenesis is a theory of selection relying on the phenomenon of symbiosis."
- **1967:** [Lynn Margulis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lynn_Margulis) (then publishing as Lynn Sagan) publishes "On the Origin of Mitosing Cells" in *Journal of Theoretical Biology* after ~15 rejections. This is the landmark paper. URL: [https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0022519367900793](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0022519367900793) DOI: 10.1016/0022-5193(67)90079-3
- **1981:** Margulis publishes *Symbiosis in Cell Evolution* — extends theory to spirochaetes as origin of eukaryotic flagella (this part remains disputed).
- **1980s–2000s:** Genetic sequencing confirms endosymbiotic origins of mitochondria and chloroplasts. Theory moves from fringe to consensus.

**The deeper claim:** Margulis and [Dorion Sagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dorion_Sagan) argued: *"Life did not take over the globe by combat, but by networking."* This is a direct challenge to the neo-Darwinian emphasis on competition as evolution's primary driver. Symbiogenesis foregrounds **cooperation, merger, and interdependence** as evolutionary forces at least as important as competition.

**What's still contested:** The flagella-as-spirochaetes hypothesis (Margulis's extension) is not supported. But the core mitochondria/chloroplast endosymbiosis is established. Active research: secondary endosymbiosis (one eukaryote engulfing another), horizontal gene transfer between endosymbionts and host genomes.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Is it fast-evolving?** The core biology is settled — not edge of chaos there. But the *theoretical and philosophical implications* are actively being developed:

- **STS / feminist science studies:** Haraway's sympoiesis (making-together) concept is generating new work in multispecies ethnography and more-than-human design.
- **ALife:** The question of how to model symbiogenetic dynamics in artificial systems is genuinely open. "Artificial Symbiogenesis" (Bull & Fogarty, 1995) was early; recent work explores open-ended evolution and major evolutionary transitions.
- **Philosophy of biology:** What counts as an individual organism when organisms are composed of former organisms? The "holobiont" concept (organism + microbiome as unit of selection) is actively debated.
- **Machine ethology / AI:** If cooperation is as powerful an evolutionary driver as competition, what does this mean for multi-agent AI systems? The "merger" logic of symbiogenesis hasn't been seriously explored in machine behavior contexts.

**Verdict:** The biology is stable. The *philosophical and design implications* are still being worked out — edge of chaos in adjacent humanistic/design fields.

## Sub-topics & Trends (last 3–5 years)

- **Holobiont theory** — organism + microbiome as evolutionary unit. Key figures: [Eugene Rosenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Rosenberg), Ilana Zilber-Rosenberg. Hotly debated: does the holobiont constitute a unit of selection? URL: [https://www.nature.com/articles/nrmicro2787](https://www.nature.com/articles/nrmicro2787)
- **Secondary and tertiary endosymbiosis** — diatoms, dinoflagellates. Complexity stacking.
- **Sympoiesis (Haraway)** — "making together" as alternative to autopoiesis. See *Staying with the Trouble* (2016). Directly applicable to more-than-human design.
- **Symbiogenesis in ALife** — open-ended evolution models incorporating merger/integration dynamics. ALIFE community slowly engaging.
- **Philosophy of individuality** — [Peter Godfrey-Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Godfrey-Smith) on Darwinian populations and individuality; [Scott Gilbert](https://www.swarthmore.edu/profile/scott-gilbert) on symbiotic view of development.
- **Mycorrhizal networks** — "Wood Wide Web" as contemporary popularization of symbiogenetic thinking (though distinct from endosymbiosis proper). Anna Tsing's *Mushroom at the End of the World* is adjacent.

## Why Interesting

Symbiogenesis is the foundational biological argument that **cooperation, not just competition, drives the emergence of complexity**. This is directly relevant to every thread in Amber's research:

- **ALife:** It's the paradigmatic example of a major evolutionary transition — the kind of qualitative leap that open-ended evolution research aspires to explain and produce artificially.
- **More-than-human design:** Haraway built sympoiesis directly on Margulis. Every time Amber reads Haraway, she's reading Margulis filtered through feminist STS.
- **Agent ethology:** Symbiogenesis offers a model of agent behavior (organisms-as-agents) where merger and interdependence are as important as competition. The "machine behavior" field is still largely competition-centric — this is a genuine gap.
- **Machine death/mortality:** If organisms are assemblages, what constitutes the death of an assembled entity? This is philosophically live and directly connected to Amber's mortality thread.
- **Cosmotechnics:** Margulis's work was explicitly anti-Western in its emphasis on cooperation and networking over combat — there's a cosmotechnical angle in how different traditions foreground symbiosis vs competition.

The research taste match: interdisciplinary (biology × philosophy × STS × design), practice-implications (not just history of science), feminist (Margulis fought the establishment; Haraway politicized her findings), edge-adjacent (the implications are still being worked out). **Strong ⭐⭐⭐**.
