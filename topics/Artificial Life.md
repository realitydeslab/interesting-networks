# Artificial Life

**Life-as-it-could-be.** Artificial Life (ALife) is the study of life and life-like processes through simulation, synthesis, and theory. Unlike biology (which studies "life as we know it"), ALife explores "life as it could be" — asking what principles make something alive, adaptive, creative, or evolving, regardless of substrate. The field spans software simulation ("soft" ALife), robotics ("hard" ALife), and biochemistry ("wet" ALife).

Named by **Christopher Langton** in 1986, ALife has roots in **John von Neumann's** self-reproducing automata (1940s-50s), **Conway's Game of Life** (1970), **Thomas Ray's Tierra** (1990s), and **Karl Sims' evolved virtual creatures** (1994). After a quiet period in the 2000s-2010s, ALife is now experiencing a major resurgence driven by the convergence of LLM-based agent simulations, open-endedness research, and the realization that understanding "life" is prerequisite to understanding intelligence.

## Why It Matters NOW

The field is at a remarkable inflection point. Three forces are converging:

1. **LLM agents as artificial life** — Generative agents (Joon Sung Park), Concordia (Joel Leibo), and LLM-based social simulations are essentially ALife experiments. When you put 25 LLM agents in a virtual town and they form relationships, gossip, and organize a party — that's artificial life. The entire "agent" wave in AI is, whether it knows it or not, an ALife project.

2. **Open-endedness as the missing piece for ASI** — The open-endedness community (Stanley, Lehman, Clune, Ha) argues that true superintelligence requires open-ended evolution — systems that keep producing novelty forever. This connects ALife directly to the most important question in AI safety and capability.

3. **Machine behavior as behavioral science** — Iyad Rahwan's framing of studying AI systems the way we study animal behavior is fundamentally an ALife perspective. Amber's own "Agent Ethology" research sits squarely here.

## Active Sub-topics & Trends (2022-2026)

### Open-Endedness
Can evolution run forever, producing endless novelty? The central unsolved problem of ALife.
- **Key people:** [[Ken Stanley]] (novelty search, Lila Sciences), [[Joel Lehman]] (evolution through large models), [[Jeff Clune]] (AI-generating algorithms, quality-diversity), [[David Ha]] (Sakana AI), Tim Taylor
- **Key paper:** [Open-Endedness is Essential for Artificial Superhuman Intelligence](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.04268) (2024)
- **Key paper:** [Evolution through Large Models](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.08896) (Lehman et al., 2022) — using LLMs as mutation operators in evolutionary search
- **Status:** Very hot. The bridge between ALife and frontier AI. Lila Sciences and Sakana AI are commercial bets on this.

### LLM Agents as Artificial Life
Multi-agent LLM simulations are the new Tierra/Avida.
- **Key people:** [[Joon Sung Park]] (Generative Agents), [[Joel Z Leibo]] (Concordia), [[Takashi Ikegami]] (emergence of individuality)
- **Key paper:** [Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03442) (Park et al., 2023)
- **Key paper:** [Spontaneous Emergence of Agent Individuality through Social Interactions in LLM-Based Communities](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.03252) (2024) — agents develop distinct personalities through interaction
- **Key paper:** [Voyager: An Open-Ended Embodied Agent](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.16291) (2023) — LLM-powered lifelong learning agent in Minecraft
- **Status:** Explosive growth. Every major AI lab is running multi-agent simulations. The ALife community is the intellectual home for understanding what's emerging.
- **Amber connection:** Agent Ethology is essentially this — studying emergent agent behavior. Very strong fit.

### Active Inference
Karl Friston's Free Energy Principle as a theory of life itself.
- **Key people:** [[Karl Friston]], Maxwell Ramstead (VERSES AI), [[Blaise Agüera y Arcas]]
- **Key institution:** [[Active Inference Institute]]
- **Status:** Increasingly connected to ALife. Active inference provides a principled mathematical framework for understanding why agents do what they do. Growing community, multiple textbooks published.

### Artificial Chemistry & Origin of Life
Computational and wet-lab approaches to creating life from scratch.
- **Key people:** [[Sara Imari Walker]] (assembly theory), [[Lee Cronin]] (chemputers, chemical computers), Kate Adamala (synthetic cells), Juan Perez-Mercader (biochemistry-free artificial life)
- **Key venue:** ALIFE 2024 Copenhagen featured several origin-of-life keynotes
- **Status:** Active but more niche. Assembly theory (Walker + Cronin) is the breakout concept — a way to measure "life-likeness" of any molecule.

### Evolutionary Computation Meets Deep Learning
ALife's practical legacy: using evolution to design neural networks.
- **Key people:** [[David Ha]] / [[Sakana AI]], [[Jeff Clune]], Jean-Baptiste Mouret (MAP-Elites)
- **Key result:** Sakana AI's model merging — using evolutionary approaches to combine pretrained LLMs, creating new capabilities without training from scratch
- **Key paper:** [The AI Scientist: Towards Fully Automated Open-Ended Scientific Discovery](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.06292) (Sakana AI, 2024)
- **Status:** Very hot. Commercial traction. Sakana AI is valued at $1B+.

### Machine Behavior
Studying AI systems as a behavioral science.
- **Key people:** [[Iyad Rahwan]] (coined the field), [[Joel Z Leibo]], Amber (Botao Hu — Agent Ethology!)
- **Key paper:** [Machine Behaviour](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-019-1138-y) (Rahwan et al., Nature 2019)
- **Key institution:** [[Machine+Behavior]] community, Center for Humans and Machines (Max Planck)
- **Status:** Amber is already in this space. The connection between machine behavior and ALife is natural and underexplored.

### Bioelectric Intelligence & Morphogenesis
Michael Levin's work on how cells and tissues compute.
- **Key people:** Michael Levin (Tufts), Katie Bentley (Francis Crick Institute)
- **Key concept:** Cells as agents. Tissues as multi-agent systems. Development as collective intelligence.
- **Status:** Rapidly growing. Levin's ideas went mainstream via YouTube talks and ALIFE 2025 keynote.

### Digital Ecosystems & Virtual Ecologies
Environments where digital organisms evolve and interact over time.
- **Key people:** Emily Dolson (Michigan State), Josh Bongard (Vermont), [[Hiroki Sayama]]
- **Classic systems:** Tierra, Avida, NEAT
- **Modern systems:** Neural cellular automata, Lenia (Bert Wang-Chak Chan)
- **Key paper:** Lenia — continuous cellular automata showing life-like behavior
- **Status:** Steady. The aesthetic/artistic dimension of these systems is underexplored.

### Bio-Art & ALife Art
Art practice informed by ALife principles.
- **Key people:** [[Takashi Ikegami]] (art keynote ALIFE 2025), Nao Tokui (Qosmo), Tatsuya Daikoku
- **Key venues:** [[Ars Electronica]], ALIFE art sessions
- **Status:** Small but growing. ALIFE conferences now have dedicated art keynotes and sessions. The bridge between ALife science and creative practice is Amber's sweet spot.

### Embodied ALife / Evolutionary Robotics
Physical robots that evolve and self-organize.
- **Key people:** [[Josh Bongard]] (Vermont), Hod Lipson (Columbia), David Hanson (Hanson Robotics, Sophia)
- **Status:** Ongoing. Hanson brought Sophia the Robot to ALIFE 2025 as a special event.

## Open Questions

1. **Can we achieve open-ended evolution in silico?** — The holy grail. No system has yet produced evolution as creative as nature's. Can LLMs change this?
2. **Are LLM agent societies "alive"?** — When generative agents form communities, develop culture, and surprise their creators, what are they?
3. **What is the minimal definition of life?** — Assembly theory, autopoiesis, self-reproduction — still debated.
4. **Can ALife principles produce superhuman AI?** — The open-endedness → ASI argument. If true, ALife is central to AI's future.
5. **How do we study machine behavior scientifically?** — Observation, experiment, theory — we need an ethology for AI.
6. **What are the ethics of creating artificial life?** — If we make digital organisms that evolve and suffer, what are our obligations?
7. **Can ALife bridge the two cultures?** — Art, science, philosophy, engineering — ALife naturally sits at the intersection.

## Why Interesting

ALife is Amber's natural intellectual home. The field asks the deepest questions — what is life, what is creativity, what is intelligence — through *making* rather than just theorizing. It's inherently interdisciplinary (CS × biology × art × philosophy), practice-based, and at the edge of chaos. The convergence of LLM agents, open-endedness research, and machine behavior is creating a moment where ALife thinking is suddenly essential to understanding the most important developments in AI. Amber's unique position — bridging HCI, design, social computing, machine behavior, and agent ethology — is exactly what the ALife community needs and doesn't have enough of. This is not a field to watch from the outside; this is a field to enter.
