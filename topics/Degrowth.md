# Degrowth

An academic movement and political-economic critique arguing that the planned, democratic reduction of production and consumption is necessary to address ecological and social crises. Degrowth is not recession — it is a *deliberate* transformation away from GDP-growth as the organizing principle of society, toward well-being, care, ecological health, and conviviality. It intersects with ecological economics, eco-socialism, post-colonial theory, and increasingly with technology critique.

## Domain Summary

Degrowth emerged from 1970s eco-political thought, catalyzed by André Gorz's use of the French term *décroissance* (1972) and the Club of Rome's *Limits to Growth* (1972). It was revived as an academic research programme in the 2000s, centered at ICTA-UAB (Barcelona) with Giorgos Kallis, Serge Latouche's provocation from France, and later Jason Hickel's global-justice framing.

**Core claims:**
1. GDP growth requires increasing resource throughput; absolute decoupling at sufficient scale is empirically contested.
2. Growth causes ecological overshoot (climate, biodiversity, soil, water).
3. Growth is not necessary for human well-being — beyond a threshold, more GDP does not equal more flourishing.
4. The current fixation on growth is historically contingent — a product of colonial capitalism, not a natural law.
5. Democratic, planned reduction (not austerity) can improve well-being while healing ecologies.

**Key policy proposals:** 4-day work week, universal basic services, wealth caps, care economy expansion, commons governance, post-extractivist economies in the Global South, deglobalization of supply chains.

**Intellectual genealogy:** André Gorz → Ivan Illich (*Tools for Conviviality*, 1973) → Club of Rome → Herman Daly (steady-state economics) → Serge Latouche → Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen (thermodynamics of economics) → Giorgos Kallis, Jason Hickel, Kate Raworth, Tim Jackson.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Not edge of chaos.** Degrowth is a relatively mature critique (~50 years old) that has recently reached mainstream policy debates (European Parliament 2023, Lancet 2025). The intellectual core is fairly settled. However, the **intersection of degrowth with AI critique is genuinely emergent** and fast-evolving: the question of whether AI accelerates extraction/growth vs. enables sufficiency is unresolved and politically charged.

In 2025-2026, degrowth.info is explicitly framing tech-billionaire authoritarianism (Musk, Thiel, Palantir) as an expression of growth ideology — this is a new and energized angle. The movement is also engaging more with digital commons, care robotics critique, and post-extractivist AI.

**Fast-moving sub-arenas:**
- AI and degrowth: energy costs of AI, algorithmic governance of scarcity/abundance
- Degrowth × digital commons (decentralized, open-source as post-growth infrastructure)
- Degrowth × Global South (decolonial critiques, Buen Vivir, Ubuntu economics)

## Connections to Amber's Research

### → [[Cosmotechnics]]
Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics and technodiversity is a different register but has direct resonance. Both degrowth and cosmotechnics critique technological universalism — degrowth via ecological limits, cosmotechnics via cultural/epistemic diversity. Both argue against the idea that there is *one* trajectory for technology/economy. The connection: **technodiversity as degrowth's epistemological cousin** — plural technologies (convivial tools, indigenous techniques, open-source commons) vs. the single growth-maximizing trajectory. Ivan Illich's "convivial tools" (1973) anticipates both.

### → [[More-than-human Design]]
Degrowth's critique of extraction and its valorization of care, conviviality, and ecological embeddedness resonates deeply with more-than-human design. The question "what does the economy look like if non-humans are stakeholders?" maps onto degrowth's question "what does the economy look like if ecological limits are respected?" Arturo Escobar's postdevelopment work connects Buen Vivir (multispecies relationality) to degrowth.

### → [[Maintenance and Care]]
The care economy is central to degrowth: unpaid care work (currently excluded from GDP) is what degrowth wants to valorize. This directly intersects with repair, maintenance, infrastructural care as research themes.

### → [[Digital Commons]]
Digital commons (open-source, P2P, creative commons) are increasingly framed as degrowth infrastructure — replacing proprietary growth-maximizing platforms with commons-governed abundance.

### → AI and Technology Critique
**This is the most live connection for Amber.** Key questions:
- AI's energy footprint as emblem of growth imperatives (data centers = new extractivism)
- AI for care vs. AI for surveillance/optimization (which economic paradigm does AI serve?)
- Machine behavior under degrowth: what does an AI agent look like in a post-growth economy?
- "Convivial AI" as a research framing — AI that empowers rather than monopolizes
- Tech-authoritarianism (Thiel, Musk) as growth ideology incarnate — degrowth as political counter

### → Speculative Design
Degrowth + speculative design = *designing for sufficiency*, futures of less but better. There's an emerging practice of degrowth design fiction. The question "what does a degrowth future look like materially, experientially, aesthetically?" is a live design research question.
