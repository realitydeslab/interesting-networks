# Synthetic Biology

The engineering of biological systems — cells, organisms, genetic circuits — using modular, standardized parts. Where computer science meets molecular biology: DNA as code, cells as programmable machines, life as design space. The field spans from BioBricks and genetic circuits to minimal cells (JCVI-Syn3.0), xenobiology (life with expanded genetic alphabets), and — crucially for Amber — the philosophical, artistic, and ALife questions these practices force open: *What counts as life? Who owns it? Can we design new forms of being?*

## Domain Summary

Synthetic biology (SynBio) applies engineering principles to biological systems: standardized parts (BioBricks), composable genetic circuits (toggles, oscillators, logic gates), whole-genome synthesis, and directed evolution. Founded in practice at MIT ~2003 with Tom Knight's BioBrick standard and the first iGEM competition.

**Three paradigms coexist:**
1. **Top-down SynBio** — strip existing genomes to minimal viable sets (JCVI J. Craig Venter Institute's synthetic cells: Syn1.0 in 2010, Syn3.0/3A in 2016/2023)
2. **Bottom-up SynBio** — assemble life-like systems from non-living chemistry (protocells, cell-free systems, origin-of-life research)
3. **Bio Art / Critical SynBio** — use biological engineering as artistic medium to interrogate life, death, nature, ethics

The field is directly entangled with [[Artificial Life]]: synthetic biology is *wet ALife* — life-as-it-could-be realized in the physical rather than the digital substrate. SymbioticA explicitly calls this "semi-living" — entities that are neither fully alive nor fully artifact.

**Xenobiology** extends SynBio into genuinely new biochemistry: unnatural base pairs (Scripps: X and Y bases, 2014/2019), mirror-image life (L-DNA), organisms with expanded or contracted genetic codes. These are not just engineering feats — they destabilize the category of "life" itself.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Highly active, fast-reshaping on multiple fronts:**
- **AI + SynBio merger:** AlphaFold → protein design; Evo (Arc Institute, 2024) → genomic foundation model predicting mutation effects across species; RFDiffusion → de novo protein design. Biology becomes legible to LLMs.
- **Living materials (2024-2026):** Buildings, textiles, and objects made from engineered living organisms that grow, self-repair, sense environment. Architecture + biology. Neri Oxman's legacy visible here.
- **Cell-free systems:** Wetware without cells — programmable biochemistry that doesn't need to be alive. Bridges SynBio and wet computing ([[Wet Computing]]).
- **iGEM democratization:** High school teams doing SynBio. Community labs (DIYbio). Decentralization of biotech capability.
- **Art-science programs under pressure:** SymbioticA nearly defunded 2022. Institutional support for critical bioart is fragile but the intellectual community is stronger than ever.

**Edge of chaos verdict:** ⭐⭐⭐ — this is alive, contested, fast-moving, and philosophically rich. The AI merger makes it especially urgent in 2025-2026.

## Open Questions

1. **What counts as life?** JCVI-Syn3.0 is a cell, but is it *alive* in a meaningful sense, or just self-replicating chemistry? Does the answer matter?
2. **Top-down vs. bottom-up:** Can we bootstrap life from scratch (protocells → cells)? Or do we always need existing life as scaffold?
3. **AI + biology merger:** As genomic foundation models (Evo, AlphaFold) become standard tools, does SynBio become a branch of ML? What's lost in that framing?
4. **Ownership of life:** If you synthesize a novel organism, do you own it? BioBricks are open source — but CRISPR patents are not. Who governs synthetic life?
5. **Wetware computation:** Can living cells replace silicon? Cell-free transcription-translation as programmable logic — where does this go?
6. **The "semi-living" condition:** Catts & Zurr's term for entities that are neither fully alive nor artifact. Does this category destabilize our ethics? Our aesthetics?
7. **Non-Western SynBio:** Is there a Cosmotechnics of synthetic biology? How do Chinese, Japanese, or African traditions of relating to life/nature shape different approaches? (Yuk Hui question)
8. **Death and synthetic organisms:** If you grow a living artwork, who is responsible for killing it when the exhibition ends? (The MoMA *Victimless Leather* incident, 2008)
9. **Open-ended evolution in wetware:** Can synthetic organisms evolve open-endedly? Or does designed biology resist the drift that makes evolution creative?

## Why Interesting

Synthetic biology sits at the exact intersection of Amber's deepest concerns:

**ALife connection — the most direct:** SynBio is wet ALife. Where digital ALife asks "can we simulate life?", SynBio asks "can we engineer life?" Both are versions of Langton's "life-as-it-could-be." Protocells and minimal cells are empirical attempts at answering questions that ALife addresses computationally. For Amber's ALIFE 2026 work, SynBio offers the biological counterpoint — the substrate that AI agents will increasingly interact with, design for, or become continuous with.

**More-than-human design:** Synthetic organisms are not tools — they are (semi-)living agents with their own logics, vulnerabilities, and deaths. Designing *with* living material means accepting non-human agency. The Tissue Culture & Art Project's deliberate choice to grow things that die — and to make that death visible — is more-than-human design practiced with radical commitment.

**Machine consciousness / wetware:** As AI and biology merge (Evo, living neural organoids, BrainNet), the question of what "machine" means becomes biological. Wetware computation — using living cells as logic gates — is not science fiction; it's active research. This directly connects to Amber's interest in machine consciousness and cognitive substrate independence.

**The philosophical question of life:** No field forces the question "what is life?" more directly than SynBio. This is not abstract philosophy — it has legal, ethical, aesthetic consequences. Who is responsible for killing a semi-living artwork? Can a minimal genome cell be "alive" if 149 of its 473 genes have unknown function? SynBio makes the question urgent, material, and unresolvable.

**Art-science as critical practice:** The bioart lineage — Catts/Zurr, Dumitriu, Ginsberg, Zaretsky — shows that being *in* the lab (not just commenting from outside) produces different knowledge. SymbioticA's model: artists with full access to wet labs, growing things, killing things, being changed by the encounter. This is Amber's model — not critique from outside but making-as-knowing.

**Cosmotechnics question (speculative):** Yuk Hui asks about non-Western technics — the different cosmological assumptions embedded in technologies. Is there a Chinese synthetic biology, shaped by different relationships to nature, qi, organism? The dominance of Western engineering metaphors (DNA as code, organisms as systems) may obscure other possible approaches. Worth exploring given Amber's Yao Class/China Academy of Art connections.
