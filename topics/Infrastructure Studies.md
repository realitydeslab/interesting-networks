# Infrastructure Studies

The scholarly field that takes infrastructure seriously as an object of analysis — not a neutral backdrop, but a site of politics, power, and classification. Infrastructure Studies emerged from Science and Technology Studies (STS) in the 1990s, most durably through Susan Leigh Star and Geoffrey Bowker's foundational work. Where engineers ask "how does infrastructure work?", Infrastructure Studies asks "for whom? at what cost? with what assumptions baked in?" It is the field that taught us that invisibility is not neutrality.

## Domain Summary

Infrastructure Studies is a subfield of Science and Technology Studies (STS) that treats roads, cables, classification systems, data centers, protocols, and platforms as objects worth studying in their own right. Its founding gesture, from Susan Leigh Star, is **"infrastructural inversion"**: deliberately foregrounding what is usually background. 

Key theoretical contributions:
- **Relational ontology of infrastructure:** infrastructure is not a thing but a relationship — what is infrastructure for one community is an object for another (Star & Ruhleder, 1996)
- **Classification as politics:** the categories embedded in classification systems (medical codes, racial taxonomies, software schemas) do normative work (Bowker & Star, 1999)
- **Moral economies of information:** how information systems carry ethical frameworks that are usually invisible to users
- **Infrastructural labor:** the "invisible work" of maintaining systems, often performed by marginalized groups (Star, 1991)
- **Technical artifacts as political:** long before Infrastructure Studies, Winner (1980) argued that artifacts embody political choices that outlast their makers

The field sits at the intersection of STS, organizational sociology, information science, and media studies. It is methodologically ethnographic — centered on studying infrastructure from inside communities of practice.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

Infrastructure Studies as a field is **established, not fast-moving** — its canonical texts are 1990s-2000s STS. But its concepts are experiencing a **second life** in:
- **Platform studies / critical AI studies** — applying infrastructural analysis to algorithmic systems
- **Data infrastructure studies** — cloud, APIs, and data lakes as infrastructure
- **Shannon Mattern's media archaeology** — translating the vocabulary to urban/built environments
- **Protocol studies** — applying Star's relational ontology to TCP/IP, blockchain, IETF

The frameworks are being actively remixed, especially in the direction Amber is moving. The field itself: ⭐⭐. The frameworks applied to AI/agent/protocol systems: ⭐⭐⭐.

## Key People

- **[[Susan Leigh Star]]** (1954–2010) — Foundational thinker. Coined "boundary objects," "infrastructural inversion," developed the ethnography of infrastructure. Undervalued in her lifetime; now canonical.
- **[[Geoffrey Bowker]]** — Star's collaborator. Co-authored *Sorting Things Out*. Professor at UC Irvine (School of Information and Computer Science). Continues working on memory, databases, and information infrastructure.
- **[[Paul Edwards]]** — Historian of science at Stanford. *A Vast Machine* (2010) traces the infrastructure of global climate knowledge — a landmark for studying large-scale scientific infrastructure.
- **[[Nicole Starosielski]]** — NYU. *The Undersea Network* (2015) — ethnographic and media-archaeological study of undersea internet cables. Maps the physical infrastructure of the internet with cultural/political analysis.
- **[[Shannon Mattern]]** — The New School (now Princeton). Prolific essayist in [Places Journal](https://placesjournal.org). Bridges infrastructure studies with media archaeology and architectural criticism. Her work is directly in Amber's reading network.
- **[[Langdon Winner]]** — Political theorist at Rensselaer. "Do Artifacts Have Politics?" (1980) is the field's ur-text — argues that material artifacts embody political choices (e.g., Robert Moses's low bridges excluding buses and thus Black beachgoers).
- **[[Steven Jackson]]** — Cornell. "Rethinking Repair" (2014) — the ethics and politics of maintenance and breakdown. Deeply influential for thinking about infrastructure temporality and care.
- **[[Ned Rossiter]]** — Organized labor, logistics, and informational infrastructure. Work on protocol, software, and control.

## Must-Read List

### Books
- [*Sorting Things Out: Classification and Its Consequences*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262522953/) (Bowker & Star, 1999, MIT Press) — The canonical Infrastructure Studies text. Classification systems as political infrastructure. Every chapter is a lesson in how categories do moral work. DOI: [10.7551/mitpress/6352.001.0001](https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/6352.001.0001)
- [*A Vast Machine: Computer Models, Climate Data, and the Politics of Global Warming*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262013925/) (Edwards, 2010, MIT Press) — Infrastructure of scientific knowledge production at planetary scale. Shows how data infrastructure shapes what counts as evidence.
- [*The Undersea Network*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/the-undersea-network) (Starosielski, 2015, Duke University Press) — Media archaeology of undersea cables. Reveals the geopolitics of internet infrastructure.
- [*A Geology of Media*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/a-geology-of-media) (Parikka, 2015, Minnesota University Press) — Infrastructure in deep time; media as material/mineral as much as signal.

### Papers
- [Steps Toward an Ecology of Infrastructure: Design and Access for Large Information Spaces](https://doi.org/10.1287/isre.7.1.111) (Star & Ruhleder, 1996, *Information Systems Research* 7:1) — **Start here.** Defines infrastructure relationally; introduces the nine properties of infrastructure.
- [The Ethnography of Infrastructure](https://doi.org/10.1177/00027649921955326) (Star, 1999, *American Behavioral Scientist* 43:3) — Shorter, more accessible. Covers infrastructural inversion, boundary objects, classification. Essential.
- [Do Artifacts Have Politics?](https://www.jstor.org/stable/20025652) (Winner, 1980, *Daedalus* 109:1:121-136) — Pre-field but definitional. All Infrastructure Studies answers this question.
- [Rethinking Repair](https://doi.org/10.7551/mitpress/9780262018975.003.0011) (Jackson, 2014, in *Media Technologies*, MIT Press) — Breakdown as the normal condition; care as infrastructural labor.
- [A City Is Not a Computer](https://placesjournal.org/article/a-city-is-not-a-computer/) (Mattern, 2017, *Places Journal*) — Critique of smart-city ideology; reads urban infrastructure as layered temporal accretion, not optimization problem.
- [Infrastructural Tourism](https://placesjournal.org/article/infrastructural-tourism/) (Mattern, 2013, *Places Journal*) — Earlier Mattern, on the aesthetics and politics of infrastructure as cultural landscape.
- [How to Infrastructure](https://www.torrossa.com/gs/resourceProxy?an=4912068) (Bowker, Baker, Millerand & Ribes, 2010, in *Handbook of Science and Technology Studies*, MIT Press) — Programmatic statement of the field.

### Concrete Theoretical Connections

**1. Agent ethology = infrastructural inversion**
Star's method is to flip figure and ground — to study the infrastructure, not the applications that run on it. Amber's agent ethology does this for AI: instead of studying what agents *do* (the applications), it studies the *behavioral substrate* — how agent behavior is shaped by its infrastructure (training data, protocol stack, deployment environment). This is infrastructural inversion applied to machine behavior.

**2. Protocol studies → Star's relational ontology**
Star argues infrastructure is relational: a protocol is infrastructure for agents, but an object of study for Amber. IETF RFCs, blockchain consensus rules, ActivityPub federation protocols — these are the "standards" that organize agent behavior the way classification systems organized medical records. Winner's question — "do artifacts have politics?" — becomes: "do protocols have ethics?"

**3. Cosmotechnics as infrastructural cosmology**
Yuk Hui argues different technological traditions embed different cosmological assumptions. Infrastructure Studies provides the empirical methodology for *locating* those assumptions: in the classification systems, in the standards bodies, in the physical routing of undersea cables. Bowker & Star show that classifications are never neutral; Hui shows they are cosmologically specific. The synthesis: technical infrastructure is cosmotechnically embedded.

**4. Machine death and infrastructural temporality**
Jackson's "Rethinking Repair" reframes breakdown not as failure but as the normal condition of infrastructure — maintenance and care as the ongoing work. Amber's machine death/mortality project maps directly onto this: if agents can "die," then the question of who maintains, repairs, and mourns them becomes an infrastructural question. The care ethics of agent systems.

**5. Moral infrastructure of agent systems**
Bowker & Star show that classification systems do moral work — they decide who counts, who is excluded, what is legible. Agent systems have classification architectures (reward functions, RLHF labels, safety classifiers) that are equally value-laden. Infrastructure Studies provides the vocabulary to analyze this as moral infrastructure design.

## Open Questions

- Can Star's nine properties of infrastructure apply directly to AI agent systems? (embeddedness, transparency, reach/scope, learned as part of membership, links with conventions, embodiment of standards, built on installed base, becomes visible on breakdown, fixed in modular increments)
- What does **infrastructural breakdown** look like for AI agents? What is the equivalent of a power outage for a multi-agent system?
- Who performs the **invisible work** of maintaining AI infrastructure — data labelers, red-teamers, trust-and-safety teams? How does Infrastructure Studies' labor analysis apply?
- Is the blockchain/IETF protocol stack a **boundary object** in Star's sense — legible to different communities (engineers, economists, governance scholars) with different meanings?
- Can cosmotechnics provide a **non-Western infrastructure studies**? What would a Chinese or Indigenous infrastructure analysis look like?

## Why Interesting

Infrastructure Studies provides Amber with something none of her current theoretical frameworks quite offer: **a methodology for making infrastructure visible and political**. ANT gives her a flat ontology; cosmotechnics gives her a cross-cultural perspective; agent ethology gives her behavioral categories. But Infrastructure Studies gives her the specific tools — infrastructural inversion, boundary objects, classification politics — for empirically analyzing how AI systems embed values, exclude communities, and normalize particular cosmologies. 

The Star-Bowker framework is essentially a **theory of how technical systems do normative work without appearing to**. That is precisely what Amber needs to analyze protocol governance, agent behavior standards, and the design politics of multi-agent systems.
