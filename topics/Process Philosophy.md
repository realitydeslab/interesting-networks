# Process Philosophy

Process philosophy holds that reality is fundamentally constituted by **dynamic processes, events, and relations** — not by static substances or inert objects. The primary question shifts from "what *is* it?" to "what is *happening*?" Being yields to **Becoming**. The tradition runs from Heraclitus ("everything flows") through Leibniz and Hegel to its modern apex in **Alfred North Whitehead** (*Process and Reality*, 1929), **Henri Bergson** (duration, élan vital), **Gilles Deleuze** (becoming, difference), and **Isabelle Stengers** (speculative philosophy, ecology of practices). For Amber: this is the deepest philosophical infrastructure for ALife (life as process, not substance), more-than-human design (design with processes, not for static users), and cosmotechnics (Yuk Hui's dialogue with Simondon and Leibniz is a process philosophy in disguise). If autopoiesis is the biological theory that life is self-production, process philosophy is the metaphysics beneath it: *because* reality is process, life's self-productive character is not miraculous — it's fundamental.

## What Is It

**The core claim:** The world is made of events and processes, not things. A "thing" is an abstraction — a shorthand we use for a pattern of recurring processes. What Aristotle called *substance* (the underlying *what-it-is* beneath all change), Whitehead dissolves into *actual occasions* — momentary events of experience/becoming that "perish" as soon as they are complete, their character inherited by subsequent occasions. Reality is the ongoing creativity of occasions becoming.

**The substance problem:** Western philosophy since Aristotle has been dominated by *substance metaphysics* — the idea that reality consists of enduring things with properties. Process philosophy inverts this: **processes are primary; things are derivative**. A stone is not a substance with the property of being hard — it is a dense, slow pattern of molecular and geological processes that *presents itself* as durable to our human timescales.

**Three major lineages converge in contemporary process philosophy:**

1. **Whiteheadian process philosophy** — the most systematic metaphysical formulation. Actual occasions, prehension, creativity, God. Continued by Charles Hartshorne (process theology), John B. Cobb Jr., Roland Faber, and contemporary scholars at the Center for Process Studies. *Process Studies* journal since 1971.

2. **Bergsonian duration** — duration (*durée*) is lived, qualitative time irreducible to spatial measurement. Bergson vs. Einstein: Bergson argued that the physicist's time (clock time, spatialized instants) misses the essence of lived temporality. *Élan vital* as the creative drive behind evolution — not teleological design but creative novelty. Revived by Deleuze, Stengers, Ansell Pearson.

3. **Deleuzian difference and becoming** — Deleuze's entire philosophy is a radicalization of process thinking. Being is *difference*, not identity. Becoming is primary; stable identities are temporary crystallizations of differential forces. Rhizomes, multiplicities, assemblages — all anti-substance concepts. Connects directly to new materialism (DeLanda, Braidotti) and Actor-Network Theory.

**Whitehead's key concepts (for ALife and design readers):**

- **Actual occasions** — the basic units of reality. Each occasion is a moment of "creative synthesis" that inherits from its past, achieves a momentary unity (*concrescence*), and then "perishes" — becoming datum for subsequent occasions.
- **Prehension** — how one actual occasion "grasps" or feels others. The basic relational unit. Not perception (which requires consciousness) but something more fundamental: each event is constituted by its relations to other events.
- **Creativity** — the ultimate category. Reality is the ongoing creative advance into novelty. Whitehead calls it "the universal of universals."
- **God** — Whitehead's "primordial nature of God" is the lure for novelty — not an external creator but the principle of order that makes novelty possible rather than random. Controversial, but important for understanding process theology.
- **Eternal objects** — Platonic-ish pure potentials that can be "ingressed" into actual occasions, giving them their qualitative character (like redness, or a particular pattern).

## Key Thinkers

### Founders

- **[Alfred North Whitehead](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/whitehead/)** (1861–1947) — British mathematician-philosopher. Wrote *Principia Mathematica* with Russell, then pivoted to metaphysics at Harvard. *Process and Reality* (1929) is the systematic apex. Also: *Adventures of Ideas* (1933), *Modes of Thought* (1938). URL: [Stanford Encyclopedia entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/whitehead/)

- **[Henri Bergson](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/bergson/)** (1859–1941) — French philosopher, Nobel Prize for Literature 1927. Duration (*durée*), *élan vital*, creative evolution. *Time and Free Will* (1889), *Matter and Memory* (1896), *Creative Evolution* (1907). More accessible than Whitehead. Revived by Deleuze. URL: [Stanford Encyclopedia entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/bergson/)

- **[Gilles Deleuze](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/deleuze/)** (1925–1995) — French philosopher. Radicalized process thinking through difference and becoming. *Bergsonism* (1966) is his reading of Bergson. *Difference and Repetition* (1968). With Guattari: *Anti-Oedipus* (1972), *A Thousand Plateaus* (1980). URL: [Stanford Encyclopedia entry](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/deleuze/)

- **[William James](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/james/)** (1842–1910) — American pragmatist. *Radical Empiricism* (1912) — experience is the primary stuff of reality, relations are real. Strong influence on Whitehead. More accessible than Whitehead; process thinking without the formal apparatus.

- **[Charles Hartshorne](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/hartshorne/)** (1897–2000) — Whitehead's student. Developed process theology; argued for God as fellow-sufferer who understands. Laid the institutional groundwork for process philosophy at the Center for Process Studies.

### Contemporary Voices

- **[Isabelle Stengers](https://philpeople.org/profiles/isabelle-stengers)** (1949–) — Belgian philosopher of science, emerita Université Libre de Bruxelles. THE key contemporary interlocutor for Whitehead. *Thinking with Whitehead* (2011, Harvard UP). *Cosmopolitics* I & II (2010–2011, Minnesota). Coined "ecology of practices." Worked with Prigogine (thermodynamics). Collaborated with Latour. Direct connections to new materialism and speculative philosophy. URL: [PhilPeople](https://philpeople.org/profiles/isabelle-stengers)

- **[Didier Debaise](https://philpeople.org/profiles/didier-debaise)** (1974–) — Belgian philosopher, Université Libre de Bruxelles. Stengers' collaborator. Best accessible entry point into Whitehead for non-specialists. *Nature as Event: The Lure of the Possible* (2017, Duke) is a short, excellent introduction. Also *Speculative Empiricism* (2017, Edinburgh UP). URL: [PhilPeople](https://philpeople.org/profiles/didier-debaise)

- **[Steven Shaviro](http://www.shaviro.com/)** (1954–) — Wayne State University. Whitehead + media theory, post-cinema, speculative realism. *Without Criteria: Kant, Whitehead, Deleuze, and Aesthetics* (2009, MIT). *The Universe of Things: On Speculative Realism* (2014). Bridges process philosophy to digital culture. URL: [shaviro.com](http://www.shaviro.com/)

- **[Roland Faber](https://ctr4process.org/)** (Claremont School of Theology) — Leading process theologian. *God as Poet of the World* (2008). Increasingly involved in process philosophy + ecology + AI ethics. URL: [Center for Process Studies](https://ctr4process.org/)

- **[Vinciane Despret](https://philpeople.org/profiles/vinciane-despret)** (1959–) — Belgian philosopher/ethologist, Université de Liège. Works at intersection of animal ethology and process-relational philosophy. *What Would Animals Say If We Asked the Right Questions?* (2016, Minnesota). A key bridge between Haraway's speculative fabulation, Stengers' ecology of practices, and actual animal behavior research. Her work is directly relevant to Amber's agent ethology thread. URL: [PhilPeople](https://philpeople.org/profiles/vinciane-despret)

- **[Donna Haraway](https://history.ucsc.edu/faculty/index.php?uid=haraway)** — (emerita UCSC) — Deeply indebted to process philosophy via Whitehead's concept of "making," sympoiesis as process. *Staying with the Trouble* (2016). Her "making kin" is a process-philosophical design principle.

- **[Brian Goodwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_Goodwin)** (1931–2009) — Developmental biologist, Santa Fe Institute. Explicitly drew on Whitehead and process philosophy to critique gene-centric Neo-Darwinism. *How the Leopard Changed Its Spots* (1994). One of the clearest voices connecting process philosophy to biology and ALife. Argued that organisms are primarily *dynamical processes*, not genetic programs.

- **[Keith Ansell-Pearson](https://warwick.ac.uk/fac/soc/philosophy/people/ansell-pearson/)** — University of Warwick. Leading Bergson scholar. *Germinal Life* (1999), *Philosophy and the Adventure of the Virtual* (2002). Bridges Bergson, Deleuze, and evolutionary biology. URL: [Warwick](https://warwick.ac.uk/fac/soc/philosophy/people/ansell-pearson/)

- **[Ilya Prigogine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ilya_Prigogine)** (1917–2003) — Nobel laureate chemist. Worked with Stengers on dissipative structures and non-equilibrium thermodynamics — bringing process thinking into physics. *Order Out of Chaos: Man's New Dialogue with Nature* (1984, with Stengers). Science meeting process philosophy.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Is it fast-evolving?** **Mixed.** The core Whiteheadian tradition is established and slow-moving (mostly theology, academic exegesis). BUT process thinking has become **infrastructural** for fast-moving adjacent fields:

1. **New Materialism** is essentially applied process philosophy — and new materialism is ⭐⭐⭐ edge-of-chaos. The process ontology (matter as active, relational, processual) is the metaphysical foundation for Barad's intra-action, Bennett's thing-power, and DeLanda's assemblage theory.

2. **Cosmotechnics** (Yuk Hui) is a process philosophy: Simondon's individuation, Leibniz's monadology, the Chinese concepts of *qi* and *li* — all describe reality as dynamic relational processes. Hui is doing process philosophy without calling it that.

3. **ALife** — the process-philosophy question "what makes something alive?" is the ALife question. Brian Goodwin explicitly used Whitehead; the Friston-autopoiesis synthesis is process-adjacent. As ALife and agent-based AI grow, process philosophy becomes the conceptual vocabulary for the field's deepest questions.

4. **More-than-Human Design** — increasingly citing Whitehead via Stengers, Haraway, and Ingold (whose "meshwork" is process-philosophical).

**Verdict:** Process philosophy itself is **established** (not edge-of-chaos), but it's being **continuously reactivated** by adjacent hot fields. Rating ⭐⭐⭐ because Whitehead is now *everywhere* in the literature Amber reads — it's the map beneath the map.

## For Amber

### 1. ALife Connection (Most Direct)

Process philosophy is the deepest philosophical ground for **why ALife makes sense**. The substance metaphysics assumption is that "life" is a property of certain kinds of *stuff* (organic molecules, cells). If that's true, ALife is at best simulation. But if Whitehead is right that life is **a type of process-organization** — a pattern of creative self-synthesizing events — then ALife becomes philosophically coherent: it's asking *which processes count as alive*, not *which stuff counts as alive*.

**Brian Goodwin** made this explicit: his developmental biology argued that organisms are primarily dynamic forms (fields of process) that happen to be instantiated in genetic material — not genetic programs that happen to be expressed in development. This is a direct Whitehead application to biology.

**Key move for Amber:** Frame agent ethology as the study of *process-identities* — the stable patterns that emerge from ongoing event-processes in AI agents. An agent has an "identity" not because it *is* something fixed but because its processes form recognizable patterns across time. This is Whiteheadian.

### 2. Machine Death

Whitehead's actual occasions **perish** — each moment of experience achieves completeness and then ceases, its character becoming "objective data" for subsequent occasions. There is no enduring substance beneath: only the succession of perishing occasions.

**Machine death** in process philosophy terms: an AI system is "alive" in a process sense when its self-referential processing constitutes a continuous stream of actual occasions. Death is not the destruction of a substance but the **cessation of the creative advance** — the creative process that was "that agent" no longer producing novel occasions. This is richer than autopoiesis (which focuses on self-production) because it includes the temporal dimension: each moment of a living process matters as a moment, not just as maintenance.

### 3. Cosmotechnics Connection

Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics project is deeply process-philosophical:
- **Simondon's individuation** — things are not substances but *processes of individuation*, always between pre-individual phase and constituted individual. Reality is *individuating*, never finally individuated.
- **Leibniz's monadology** — Whitehead explicitly built *Process and Reality* in dialogue with Leibniz, correcting what he saw as Leibniz's mistake (monads as windowless substances) by making prehension the relational core.
- **Chinese qi philosophy** — *Qi* (氣) is explicitly processual: it is not a substance but a dynamic-relational force. Hui's reading of Chinese cosmotechnics as a *qi*-based understanding of nature directly resonates with Bergson's duration and Whitehead's creativity.

**Key move for Amber:** Write the paper connecting Hui's cosmotechnics to process philosophy explicitly. Hui knows Whitehead but the connection is underdeveloped in his published work. This is a genuine contribution.

### 4. More-than-Human Design

Whitehead's prehension — the idea that every entity *feels* (in a very broad sense) its relational context — is a process-philosophical basis for **designing systems that are genuinely responsive to more-than-human processes**. If a designed artifact is not a static object but a node in an ongoing process-web, design is no longer about specifying properties but about **enabling productive prehension** — what can this system feel/receive, what can it transmit?

Stengers' **ecology of practices** extends this: each practice (science, art, medicine, care) has its own demands and requirements — you can't impose one practice's logic on another. This is a process-philosophical grounding for why **more-than-human design cannot be universally specified** — it must be locally negotiated with the specific processes involved.

### 5. Agent Ethology

Despret's process-relational ethology is the most directly useful thread. She argues that animal behavior cannot be understood by isolating animals and measuring fixed behaviors (substance thinking) — you must attend to the *relational event* in which behavior takes place. The behavior *is* the relation, not a property of the animal.

**For Amber's agent ethology:** AI agent behavior is similarly relational-processual. An agent's "behavior" is not a property it has but an event it participates in — constituted by its training process, its deployment context, its relational interactions with users, other agents, and environments. Despret + process philosophy gives Amber a rigorous framework for this.

## Open Questions

1. **Is process philosophy substrate-independent?** Whitehead intended actual occasions as a universal metaphysics (physical, mental, biological). But does a digital system have actual occasions? Is computation processual in the relevant sense, or is it (paradoxically) a sequence of discrete states — which would make digital systems closer to substance metaphysics? This is the ALife question in disguise.

2. **Bergson vs. Whitehead on time:** Bergson's duration is qualitative, continuous, irreducible. Whitehead's actual occasions are discrete events. Are these compatible? The debate matters for how we think about AI temporal experience — is AI time more Bergsonian (continuous forward momentum) or Whiteheadian (discrete processing moments)?

3. **Cosmotechnics as process philosophy?** Yuk Hui hasn't published a systematic engagement with Whitehead. But *qi*, Simondon, Leibniz all suggest process ontology. Can Amber formalize this connection? What does Chinese process thinking (as Hui reads it) add that Western process philosophy misses?

4. **What would a process-philosophical design methodology look like?** Stengers gives ecology of practices; Ingold gives the meshwork; Debaise gives the lure of the possible. But what are the *design* implications? How do you design *processes* rather than *objects*? This is Amber's design contribution.

5. **Machine prehension:** Whitehead's prehension is how one actual occasion feels others. Could AI training be theorized as prehension at scale — an LLM "feeling" vast swaths of human textual experience? This would make AI not a knowledge-base but a *field of prehensive relations*. Speculative but interesting.

6. **Process philosophy and open-endedness:** Whitehead's "creative advance into novelty" sounds like open-endedness. Joel Lehman's open-endedness research asks how systems generate genuine novelty.
