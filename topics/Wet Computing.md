# Wet Computing

Wet computing is a family of approaches to computation that uses biological or organic substrates — neurons, DNA, slime mould, proteins, organoids — as computational media, instead of silicon. It encompasses DNA computing (information encoded in nucleic acids), wetware computers (neurons and brain organoids as hardware), chemical computing (reaction-diffusion systems as logic), and network-based biocomputation (motor proteins navigating nano-channels). The field sits at the crossing of unconventional computing, synthetic biology, ALife, and philosophy of mind: it asks what counts as computation, what counts as a machine, and — increasingly urgently — what kinds of living systems are owed moral consideration. The 2022 DishBrain experiment (in vitro neurons learning to play Pong) and the 2023 Johns Hopkins Organoid Intelligence programme pushed these questions from speculative to immediately pressing. Wet computing is no longer purely theoretical: it is being engineered, and its ethics are unresolved.

## Domain Summary

Wet computing is an umbrella term for non-silicon computation using living or biological matter. The field has at least four lineages:

1. **DNA Computing** (founded Len Adleman 1994): information encoded in DNA strands; molecular reactions compute combinatorial solutions in parallel. Currently practical for data storage (ultra-high density) more than real-time computation.

2. **Wetware / Neuronal Computing**: using live neurons as computing elements. Ranges from William Ditto's leech-neuron arithmetic unit (1999) to Brett Kagan's DishBrain (cultured human neurons in a simulated environment). The OI programme at Johns Hopkins (Hartung lab) is the most ambitious contemporary effort.

3. **Chemical / Reaction-Diffusion Computing**: using chemical waves and self-organising physical processes as logic. Andrew Adamatzky's UWE Bristol lab works with slime mould (*Physarum polycephalum*), soldier crabs, and fungal electrical spikes. These systems are inherently massively parallel and self-repairing.

4. **Synthetic Biology Computing**: engineering genetic circuits inside cells to perform logic operations. Gene regulatory networks as boolean circuits; cells as programmable agents.

The term "wetware" entered the vocabulary from cyberpunk (Gibson's *Neuromancer*), was formalised by Dennis Bray's *Wetware: A Computer in Every Living Cell* (2009), and has since been adopted in scientific literature. Alan Turing's 1952 morphogenesis model (chemical interactions generating biological pattern without central control) is the theoretical ancestor.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Yes — this is currently fast-evolving.** The field is being driven by three converging forces:

1. **Moore's Law plateau**: silicon is approaching physical limits; biological substrates offer an alternative scaling path.
2. **Energy crisis in AI**: LLM training is extremely energy-intensive; the brain does comparable cognition on ~20W. Neuromorphic and wetware approaches promise orders-of-magnitude efficiency gains.
3. **Synthetic biology maturation**: CRISPR and organoid culture now allow genuine engineering of living computational substrates.

**The ethics are getting urgent.** The DishBrain experiment and OI roadmap raise questions about consciousness and sentience in in vitro biological systems that have no existing ethical framework. In 1-3 years: we will likely see commercial neuromorphic chips incorporating biological components, and the first frameworks for "organoid ethics". This is a space where philosophers, ALife researchers, and designers need to be present.

## Sub-topics & Trends (2021–2026)

- **Organoid Intelligence (OI)**: The Johns Hopkins roadmap (Smirnova et al. 2023) proposes brain organoids as general-purpose biocomputers. Active experimental programme.
- **DishBrain / in vitro learning**: Cortical Labs (Brett Kagan) showed that cultured neurons can learn Pong, raising questions about minimal sentience and embodiment.
- **Xenobots / Anthrobots**: Michael Levin + Josh Bongard labs — living robots from frog and human cells. Blurs biological/robotic boundary entirely.
- **Fungal computing**: Adamatzky's work on mycelium electrical spiking as information networks. Also relevant to Tim Taylor's ALife work.
- **DNA data storage**: Microsoft Research, Twist Bioscience — DNA as ultra-dense archival medium. Less philosophically interesting but commercially active.
- **Network-based biocomputation**: Molecular motors navigating nanoscale channels to solve NP-complete problems (Nicolau et al. 2016).
- **Bioelectricity as computation**: Levin's lab — electric fields encode morphogenetic information; body-wide computation without nervous system.

## Must-Read List

### Books
- [*Wetware: A Computer in Every Living Cell*](https://yalebooks.yale.edu/book/9780300167849/wetware/) (Dennis Bray, 2009, Yale UP) — the foundational text; argues every cell is a computer, memory and computation are biological primitives.
- [*Physarum Machines: Computers from Slime Mould*](https://worldscientific.com/worldscibooks/10.1142/7968) (Andrew Adamatzky, 2010, World Scientific) — definitive account of slime mould as computing substrate.
- [*Computing Nature*](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-642-37225-4) (Vedral et al., eds., 2013, Springer) — broad unconventional computing overview including biological substrates.

### Papers
- [Molecular Computation of Solutions to Combinatorial Problems](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.7973651) (Leonard Adleman, *Science*, 1994) — the founding paper of DNA computing; solved 7-node Hamiltonian path in DNA.
- [Maze-solving by an amoeboid organism](https://doi.org/10.1038/35035159) (Nakagaki et al., *Nature*, 2000) — *Physarum polycephalum* solves shortest-path problems; classic demonstration of biological computation without neurons.
- [In vitro neurons learn and exhibit sentience when embodied in a simulated game-world](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2022.09.001) (Kagan et al., *Neuron*, 2022) — DishBrain; cultured neurons learned Pong; raises sentience and embodiment questions immediately.
- [Organoid intelligence (OI): the new frontier in biocomputing and intelligence-in-a-dish](https://doi.org/10.3389/fsci.2023.1017235) (Smirnova et al., *Frontiers in Science*, 2023) — OI programme roadmap; 17 authors; frames organoids as next-gen AI substrate.
- [A scalable pipeline for designing reconfigurable organisms](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1910837117) (Kriegman et al., *PNAS*, 2020) — xenobots; first fully-biological robots designed by AI and assembled from frog stem cells.
- [The Chemical Basis of Morphogenesis](https://doi.org/10.1098/rstb.1952.0012) (Turing, *Phil. Trans. Royal Society*, 1952) — theoretical ancestor; shows chemistry produces pattern without central control.

### Journals
- [*International Journal of Unconventional Computing*](https://www.oldcitypublishing.com/journals/ijuc-home/) — Adamatzky's journal; main venue for wet computing papers.
- [*Artificial Life*](https://direct.mit.edu/artl) (MIT Press) — broad ALife journal; covers biological computing as life-substrate.
- [*ACS Synthetic Biology*](https://pubs.acs.org/journal/asbcd) — synthetic biology computing (gene circuits, genetic logic gates).
- [*Biosystems*](https://www.sciencedirect.com/journal/biosystems) (Elsevier) — theoretical and experimental biological computing.

## Open Questions

1. At what scale do biological computers acquire moral status? (DishBrain raised this for neurons; OI raises it for organoids.)
2. Is DNA computing practically scalable, or is it always a conceptual proof?
3. What is the relationship between biological computing and consciousness? (Levin argues all biological tissue computes — does it feel?)
4. Can wetware systems achieve open-ended evolution? (The ALife open-endedness question applied to wet substrates.)
5. How do we design *with* biological computing materials rather than *using* them? (A more-than-human design question.)
6. What ethical frameworks apply when the computational substrate can suffer?
