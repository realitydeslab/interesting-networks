Design that decenters the human as the primary stakeholder. More-than-human design considers nonhuman agencies — animals, plants, machines, ecosystems, materials, technologies — as active participants and co-designers in design processes. This approach challenges anthropocentric design paradigms and seeks to create systems, interfaces, and experiences that account for the agency, interests, and perspectives of non-human entities.

## Domain Summary

More-than-human design emerged from feminist technoscience (Donna Haraway), posthuman theory, and participatory design traditions. It represents a fundamental shift from "user-centered" to "world-centered" design, questioning who gets to be considered a stakeholder in design decisions. The field spans interaction design, speculative design, critical design, environmental design, and AI/machine behavior research.

The field has evolved from theoretical provocations (1990s-2000s) to practical methodologies (2010s) to established academic programs and conferences (2020s). It now includes concrete design methods, evaluation frameworks, and institutional support.

Key theoretical foundations:
- **Feminist technoscience** (Haraway): situated knowledges, companion species, symbiogenesis
- **Actor-Network Theory** (Latour): non-human agency, hybrid networks
- **Object-Oriented Ontology** (Harman, Morton): flat ontology, hyperobjects
- **Posthumanism** (Braidotti, Barad): entanglement, intra-action, response-ability

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Fast-evolving:** ✅ The field is rapidly institutionalizing. New academic programs launched 2022-2024. Major design conferences (CHI, DIS, RTD) now have dedicated more-than-human tracks. EU and UK funding increasingly supports more-than-human research.

**Fast-shaping:** ✅ Methodological frameworks crystallizing. "More-than-human participation" moving from critique to concrete design methods. Integration with AI/ML creating new hybrid approaches (algorithmic participation, machine agencies).

**Edge drivers:**
- **Climate crisis urgency** — design's complicity in environmental destruction demands new approaches
- **AI agency questions** — what does it mean to design with/for intelligent machines?
- **Indigenous design justice** — decolonizing design through non-Western ontologies
- **Materials research** — biodesign, living systems, smart materials as co-designers

**Why churning:** The field is still defining its boundaries. Overlap/tension with animal-computer interaction, environmental design, critical design, AI ethics. No consensus on methods or evaluation. This productive confusion creates space for innovation.
