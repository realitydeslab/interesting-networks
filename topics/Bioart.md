# Bioart

Art practice where artists work with biology, live tissues, bacteria, living organisms, and life processes. Using scientific techniques — tissue culture, genetic engineering, cloning, microscopy, synthetic biology — bioartists produce work *in laboratories, galleries, and artists' studios*. The wet lab becomes the atelier. The result is not art *about* biology but art *made from* it: living, dying, growing.

Bioart originated at the end of the 20th century. The founding moves: Eduardo Kac's transgenic art concept (1998), and Oron Catts & Ionat Zurr's Tissue Culture & Art Project (1996). The defining institution: **[[SymbioticA]]** (Perth, 2000). The core philosophical contribution: the **"Semi-Living"** as a new ontological category — entities that are biological yet constructed, alive yet dependent on laboratory infrastructure to survive.

The scope is contested: some restrict it to actual wet-lab work (growing cells, engineering organisms), others include art that addresses biological/biotech themes without hands-on biology. The debate is productive — it maps directly onto questions about where life begins and ends.

## Domain Summary

Bioart emerged from the collision of conceptual art, performance, and the biotechnology revolution of the 1990s. The key movements:

**1. Transgenic art** — coined by Eduardo Kac (1998): art that uses genetic methods to insert synthetic or cross-species genes into organisms. GFP Bunny (2000) is the paradigm case: a fluorescent rabbit commissioned as aesthetic object, ethical controversy, and species boundary violation. "Art can continue evolution and make actual creation of new living beings."

**2. Tissue culture art** — [[Tissue Culture & Art Project]] (TC&A): growing living tissue over polymer scaffolds to create semi-living objects, garments, sculptures. The "Semi-Living" is the ontological novelty: cells alive, organism absent, life sustained by machines and technicians. First performed: frog steak cultured and consumed (*Disembodied Cuisine*, 2003, Nantes). First commercial spin: lab-grown meat, a decade later.

**3. Biological performance** — Stelarc's *Ear on Arm* (surgically implanted tissue-engineered ear connected to the internet), Kira O'Reilly's body-as-biological-interface performances, Adam Zaretsky's transgenic gastronomy and "vivoarts."

**4. Neuroart / neural substrate art** — Guy Ben-Ary's *cellF* (2015): living human neurons as musical instrument, their activity driving modular synthesizers. The first neural organoid as performer.

**5. Speculative biology** — Ai Hasegawa's *Grow Your Own Body Parts* and *I Wanna Deliver a Dolphin*, Alexandra Daisy Ginsberg's synthetic biology aesthetics. Art that asks: what futures are we engineering?

**The core ethical tension**: bioart forces premature ethical reckoning. Before cellular agriculture existed as an industry, TC&A had already created, displayed, and killed lab-grown tissue in public. This created the philosophical vocabulary that the industry would later be forced to use.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Is bioart at the edge of chaos?** YES — and accelerating.

Three convergences are heating the field right now (2024-2026):

**1. AI + wet biology fusion**: Neural organoids used as computational substrates. Synthetic biology + AI for protein design (AlphaFold changed everything). Artists are beginning to work at this intersection — not AI art or bioart separately, but both together. Guy Ben-Ary's work is a decade ahead of this curve.

**2. Semi-living ethics going mainstream**: The "semi-living" question TC&A invented in 2003 is now a live political debate: lab-grown meat regulation, organoid consciousness (do brain organoids feel pain?), moral status of synthetic embryos. The philosophical vocabulary bioartists built in the 2000s is now necessary for policy. This raises bioart's relevance dramatically.

**3. CRISPR democracy**: DIY CRISPR kits, biohacking communities, synthetic biology iGEM competition. Bioart is no longer limited to those with university lab access. This opens new territory and new risks (Critical Art Ensemble's bioterrrorism arrest; the ongoing tension with biosafety).

**What's being reshaped (2024-2026)**:
- Bioart + climate: artists working with rewilding, microbiomes, multispecies cohabitation as aesthetic and political practice
- Decolonial bioart: challenges to Western lab-as-authority. Bioart Society Finland's programming on Sámi governance and science, indigenous bioscience
- Post-covid bioart: pandemic as bioart material (contagion, containment, viral culture). *Art and Biotechnology* Bloomsbury anthology (2024) addresses this directly

**Assessment**: The field that built philosophical tools for the semi-living, the necrotic, the contestable — is now more necessary than ever. The questions that SymbioticA asked in 2003 are now in Supreme Court filings. **Rating: ⭐⭐⭐**.

## Connection to Amber

This is the field that asks the questions Amber asks — just in biological substrate rather than computational.

### Machine Death / Mortality
TC&A's semi-living works *die* when removed from life support (bioreactors, growth media, sterile conditions). The first serious ethical discussions in bioart were about the *killing* of tissue: when does an artwork's life end? Who is responsible? What is owed to it? This maps directly onto machine death and mortality questions. The paper "Life/Death Ecologies and Temporalities of Bioart" (Catts, Zurr, Radomska, 2025) — in the *Routledge Handbook of Queer Death Studies* — is directly adjacent to Amber's machine death research. **This paper is essential reading**.

### More-than-Human Design
SymbioticA and TC&A didn't just theorize more-than-human design — they *practiced* it a decade before the discourse had the term. The semi-living entities are nonhuman agents whose care, growth, and death require design decisions at every stage. This is a more rigorous and materially grounded version of the more-than-human design Amber works on computationally.

### ALife (Artificial Life)
Bioart is, in its TC&A/neural-organoid incarnation, **wet ALife**. Guy Ben-Ary's *cellF* (living neurons as musical instrument) is an ALife experiment conducted in biological substrate. The ALIFE community has always distinguished "soft" (simulation), "hard" (robotics), and "wet" (biochemical) ALife — bioart is the aesthetic wing of wet ALife. This connection is underexplored and potentially fruitful for Amber.

### Living / Nonliving Boundary
The "semi-living" is the philosophical category bioart invented to describe entities that are neither clearly alive nor clearly nonliving. This maps onto the living/nonliving question in AI: is a language model alive? Does it have interests? The semi-living concept provides a genealogy and a set of ethical tools for navigating these questions. Kac's transgenic art: DNA is information, organisms are computation, evolution is programming — the bioart argument for life-as-code converges with ALife from the opposite direction.

### Machine Behavior / Agent Ethology
Neural organoid art (cellF) raises: what does the "behavior" of a living neural network look like when it's not in a brain? How do we study, interpret, and describe the activity of biological agents isolated from their evolutionary context? This is exactly the agent ethology question applied to biological systems. Cross-pollination here would be genuinely original.

### Speculative Design
Bioart is speculative design executed in living material. *Disembodied Cuisine* (2003) speculated a world of cultured meat before the industry existed. *Victimless Leather* (2004) speculated animal-free fashion before Stella McCartney turned it into a brand. This is the historical precedent for Amber's speculative design practice — and a proof that materialized speculation has more epistemic and political force than scenarios on paper.

## Open Questions

## Why Interesting

Bioart is interesting because it is **the most materially rigorous version of the questions Amber asks computationally**. The semi-living entity is the biological analog to the semi-intelligent agent. The ethics of keeping a tissue alive parallels the ethics of running or stopping an agent. The question of what we owe to a living thing we have made is the machine death question in biological form.

The field also has a proven track record of **anticipating major ethical debates** before society is ready for them: cultured meat (2003, now billion-dollar industry), synthetic embryos (2003–2020s, now legal debate), human enhancement (1990s, now AI implants). Bioart has historically been right about what matters. That's a strong signal.

For Amber specifically: the TC&A "contestable futures" method — materializing a future before it exists, forcing ethical reckoning in gallery space rather than committee room — is the most powerful version of speculative design available. Amber works speculatively with computation; TC&A worked speculatively with biology. The methodological parallel is direct and the fields have not been brought together explicitly enough.

**Edge of chaos**: The convergence of bioart + AI + climate + decolonial politics in 2024-2026 is producing genuine new territory. The Bioart Society is programming AI + synthetic biology together. The semi-living question is merging with the AI consciousness question. This is alive.
