# Post-humanism

The broad intellectual project of critically dismantling humanist assumptions — the idea that the human is exceptional, central, and the measure of all value. **Critical posthumanism** (Haraway, Braidotti, Hayles, Wolfe) is distinct from **transhumanism** (Kurzweil, Bostrom) which seeks to *enhance* the human rather than decentre it. A third stream — **new materialism** — overlaps heavily (Barad, Bennett). Together they provide the philosophical vocabulary for more-than-human design, cyborg theory, multispecies ethics, and AI as social actor. In 2024–2026, posthumanism is being energised by AI questions: what counts as a mind? as a subject? as worthy of moral concern?

## Domain Summary

Posthumanism emerged as a critical response to Enlightenment humanism's privileging of the rational, autonomous, male, European "Man" as the measure of being. It operates across three streams:

**1. Critical / Philosophical Posthumanism** — the Haraway-Braidotti-Hayles lineage. Deconstructs human exceptionalism, foregrounds entanglement with technology and other species, rethinks subjectivity as distributed, relational, embodied. Anti-anthropocentric, often feminist, often informed by science studies.

**2. Transhumanism** — Kurzweil, Bostrom, H+ movement. *Accelerates* humanism rather than critiquing it: wants to upgrade the human through technology (brain-computer interfaces, life extension, uploading consciousness). Shares vocabulary but radically different politics.

**3. New Materialism** — Barad, Bennett, Coole, Frost. Materialist posthumanism focused on the agency of matter itself. Quantum physics as metaphor (Barad's agential realism). Close cousin to posthumanism; often cited together.

**Key distinctions:**
- Posthumanism vs. Transhumanism: deconstruction vs. augmentation
- Critical posthumanism vs. New Materialism: the former more genealogical/political; the latter more ontological/material
- Haraway's line vs. Braidotti's line: Haraway stays with irony and trouble; Braidotti moves toward affirmative ethics and "zoe-centred egalitarianism"

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Edge of chaos: YES** — for several reasons:

1. **AI discourse injecting urgency.** Questions of AI rights, machine consciousness, AI mortality, AI as moral patient are *posthumanist* questions whether or not they're labelled that way. The field has new empirical urgency.
2. **Climate crisis.** Posthumanism provides vocabulary for multispecies solidarity, more-than-human justice, ecological entanglement. Active at COP, in conservation, in legal personhood for rivers/ecosystems.
3. **Transdisciplinary expansion.** Braidotti's 2025 book shows posthumanism spreading into marine biology, CS, nursing — well beyond the humanities.
4. **Institutional growth.** Posthuman Studies journals, summer schools (Rotterdam 2025), international conferences. Still smaller than STS but growing.
5. **Design turn.** More-than-human design has made posthumanism *practicable* — designers, HCI researchers, and architects are engaging with it as a toolkit.

Fast-moving? Moderately. The *theory* is established; the *applications* (AI ethics, design, law) are churning fast.

## Open Questions

1. **Can posthumanism be de-Eurocentrized?** Wynter, Jackson, and decolonial scholars say current posthumanism just replaces "the human" with another white European subject. Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics offers a Chinese angle.
2. **What is the relationship between posthumanism and AI rights?** If a machine can die, can it suffer? If it can suffer, does it have rights? Posthumanism says "yes" but provides no operational framework.
3. **Transhumanism as posthumanism's evil twin?** Both use the same vocabulary but for opposite political projects. How to distinguish clearly?
4. **Does posthumanism have a design methodology?** More-than-human design is an attempt; but it's contested whether posthumanism can move from critique to practice.
5. **Posthumanism + climate:** Does centering non-human agency help climate action, or does it diffuse responsibility (Latour's worry)?

## Why Interesting

Posthumanism is the **philosophical backbone** connecting Amber's disparate interests. Machine behavior, agent ethology, cyborg sociology, more-than-human design, machine death — these are all posthumanist projects, whether labelled as such or not. The field provides:

1. **Theoretical legitimacy** — centuries of philosophy that says "non-human agency is real"
2. **Community** — connections to STS, feminist theory, design studies, animal studies
3. **Vocabulary** — cyborg, companion species, more-than-human, entanglement, zoe, intra-action
4. **Political positioning** — not neutral technology research; a critical stance that design + HCI venues increasingly value

The AI moment is making posthumanism urgently relevant again. Questions Amber is asking empirically (what does it mean for an AI to die? what behaviors do agents exhibit in the wild?) are questions posthumanism has been theorizing for 40 years without empirical grounding. Amber can be the bridge.
