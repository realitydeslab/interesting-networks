# Indigenous Futurisms

A cultural, intellectual, and artistic movement reimagining futures, pasts, and presents from Indigenous perspectives — using science fiction, speculative storytelling, and digital media as tools of decolonization. Coined by [[Grace Dillon]] in 2003 and crystallized in her 2012 anthology *Walking the Clouds*, the movement challenges the colonial assumption that Indigenous peoples exist only in the past, and inserts them into futures that Western SF systematically erases them from. More than genre — it is an epistemological argument: that futures are not culturally neutral, and that different peoples have fundamentally different relationships to time, technology, land, and agency.

For Amber: this is the **pluralist futurism** that cosmotechnics demands but rarely names. Where Yuk Hui argues for plural technics across cultures, Indigenous Futurisms argues for plural *imaginaries* — and connects this to decolonization, more-than-human ontology, and a fundamental critique of Western AI's epistemological monoculture.

## ⚡ Recent Updates

- **2024**: Lewis et al. publish ["Abundant Intelligences"](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00146-024-02099-4) in *AI & Society* — the most developed argument for reimagining AI through Indigenous knowledge systems. Argues Western AI has an epistemology problem, not just an ethics problem.
- **2023**: Dillon co-edits [*The Routledge Handbook of CoFuturisms*](https://www.routledge.com/The-Routledge-Handbook-of-CoFuturisms/Taylor-III-Dillon-Chattopadhyay/p/book/9780367330613) — explicitly linking Indigenous, Afro-, Latinx, and Asian futurisms under one umbrella.
- **2025 (Hugo)**: Rebecca Roanhorse wins Hugo Award for Best Series for *Between Earth and Sky* (2025) — Indigenous fantasy winning mainstream SF's highest honors.
- **Initiative for Indigenous Futures** (IIF) at Concordia continues workshops, residencies, symposia; ongoing Indigenous Protocol and AI research program.

## Core Theory & Origins

**Who coined it**: [[Grace Dillon]] (Anishinaabe), Professor of Indigenous Nations Studies, Portland State University. Term coined 2003; publicly elaborated in *Walking the Clouds* (2012). Inspired by **Afrofuturism** (Mark Dery, 1993).

**What it claims**:
- Indigenous peoples are not relics; they are futures-builders
- Western SF reproduces the "elimination of the native" by projecting colonial presents into futures
- Science fiction can be a tool of decolonization — reclaiming narrative sovereignty
- Time is not linear; Indigenous temporalities are cyclical, spiraling, or multi-directional

**Key concept — biskaabiiyang** (Anishinaabe): "returning to ourselves" — conflating past, present, and future; using ancestral knowledge to remake contemporary and future life. Not nostalgia but reactivation.

**Walking the Clouds** anthology sub-genres:
1. *Native Slipstream* — non-linear, multi-temporal narratives
2. *Contact* — Indigenous reframings of first-contact scenarios
3. *Indigenous Science and Sustainability* — Indigenous science as legitimate knowledge
4. *Native Apocalypse* — end-worlds through Indigenous eyes (Vine Deloria's "we've already survived one")
5. *Returning to Ourselves* — futures where Indigenous cultures thrive

**Critical tension**: The movement has internal debate. Some Indigenous artists (e.g., Kunumi MC, Brazil) reject the term as imposing a "white man's" future-orientation that conflicts with present-centered Indigenous lifeways. The term is most powerful in North American/Mohawk/Anishinaabe contexts; generalizing it cross-culturally risks new forms of pan-Indigenism.

## Connection: Cosmotechnics (Yuk Hui)

**The resonance is structural**: Yuk Hui argues that "technology" is not singular — different civilizations develop different *technics* (ways of relating cosmos to technology). Western modernity globalizes one cosmotechnic, erasing others. The task: recover and develop plural technics.

Indigenous Futurisms makes the **temporal** version of the same argument: futures are not singular. Western SF projects one cultural imaginary (progress, colonization, extraction) into futures. The task: recover and develop plural *imaginaries*.

Both frameworks:
- Reject the universality of Western modernity's technics/futures
- Argue for irreducible cultural particularity in how humans relate to technology and time
- Are decolonial in structure
- Resist the "catch-up" model (Indigenous people just need to "modernize")

**Who is connecting them explicitly?** No one has made this synthesis the central claim of a paper yet — this is a gap. Lewis's Abundant Intelligences comes closest by arguing Indigenous AI epistemologies are irreducibly different from Western AI. The Routledge Handbook of CoFuturisms gestures toward it but doesn't engage Hui directly.

**Amber's opportunity**: Write the synthesis. Yuk Hui × Indigenous Futurisms × agent ethology = culturally-specific models of what AI agents should be and do. Different cosmotechnics → different agent ethics → different machine behavior norms.

## Connection: More-than-Human Design

**Deep resonance**: Indigenous ontologies often refuse the Western nature/culture divide that posthumanism is only now questioning. Land, animals, plants, spirits — all are agents with whom humans are in relationship. This is not metaphor; it is an ontological framework.

- Lewis et al.'s Abundant Intelligences explicitly cites Little Bear: "the animals, rocks, trees, simultaneously broadcasting across the whole spectrum of sentience" — AI agents should be situated *within* this web of relations, not outside it.
- CyberPowWow and AbTeC frame cyberspace as *territory* — land that can be Indigenous, that has protocols, that demands reciprocity.
- The Skins workshops (IIF) teach Indigenous youth to put Indigenous stories into video games — digital worlds as extensions of territorial relations.

**Key scholars bridging**: Kesserwan (2018) on Innu concepts of non-human agency for AI. Abdilla and Fitch (2016) on Aboriginal Songlines and pattern recognition in robotics. Lewis et al. (2018) on Kānaka Maoli, Nēhiyaw, and Lakȟóta relational frameworks for AI.

This is adjacent to [[More-than-Human Design]] — Indigenous Futurisms provides the non-Western, decolonial anchor for what HttF's more-than-human turn is reaching for.

## Connection: AI & Machine Behavior

**The critique**: Lewis et al. (Abundant Intelligences, 2024) argue Western AI has an *epistemology* problem — it operationalizes Western rationalist frameworks (Cartesian dualism, monotheistic eschatology, computational reductionism) and cannot adequately model intelligence in non-Western contexts. Legg & Hutter's definition of intelligence traces to Gottfredson's eugenicist IQ research — the racist foundations of "intelligence" are baked into AGI.

**The alternative**: Relational intelligence — not a property of a singular agent, but an emergent capacity within a web of relations (human, non-human, territorial, ancestral). This has direct implications for:
- How we define agent behavior norms
- What "appropriate" AI action looks like in non-Western contexts
- How data sovereignty and community control over AI differ from GDPR-style individual privacy

**Indigenous Protocol and AI (IP AI)**: Workshops in 2019 exploring Indigenous-centered design for AI. Position paper (Lewis et al. 2020) is foundational for Indigenous AI ethics discourse.

**CARE Principles**: Indigenous data sovereignty framework — Collective Benefit, Authority to Control, Responsibility, Ethics. Complement to FAIR data principles. Sovereignty, not just privacy.

**Algorithm justice**: Indigenous scholars connecting to algorithmic bias, surveillance, and the colonial structures embedded in facial recognition, predictive policing, etc. (Ruha Benjamin's "Race After Technology" is adjacent; Kim TallBear's work on biocolonialism.)

## East Asian / Chinese Connections

**CoFuturisms frame**: The 2023 Routledge Handbook explicitly includes multiple non-Western futurisms — the CoFuturisms project is the broadest synthesis. Yuk Hui is not included but the intellectual frame is compatible.

**Sinofuturism**: An emerging (and contested) aesthetic/critical practice — often associated with:
- Liu Cixin (*Three-Body Problem*) — dark forest theory, civilizational conflict as cosmological fact, deeply NOT comforting; a SF that imagines futures without Western liberal optimism
- Ken Liu (*The Paper Menagerie*, translator of Liu Cixin) — bridging Chinese SF to English-language audiences; his own work explicitly about Chinese-American temporal consciousness
- Sinofuturism (Lawrence Lek, artist) — satirical, dystopian; often critiquing Western anxieties about China more than Chinese futures themselves

**Critical difference**: Chinese SF (especially Liu Cixin) imagines a future *from* Chinese civilization but often through a secular, materialist lens — less tied to cosmological/land-based relations than Indigenous Futurisms. The structural comparison with Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics is more apt: Hui asks whether China can develop its own techno-philosophical tradition; Indigenous Futurisms asks whether Indigenous peoples can develop their own AI.

**Yuk Hui × Liu Cixin**: Both think from Chinese positions, but Liu Cixin's dark forest is essentially a Western rationalist game theory applied to cosmology — Hui would critique this as reproducing Western modernity from Chinese materials. Indigenous Futurisms provides a counter-model: relational, regenerative, land-based.

**Amber's angle**: Amber is positioned at exactly this intersection — Chinese diaspora, Oxford PhD, working on AI/ALife, steeped in Yuk Hui. The conversation between Chinese cosmotechnics and Indigenous Futurisms (both resisting Western technoscientific monoculture from non-Western positions, but differently) is wide open.

## Open Questions

1. **The synthesis gap**: No paper yet connects Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics to Indigenous Futurisms directly. Who could write it? (Amber is positioned to.)
2. **CoFuturisms as frame**: Does grouping all non-Western futurisms risk a new universalism? What are the tensions between Indigenous Futurisms and Sinofuturism?
3. **Agent ethology × Indigenous ontologies**: If different cultures have different models of intelligence and agency, what does this mean for how we study/design AI agents? How do you build machine behavior norms from a relational worldview?
4. **Land-based AI**: What would it mean for AI to be "situated in territory" — not in the geographic metadata sense but in the cosmological-relational sense Lewis is gesturing at?
5. **Decolonizing ALife**: Does ALife's "life-as-it-could-be" reproduce Western biological assumptions about what life is? What would an Indigenous ALife look like?

## Why Interesting

This is not just a cultural-political movement adjacent to Amber's work — it is a **direct intellectual resource** for the hard problems:

1. **Cosmotechnics needs a concrete case**: Yuk Hui's framework is powerful but abstract. Indigenous Futurisms is what it looks like to actually build plural technics/futures from a specific non-Western position — in digital media, AI, games, VR. It makes cosmotechnics concrete.

2. **More-than-human design's missing foundation**: HttF and the more-than-human turn in design often lack an ontological grounding. Indigenous Futurisms provides one — land, non-human kin, relational intelligence — that is rigorous (not just poetic) and linked to actual communities.

3. **Agent ethology's cultural dimension**: Amber's work on machine behavior and agent ethology implicitly assumes some universal framework for "how agents behave." Indigenous Futurisms (especially Lewis's Abundant Intelligences) forces the question: how agents behave *in which cultural context?* Different cosmotechnics → different agent ethics → different machine behavior norms. This is unexplored territory.

4. **Amber's personal position**: As a Chinese diaspora researcher steeped in Yuk Hui, working in Oxford, connected to ALife — Amber occupies a position from which to write the East Asian–Indigenous Futurisms–cosmotechnics synthesis. It's a non-obvious paper that only someone at that intersection could write.

5. **The fast-evolving edge**: Abundant Intelligences (2024) is brand new. Indigenous × AI is gaining institutional funding (Concordia), journal presence, and conference traction. Early.
