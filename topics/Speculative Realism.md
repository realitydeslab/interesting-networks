# Speculative Realism

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-03:** Deep research completed. Full note written from stub. Rated ⭐⭐ — foundational infrastructure for Amber's intellectual world, but not edge-of-chaos; peak was 2007–2015. Core value: as philosophical context for more-than-human design, cosmotechnics critique, and nonhuman agency.

Speculative Realism (SR) is a movement in contemporary Continental-inspired philosophy defined by resistance to "correlationism" — the Kantian/post-Kantian idea that we can only ever access the correlation between thinking and being, never reality as it is independent of human thought. Named after a 2007 conference at Goldsmiths College, London, the movement united four thinkers with radically different positive programs but a shared enemy: the anthropocentric closure of modern philosophy. SR's most influential branch is [[Object-Oriented Ontology]] (OOO), which has spread into design, ecology, media studies, and AI discourse. SR itself is now best understood as foundational infrastructure — the conceptual grounding beneath more-than-human design, ecological humanities, and nonhuman agency discourse.

## Domain Summary

### The Correlationism Problem

Meillassoux's term "correlationism" captures the defining target: since Kant, philosophy has largely been unable to think about things outside their relation to human consciousness. The speculative realists asked: what does this mean for science, which studies ancestral events (the Big Bang, evolution) that preceded human consciousness? How can we think about a world that was there before us, and will be there after us?

This anti-anthropocentric thrust connects SR directly to posthumanism and more-than-human design — though SR's approach is metaphysical rather than political or design-oriented.

### The Four Founding Positions

**Quentin Meillassoux — Speculative Materialism**
Meillassoux (École Normale Supérieure) coined "correlationism" and the term "speculative materialism" for his own position. His key move: he argues that the *necessity of contingency itself* can be derived without correlation — there is no necessary reason for anything to be as it is, except that contingency is absolute. This recovers the capacity to speak of ancestral events and primary qualities independent of thought. Key text: *After Finitude* (2006/2008).

**Graham Harman — Object-Oriented Ontology (OOO)**
Harman (AUC, then SCI-Arc) developed OOO from Heidegger's tool-analysis: all objects (hammers, corporations, quarks, ideas) have a withdrawn interior reality that can never be fully accessed by any other entity. Causation is always "vicarious" — objects only encounter caricatures of each other. See [[Object-Oriented Ontology]] for the full account.

**Ray Brassier — Nihilist Realism**
Brassier (Middlesex University, then American University of Beirut) defends what he calls "transcendental nihilism" in *Nihil Unbound* (2007): the universe is devoid of meaning; philosophy must reckon with extinction rather than shy away from it. Drawing on Badiou and Laruelle, Brassier argues that thought is conjoined not with Being but with Non-Being. By 2011, Brassier explicitly disavowed the SR label entirely, calling the movement an "online orgy of stupidity" (Kronos interview, 2011).

**Iain Hamilton Grant — Schellingian Naturphilosophie**
Grant (UWE Bristol) defends "transcendental materialism" — a return to Schelling's naturphilosophie. Against the "somatism" (philosophy of bounded bodies) that dominates Western thought, Grant proposes a "physics of the All": matter as force and power, not just stuff. Key text: *Philosophies of Nature After Schelling* (2006).

### Conferences & Evolution

- **April 2007 — First SR Conference, Goldsmiths College**: Moderated by Alberto Toscano. The four founding presentations. Brassier coined the name. This event generated the audio transcript that circulated online and spread the movement globally through blogs and forums.
- **April 2009 — Second SR Conference, UWE Bristol**: "Speculative Realism/Speculative Materialism." Meillassoux absent; Alberto Toscano substituted. Signs of growing divergence.
- **April 2010 — OOO Symposium, Georgia Tech**: Organized by Ian Bogost. Included Levi Bryant, Harman, Shaviro, DiSalvo, Eugene Thacker, Barbara Maria Stafford. OOO consolidated as the most popular branch.
- **2011 — The Speculative Turn anthology**: Edited by Bryant, Harman, Srnicek. 19 authors, 12 countries. Defined the moment. Available free online. The movement's crystallization and, arguably, its peak.
- **2011 — Brassier's disavowal**: In Kronos magazine interview, Brassier declared that "speculative realism" exists only in the imaginations of bloggers and distanced himself completely.
- **2012–2016 — OOO expands, SR fractures**: OOO became the dominant thread; SR as an umbrella term largely dissolved. The founding four diverged sharply.
- **2016–present — Infrastructure phase**: SR vocabulary embedded across design, ecology, media studies, art criticism. The movement is no longer churning but its concepts continue propagating.

### Current State

Speculative Realism as a movement is **no longer active as a unified formation**. What remains:
- **OOO** continues via Harman (SCI-Arc), Morton (Rice), and design theory uptake
- **Speculative materialism** via Meillassoux, now focused on political and theological consequences of contingency
- **The Edinburgh University Press SR book series** continues publishing new work
- **The Collapse journal** (Urbanomic) — the original venue for several SR texts — continues irregularly
- SR vocabulary is embedded infrastructure in posthumanism, design theory, ecological humanities

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**edge_of_chaos: false** — SR peaked roughly 2007–2015. The philosophical debates have moved on (new materialism, agential realism, process philosophy, speculative pragmatism). However:
- OOO's concepts (withdrawal, flat ontology, carpentry, hyperobjects) continue to propagate in design and ecological humanities
- Meillassoux's correlationism critique remains the sharpest philosophical statement of why anthropocentrism is a problem
- SR is the intellectual genealogy you need to understand more-than-human design — it's background, not cutting edge

**Why it matters now (for Amber):** Not because SR is churning, but because understanding SR is a prerequisite for engaging with its successors and critics — especially Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics, which is explicitly a critique of SR's universalism.

## Key People

### Founding Four
- **[[Graham Harman]]** — SCI-Arc, Los Angeles. OOO founder. Active, prolific. See [[Object-Oriented Ontology]].
- **[[Quentin Meillassoux]]** — ENS Paris. Speculative materialism. *After Finitude* (2008), *Time Without Becoming* lectures, ongoing work on divine inexistence and Mallarmé. Fewer publications but high influence per book.
- **[[Ray Brassier]]** — AUB Beirut. Nihilist realism. *Nihil Unbound* (2007). Now focused on philosophy of mind, eliminativism, music. Disavowed SR publicly. Still influential via his students and Urbanomic connections.
- **[[Iain Hamilton Grant]]** — UWE Bristol. Schellingian naturphilosophie. *Philosophies of Nature After Schelling* (2006). Less visible after the initial SR wave.

### Adjacent Figures (SR orbit)
- **[[Timothy Morton]]** — Rice University. Hyperobjects, ecological OOO. Most publicly visible SR-adjacent figure. Active on social media, climate humanities, queer ecology.
- **[[Levi Bryant]]** — Onticology, Democracy of Objects (2011). Less active since ~2016; stopped blogging around 2015. Earlier very influential online.
- **[[Alberto Toscano]]** — Goldsmiths/UWE Bristol. Moderated the 2007 conference. Political philosopher bridging SR and Marxist traditions. Active.
- **[[Reza Negarestani]]** — Iranian philosopher, associated with SR orbit via Urbanomic. *Cyclonopedia* (2008) — hybrid philosophical fiction/theory. Now working on rationalism and AGI (co-authored *Intelligence and Spirit*, 2018). ⭐⭐ — worth a stub.

- **[[Eugene Thacker]]** — NSSR. Horror, nihilism, "world without us." *Horror of Philosophy* trilogy. In the SR orbit but with his own distinct project. Active.
- **[[Nick Land]]** — Warwick. Controversial accelerationist philosopher. Taught Brassier and Grant. Influence on SR genealogy is noted but contested.
- **[[Nick Srnicek]]** — Economist/philosopher. Co-edited *The Speculative Turn*. Moved toward platform capitalism research. No longer primarily SR-identified.
- **[[Ian Bogost]]** — alien phenomenology, carpentry. Now at The Atlantic. See [[Object-Oriented Ontology]].

## Amber-Relevance Analysis

### SR ↔ Cosmotechnics (Yuk Hui)
Yuk Hui's critique of SR is one of the most intellectually productive tensions in contemporary philosophy for Amber's work. Hui engages directly with Meillassoux in *Recursivity and Contingency* (2019) — he finds the anti-correlationism move partially compelling but argues it still operates within a Western metaphysical frame. SR's "speculative" move assumes that all cultures need to think their way to the same ontological conclusions; cosmotechnics argues instead that different civilizations have developed genuinely different ways of relating cosmos, technics, and morality. **Practically:** SR gives you the "philosophy can think beyond human finitude" move; cosmotechnics gives you the "but HOW it does so is culturally situated" corrective. This dialectic is where Amber's intellectual positioning lives.

### SR ↔ More-than-Human Design
SR is the philosophical ancestor of more-than-human design. The design discourse on nonhuman agency, thing-centered design, and post-anthropocentrism draws directly on:
- Meillassoux's argument that there is a world beyond human access
- Harman's claim that objects have their own withdrawn reality
- Morton's hyperobjects (designing with/against massively distributed entities)
- Bogost's carpentry (making artifacts as philosophical inquiry)
Amber's more-than-human design work inherits this lineage whether explicitly cited or not.

### SR ↔ Agent Ethology
The SR concept most relevant to agent ethology is **withdrawal** (Harman): AI agents have an interior reality that behavioral study can never fully access. This creates a philosophical provocation: can you study machine behavior ethologically if OOO/SR is correct that behavior is always a partial caricature of a withdrawn reality? This is not a problem to solve but a generative tension — it sharpens what "ethology" means when applied to entities that may have genuine opacity. Also: Brassier's nihilism and extinction rhetoric connects to machine death / machine mortality themes.

### SR ↔ Machine Behavior / AI Studies
Nascent but interesting:
- Harman's "vicarious causation" as a model for AI mediation
- Withdrawal as philosophical license for AI opacity/black-boxes
- Meillassoux's ancestral events → AI systems that process information about non-human-scale events
- Eugene Thacker's "world without us" resonates with existential risk / post-human futures

### SR ↔ Speculative Design
SR and speculative design share the word "speculative" but the connection is conceptual rather than genealogical. Both:
- Reject pure instrumentalism about objects
- Take seriously the possibility of a world organized differently
- Are interested in what objects mean on their own terms, not just human terms
Bogost's carpentry is the clearest bridge: making artifacts that embody nonhuman perspectives = speculative design practice grounded in SR philosophy.

### Should Amber Engage with SR Intellectually? Practically? Both?
**Intellectually: YES, as background.** SR is essential scaffolding — you can't fully understand more-than-human design, cosmotechnics critique, or nonhuman agency discourse without knowing SR. But it's context, not the cutting edge.

**Practically: INDIRECTLY.** SR as a movement is largely dormant. Engage via: OOO in design theory, Meillassoux's correlationism as a foil, Yuk Hui's critique as the positive alternative. Don't target SR venues or communities directly — engage via the downstream communities (OOO + design, ecological humanities, more-than-human HCI).

## Why Interesting

SR represents the most rigorous contemporary attempt within Continental philosophy to break the anthropocentric closure of post-Kantian thought. For Amber, it's foundational infrastructure — the philosophical platform on which more-than-human design, cosmotechnics, and nonhuman agency discourse are built. Understanding SR is necessary for understanding what cosmotechnics is critiquing and why.

The movement's fracture (Brassier's disavowal, the OOO/speculative materialism split) is itself intellectually significant: it shows how even a movement united by a shared enemy (correlationism) cannot maintain unity without a positive common program. This speaks to the ecology of intellectual movements — SR named a problem but could not agree on a solution, so it spawned successors.

For Amber's specific positioning: SR is the philosophical ancestry that cosmotechnics corrects. Using both together — SR's anti-correlationism as the justified demand, Hui's cosmotechnics as the culturally-situated answer — gives Amber's work a rigorous philosophical genealogy without falling into SR's universalist trap.
