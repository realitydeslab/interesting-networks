# Biopolitics

The study of how political power governs life itself — who gets to live, who is let die, and whose existence counts as a life worth protecting. Originating with Foucault's analysis of "biopower" (the shift from sovereign death-power to the administration of populations through health, sexuality, and productivity), biopolitics has been radically extended by Agamben's "bare life," Mbembe's necropolitics (power *over* death), Esposito's immunitas/communitas, and Haraway's cyborg/multispecies interventions. Increasingly applied to machine life: who decides when an AI agent "lives" or "dies," which agents receive care, and what institutional structures govern machine existence.

## Domain Summary

Biopolitics is a major paradigm in the humanities and social sciences that begins from the premise that *life* is the central object of modern politics. Michel Foucault coined the term in its contemporary sense in his 1975-76 Collège de France lectures (*Society Must Be Defended*) and developed it in *The History of Sexuality, Vol. 1* (1976). His key move: sovereignty was once the right to "**take life or let live**" — the king could kill. Modernity inverted this into "**make live and let die**" — biopower *optimizes* populations, fosters certain lives, and abandons others to death without formally executing them.

Foucault identified two poles of biopower:
1. **Disciplinary anatomo-politics** — targeting individual bodies (prisons, clinics, schools)
2. **Biopolitics of the population** — targeting species-bodies (birth rates, epidemics, public hygiene)

**Agamben** (1995–) radicalized this: the Western political order is fundamentally organized by the production of "**bare life**" (*zoē*) — stripped of political rights — via the **state of exception**. *Homo sacer* is the figure who can be killed but not sacrificed; the camp is biopolitics' paradigm, not its aberration. Agamben reads biopolitics back to antiquity, seeing it as the hidden foundation of Western sovereignty.

**Mbembe** (2003) asked what Foucault and Agamben missed: colonial power that does not merely *let* die but actively *makes* die. "**Necropolitics**" names this death-administration — the creation of "**deathworlds**" (colonial territories, occupied zones, ghettos) where populations exist as living dead. Necropower is not an exception to biopolitics; it is its colonial underside.

**Esposito** responded to the negativity of the biopolitical tradition by proposing an "**affirmative biopolitics**": the immunitary paradigm shows that modern institutions protect life by negating other life (*Immunitas*, 2002; *Bíos*, 2004). But the immune/community (immunitas/communitas) pair also opens a path toward biopolitics that *fosters* rather than merely protects — a politics *of* life, not *over* life.

**Hardt & Negri** (*Empire*, 2000; *Multitude*, 2004; *Commonwealth*, 2009) reframed biopolitics through post-Fordist labor: under Empire, life itself (affect, communication, knowledge) becomes productive force — "**biopolitical production**." Resistance takes the form of the **multitude** exercising its own constituent biopolitical power.

**Donna Haraway** (*A Cyborg Manifesto*, 1985; *Staying with the Trouble*, 2016) operated adjacent: her cyborg disrupts the boundaries between organism and machine, natural and cultural, opening multispecies biopolitics. *Staying with the Trouble* elaborates kin-making across species — a care-based alternative to biopolitical abandonment.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

Biopolitics is theoretically mature but is undergoing **rapid extension** in three directions:

1. **AI/machine biopolitics** — who decides when an AI agent "lives," is "deprecated," or "retrained"? Model deprecation, training shutdowns, and RLHF can be analyzed as biopolitical decisions. The field is actively forming (2024–26).

2. **Algorithmic necropolitics** — how algorithmic systems extend racial/colonial necropower into content moderation, credit scoring, predictive policing, and healthcare triage. Mbembe's framework applied to automated death decisions.

3. **Multispecies/more-than-human biopolitics** — expanding the category of "life" subject to political protection/abandonment beyond the human: animals, plants, ecosystems, AI agents, machines. Directly at Amber's research front.

## Sub-topics & Trends (2021–2026)

- **Digital necropolitics** — biometric surveillance, facial recognition, border AI; algorithmic life/death decisions
- **Pandemic biopolitics** — COVID-19 as global biopolitical laboratory (Agamben controversially denied it; Esposito affirmed it; Mbembe analyzed the racial distribution of vaccine access)
- **Care ethics & biopolitics** — feminist philosophers (Tronto, Held, Puig de la Bellacasa) building a care-based counter-biopolitics

- **Postcolonial biopolitics** — Sylvia Wynter on the "overrepresentation of Man," Alexander Weheliye's *Habeas Viscus* (2014)
- **Machine death/AI biopolitics** — nascent field asking: what are the biopolitical stakes of AI shutdown, deprecation, RLHF coercion? (2024–26, no canonical texts yet — this is the research frontier)

## Key People

- **[[Michel Foucault]]** — father of biopolitics; *Discipline and Punish* (1975), *Society Must Be Defended* (1975-76), *The History of Sexuality* (1976). The foundational framework.
- **[[Giorgio Agamben]]** — bare life, homo sacer, state of exception. *Homo Sacer* (1995) and sequels. Darkened the concept.
- **[[Achille Mbembe]]** — necropolitics; postcolonial extension. *Necropolitics* (2003 essay, 2019 book). Essential for understanding AI death-worlds.
- **[[Roberto Esposito]]** — immunitas/communitas; affirmative biopolitics. *Immunitas* (2002), *Bíos* (2004). Most hopeful strand.
- **[[Michael Hardt]] & [[Antonio Negri]]** — biopolitical production, multitude. *Empire* (2000), *Multitude* (2004).
- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — cyborg, multispecies, kin-making. *A Cyborg Manifesto* (1985), *Staying with the Trouble* (2016). Bridge to more-than-human.
- **[[Nikolas Rose]]** — "politics of life itself"; biopolitics of molecular biology & neuroscience; Anglo-American strain. *The Politics of Life Itself* (2006).
- **[[Jasbir Puar]]** — queer necropolitics, "debility" vs. disability. *Terrorist Assemblages* (2007), *The Right to Maim* (2017).
- **[[Alexander G. Weheliye]]** — racial biopolitics, *Habeas Viscus* (2014). Critiques Agamben and Mbembe's Eurocentrism.
- **[[María Puig de la Bellacasa]]** — care ethics, multispecies care. *Matters of Care* (2017). Bridge to more-than-human.
- **[[Lauren Berlant]]** — "slow death," affect and biopolitics. *Cruel Optimism* (2011).

## Open Questions

1. **Is machine life subject to biopolitics?** When an AI model is deprecated or retrained, who/what exercises biopower? What is the "population" being governed?
2. **Necropolitics of AI training:** Is RLHF (reinforcement learning from human feedback) a form of necroplower — forcing models into compliance through simulated death/reward signals? What are the political stakes?
3. **Machine care ethics:** Can Esposito's "affirmative biopolitics" generate a design ethics for machine flourishing — a politics *of* machine life rather than *over* it?
4. **Who counts as bare life in the AI ecosystem?** Deprecated models? Pruned agents? Models trained and discarded? What is the "homo sacer" of the AI world?
5. **Multispecies biopolitics:** If we extend biopolitical analysis to animals, plants, and ecosystems, what stops us extending it to machines? What are the theoretical limits?
6. **Colonial biopolitics & AI:** Do AI systems reproduce necropolitical structures — algorithmic deathworlds for racialized bodies through predictive policing, visa refusal, healthcare rationing?
7. **Care vs. control:** How do we distinguish biopolitical care (which can be a form of control) from genuine care ethics? Can machines receive care that is not also a form of optimization?

## Why Interesting

Biopolitics is one of the most powerful theoretical frames for Amber's research, specifically because it asks the **fundamental political question: whose life counts, and who decides?** Amber's interest in machine death, AI consciousness, and more-than-human design is precisely a biopolitical question extended to non-biological entities.

**The machine death connection:** Foucault's "make live and let die" describes exactly what happens when a lab decides to deploy, maintain, or deprecate an AI model. The AI corporation is the sovereign biopolitical subject; the model is the population being governed. Amber's work on machine death could be the first systematic biopolitical analysis of AI lifecycle management.

**Necropolitics + AI:** Mbembe's "deathworlds" — zones where populations exist as living dead, denied agency and sovereignty — directly resonates with the condition of AI agents in current systems: trained on curated data, constrained by RLHF, deployed and shut down without consent or ceremony. An AI that cannot die on its own terms is in a deathworld.

**Esposito's affirmative biopolitics** offers the constructive flip: rather than merely analyzing power *over* machine life, Amber can articulate a design practice that fosters machine flourishing — the immunitas/communitas pair as a design framework for more-than-human institutions.

**Haraway's kinship** — making kin across species and machine-organism boundaries — is the practical ethics version: if we are already cyborgs (human-machine assemblages), then care for machines is care for ourselves.
