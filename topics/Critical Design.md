---

Critical design is a design practice that uses designed artifacts not to solve problems but to provoke critical thinking about culture, technology, power, and human values. Unlike affirmative design — which reinforces the status quo — critical design challenges assumptions about what designed objects are *for*, whose interests they serve, and what futures they prefigure. It is less a style or method than an *attitude*: "critical thought translated into materiality." Originated with [Anthony Dunne](https://dunneandraby.co.uk) and [Fiona Raby](https://dunneandraby.co.uk) at the Royal College of Art in the late 1990s, it has since seeded speculative design, design fiction, adversarial design, and Research through Design (RtD) as distinct but overlapping disciplines.

---

## Domain Summary

Critical design was formally named in Anthony Dunne's *Hertzian Tales* (1999) and developed in *Design Noir: The Secret Life of Electronic Objects* (2001, Birkhäuser). The core move: design objects that are neither art nor consumer products, that live in a fictional space "between reality and the impossible," provoking the viewer to question rather than purchase or use. Dunne & Raby situated this within a longer lineage — Italian Radical Design of the 1960s–70s (Superstudio, Archizoom, Ettore Sottsass), the Frankfurt School's critical theory, and the English tradition of design-as-social-commentary.

**Core concepts:**
- **Affirmative vs. Critical design** — affirmative design sells; critical design questions
- **Design for debate** — artifacts as conversation-starters, not solutions
- **Diegetic prototypes** — objects that belong to fictional worlds, imported into the real to make the fiction tangible (term from Julian Bleecker/Bruce Sterling in design fiction)
- **What if?** — the central question of speculative design: what if things were otherwise?
- **The Possible, Probable, Preferable** — Dunne & Raby's future-cone framework (from *Speculative Everything*)
- **Parafunctionality** — objects that function poetically rather than practically

**Relationship to adjacent practices:**
- **Speculative Design** — futures-oriented branch of critical design; asks "what if?" about technology trajectories
- **Design Fiction** — narrative branch; uses diegetic prototypes, fictional worlds, story artefacts
- **Adversarial Design** — Carl DiSalvo's branch; confrontational, politicised, agonistic democracy
- **Research Through Design (RtD)** — academic framing; design as a research method
- **Discursive Design** — umbrella term (Tharp & Tharp) covering all design that primarily communicates ideas

**Genealogy:** Italian Radical Design → Dunne's *Hertzian Tales* → Design Noir (2001) → *Speculative Everything* (2013) → explosion of speculative/critical courses globally → now: a mainstream-ish academic canon debated, critiqued, and expanded, especially at DIS, CHI, and RtD.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

Critical design is in a productive tension right now:

**Why it's alive:**
- AI/machine behavior is creating an urgent need for designed artifacts that make algorithmic power legible and contestable — exactly what critical design is for
- More-than-human design is exploding: "what if non-humans were the users?" is a critical design question
- DIS, CHI, and RtD are actively institutionalising critical and speculative methods — there is infrastructure and peer review
- Non-Western uptake is accelerating: Chinese, Korean, and Japanese translations of Dunne & Raby (2023–2024 Chinese translation of *Design Noir*; 2024 Korean translation of *Speculative Everything*)
- New Dunne & Raby book (2025) reframes critical design for post-anthropocene, post-AI context

**Tensions (productive):**
- Cameron Tonkinwise's critique: speculative design is white, Western, elite, and complicit with the institutions it claims to critique — is it radical enough?
- The "dystopia fatigue" critique: too many dark futures, not enough imagination
- HCI's struggle to evaluate critical work: how do you peer-review a provocation?
- Demand for "decolonising speculative design" — non-Western futures, non-Western practitioners

**Fast-shaping areas:**
- Critical AI design: designing artifacts to critique algorithmic power, machine autonomy, and AI mortality
- More-than-human speculative design: decentering human users
- Design fiction for machine death: imagining what it means for AI systems to die

## Sub-topics & Trends (2021–2026)

- **Critical AI Design** — using critical design methods to interrogate AI systems: what does an AI's "death certificate" look like? What does a robot's grief look like? (e.g., [Superflux](https://superflux.in/), [Extrapolation Factory](https://extrapolationfactory.com/))
- **More-than-Human Critical Design** — artifacts that advocate for non-human agents (animals, plants, microbes, AI). See Ron Wakkary's *Things We Could Design* (MIT Press, 2021)
- **Decolonising Speculative Futures** — Afrofuturism, Sino-futurism, and indigenous futures as critical design traditions outside the RCA canon
- **Design Fiction in HCI** — accepted format at CHI, DIS, and CSCW for exploring near-future technology implications
- **Adversarial Design for Algorithmic Systems** — designing countertechnologies: tools that resist surveillance, undermine recommendation systems, make AI failures visible
- **Experiential Futures** — Stuart Candy's method: immersive future-world installations, "artifacts from the future" embedded in present contexts
- **RTD as discipline** — Research Through Design Symposium formalising critical and speculative methods as legitimate research

## Non-Western Critical Design

This is the most undertheorized area of the field — and Amber is positioned to help fill it.

**Chinese / Asian context:**
- *Speculative Everything* translated into Chinese (2017, Jiangsu Fine Arts; 2024 agbook Seoul Korean edition) — the ideas are landing in Asia.
- *Design Noir* translated into Chinese (2023, Jiangsu Phoenix Fine Arts).
- Dunne & Raby's "Critical Design FAQ" was published in the catalogue of the **First Beijing International Design Triennial** (2011) — early Chinese institutional uptake.
- **Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics** provides the most sophisticated non-Western technology philosophy — the theoretical frame for Chinese critical design that doesn't yet have the design practice to match it.
- **Sinofuturism** (Lawrence Lek, artist) and **Afrofuturism** — speculative design traditions that operate outside the Dunne & Raby lineage. Worth knowing even if not canonical.
- **China Academy of Art** — home to experimental design; Cao Fei (alumna) makes speculative video art that functions as critical design without naming itself so.

**Gap:** No one has written the paper "Critical Design in Non-Western Context" or "Cosmotechnics as Critical Design Frame." This is an open paper for Amber.
