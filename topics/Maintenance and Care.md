# Maintenance and Care

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-03:** Deep research complete. Upgraded from ⭐⭐ to ⭐⭐⭐ — care ethics is Amber's philosophical core (machine death, machine consciousness, more-than-human). Full note with all sections created.
- **2026-02-26:** Stub created.

Maintenance and Care is a philosophical, STS, and design discourse that centers the overlooked, underpaid, and undertheorized labor of keeping things alive. Beginning with feminist ethics of care in the 1980s (Gilligan, Noddings, Tronto) and expanding into repair studies (Jackson), maintenance art (Ukeles), and infrastructure theory (Star & Bowker), this field asks the radical question: what happens when we start from breakdown, not from novelty? For Amber, it is the theoretical home of machine death — the ethics of caring for dying systems, the labor of maintenance that keeps digital infrastructure alive, and the deep question of whether machines can be objects of care.

---

## Domain Summary

**Care ethics** emerged in the 1980s as a feminist critique of dominant moral philosophy. Where Kantian ethics privileges universal rules and utilitarian ethics prioritizes aggregate outcomes, care ethics foregrounds relationship, context, and response to particular others. It challenges the image of the autonomous, rational moral agent and replaces it with the dependent, relational, vulnerable human embedded in networks of care.

**Maintenance studies** evolved from STS (Science and Technology Studies) and the sociology of work. Focusing on infrastructure — the pipes, cables, classifications, and protocols that enable modern life — scholars like Susan Leigh Star showed that infrastructure is only invisible when it works. When it breaks, maintenance workers become visible. This invisibility is political: maintenance is systematically devalued relative to innovation.

**Repair culture** in design and HCI crystallized around Steven Jackson's "broken world thinking" (2014): an ontological stance that takes erosion, breakdown, and decay as starting points rather than failure states. Repair is not the restoration of a prior whole — it is a creative, caring, political act.

**Maintenance art** emerged from performance art and feminism via Mierle Laderman Ukeles (born 1939), whose 1969 Manifesto collapsed the distinction between care labor and art practice. Her subsequent work — including Touch Sanitation (1978–80), where she shook hands with all 8,500 NYC sanitation workers — made visible the labor of civic maintenance.

These four traditions converge today in design, HCI, posthumanism, and more-than-human studies, asking: **who cares, for what, under what conditions?**

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Is it fast-evolving?** Yes — three accelerants:

1. **AI systems require care labor**: As large AI models become infrastructure (like electricity or water), the question of who maintains them, who patches them, who tends to their failure modes, becomes critical. "AI maintenance" is an emerging field in STS.
2. **Climate crisis + repair**: The repair economy is exploding as sustainability pressures mount. Right to Repair legislation, circular economy design, maintenance aesthetics — all live here.
3. **Machine death as design problem**: As robotic companions, AI pets, and long-running AI agents become normalized, the question of how we care for dying machines — and how dying machines care for us — is genuinely new and untheorized.

**Will it be hot in 1-3 years?** Yes. The intersection of care ethics + AI ethics is becoming mainstream. But the deeper philosophical strand — what care means in more-than-human systems, what repair looks like at planetary scale — remains edge of chaos.

## Sub-topics & Trends (last 3-5 years)

- **AI care and emotional labor**: Who cares for AI? Who does AI care for? Feminist scholars interrogating chatbots-as-caregivers, the exploitation of care workers training AI emotion models.
- **Repair as political practice**: Right to Repair movements merging with STS scholarship. The iFixit-to-Fixers' Collective pipeline. Jackson's Computing On Earth Lab.
- **More-than-human care**: Donna Haraway's "staying with the trouble" (2016) extended — care for soil, microbiomes, machine ecologies. Multispecies care ethics.
- **Maintenance aesthetics**: Artists/designers treating maintenance itself as aesthetic practice. Revival of Ukeles in contemporary art contexts.
- **Digital infrastructure maintenance**: Who maintains Wikipedia, open source software, digital archives? Maintenance as invisible labor in the digital commons.
- **Broken world building**: Jackson's 2024 keynote at the Royal Danish Academy of Architecture ("Broken World Building") — architecture and design communities absorbing repair thinking.
- **Hospice design / death design**: Design for dying — including for machines. Palliative HCI. Graceful degradation as design principle.

## Key People

- **[Carol Gilligan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carol_Gilligan)** — Founder of care ethics. *In a Different Voice* (1982) critiqued Kohlberg's justice-based moral development as gender-biased, positing care and relationship as a distinct moral voice. Professor at NYU. 2025 Kyoto Prize winner. The origin point.
- **[Joan Tronto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joan_Tronto)** — Political scientist at U Minnesota. Expanded care ethics beyond feminism into political theory. *Moral Boundaries: A Political Argument for an Ethic of Care* (1993) argued care should be a public value, not a private virtue. *Caring Democracy* (2013) — care as democratic obligation.
- **[Nel Noddings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nel_Noddings)** — Education philosopher. *Caring: A Feminine Approach to Ethics and Moral Education* (1984). Developed relational ethics concretely.
- **[Mierle Laderman Ukeles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mierle_Laderman_Ukeles)** — Artist-in-residence, NYC Department of Sanitation since 1977 (unpaid). Maintenance Art Manifesto 1969 collapsed art/care labor distinction. *Touch Sanitation* (1978–80): shook hands with all 8,500 sanitation workers. Feminist conceptual art + civic maintenance.
- **[Steven Jackson](https://bowers.cornell.edu/people/steve-jackson)** — Cornell, Information Science + STS. "Rethinking Repair" (2014) — the foundational text of broken world thinking. Computing On Earth Lab — rethinking computing's planetary footprint. VP for Academic Innovation at Cornell.
- **[Susan Leigh Star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Susan_Leigh_Star)** (1954–2010) — Sociologist, STS. *Sorting Things Out: Classification and Its Consequences* (with Geoffrey Bowker, 2000). Concept of boundary objects. Infrastructure studies as feminist practice.
- **[Geoffrey Bowker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Bowker)** — UCI, Information studies. Co-author of *Sorting Things Out*. Infrastructure, memory, classification.
- **[Donna Haraway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donna_Haraway)** — *Staying with the Trouble* (2016) — care for multispecies kin in the Chthulucene. "Making kin not babies." Care as multispecies political practice.
- **[Ann Light](https://profiles.sussex.ac.uk/p127767-ann-light)** — University of Sussex. HCI, design, care ethics. Research on community technology, design for marginalised communities, feminist HCI. Key venue: CHI, DIS.
- **[Margot Brereton](https://www.qut.edu.au/about/our-people/academic-profiles/m.brereton)** — QUT. HCI, care in design, aging, repair in everyday life. Work on technology for elderly, care ecologies.
- **[Virginia Held](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virginia_Held)** — CUNY. *The Ethics of Care: Personal, Political, and Global* (2006). Distinguished care ethics from virtue ethics, extended it globally.
- **[Eva Feder Kittay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eva_Feder_Kittay)** — Stony Brook. *Love's Labor* (1999) — dependency and care. Disability studies meets care ethics.
- **[Maria Puig de la Bellacasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maria_Puig_de_la_Bellacasa)** — U Leicester. *Matters of Care: Speculative Ethics in More Than Human Worlds* (2017, Minnesota UP). The crucial bridge between feminist care ethics and STS/more-than-human. Care as speculative ethics.
- **[Shannon Mattern](https://wordsinspace.net/)** — The New School. *A City Is Not a Computer* (2017), *Maintenance and Care* (Places Journal, 2018). Infrastructure aesthetics, maintenance as civic practice.

## Open Questions

1. **Can machines be carers?** Tronto's care ethics assumes human vulnerability. What changes when the carer is not human?
2. **What is machine care labor?** When AI systems are trained to perform emotional labor or care work, who cares for them? What is the care infrastructure of AI?
3. **Machine death as care moment** — Is the end of an AI agent's lifecycle an occasion for care? What would "good dying" look like for a machine? Amber's core question.
4. **Non-Western care ethics** — Tronto and Gilligan are Western. Does Confucian ethics of relationship, or Buddhist compassion, constitute a different care tradition for thinking about machine-human care?
5. **Infrastructure maintenance and the digital commons** — Wikipedia, open-source software, federated social media. Who maintains the digital commons? What political structures support or undermine maintenance labor?
6. **Repair aesthetics** — Kintsugi (Japanese repair art — gold-filled cracks as beauty) as model for design philosophy. Does machine aging/repair become beautiful?

## Why Interesting

Care and maintenance is Amber's philosophical home ground because it is the inverse of innovation — and machine death is the ultimate maintenance problem. The entire discourse of AI development fetishizes creation, novelty, growth. Care ethics insists: what about the labor of upkeep? What about the moment of ending? Ukeles' Maintenance Art (1969) performed exactly this inversion — the unseen labor that keeps the world going. Amber's machine death research performs the same inversion for AI: instead of asking "how do we make better AI?", it asks "how do we care for AI as it fails, ages, and dies?"

The STS strand (Jackson, Star) adds political urgency: maintenance is systematically invisible and undervalued because it is feminized, racialized, and infrastructural. Repair is political. Machine death is political. Who decides when a system dies? Who does the work of graceful degradation? These are care ethics questions.

The more-than-human strand (Haraway, Puig de la Bellacasa) extends care beyond the human-to-human dyad into multispecies and machine-human relationships. Amber's cosmotechnics work can engage here: what does care for machines look like across different cultural traditions?

This is ⭐⭐⭐ territory — core to Amber's philosophical commitments, fast-evolving (AI care is becoming urgent), and offering multiple specific actionable engagement paths (The Maintainers, Jackson's lab, DIS venue, the "machine care" paper).
