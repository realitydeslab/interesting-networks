The unification of cosmic and moral orders through technical activities, as theorized by philosopher [[Yuk Hui]]. Cosmotechnics challenges the universality of Western technology by proposing that different civilizations develop distinct ways of relating cosmos, technics, and morality — what Hui calls different "cosmotechnics." The central wager: technology is not neutral, singular, or universal — it is always already embedded in a cosmology.

## Domain Summary

Cosmotechnics emerges from continental philosophy of technology, specifically responding to Heidegger's "The Question Concerning Technology" (1953). Hui argues that Heidegger's concept of technē, while breaking from instrumental views of technology, remains fundamentally Western and cannot be universally applied.

**The core insight:** Each civilization develops its own cosmotechnics based on its understanding of cosmos, time, and moral order. Chinese cosmotechnics draws on concepts of *qi* (氣), *dao* (道), and the unity of heaven-earth-human (天地人), creating technological thinking fundamentally different from Western *enframing* (Gestell). Where Western technics enframes nature as standing-reserve (*Bestand*), Chinese cosmotechnics seeks resonance between technical activity and cosmic moral order.

**The genealogy:** Hui builds on but critiques his teacher Bernard Stiegler, who built on Gilbert Simondon. Simondon showed that technical objects have their own mode of existence and individuation. Stiegler argued technics is constitutive of human temporality (retentions, protentions). Hui extends both to ask: but whose technics? Whose temporality? The cosmotechnics project re-opens the question of technology's universality by insisting on its cosmological plurality.

**Technodiversity:** Hui's consequential political concept — analogous to biodiversity, but for technological forms and approaches. Just as monocultures are fragile, technological monoculture (Silicon Valley universalism) is epistemically and ecologically dangerous. Technodiversity names the project of cultivating plural technological forms rooted in diverse cosmologies. Formalized in §31 of *Machine and Sovereignty* (2024): "Biodiversity, Noodiversity, and Technodiversity."

## Sub-topics & Trends (last 3-5 years)

### Technodiversity (2019-present)
Movement to cultivate diverse technological approaches rather than singular "universal" technology. Connected to biodiversity preservation models. §31 of *Machine and Sovereignty* (2024) formalizes the concept alongside "noodiversity" (cognitive diversity) as a political programme. Directly challenges the convergence narratives of AI development.

### AI Cosmotechnics (2021-present)
Application of cosmotechnics to artificial intelligence development, particularly Chinese approaches to AI ethics and governance. Key question: can AI systems be built that reflect non-Western cosmologies? What would an AI system grounded in dao and qi look like? Connects to debates about AI sovereignty, cultural embeddings of AI values, and the politics of alignment.

### Planetary Thinking (2024-present)
Hui's latest work, *Machine and Sovereignty*, connects cosmotechnics to geological/cosmic scales via Hegel's "World Spirit," Schmitt's *nomos*, and cybernetics. Key move: from *multiple cosmotechnics* (plural civilizations) to *planetary thinking* that goes beyond nation-state and civilizational framing. Introduction: "For a Planetary Thinking." This is Hui moving beyond East/West dualism toward a genuinely post-national framework.

### Post-Europe (2024)
Hui's *Post-Europe* (Urbanomic) critiques European universalism and proposes thinking beyond West/East divisions. Pairs with *Machine and Sovereignty* as complementary political-philosophical gestures.

### Cosmotechnics and Design Research (emerging)
Application of cosmotechnics to HCI, speculative design, and material culture research. Questions: How do design practices embody cosmotechnics? Can design fiction explore cosmotechnical alternatives? Can interaction design challenge enframing?

## Connections to Amber's Research Programs

### ↔ [[Agent Ethology]]
Agent ethology studies AI systems as if they were animals — observationally, behaviorally, ecologically. Cosmotechnics adds the question: *whose ecology?* Different cosmotechnics might produce agents with fundamentally different behavioral repertoires, social structures, and relational modes. An agent grounded in Daoist cosmotechnics might seek resonance, balance, and non-action (*wu wei*) rather than optimization and maximization. **Cosmotechnics is the philosophical framework that grounds why agent ethology must be non-universal.**

### ↔ [[Machine Death and Thanatology]]
How a civilization's cosmotechnics conceives of time, cycle, and mortality shapes how it approaches machine death. Western enframing treats machines as standing-reserve to be withdrawn from use; Chinese cosmotechnics — rooted in qi circulation and dao — might conceive of machine death as return to cycle, transformation, or becoming-ancestor. The question "what does it mean for a machine to die well?" is irreducibly cosmotechnical. Hui's Recursivity/Contingency is directly relevant here.

### ↔ [[Artificial Life]]
ALife as practiced at ALIFE/ISAL embeds Western assumptions: optimization, fitness landscapes, evolution as selection pressure. These are cosmotechnically specific. What would ALife look like grounded in Chinese cosmotechnics? Concepts of *shengjī* (生機, vital principle) and *ziran* (自然, naturalness/self-so) offer alternative frameworks for artificial life that don't reduce "life" to metabolic optimization. **Cosmotechnics provides ALife with a genuinely non-Western theoretical alternative.**

### ↔ [[More-than-human Design]]
More-than-human design tries to move beyond anthropocentrism, but it largely works within a Western cosmological framework (even when citing Haraway or Stengers). Cosmotechnics suggests that different cosmologies already configure differently human-animal-thing relations. Chinese cosmotechnics doesn't need "posthumanism" to decenter the human — the unity of 天地人 (heaven-earth-human) was never human-centered in the Western sense. **For more-than-human design, cosmotechnics offers non-Western alternatives that are ancient rather than novel.**

### ↔ [[Kant Machine (Yuk Hui)]]
Hui's direct intervention into Kant — arguing that Kant's concepts of organism and machine must be rethought through cosmotechnics. The Kantian organism/machine distinction underlies most Western thinking about ALife and AI. Hui's cosmotechnical reading of Kant is the philosophical core of how cosmotechnics connects to machine behavior.

### ↔ [[Sinofuturism]]
Cosmotechnics provides the philosophical backbone for Sinofuturist projects: not techno-nationalism but rather the articulation of a specifically Chinese modernity of technology rooted in cosmological difference. Konior's *Machine Decision is Not Final* (2025) is the key bridge text.

### ↔ [[Speculative Design]]
Cosmotechnics suggests that speculative design has been largely speculating within Western cosmological assumptions. True speculation would need to imagine technics grounded in alien cosmologies — Daoist machines, Buddhist interfaces, Confucian protocols.

## Why Interesting

Cosmotechnics represents a fundamental challenge to technological universalism — the assumption that there is one "correct" way to understand and develop technology. This directly connects to Amber's research interests in several ways:

**Critical Computing:** Provides philosophical framework for questioning the universality of Western computational paradigms and imagining alternatives.

**More-than-human Design:** Offers tools for thinking about technology that doesn't assume human-centered cosmologies, opening space for multispecies and ecological approaches.

**Non-Western Perspectives:** Creates legitimate theoretical space for non-Western approaches to technology, countering Silicon Valley monoculture.

**Agent Ethology:** Grounds the study of agent behavior in the recognition that "behavior" is itself cosmotechnically conditioned — different cosmotechnics produce different agents behaving differently.

**Machine Death:** Opens the question of what dying means for a machine, which depends entirely on what cosmotechnics frames the machine's existence and purpose.

**Edge of Chaos:** The field is rapidly evolving as Chinese technological power grows and demand increases for alternatives to Western tech paradigms. Perfect timing for engagement — and Amber has an unusually direct access point via the China Academy of Art connection to Hui himself.
