# Algorithmic Governance

The study and critique of how algorithms produce social ordering — shaping behavior, distributing resources, managing populations, and encoding power — across both public and private sectors. Distinct from "AI regulation" (governance *of* algorithms) and "platform governance" (narrower, platform-specific), algorithmic governance focuses on governance *by* algorithms: how computational systems replace or augment human judgment in consequential decisions. Rooted in STS, critical sociology, legal theory, and media studies, the field is now in rapid evolution as agentic AI systems raise governance challenges that existing frameworks were not built for.

## Domain Summary

Algorithmic governance covers the multiplicity of ways computational algorithms become instruments of social coordination and control. The concept was theorized from multiple angles:

- **Governmentality angle (Foucault-influenced):** [[Antoinette Rouvroy]] and Thomas Berns coined "algorithmic governmentality" (2009/2013) — arguing that algorithms bypass the classical subject of governance (the individual who can be addressed, reasoned with, persuaded) in favor of governing through statistical correlations in behavioral data. Power becomes pre-emptive rather than reactive, operating on populations before deviance occurs.

- **Infrastructure/opacity angle:** [[Frank Pasquale]] argued in *The Black Box Society* (2015) that opaque algorithmic systems concentrate power in finance and online platforms, operating without meaningful accountability. His "black box" concept — systems that take inputs and produce outputs with no legible interior — became a touchstone.

- **Platform governance angle:** [[Tarleton Gillespie]] ("The Relevance of Algorithms", 2014; *Custodians of the Internet*, 2018) showed how platform algorithms don't merely organize content but actively constitute what counts as legitimate speech, who is visible, what is public. Platforms as norm-setters, not neutral conduits.

- **Structural discrimination angle:** [[Safiya Umoja Noble]] (*Algorithms of Oppression*, 2018) and [[Virginia Eubanks]] (*Automating Inequality*, 2018) documented how algorithmic systems systematically harm marginalized people — search results encode racism, welfare algorithms punish poverty.

- **Legal regulation angle:** [[Karen Yeung]] (UCL) theorized "algorithmic regulation" as a distinct regulatory modality with specific accountability challenges; [[Mireille Hildebrandt]] (Radboud) connects algorithmic governance to rule-of-law and constitutional concerns.

- **Infrastructure critique:** [[Kate Crawford]] (*Atlas of AI*, 2021) situates algorithmic systems within material extractive infrastructures — data centers, labor, rare earth materials — challenging the assumption that algorithms are immaterial or neutral.

The field deliberately straddles governance *by* algorithms (algorithmic authority) vs. governance *of* algorithms (oversight regimes), though most critical scholars focus on the former.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Status: Edge of chaos — actively reshaping.**

Three forces are destabilizing the field:

1. **Agentic AI**: LLM-based autonomous agents don't make single decisions — they take chains of consequential actions. Existing algorithmic governance frameworks (explainability, auditing, bias metrics) were designed for classifiers, not agents. Completely new accountability architectures needed.

2. **EU AI Act entering enforcement**: The world's first comprehensive AI regulation is creating a major empirical laboratory. High-risk AI systems (biometric ID, credit scoring, welfare allocation, justice) must meet new standards. The compliance gap between requirements and current practice is enormous. Researchers are scrambling to document what "compliance" even means.

3. **Platform accountability in transition**: DSA (Digital Services Act) in EU creating new content moderation transparency requirements. US stalled on federal AI legislation. The global divergence creates governance arbitrage — companies route algorithmic systems through less-regulated jurisdictions.

**Heating up:** Algorithmic auditing as a practice; rights to explanation; automated decision-making in public services; agentic AI governance; decentralized governance through DAOs.

## Sub-topics & Trends (last 3–5 years)

- **Algorithmic auditing**: Methodologies for auditing black-box systems from outside (sock puppet accounts, API probing, leaked data). AUDIT-AI, AlgorithmWatch, external auditor frameworks.
- **Explainability / XAI**: Technical and legal push for systems that can explain their decisions. Connected to GDPR's "right to explanation" and EU AI Act.
- **Automated content moderation**: Scale makes human moderation impossible; algorithmic moderation embeds values opaquely. Debate over who sets the norms.
- **Algorithmic management**: Gig workers managed by algorithms (Uber, Amazon warehouses) — performance scoring, scheduling, discipline with no human in the loop.
- **Predictive systems in public services**: Welfare fraud detection, child protection, recidivism prediction — high-stakes, deeply contested.
- **Decentralized governance / DAOs**: Blockchain-based governance as alternative to corporate algorithmic control — or just algorithmic governance with different owners.
- **AI governance for agentic systems**: Newest frontier — multi-agent systems, tool-using LLMs, autonomous agents operating in the open web.

## Open Questions

1. **Can algorithmic governance frameworks apply to agentic AI?** Existing accountability tools (explainability, auditing, bias metrics) were designed for classifiers. Agents take chains of actions, pursue goals, interact dynamically. How do you audit a process rather than a decision?

2. **Governance by vs. of algorithms**: Most critical work focuses on governance *by* algorithms (how algorithms exercise power). But governance *of* algorithms (oversight, regulation) is increasingly urgent. How do these interact? Does better governance-of enable more ethical governance-by?

3. **Decentralized alternatives**: DAOs and blockchain governance promise to distribute power differently. But do they escape algorithmic governance or just redistribute it? Who writes the smart contracts? Who audits the DAO?

4. **The interpretability gap**: Deep learning systems are often not interpretable even to their designers. "Right to explanation" becomes hollow if the explanation is post-hoc rationalization. What does meaningful algorithmic accountability look like for opaque systems?

5. **Sovereignty and AI agents**: When AI agents act on behalf of users, users, platforms, and states — who is accountable? Amber's "Trustworthy Agentic Web" directly addresses this.

6. **Non-Western frameworks**: Most algorithmic governance discourse is EU/US-centric. China's social credit system is often misrepresented. What do non-Western governance traditions offer?

7. **Algorithmic governance of AI systems**: As AI is used to moderate, audit, and govern other AI systems — what are the recursive governance challenges?
