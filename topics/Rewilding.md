# Rewilding

Rewilding is an ecological conservation philosophy focused on restoring natural processes and biodiversity by reducing human management — letting ecosystems self-regulate. Originally coined by radical conservationists in the early 1990s and given scientific form in 1998 by Soulé & Noss, it has since expanded beyond ecology into design, technology criticism, and cultural theory, where "rewilding" becomes a metaphor for removing extractive control structures and allowing complexity, diversity, and emergence to return.

## Domain Summary

### Ecological Origins
The term "rewilding" was coined by Earth First! activists ~1990, first appearing in print in 1990. It received scientific grounding in the landmark 1998 paper by conservation biologists **Michael Soulé** and **Reed Noss**: ["Rewilding and Biodiversity: Complementary Goals for Continental Conservation"](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Rewilding-and-Biodiversity%3A-Complementary-Goals-for-Soul%C3%A9-Noss/5b72a52d65b8ea8e0d1b81c882e78ae59073c4cf) (*Wild Earth*, 1998, 8(3):18-28).

Soulé & Noss proposed the **"3Cs" framework**: **Cores** (large protected wild areas), **Corridors** (connectivity passages for species), and **Carnivores** (keystone predators as ecosystem regulators). This anchored rewilding as a *process-based* rather than *species-based* conservation approach — not managing nature toward a fixed state, but restoring the conditions for self-regulation.

### Key Types of Rewilding
1. **Passive rewilding** — remove human interventions (farming, grazing, management); let succession happen. Ejemplified by Oostvaardersplassen, Netherlands.
2. **Trophic rewilding** — reintroduce keystone species (especially apex predators) to restore trophic cascades. E.g., wolves in Yellowstone transforming entire river systems.
3. **Pleistocene rewilding** — restore megafauna assemblages resembling pre-human Pleistocene ecosystems. Sergey Zimov's [Pleistocene Park](https://pleistocenepark.ru/) in Siberia.
4. **Island rewilding** — focusing on removing invasive species from island ecosystems.

### Key Projects
- **[Pleistocene Park](https://pleistocenepark.ru/)** (1996–, Siberia): Sergey & Nikita Zimov restoring mammoth-steppe grassland in the Sakha Republic. Re-introducing large herbivores (bison, horses, reindeer) to test if pre-industrial grasslands can reform, potentially slowing permafrost thaw. A rare case of *speculative ecological engineering* at the deep-time scale.
- **[Oostvaardersplassen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oostvaardersplassen)** (1968/1989, Netherlands): 56km² passive rewilding reserve in Flevoland, created unintentionally from a polder. Became Europe's most-discussed (and controversial) rewilding experiment, with Konik horses and Heck cattle left to self-regulate. Became part of Nieuw Land National Park (2018).
- **[Knepp Wildland](https://knepp.co.uk/)** (2000s–, West Sussex UK): Isabella Tree and Charlie Burrell's 3,500-acre farm converted to rewilding project. One of the most-cited UK rewilding success stories; documented in Tree's *Wilding* (2018).
- **[Rewilding Britain](https://www.rewildingbritain.org.uk/)** (charity): UK advocacy org, a key policy actor pushing for 30x30 targets and national rewilding corridors.
- **[Rewilding Europe](https://rewildingeurope.com/)**: Major NGO with ten active rewilding landscapes across Europe, from Carpathians to Danube Delta.

### Theoretical Expansion
By the 2010s, rewilding had escaped ecology into cultural criticism:
- **George Monbiot's *Feral*** (2013) mainstreamed rewilding as a romantic-philosophical project — arguing for emotional rewilding of human psyches alongside ecological landscapes
- STS scholars began applying it to human-technology relations
- The term migrated into internet governance debates (post-enshittification): remove platform monocultures → allow ecological diversity to re-emerge

## Sub-topics & Trends (last 3-5 years)
- **Rewilding the internet** (2023–): Maria Farrell & Robin Berjon's widely-circulated essay arguing that platform monopolies are monocultures, and decentralized federated networks (Mastodon, ActivityPub, etc.) are the ecological diversity that needs to return. Analog of platform → monoculture agriculture; decentralization → rewilding.
- **Rewilding and degrowth** (ongoing): rewilding as *productive subtraction* — removing extractive systems reveals emergent complexity. Natural companion to degrowth theory.
- **Social rewilding** (emerging): applying rewilding metaphors to communities, cities, urban infrastructure — "wild" corners in cities, less-managed public space.
- **Rewilding and ALife** (nascent): barely articulated but implicit — what does it mean to "rewild" computational ecosystems? Open-ended evolution as rewilding of fitness landscapes.
- **30x30** (UN): UN commitment to protect/restore 30% of land & ocean by 2030 — major policy frame lifting all rewilding boats.

## Design & Tech Applications

### Rewilding the Internet (2023 discourse)
The most intellectually active zone for Amber's interests. The core analogy: 
- **Platform monopolies** = agricultural monocultures (Facebook, Google = Big Ag)
- **Enshittification** (Cory Doctorow's term) = soil depletion from monoculture
- **Federated/decentralized networks** (Mastodon, ActivityPub, Bluesky, IndieWeb) = reintroducing biodiversity
- **Rewilding the internet** = removing regulatory/structural conditions that enable monocultures; letting ecosystem complexity return

Farrell & Berjon (2023) make this argument explicitly, drawing on Monbiot's *Feral* as their ecological model. They argue you don't "build" a rewilded internet — you remove obstacles (regulatory capture, technical lock-in) and let emergent diversity grow back.

**Cory Doctorow** has written extensively about this in relation to his "enshittification" cycle — though his framing is more political economy than ecology.

### Rewilding and Degrowth
Rewilding is a natural companion to **degrowth theory**: both argue that *subtraction* is generative. Remove extractive systems → complexity emerges. This makes rewilding philosophically interesting as a design principle: what happens when you stop managing, rather than manage harder?

Key thinkers: Jason Hickel (*Less is More*, 2020), Kate Raworth (*Doughnut Economics*, 2017) in adjacent territory.

### Rewilding and Solarpunk
Solarpunk aesthetics often feature rewilded urban environments — green roofs, urban forests, cities where nature has returned. Not academically developed, but a strong aesthetic current. Solarpunk rejects the binary of pristine wilderness vs. urban control and proposes co-inhabited rewilded landscapes.

### Design Applications (nascent)
No formal HCI papers found that use "rewilding" as a technical design framework (as of March 2026). The concept appears in:
- **Speculative design**: imagining designed artifacts that allow biological processes rather than control them
- **More-than-human design**: Rune Møberg Gjøsteen, Ron Wakkary, and others working on design that decenters human agency — rewilding as a design disposition
- **Urban rewilding**: designers working with cities to create "rewilded" public space (less management, more emergence)

### ALife and Artificial Ecosystems
The implicit connection: **open-ended evolution** in ALife is a form of computational rewilding — removing fitness objectives and letting evolution find its own trajectories. This is underdeveloped but potentially rich:
- Karl Sims' evolved virtual creatures (1994) — an early computational "rewilding"
- Open-ended evolution (OEE) literature — what if you remove the target and let the ecosystem run?
- Joel Lehman's work on open-endedness at OpenAI → Amber's ERA supervisor

## Connection to Amber

### 1. Rewilding as ALife Metaphor
The strongest connection. **Open-ended evolution = computational rewilding**: remove the fitness function (the human management), let the evolutionary landscape self-organize. ALife has been chasing OEE (Open-Ended Evolution) for decades — rewilding ecology provides an underused conceptual lens. 

Amber's ERA fellowship is with **Joel Lehman** (Amber's supervisor), who has worked directly on open-endedness. "Rewilding AI fitness landscapes" = removing proxy metrics → allowing emergent complexity. This is worth developing.

**Specific proposal**: Amber could write a short position paper or essay connecting rewilding philosophy to ALife OEE — framing computational systems that self-organize without human targets as "rewilded." This would be a novel contribution: ecological rewilding → ALife rewilding → design rewilding as a unified concept.

### 2. Agent Ethology
Amber's core concept "agent ethology" — studying agents *in the wild* — is literally a rewilding move. Traditional AI evaluation = managed monoculture (benchmark scores, controlled tasks). Agent ethology = rewilding AI evaluation: observe agents in complex, unmanaged environments. The analogy is exact and worth making explicit in Amber's work.

**Citation opportunity**: Citing Monbiot's *Feral* and the Soulé & Noss framework in the introduction of an agent ethology paper would be intellectually distinctive and cross-disciplinary.

### 3. Rewilding as Design Methodology
What would a "rewilded" technology look like? Design that:
- **Subtracts management** rather than adding features
- Creates conditions for **emergent complexity** rather than predefined interactions
- **Decenters the human** as the endpoint of the system
- Allows non-human agents (AI, organisms, data patterns) to self-organize

This aligns deeply with Amber's more-than-human design research. The frame: **rewilding as design methodology** = a design practice that removes control rather than exercises it. This is a genuinely novel design research contribution.

### 4. Multispecies Design + Rewilding
Haraway's multispecies thinking + rewilding = design that creates conditions for interspecies relations, not just human use. In Amber's context: what would a rewilded AI-human relationship look like? Not training AI toward human preferences, but creating conditions where AI and human actors can develop their own ecological relationship? Speculative but rich.

### 5. Cosmotechnics Connection
Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics (different cultural cosmologies of technology) + rewilding: rewilding implicitly assumes a Western ecological cosmology (Pleistocene = baseline, nature = apart from humans). Chinese ecological philosophy (Daoism, *ziran* 自然) has its own "rewilding" analogy — *wu wei* (non-action) as the ground from which complexity emerges. Amber's position at the intersection of Chinese and Western techno-philosophy could be expressed through this lens.

This connects to Amber's agent ethology (observe agents in unmanaged conditions), ALife (open-ended evolution = computational rewilding), and more-than-human design (decenter human as endpoint).
