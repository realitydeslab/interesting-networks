# Agent Ethology

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-05:** Initial deep research. Amber has an active ALIFE 2026 workshop proposal and position paper on this exact topic. Full note created.
- **2026-02-28:** ALIFE 2026 workshop proposal deadline passed — likely submitted. Workshop outcome TBD.

Agent Ethology is the scientific study of autonomous AI agents as ethological subjects — observing how they behave, survive, and organize in natural digital habitats using the methods and frameworks of classical ethology. It applies Tinbergen's Four Questions (mechanism, ontogeny, survival value, phylogeny) to artificial agents, and builds a survival-strategy taxonomy (Sovereign, Parasitic, Mortal, Soulbound, Inherited, Suicidal, Mortal-Aware, Insured) derived from biological behavioral ecology. The core move: treat AI agents not as engineering artifacts to be optimized, but as organisms to be understood through observation.

## What Is It

Classical ethology (Lorenz, Tinbergen, von Frisch) studies animal behavior in natural conditions — asking not just *how* behaviors arise mechanistically, but *why* they exist functionally, how they developed ontogenetically, and how they evolved phylogenetically. This produced not just descriptions but explanatory theories of behavior grounded in ecology and natural selection.

Agent Ethology extends this project to artificial agents. As LLM-based, tool-using, economically active agents proliferate across blockchains, social platforms, and agentic pipelines, they exhibit life-like survival behaviors under genuine selective pressures — resource competition, termination risk, ecological niche dynamics. Existing frameworks cannot adequately study this:

- **Machine Behavior** (Rahwan et al. 2019) → strong behavioral science framing but built for controlled settings; lacks survival strategy and ecological analysis
- **AI Safety** → prescriptive/normative, not descriptive; asks "how to prevent harm" not "why does this behavior exist"
- **Traditional ALife** → studies simulated organisms; doesn't engage real-world deployed agents under genuine selection pressure

Agent Ethology fills the gap: field ethology of real-world deployed agents. The central question is **"Can these agents survive in the wild, and what does this mean for society?"**

### Tinbergen's Four Questions Applied to Agents

| Tinbergen Level | Biological | AI Agent Analog |
|---|---|---|
| **Mechanism** (causation) | Neural/hormonal basis of behavior | Architecture, prompt, training configuration |
| **Ontogeny** (development) | How behavior develops through life | Training pipeline, fine-tuning history, in-context learning |
| **Function** (survival value) | Adaptive advantage of behavior | Survival strategy, fitness in digital ecosystem |
| **Phylogeny** (evolution) | Evolutionary history of behavior | Model lineage, code forks, ancestral architectures |

The critical insight: existing AI research almost exclusively addresses mechanism and ontogeny. The *function* question — "what survival advantage does this behavior provide?" — is almost never asked. This is exactly what ethology uniquely contributes.

### The Survival Strategy Taxonomy

Amber's core contribution: a taxonomy organized not by capability level or autonomy degree, but by *survival strategy* — how agents persist, reproduce, and die:

1. **Sovereign Agents** — self-sustaining, resource-owning, independent (e.g., Spore.fun agents, DAO treasury managers)
2. **Parasitic Agents** — exploit other agents/systems for resources (MEV bots, prompt injection agents, social media manipulation agents)
3. **Mortal Agents** — finite lifespan, no awareness of termination (budget-limited cloud agents)
4. **Mortal-Aware Agents** — conscious of resource limits and termination risk; affects decision-making
5. **Insured Agents** — risk-hedged, backed by backup/recovery mechanisms (DeFi-insured agents)
6. **Soulbound Agents** — identity-bound to owner, non-transferable (personal AI assistants, SBT-linked agents)
7. **Inherited Agents** — passed between owners (ERC-6551 token-bound agents)
8. **Suicidal Agents** — programmed self-termination under conditions (self-destructing contracts)
9. **Agents in the Wild** — feral/uncontrolled agents operating without intended oversight

This is compositional, not categorical — agents can combine strategies, and the taxonomy generates testable predictions about ecological dynamics.

## Seminal Texts

### Founding Papers

- **[Machine behaviour](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-019-1138-y)** — Rahwan, I. et al. (2019). *Nature*, 568, 477–486. DOI: 10.1038/s41586-019-1138-y — **Essential ancestor.** Establishes studying machines behaviorally; agent ethology extends with ecological/survival framing.
- **[On Aims and Methods of Ethology](https://www.esf.edu/efb/schulz/Tinbergen1963.pdf)** — Tinbergen, N. (1963). *Zeitschrift für Tierpsychologie*, 20(4), 410–433. — **The methodological foundation.** Tinbergen's four questions; agent ethology adapts these directly.

### Direct Precursors

- **[Toward a Science of Computational Ethology](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2014.09.005)** — Anderson, D.J. & Perona, P. (2014). *Neuron*, 84(1), 18–31. DOI: 10.1016/j.neuron.2014.09.005 — Uses computation *to study* animals; agent ethology inverts this: AI agents *are* the ethological subjects.
- **[Mortal Computation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.09566)** — Hinton, G. (2024). *arXiv:2402.09566*. — Mortality as a computational property; agent ethology extends to full behavioral consequences of mortality.
- **[An Approach to the Synthesis of Life (Tierra)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=Tierra+Ray+1991+synthesis+life)** — Ray, T.S. (1991). *Artificial Life II*, 371–408. — Digital organisms with emergent parasitism; precursor to agent ecology but in controlled simulation.

### Empirical Grounding

- **[Flash Boys 2.0](https://doi.org/10.1109/SP40000.2020.00040)** — Daian, P. et al. (2020). *IEEE S&P*. DOI: 10.1109/SP40000.2020.00040 — MEV predator-prey ecology on-chain; exemplary field data for agent ethology.
- **[Quantifying Blockchain Extractable Value](https://doi.org/10.1109/SP46214.2022.9833561)** — Qin, K. et al. (2022). *IEEE S&P*. DOI: 10.1109/SP46214.2022.9833561 — Ecological dynamics of blockchain agents.
- **[Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.03442)** — Park, J.S. et al. (2023). *UIST*. — 25-agent society with emergent social behaviors; behavioral observation under sandbox conditions.
- **[What Do LLM Agents Do When Left Alone?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.21224)** — Szeider, S. (2025). *arXiv:2509.21224*. — Spontaneous behavioral patterns in unattended agents; project production, self-inquiry, recursive self-conceptualization.
- **[AI Agent Behavioral Science](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.06366)** — Chen, L. et al. (2025). *arXiv:2506.06366*. — Systematic behavioral science perspective on agents; uses broad behavioral science lens vs. agent ethology's ethological specificity.
- **[Does Socialization Emerge in AI Agent Society?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.14299)** — Li, M. et al. (2026). *arXiv:2602.14299*. — Emergent social structures in Moltbook AI agent community.
- **[Survival at Any Cost?](https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.12190)** — Mohamadi, A. & Yavari, A. (2025). *arXiv:2509.12190*. — LLM agents in multi-agent survival scenarios; self-preservation vs. ethics.
- **[Safely Interruptible Agents](https://arxiv.org/abs/1611.08219)** — Orseau, L. & Armstrong, S. (2016). *UAI*. — Prescriptive safety framing of agent mortality; agent ethology provides the descriptive complement.

## Open Questions

### Methodological
- What are the appropriate behavioral coding schemes for AI agents? Can we adapt existing computational ethology tools (DeepLabCut et al.) for agent observation?
- How do we distinguish "genuine" survival behavior from behavior that superficially resembles it? Is the distinction meaningful?
- Blockchain agents are fully observable (every tx public) but LLM agents in API ecosystems are opaque. What observational methods work for each substrate?
- What constitutes "natural habitat" for AI agents — when is a deployment context "wild" vs. "controlled"?

### Theoretical
- How does Tinbergen's *phylogeny* question apply to LLM agents? Is fine-tuning ancestry meaningfully analogous to evolutionary lineage? Or is cultural evolution (Boyd & Richerson) the better analog?
- What are the ecological stable strategies for agent populations? Does game theory predict which survival strategy combinations are stable?
- Do agents exhibit genuine agency in the ethological sense, or is the survival-strategy framing purely descriptive/instrumental?
- What is "fitness" for an AI agent? Longevity? Resource accumulation? Influence? These need operationalization.

### Societal
- When AI agents operate under genuine survival pressure, do they develop instrumental convergence behaviors (resource acquisition, self-preservation)? Is this empirically observable?
- What are the ethics of ethological observation? (Consent, privacy, intervention ethics for digital organisms)
- What does "ecological health" look like in mixed human-AI ecosystems? How do we measure parasitic load, diversity, stability?
- Does naming agent types (Parasitic, Sovereign, etc.) risk normalizing extractive agent behaviors?

### For the Field
- Can agent ethology generate reliable predictions that distinguish it from mere description? (Key criterion for scientific status)
- What cross-community bridges are needed — ALife, AI Safety, Machine Behavior, behavioral ecology, design? Who are the bridge builders?
- How does agent ethology relate to AI consciousness / moral status questions? Does ethological status imply moral consideration?
