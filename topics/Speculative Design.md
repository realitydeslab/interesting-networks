# Speculative Design

Design as a critical practice for imagining, provoking, and debating possible futures — not solving problems, but posing questions through designed artifacts. Coined by [[Anthony Dunne]] and [[Fiona Raby]] at the Royal College of Art's Design Interactions programme, speculative design uses prototypes, films, and scenarios to make abstract futures tangible. It sits at the intersection of design, philosophy of technology, futures studies, art, and social critique. Deeply relevant to Amber's entire research agenda: speculative design is the methodological backbone of [[Wendi Yan]]'s practice, and closely entangled with [[More-than-human Design]], [[Design Fiction]], [[Critical Design]], [[Bioart]], [[Multispecies Design]], and [[Sinofuturism]].

## Domain Summary

Speculative design is a design practice that treats designed objects and scenarios not as solutions but as *arguments* — provocations that invite audiences to debate the future rather than consume a product. It uses prototypes, films, scenarios, and installations to make hypothetical worlds present, legible, and debatable.

**Intellectual lineage:**
- Italian Radical Design (1960s-70s) — Ettore Sottsass, Superstudio, Archizoom. Anti-capitalist, provocative.
- [Critical design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_design) — first articulated by Anthony Dunne in *Hertzian Tales* (1999). Uses design to expose assumptions, not solve problems.
- Speculative Design — Dunne & Raby's codification in *Speculative Everything* (2013). Broader than critical design; includes social fiction, not just product critique.
- [Design Fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Design_fiction) — a cousin practice, coined by Bruce Sterling (2005), theorized by Julian Bleecker (2009). Uses diegetic prototypes to suspend disbelief. More narrative/story-world focused.
- [Experiential Futures (XF)](https://futuryst.blogspot.com/2018/10/experiential-futures-brief-outline.html) — Stuart Candy's framework. Makes futures physically immersive at 1:1 scale.

**The A/B Manifesto (Dunne & Raby):**

| A (Affirmative) | B (Speculative) |
|---|---|
| Problem solving | Problem finding |
| Provides answers | Asks questions |
| Design for production | Design for debate |
| For how the world is | For how the world could be |
| Science fiction | Social fiction |
| Consumer | Citizen |
| Makes us buy | Makes us think |

**Core move:** Design as a medium for philosophical inquiry, not commercial output. Artifacts function rhetorically — the designed object is an *argument* materialized.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Is this fast-evolving? YES — for complex reasons.**

Speculative design has entered a critical inflection point. The field is simultaneously:
- **Being institutionalized** (taught at RCA, Parsons, MIT, China Academy of Art, Goldsmiths) → becoming a legible academic discipline
- **Being critiqued as elitist** — the Lupton/Shea critique (2012) that SD is "elite British designers dreaming up weird futures" without community accountability
- **Being absorbed into AI discourse** — speculative AI artifacts, AI ethics through design fiction, more-than-human AI design
- **Expanding globally** — non-Western practitioners reframing SD through Sinofuturism, Afrofuturism, Indigenous Futurisms, Gulf Futurism. The field's Western-centric assumptions are under pressure.
- **Connecting to climate crisis** — Solarpunk, degrowth design, speculative ecology increasingly use SD methodology

**Driving forces:**
1. AI urgency — designing the future of AI through speculative objects has become an institutional priority (Dunne & Raby at Parsons built SD curricula around AI and social inquiry)
2. Climate foresight — SD methodology is being applied to climate scenarios, more-than-human futures
3. Non-Western expansion — Chinese, East Asian, African practitioners are reinterpreting the canon
4. Policy uptake — governments, think tanks (e.g., IFTF, Nesta) are adopting design fiction for strategic foresight

**Hot in 1-3 years?** Yes. As AI policy discussions demand imaginative tools, SD methodology is being pulled into:
- AI governance and ethics
- Climate adaptation policy
- Bioethics (synthetic biology, AI-biology intersection)
- Pedagogy reform (Design + Liberal Arts)

## Sub-topics & Trends (last 3-5 years)

### Design Fiction
The cousin practice focused on narrative/story-world building. [Julian Bleecker's Near Future Laboratory](https://nearfuturelaboratory.com/) has industrialized design fiction for corporate strategy — packaging futures as design artifacts (the *Ikea Catalog from the Future* is the canonical example). Recent trend: AI-generated design fictions.

### Experiential Futures (XF)
[Stuart Candy's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stuart_Candy) practice of making futures 1:1 scale and sensory. Works with Brian Eno on music + futures ("Sing Wild Seeds" initiative with Joseph Rowntree Foundation + Earth/Percent). First Fellow of the Long Now Foundation. Key XF tool: "The Thing from the Future" card game (with Jeff Watson/Situation Lab).

### More-than-Human / Multispecies SD
SD that decenters the human — designing for non-human agency, multispecies relations, post-anthropocentric futures. Intersection with more-than-human design, new materialism. [[Ai Hasegawa]]'s work (growing shark embryos in human bodies) is iconic.

### Speculative Biology / Bioart
[[Alexandra Daisy Ginsberg]]'s practice — AI + synthetic biology + speculative ecology (MoMA permanent collection). Growing synthetic flora to imagine biodiversity futures.

### Non-Western Speculative Design
- **Sinofuturism** — Chinese aesthetics + speculative tech futures. [[Wendi Yan]]'s worldbuilding sits here.
- **Afrofuturism** as SD — [[Afrofuturism]] has its own speculative design tradition (Afrotectopia, futurist design)
- **Indigenous Futurisms** — [[Indigenous Futurisms]] counter SD's Western assumptions
- **Gulf Futurism** — [[Gulf Futurism]] — speculative excess, petro-modernism critiqued

### AI + Speculative Design
Designing artifacts that provoke debate about AI futures. Dunne & Raby's *Designed Realities Studio* at Parsons (2016-2025) focused on AI and social inquiry. New trend: using SD as a method for responsible AI development and AI ethics communication.

### Climate + Speculative Design
Solarpunk design, degrowth design, speculative ecology. Future Observatory (Design Museum London) is the UK institutional home for this.

## Key Concepts & Debates

### Core Concepts
- **A/B Manifesto** — Dunne & Raby's binary: affirmative vs. speculative design
- **Diegetic prototype** — from David Kirby (film studies): objects that exist within a story world, making futures real within narrative. Central to design fiction.
- **Preferred futures / probable / possible / plausible futures** — the futures cone (from foresight studies): SD operates in the "possible" and "preferable" zones
- **Design for debate** — artifacts that open discussion rather than close it
- **Functional fictions** — objects that don't work but communicate a vision
- **Alternative presents** — not predicting the future, but asking "what if things were different *now*?"

### Key Debates & Critiques

**1. The Elitism Critique (Lupton/Shea, 2012)**
Speculative design is "elite British designers making weird things for galleries." Critique: SD tends to be made by and for educated Western design professionals, without community participation, without accountability to the people most affected by the futures it imagines. Response: studios like Extrapolation Factory explicitly do community-engaged SD. But the critique remains live.

**2. Optimism vs. Pessimism / Dystopia Fatigue**
SD has been accused of defaulting to dystopian aesthetics (dark, clinical, alienating objects). Monika Bielskyte's "Protopia Futures" movement is an explicit response — demanding plurality, hope, and positive possibility. Counter: Dunne & Raby argue they're not dystopian but *uncertain*.

**3. Complicity Critique**
When SD is absorbed into corporate strategy (Intel, Microsoft, IDEO, Google X all use design fiction), does it become legitimation theater? The same "what if?" process that produces critical art produces corporate roadmaps. Julian Bleecker's Near Future Laboratory explicitly serves corporate clients.

**4. Non-Western Canon**
The field was theorized at the RCA by British designers working in an Anglo-European tradition. Non-Western SD (Sinofuturism, Afrofuturism, Indigenous Futurisms, Gulf Futurism) challenges the assumptions baked into the founding frameworks. This is the most productive current tension.

**5. SD vs. Science Fiction**
SD claims to be distinct from SF — it's "social fiction" not "science fiction" (Dunne & Raby). But the boundary is unstable. Wendi Yan's work shows how design fiction and SF worldbuilding collapse into each other productively.

**6. Research through Design legitimacy**
Is SD research? Can an artifact "know" something? The RtD discourse (DRS, Research Values in Design) debates this. SD practitioners claim their objects generate knowledge not accessible through text.
