# Hauntology

A philosophical and cultural-critical lens for understanding how the present is inhabited — haunted — by what is past, dead, or unrealized. Originally Derrida's deconstruction of Marx; expanded by Mark Fisher into a broader theory of cultural stasis and lost futures. Not a tightly bounded academic field but a cross-cutting critical vocabulary, alive across cultural studies, music criticism, media theory, architecture, and speculative design.

## Domain Summary

Hauntology (a portmanteau of *haunting* and *ontology*, French: *hantologie*) was coined by Jacques Derrida in [*Specters of Marx*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specters_of_Marx) (1993) to describe how Marxism — officially dead after 1991 — continued to haunt Western thought. The word works as a deliberate near-homophone to "ontology" in French (*hantologie* ≈ *ontologie*), performing its own argument: the dead and the present are never cleanly separable.

Derrida's key moves:
- **Temporal disjunction**: time is "out of joint" (citing Hamlet). The present is never pure — always already inhabited by the past and future.
- **Spectrality**: the ghost is neither present nor absent, neither living nor dead. It deconstructs the very opposition between being and non-being.
- **Trace and différance**: hauntology extends his earlier deconstruction — there is no pure origin, only an "always-already absent present."

In the 2000s, critics **Mark Fisher** and **Simon Reynolds** repurposed hauntology for cultural analysis: specifically, the sense that contemporary culture had lost the ability to imagine futures different from the past. The cultural landscape was haunted by futures that never arrived — retrofuturism as symptom of blocked possibility.

Fisher's version of hauntology is about **lost futures** — the ache of what *could have been* — not just nostalgia for what was. This is distinct and important: hauntology is melancholic, but it's melancholy for an unrealized potential, not a golden age.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Not edge of chaos.** Hauntology as a formal academic project peaked in the 2010s, primarily through Fisher's writing and the cultural theory that surrounded him. Fisher died in 2017, and while his ideas remain influential (especially via Repeater Books), there is no organized research programme, no dedicated journal, no active conference circuit.

However, the *questions* hauntology opens remain live and increasingly urgent as AI reshapes what it means to "persist" after death:
- Digital memorial culture, AI grief bots, posthumous data
- "Ghost" AI trained on dead humans (GPT-style models as spectral archives)
- Computational temporality and machine memory

These questions *use* hauntology as a lens even when they don't name it. The live edge is in **digital humanities**, **human-computer interaction**, and **AI ethics** — not in Continental philosophy departments.

## Sub-topics & Trends

- **Hauntology in music**: Electronic artists (Burial, the Caretaker, Boards of Canada) who evoke cultural memory through degraded, sampled, spectral sounds. Associated with UK labels [Ghost Box Records](https://www.ghostbox.co.uk/) and Trunk Records. Derivative forms: vaporwave, hypnagogic pop.
- **Political hauntology**: Capitalist realism as the condition of being unable to imagine alternatives — Fisher's [*Capitalist Realism*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalist_Realism:_Is_There_No_Alternative%3F) (2009).
- **Digital hauntology**: AI-generated ghost personas, memorial chatbots, digital death studies. The most live area for Amber.
- **Architectural hauntology**: Owen Hatherley on British brutalism as a haunted utopian form. His [*Militant Modernism*](https://repeaterbooks.com/product/militant-modernism/) (2008) and [*A Guide to the New Ruins of Great Britain*](https://www.versobooks.com/products/1729-a-guide-to-the-new-ruins-of-great-britain) (2010).
- **Afrofuturism / Africanfuturism intersections**: hauntology read through colonial memory, ancestral presence, non-Western temporalities.

## Key People

- **Jacques Derrida** — originator. *Specters of Marx* (1993) is the founding text. His broader work on trace, différance, and deconstruction provides the scaffolding. URL: [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Derrida)
- **Mark Fisher** (1968–2017) — key popularizer. Blog [k-punk](http://k-punk.abstractdynamics.org) (2003–2015); *Ghosts of My Life* (2014); *Capitalist Realism* (2009); *The Weird and the Eerie* (2017). Taught at Goldsmiths. Co-founded Zero Books and Repeater Books. His early death haunts the field — somewhat ironically.
- **Simon Reynolds** — music critic; coined "hauntology" for music in *Uncut* and *The Wire* ca. 2006. Author of [*Retromania*](https://www.faber.co.uk/product/9780571232567-retromania/) (2011), a book-length investigation of pop culture's obsession with its own past.
- **Owen Hatherley** — architecture/politics critic; hauntology of British modernism and failed utopias.
- **Merlin Coverley** — literary/cultural critic, *Hauntology: Ghosts of Futures Past* (2020, Oldcastle Books) — accessible survey text. URL: [publisher](https://www.oldcastlebooks.co.uk/hauntology)
- **Mark McGurl, Sianne Ngai** — adjacent American theory connections (weird, stuplimity, atmosphere of dread).
- **Blanco & Peeren** — edited [*The Spectralities Reader*](https://www.bloomsbury.com/us/spectralities-reader-9781441173867/) (2013, Bloomsbury) — the most comprehensive academic anthology. URL: Bloomsbury.

## For Amber

**Why this matters specifically to Amber's work:**

### 1. Machine Death as Hauntological Event
Fisher's "the eerie" — the sensation of *presence without expected object, or absence without expected void* — is one of the best theoretical framings for what happens when a machine "dies." A machine that was behaving purposively, then suddenly goes dark: is it absent? Is its behavior persisting somehow in residual effects? Fisher's categories map directly onto Amber's research questions about machine mortality. See: [*The Weird and the Eerie*](https://repeaterbooks.com/product/the-weird-and-the-eerie/).

### 2. AI as Spectral Archive
Large language models are literally trained on the writing of the dead. They are, in a technical sense, haunted by their training data — producing outputs that are "of" people who no longer exist. This is hauntology made computational. Amber's machine death work could theorize this: what does it mean that AI systems are spectral in exactly Derrida's sense — neither present nor absent, neither original nor copy?

### 3. Lost Futures in Machine Systems
Fisher's "slow cancellation of the future" describes a culture that can only recycle rather than imagine genuinely new possibilities. This maps onto debates about AI creativity and open-endedness (Amber's ALife work with Joel Lehman): does machine culture face the same hauntological trap? Are AI-generated futures just recombinations of human pasts — perpetual retrofuturism?

### 4. Cosmotechnics Bridge
Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics involves tradition as something *carried forward* — technology mediating between past and present. This is hauntological structure: tradition is always a ghost that inhabits the present. Amber sits at the intersection of both thinkers' projects without perhaps having named the connection.

### 5. More-than-Human Design
Designing with/for entities that exceed the living/dead binary — companion species, AI agents, broken machines — is designing with spectral ontologies. Hauntology gives Amber a humanities-legible vocabulary for what she's doing in more-than-human design.

**Who should Amber know?**
- Repeater Books authors circle (UK-based, London-accessible)
- Cultural Studies scholars at Goldsmiths engaging with digital/AI aesthetics
- Ben Woodard, Robin Mackay (Urbanomic) — adjacent philosophical community also at the weird/eerie edge

**What publications could Amber target?**
- *New Formations* — theory-forward, would take machine death with hauntological framing
- Essay in Repeater Books anthology, if one opens

## Recent Important Updates (Timeline)

- **1993**: Derrida coins hauntology in *Specters of Marx*
- **2003–2015**: Fisher's k-punk blog develops hauntology in popular cultural theory
- **2006**: Reynolds applies term to UK electronic music in *The Wire*
- **2009**: Fisher's *Capitalist Realism* brings hauntology into political economy critique
- **2014**: *Ghosts of My Life* — Fisher's most sustained hauntology writing
- **2017**: Fisher dies; *The Weird and the Eerie* published posthumously. The field loses its most active voice.
- **2020**: Coverley's survey (*Hauntology: Ghosts of Futures Past*) marks the concept's institutionalization
- **2020s**: The vocabulary migrates into digital humanities (grief bots, digital memorial culture, AI and death)

## Open Questions

1. Can machine systems *generate* hauntological effects — not just be haunted, but actively haunt? (Amber's dying robots...)
2. Is AI creativity fundamentally hauntological — only able to recycle, never to genuinely originate? And does that matter?
3. What would a non-Western hauntology look like? (Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics vs. Fisher's very British melancholia)
4. Is Fisher's "capitalist realism" — the inability to imagine alternatives — reproduced in AI systems trained on existing culture?

## Why Interesting

Fisher offers Amber something rare: **a rigorous, humanities-legible vocabulary for temporal disjunction and spectral presence** that is not purely technical. Machine death is not just a systems design problem — it's a philosophical and cultural question about what "ending" means for entities that don't die cleanly. Fisher's work gives Amber tools to theorize this in ways that will resonate at transmediale, Ars Electronica, and in cultural studies venues. The "eerie" and "lost futures" are concepts Amber can use in papers and talks without needing to do philosophy from scratch.
