# Digital Commons

Shared digital resources — software, knowledge, data, cultural content — that are collectively produced and governed by a community for public use. Rooted in Elinor Ostrom's Nobel-winning commons theory, extended by Yochai Benkler's "commons-based peer production," and now urgently relevant as AI training data, open weights models, and protocol-based networks reshape what commoning means. The defining question: can community self-governance prevent digital enclosure by platforms and corporations — and what happens when autonomous AI agents participate in or undermine commons governance?

## Domain Summary

Digital commons are information and knowledge resources that are:
1. **Collectively created** — by communities, often using distributed peer production
2. **Governed by the community** — not markets or states alone; the community can intervene in governing their resources and interactions
3. **Open and non-exclusive** — generally freely available to third parties; oriented toward use/reuse rather than commodity exchange
4. **Sustained by licenses** — GPL, Creative Commons, Peer Production License preserve commoning

Mayo Fuster Morell's canonical definition: *"Information and knowledge resources that are collectively created and owned or shared between or among a community and that tend to be non-exclusive, that is, be (generally freely) available to third parties. Thus, they are oriented to favor use and reuse, rather than to exchange as a commodity."*

**Historical lineage:**
- 1980s: Richard Stallman's Free Software movement — first organized digital commons
- 1990s: Wikipedia, open source, Creative Commons
- 2001-2006: Yochai Benkler coins "commons-based peer production" (CBPP), theorizes networked information economy
- 2006+: David Bollier and Silke Helfrich develop commoning as active practice (not just resource), distinct from "the commons" as passive noun
- 2019: *Free, Fair and Alive* — ontological shift: commons as verb (commoning), not noun
- 2022-2026: AI training data commons, open weights movement, protocol-based commons emerge as new frontier

**Tragedy of the commons vs. Ostrom:** Garret Hardin (1968) argued commons inevitably collapse via overuse. Ostrom (1990, Nobel 2009) empirically refuted this: communities develop institutional rules to sustain shared resources without privatization or state control. Her 8 design principles for commons governance became foundational.

**Digital commons vs. platform enclosure:** The central political tension. Platforms (Meta, Google, X) extract value from commons-produced knowledge, then enclose it. Web 2.0 user-generated content is commoning labor captured by capital. The AI moment intensifies this: LLMs trained on commons data, outputs proprietary.

**Data commons vs. knowledge commons vs. AI commons:** Emerging distinctions:
- *Data commons*: shared datasets under open licenses (e.g., Open Street Map, Wikidata)
- *Knowledge commons*: Wikipedia, arXiv, open access science
- *AI commons*: open weights models (Llama, Mistral), shared training data (The Pile, Common Crawl), evaluation benchmarks as commons
- *Protocol commons*: ActivityPub, AT Protocol, Nostr — federated infrastructure as commons

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Fast-evolving.** Three concurrent pressures making this red-hot 2024-2026:

1. **AI enclosure crisis** — LLMs trained on commons (Wikipedia, GitHub, Common Crawl) producing closed commercial products. Authors, creators, coders discovering their commoned work was training data. Legal battles (New York Times v. OpenAI, artists suing Stability AI) forcing reconsideration of what commoning means when AI harvests without reciprocity.

2. **Open weights movement** — Llama 2/3, Mistral, Gemma positioning as "open source AI" while base weights vs. fine-tuning vs. data openness remain contested. Is an open weights model a digital commons? The Open Source Initiative (OSI) released its first Open Source AI Definition in 2024, contested heavily.

3. **Agentic commons** — NEW FRONTIER: if AI agents act in commons (Wikipedia edits, open source contributions, governance participation), what does commoning become? Who governs agents? Agents can scale contribution AND enclosure simultaneously. This is exactly Amber's territory.

4. **Protocol renaissance** — Bluesky/AT Protocol, ActivityPub/Mastodon, Nostr building federated infrastructure as commons alternative to platform centralization. "Exit" rather than "voice" as commons strategy.

**Will be hot in 1-3 years:** Yes. The AI commons question — who owns AI training data, who governs open models, how do agents participate in commons — is becoming central to technology law, governance, and political economy.

## Key People

- **[[Elinor Ostrom]]** (1933–2012) — Political scientist, Indiana University. Nobel Prize 2009 for "analysis of economic governance, especially the commons." Empirically refuted Tragedy of the Commons. Developed 8 design principles for sustainable commons governance via her Institutional Analysis and Development (IAD) framework. Foundational for all digital commons theory.

- **[[Yochai Benkler]]** — Berkman Professor, Harvard Law School; faculty co-director, Berkman Klein Center. Coined "commons-based peer production" (2001). *The Wealth of Networks* (2006) is the key text theorizing how networked information enables new forms of social production beyond firm and market. URL: <http://benkler.org>

- **[[David Bollier]]** — Activist, writer, policy strategist. Director, Reinventing the Commons Program, Schumacher Center for a New Economics. Co-founded Commons Strategies Group with Silke Helfrich. Key books: *Think Like a Commoner* (2014), *Free Fair and Alive* (2019, with Helfrich). Blogs at <https://bollier.org>. Berlin Prize 2012.

- **[[Silke Helfrich]]** (1967–2021) — German author, activist, scholar. Co-authored *Patterns of Commoning* (2015) and *Free, Fair and Alive* (2019) with Bollier. Former regional director Latin America, Heinrich Böll Foundation. Introduced "commoning" as verb (active practice) and "Ontoshift" (ontological shift to relational commons worldview). Friend and German translator of Ostrom.

- **[[Mayo Fuster Morell]]** — Social researcher. Berkman Center for Internet & Society, MIT Center for Civic Media, UAB. Provided canonical definition of digital commons. PhD thesis: first large quantitative study of CBPP communities. Co-founder of Procomuns Forum on collaborative economy. Wikipedia governance researcher.

- **[[Michel Bauwens]]** — Founder, P2P Foundation (2005). Coined "Peer-to-Peer theory." Advocates for Commons Transition. Based between Belgium and Thailand. Key connector between digital commons, open hardware, and post-capitalist economic theory.

- **[[Lawrence Lessig]]** — Harvard Law. Founded Creative Commons (2001). *The Future of Ideas* (2001), *Free Culture* (2004). Defined "code is law" principle. Central to framing internet architecture as commons governance.

## Open Questions

1. **Can AI agents be commoners?** If commoning requires intentionality, reciprocity, and governance participation, can autonomous agents commone? Or do they collapse the social contract commons requires?

2. **What governs agents in commons?** Wikipedia already faces bot governance challenges. As LLM agents contribute, edit, and govern commons, what institutional design principles apply? Ostrom's IAD in agent space?

3. **Open weights ≠ open commons?** Llama/Mistral weights are open but training data often isn't. Does an AI model constitute a commons if: (a) weights open, (b) training data closed, (c) governance undemocratic?

4. **Platform enclosure of AI commons:** If AI models are trained on Wikipedia, GitHub, Common Crawl (commons-produced) but outputs are proprietary — is this the largest enclosure in history? What's the reciprocity mechanism?

5. **Protocol as commons infrastructure:** Can federated protocols (ActivityPub, AT Protocol) sustain democratic governance at scale, or do network effects inevitably centralize them?

6. **Commoning without locality:** Ostrom's design principles assume social embeddedness, sanctions, monitoring by participants. How do you commone when participants are anonymous, global, and may be non-human?

7. **ALife as commons:** Could artificial life ecosystems — open-ended evolutionary systems — be governed as commons? What would Ostrom's principles look like for evolving digital organisms?

## Why Interesting

Digital Commons is **directly core** to Amber's [[Agentive Commoning]] concept — which cannot be theorized without it. The connection operates on multiple levels:

1. **Theoretical foundation:** Agentive Commoning takes CBPP (Benkler) and extends it to AI agents as participants in commons production. You cannot develop this concept without engaging Ostrom's design principles, Bollier/Helfrich's commoning-as-practice, and Fuster Morell's governance framework.

2. **The defining problem:** The AI moment creates a crisis for digital commons theory. Commons were theorized assuming human participants who can monitor, sanction, and renegotiate rules. AI agents change all assumptions. Amber's work directly addresses this rupture.

3. **The "Ontoshift" resonance:** Bollier and Helfrich's move from "commons" (noun/resource) to "commoning" (verb/practice) parallels Amber's more-than-human design philosophy: decentering fixed entities in favor of relations, practices, and processes. Both resist the enclosure of life into commodities.

4. **ALife connection:** Open-ended evolutionary systems (Amber's ALife interest) share deep structural features with digital commons: both involve decentralized resource management, emergent governance, and the question of what sustains productive diversity over time. Is an open-ended ALife ecosystem a kind of commons?

5. **Protocol Studies connection:** Amber's work on the Trustworthy Agentic Web engages protocol-based infrastructure. Protocols (ActivityPub, AT Protocol) are attempts to build commons-based infrastructure against platform enclosure — the digital commons question translated into network architecture.

6. **The edge question:** If agents participate in commons governance — editing Wikipedia, contributing to open source, managing shared resources — what institutional designs prevent agents from becoming engines of enclosure rather than commoning? This is both a research question and a design problem in Amber's territory.

The Digital Commons literature gives Amber a 35-year theoretical vocabulary — tragedy, governance, commoning, enclosure, peer production — to apply to the entirely new problem of what commons mean when the peers include non-human agents. This is genuinely novel intellectual territory.
