# Assemblage Theory

Assemblage Theory is a philosophical and social-theoretical framework describing reality as composed of heterogeneous, contingently connected components that produce emergent properties irreducible to their parts. Originating in Gilles Deleuze and Félix Guattari's concept of *agencement* (arrangement, layout) in *Anti-Oedipus* (1972) and *A Thousand Plateaus* (1980), it was systematized and made empirically productive by Manuel DeLanda in *A New Philosophy of Society* (2006) and *Assemblage Theory* (2016). Unlike structural or essentialist frameworks, assemblages are defined by relations of **exteriority** — components retain autonomy outside the assemblage — and by twin axes of **materiality/expressivity** and **territorialization/deterritorialization** that define how any assemblage forms, stabilizes, and dissolves. For Amber, Assemblage Theory is the ontological engine beneath [[Actor-Network Theory]], [[New Materialism]], and the emerging study of AI agent collectives: it offers vocabulary for thinking about how human and nonhuman actors — including AI agents — coalesce into dynamic, self-organizing formations with emergent behavioral repertoires.

## Domain Summary

### Origins: Deleuze, Guattari, and the *Agencement*

The French word *agencement* — the term Deleuze and Guattari actually used — means "arrangement," "fitting," or "layout." Its translation as "assemblage" by Brian Massumi introduced a subtle shift: where *agencement* emphasizes active arranging and agential processes, "assemblage" can connote a static collection. This distinction matters: assemblages are never fixed. They are **events in process** — metastable formations constantly in flux.

In [*Anti-Oedipus: Capitalism and Schizophrenia*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/anti-oedipus) (1972, English 1983), Deleuze and Guattari attacked Freudian and Lacanian psychoanalysis for reducing desire to lack and representation. Instead, they proposed **desiring-machines**: productive, connective processes that couple with other machines to produce further flows. Society itself is a machinic assemblage — not a structure expressing a hidden essence, but a dynamic network of productive connections.

[*A Thousand Plateaus: Capitalism and Schizophrenia vol. 2*](https://www.upress.umn.edu/book-division/books/a-thousand-plateaus) (1980, English 1987) elaborates the concept systematically. The book itself is organized as plateaus rather than chapters — each plateau a self-contained intensity that can be read in any order. Key formulations:

> "In a book, as in all things, there are lines of articulation or segmentarity, strata and territories; but also lines of flight, movements of deterritorialization and destratification. Comparative rates of flow on these lines produce phenomena of relative slowness and viscosity, or, on the contrary, of acceleration and rupture. All this, lines and measurable speeds, constitutes an assemblage." (*A Thousand Plateaus*, pp. 3–4)

> "We will call an assemblage every constellation of singularities and traits deducted from the flow — selected, organized, stratified." (*A Thousand Plateaus*, p. 406)

### Core Structure: Two Axes

Every assemblage is defined by two axes:

**Axis 1: Material/Expressive**
- **Material (content):** The physical, functional, bodily dimension — human bodies, artifacts, environments, actions, reactions. What the assemblage *does*.
- **Expressive (expression):** The semiotic, symbolic, representational dimension — enunciations, statements, language, codes, laws. What the assemblage *says*.

These are not separate domains but "double articulations": each feeds back into the other. A prison assemblage has material components (walls, guards, prisoners, schedules) and expressive components (legal codes, classification systems, psychological profiles). Neither is primary.

**Axis 2: Territorialization/Deterritorialization**
- **Territorialization (coding):** Processes that stabilize, fix, and order the assemblage. Coding inscribes components into roles and relations. A city is territorialized through planning laws, street grids, property rights.
- **Deterritorialization:** Processes of destabilization — components exit, connections dissolve, new patterns emerge. Migration deterritorializes a city; new protocols deterritorialize a technical standard.
- **Reterritorialization:** The assemblage reforms, often in a transformed configuration. Deterritorialization is not dissolution but transformation.

**Machinic Assemblages vs. Collective Assemblages of Enunciation**
D&G also distinguish between:
- **Machinic assemblages** of bodies, actions, and desires (the material side)
- **Collective assemblages of enunciation** — regimes of signs, statements, and language acts (the expressive side)

Both are simultaneously present in every assemblage. An AI system like an LLM is simultaneously a machinic assemblage (compute clusters, data, gradient descent, inference hardware) and a collective assemblage of enunciation (training corpora, RLHF preferences, chat protocols, safety guidelines).

### DeLanda's Neo-Materialist Reformulation

Manuel DeLanda — Mexican-American philosopher, artist, and architect (born 1952) — is the key figure who made assemblage theory empirically tractable. Teaching at Princeton, Penn, and the European Graduate School, DeLanda synthesized Deleuze with complexity theory, nonlinear dynamics, and artificial life to produce a rigorous social ontology.

**Key works:**
- [*War in the Age of Intelligent Machines*](https://www.zonebooks.org/books/106-war-in-the-age-of-intelligent-machines) (Zone Books, 1991) — Early work applying nonlinear dynamics and AI to military history. Remarkable for connecting assemblage thinking to artificial life and machine intelligence before these fields matured.
- [*A Thousand Years of Nonlinear History*](https://www.zonebooks.org/books/109-a-thousand-years-of-nonlinear-history) (Zone Books, 1997) — Three parallel histories of geology, biology, and linguistics as self-organizing processes. Demonstrates how matter and life produce complex patterns without central control.
- [*Intensive Science and Virtual Philosophy*](https://www.bloomsbury.com/us/intensive-science-and-virtual-philosophy-9781780937991/) (Bloomsbury, 2002) — Philosophical reconstruction of Deleuze via philosophy of science; introduces "intensive differences" and "virtual multiplicities."
- [*A New Philosophy of Society: Assemblage Theory and Social Complexity*](https://www.bloomsbury.com/us/a-new-philosophy-of-society-9780826491695/) (Bloomsbury, 2006) — The foundational text for social science applications. Applies assemblage theory at multiple scales: persons, networks, organizations, cities, nation-states. Introduces **relations of exteriority** as the key ontological commitment.
- [*Philosophy & Simulation: The Emergence of Synthetic Reason*](https://www.bloomsbury.com/uk/philosophy-and-simulation-9781474252843/) (Bloomsbury, 2011) — Connects assemblage theory to computational simulation and emergence. Highly relevant for ALife and agent-based modeling.
- [*Assemblage Theory*](https://edinburghuniversitypress.com/book-assemblage-theory.html) (Edinburgh University Press, 2016) — Definitive synthesis. Responds to critics, extends to language, mind, and community.
- [*The Rise of Realism*](https://www.wiley.com/en-gb/The+Rise+of+Realism-p-9781509519026) (Polity, 2017, with Graham Harman) — Dialogue between assemblage theory and object-oriented ontology.
- [*Materialist Phenomenology: A Philosophy of Perception*](https://www.bloomsbury.com/us/materialist-phenomenology-9781350263956/) (Bloomsbury, 2021) — Most recent major work.

**DeLanda's key contributions over D&G:**
1. **Relations of exteriority:** Components are *self-subsistent* — they can enter and exit assemblages without being defined by those assemblages. This is the crucial move against holism: the parts are real, not just functions of the whole.
2. **Third axis:** DeLanda proposes a genetic/linguistic axis: resources (material resources and expressed competencies) that flow through assemblages and enable their operation.
3. **Scale-neutral application:** DeLanda applies the same conceptual framework to individuals, institutions, cities, and ecosystems — each level analytically distinct but ontologically continuous.
4. **Connection to complexity science:** Phase transitions, attractors, bifurcation points, self-organization — assemblages exhibit all these. DeLanda explicitly connects Deleuzian ontology to complexity theory and simulation.

### Jane Bennett: Vibrant Matter

[Jane Bennett](https://politicalscience.jhu.edu/directory/jane-bennett/) — Professor at Johns Hopkins, born 1957 — developed her own assemblage thinking in [*Vibrant Matter: A Political Ecology of Things*](https://www.dukeupress.edu/vibrant-matter) (Duke University Press, 2010). Bennett's key move: **thing-power** — the capacity of nonhuman entities (food, electricity, metal, garbage) to act as "quasi-agents" with their own trajectories, tendencies, and vitality.

Bennett's assemblage thinking is less systematic than DeLanda's but more politically charged. Her interest is in how recognizing the agency of nonhuman matter disrupts our political concepts of responsibility, causality, and citizenship. Her [*The Enchantment of Modern Life*](https://press.princeton.edu/books/paperback/9780691088198/the-enchantment-of-modern-life) (Princeton, 2001) is also key — on wonder as an ethical and political resource.

**Influence:** Bennett's work shaped [[New Materialism]] directly, and her "thing-power" concept is a practical vocabulary for the more-than-human design tradition Amber works in.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

Assemblage Theory as a pure philosophical framework is **established** (Deleuze/Guattari 1972–1980; DeLanda 2006–2016). The theory's core concepts will not change. However, several adjacent edges ARE actively evolving:

**1. Computational/ALife applications (ACTIVE):** DeLanda's *Philosophy & Simulation* (2011) explicitly connected assemblage thinking to agent-based modeling and emergence in computational systems. This remains largely underdeveloped — there is a significant gap between the philosophical framework and computational implementation. This gap is now being closed as AI agent collectives become empirical phenomena.

**2. AI agent assemblages (NASCENT, FAST):** As LLM-based multi-agent systems proliferate, researchers are beginning to apply assemblage vocabulary to describe how heterogeneous AI actors — with different roles, tools, and contexts — form dynamic collectives with emergent properties. This intersection is ~2–3 years from mainstream academic articulation.

**3. Multispecies assemblages (ACTIVE):** Animal studies, more-than-human geography, and multispecies ethnography have adopted assemblage theory heavily (2015–present). Journals like *Social & Cultural Geography* and *Environment and Planning D* are active.

**4. Infrastructure and urban studies (STABLE-ACTIVE):** Colin McFarlane and Ben Anderson's work on "thinking with assemblage" in urban geography remains productive. Smart cities as assemblages of data, infrastructure, bodies, and algorithms is an active area.

**5. Feminist new materialism (ACTIVE):** Karen Barad's agential realism, Donna Haraway's sympoiesis — both draw on and extend assemblage thinking in feminist directions.

**Will be important in 1–3 years:** The connection between assemblage theory and AI multi-agent systems is the emerging edge. As autonomous AI agents form emergent collectives with behavioral repertoires that exceed any individual agent's design, assemblage theory offers the ontological vocabulary to describe this: deterritorialization/reterritorialization as network topology shifts; machinic assemblages of compute/data/inference; collective assemblages of enunciation as the prompt/response/evaluation loop.

- **Feminist posthumanism:** Rosi Braidotti's *Posthuman* (2013) and *Posthuman Knowledge* (2019) synthesize assemblage with feminist posthumanism and new materialism.
- **STS applications:** How scientific knowledge itself forms as assemblages — extending ANT in a more Deleuzian direction.
- **Assemblage ethnography:** Marcus and Saka's work on assemblage as ethnographic method — moving from representation to tracking processes of formation.

## Key People

- **[[Manuel DeLanda]]** — Born 1952, Mexico City. The definitive systematizer. Princeton/Penn/EGS. His *A New Philosophy of Society* (2006) is where to start for social science applications; *Philosophy & Simulation* (2011) for ALife/computational connections. Background includes experimental filmmaking and interest in artificial life — making him unusually relevant to Amber's work on machine behavior.
- **[[Gilles Deleuze]]** (1925–1995) — French philosopher. Co-originator of assemblage concept with Guattari. *Difference and Repetition* (1968), *Logic of Sense* (1969), *Anti-Oedipus* (1972), *A Thousand Plateaus* (1980). Died 1995.
- **[[Félix Guattari]]** (1930–1992) — French psychoanalyst, political activist. Co-creator with Deleuze. Also developed "ecosophy" and schizoanalysis independently. *The Three Ecologies* (1989) is key for environmental/political dimensions.
- **[[Jane Bennett]]** — Johns Hopkins. *Vibrant Matter* (2010). Vital materialism, thing-power, political ecology of nonhuman agency.
- **[[Rosi Braidotti]]** — Utrecht. Feminist posthumanism, new materialism. *The Posthuman* (2013), *Posthuman Knowledge* (2019). Co-coined "new materialism" with DeLanda.
- **[[Karen Barad]]** — UC Santa Cruz. Agential realism, intra-action. *Meeting the Universe Halfway* (Duke, 2007). Her concept of "intra-action" (replacing "interaction") argues that agencies are not pre-given but produced through entanglements. Adjacent to but distinct from assemblage theory.
- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — UC Santa Cruz (emerita). Cyborg Manifesto (1985), *Staying with the Trouble* (2016). Sympoiesis ("making-together"), multispecies kin-making. The feminist-posthumanist voice most compatible with assemblage thinking.
- **[[Anna Tsing]]** — UC Santa Cruz. *The Mushroom at the End of the World* (2015). Assemblage as method for studying multispecies entanglements in disturbed landscapes.
- **[[Colin McFarlane]]** — Durham. Urban assemblage theory. *Learning the City* (2011). "Thinking with assemblage" as urban methodology.
- **[[John Protevi]]** — LSU. Political affect and assemblage. *Political Affect* (2009), *Life, War, Earth* (2013). Connects assemblage to political neuroscience and affect theory.
- **[[Arturo Escobar]]** — UNC. *Designs for the Pluriverse* (2018). Assemblage applied to territorial struggles, pluriversal design, ontological politics.
- **[[Tim Ingold]]** — Aberdeen. Not strictly assemblage but adjacent — "meshwork" as alternative to assemblage. *Being Alive* (2011). Good dialogue partner.
- **[[Yuk Hui]]** — Erasmus Rotterdam. Not an assemblage theorist per se, but his cosmotechnics framework engages directly with Simondon and Deleuze on technics, objects, and the relationship between technical objects and their associated milieu. His *On the Existence of Digital Objects* (2016) connects to assemblage thinking at the level of digital objects.

### Key conceptual bridge for Amber
**AI multi-agent systems ARE assemblages.** DeLanda's ontology applies directly:
- **Relations of exteriority:** Individual agents (LLMs, tools, APIs) are self-subsistent; they can be added/removed without the whole being destroyed
- **Emergent properties:** Multi-agent systems exhibit behaviors no individual agent produces — this is assemblage emergence

Hui's cosmotechnics asks *which* assemblages are possible given different cultural-philosophical traditions of technics — a question directly relevant to Amber's interest in non-Western AI futures and technodiversity.

## Adjacent Topics

- [[Actor-Network Theory]] — The closest relative. ANT and assemblage theory share relational ontology, heterogeneous actants, and rejection of pre-given social structures. Key difference: ANT focuses on network *translation* and inscription; assemblage theory focuses on *emergence* and axes of formation/dissolution. DeLanda sees D&G as more ontologically rigorous than Latour.
- [[New Materialism]] — Assemblage theory is the ontological foundation of new materialism. Bennett, Barad, Braidotti all build on D&G's anti-essentialism.
- [[More-than-human Design]] — Design practice that takes nonhuman agency seriously; assemblage theory is one of its theoretical foundations.
- [[Machine Behavior]] — Studying AI agents as behaving entities; assemblage theory offers the ontological framework for understanding multi-agent behavioral emergence.
- [[Cosmotechnics]] (Yuk Hui) — Hui engages Simondon's individuation and Deleuze on difference; his "associated milieu" concept resonates with assemblage's relational ontology.
- [[Distributed Cognition]] — Hutchins's work on cognition distributed across sociotechnical systems; compatible with assemblage's material/expressive axes.
- [[Artificial Life]] — DeLanda's explicit connection between assemblage emergence and ALife; self-organizing systems as assemblages.
- [[Posthumanism]] — Braidotti's posthumanism deploys assemblage as its ontological ground.
- [[Multispecies Studies]] — Haraway, Tsing use assemblage to study cross-species relations.
- [[Object-Oriented Ontology]] — Graham Harman's OOO is adjacent and in productive tension; DeLanda and Harman co-authored *The Rise of Realism* (2017).
- [[Cyborg Sociology]] — Haraway's cyborg is an assemblage of human and machine; assemblage theory gives it ontological precision.

## Open Questions

1. **Are AI agent collectives assemblages?** If so, what are the empirical methods for studying their formation, stabilization, and deterritorialization? This is the productive research question for Amber.
2. **Can assemblage theory generate design principles?** Escobar gestures at this in *Designs for the Pluriverse* — but how do you *design* for assemblage dynamics rather than just analyzing them post-hoc?
3. **Deterritorialization as model collapse?** When LLMs are fine-tuned, knowledge is deterritorialized and reterritorialized. Is this literally what D&G described, or only metaphorically?
4. **Assemblage vs. ANT for AI governance:** ANT's translation framework (Callon's four moments) is more operational for policy analysis. Assemblage theory is more ontologically rich. Can they be combined?
5. **Scale:** DeLanda's scale-neutral ontology is attractive — but do the same dynamics govern micro-assemblages (one AI agent + one user) and macro-assemblages (AI industry + global labor markets)?
6. **Cosmotechnics as assemblage:** Yuk Hui argues that Chinese technics form *different* assemblages than Western ones — different relations between Tao/cosmos and technical objects. Is "technodiversity" a claim about different assemblage configurations?
7. **The "relations of exteriority" problem for neural networks:** DeLanda argues assemblage components are self-subsistent. But transformer layers are not self-subsistent — they lose all function outside the model. Does this break the assemblage framing?
8. **Feminist critique:** Donna Haraway and Lucy Suchman have both worried that assemblage thinking can erase power asymmetries by treating all components as equally "heterogeneous." How does the theory account for domination, extraction, and violence?

## Why Interesting

Assemblage Theory is the ontological grammar that underlies nearly everything Amber studies. When Amber asks "how do AI agents form collectives with emergent behavioral properties?" — this is a question about assemblage formation. When Amber studies "machine death" — what happens when an assemblage loses a key component and deterritorializes? When Amber works on "cyborg sociology" — the cyborg is an assemblage of human and machine, body and code, desire and protocol.

Crucially, DeLanda's *Philosophy & Simulation* (2011) makes the explicit connection to artificial life and computational emergence that most assemblage theorists ignore. DeLanda himself came from experimental filmmaking, artificial life, and chaos theory — his background is unusual for a continental philosopher and makes him far more relevant to Amber's computational work than the STS or geography scholars who typically apply assemblage thinking.

The **key move for Amber:** importing assemblage theory's vocabulary into agent ethology would both (a) give agent ethology a rigorous ontological foundation and (b) give assemblage theory an empirical domain (AI agent populations) it currently lacks. This is a theoretical contribution that could publish in both philosophy/theory venues (*Theory, Culture & Society*, *Body & Society*) AND in computing/HCI venues (CSCW, CHI).

**The cosmotechnics connection** (Yuk Hui → Deleuze → DeLanda → assemblage) is also significant: Hui argues that different philosophical traditions produce different assemblages of technics and cosmos. Amber's interest in non-Western AI futures maps onto asking: what assemblage configurations does cosmotechnics make possible/impossible? This is a novel research question at the Hui/DeLanda intersection that nobody is currently working on.
