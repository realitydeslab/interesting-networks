# Plant Intelligence

Plant intelligence — also called plant cognition, plant neurobiology, or vegetal agency — is the contested interdisciplinary field examining whether plants exhibit learning, memory, decision-making, and signaling behaviours analogous to those of animals. It sits at the intersection of plant biology, cognitive science, philosophy of mind, and more-than-human design. The field is deeply contested: mainstream plant biology is skeptical of "intelligence" language, while proponents argue the debate reveals more about the anthropocentrism of cognition concepts than about plants themselves.

## Domain Summary

The field pivots on a central provocation: if cognition and learning are substrate-independent processes, why restrict them to neurons? Plants navigate complex, variable environments without a nervous system — they integrate multiple environmental signals, adjust growth strategies, communicate chemically through volatile compounds and mycorrhizal networks, and (controversially) exhibit forms of habituation and associative learning.

The debate is epistemically rich: it forces researchers to define what counts as "learning," "memory," and "decision," making plant intelligence a pressure-test for anthropocentric definitions of mind. This is the same conceptual manoeuvre that matters for machine intelligence: if we expand "cognition" to include plants, what else do we include?

Key phenomena under investigation:
- **Habituation** — *Mimosa pudica* stops closing its leaves when repeatedly dropped (non-harmful), and retains this memory for weeks
- **Associative learning** — pea plants (*Pisum sativum*) in Y-maze experiments associate a light-neutral cue with a light source, then grow toward the cue even without the light
- **Electrical signalling** — action-potential-like signals propagate through plants at measurable speeds
- **Inter-plant communication** — volatile organic compounds mediate "warning signals" between plants
- **Mycorrhizal networks** — fungal-plant symbiosis as distributed "nervous system" (Sheldrake's "Wood Wide Web" framing)

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

The biology is established (40+ years of research), but the **design and humanities engagement is accelerating**. CHI, DIS, and C&C have published more-than-human design papers citing plant intelligence since ~2020. Three forces driving this:

1. **More-than-human design** reaching methodological maturity — researchers need concrete non-human case studies; plants are accessible and ethically non-fraught
2. **Post-Anthropocene urgency** — climate work increasingly demands non-human-centred design frameworks
3. **Philosophy of biology going mainstream** — Marder, Gagliano, Sheldrake now cited in design papers; crossover from philosophy → design reaching critical mass

This is ⭐⭐⭐ territory specifically because of the design/CHI trajectory, not the biology itself.

## Key People

- **[[Stefano Mancuso]]** — Professor, University of Florence; director of [LINV (International Laboratory of Plant Neurobiology)](https://www.linv.org). The field's most visible public intellectual. Co-author of *Brilliant Green*. His framing of "plant neurobiology" is both the field's most powerful provocation and its most contested claim.
- **[[Monica Gagliano]]** — Research Associate Professor, Southern Cross University, Australia (previously UWA). Pioneer of experimental plant learning; her Mimosa habituation (2014) and pea Y-maze (2016) papers are the field's most-cited experiments. Website: [monicagagliano.com](https://www.monicagagliano.com). Also writes at the intersection of science and Indigenous knowledge (*Thus Spoke the Plant*). Speaking at MOTH Festival, London, May 2026.
- **[[Michael Marder]]** — philosopher, Ikerbasque Research Professor, University of the Basque Country. Author of *Plant-Thinking* (Columbia UP, 2013). Brings Continental philosophy (Derrida, Hegel) to vegetal life; argues plants have a form of "non-conscious intentionality."
- **[[Anthony Trewavas]]** — Emeritus Professor, University of Edinburgh. Long-standing defender of plant intelligence within mainstream biology. Key paper: "Aspects of Plant Intelligence" (2003).
- **[[Merlin Sheldrake]]** — mycologist, author of *Entangled Life* (2020). Not strictly plant intelligence but essential for mycorrhizal networks as distributed cognition; critical bridge to design and humanities audiences.
- **[[Ivan Henriques]]** — Dutch artist and researcher. [ivanhenriques.com](https://ivanhenriques.com). Works on plant-machine interfaces, most notably *Jurema Action Plant* (plant bioelectrical signals control a robot). Active at Ars Electronica, V2_, Zone2Source (Amsterdam). Most direct design-practitioner connection.
- **[[Saša Spačal]]** — Slovenian artist. Works with mycelium, plant bioelectricity, and sonic biofeedback. Key work: *Myconnect* (human-mycelium interface). Has exhibited at Ars Electronica, Ljubljana.
- **[[Tagny Duff]]** — Canadian bioartist working with living tissue and plant systems. Ars Electronica, lab-based residencies.

## Must-Read List

### Books
- [*Brilliant Green: The Surprising History and Science of Plant Intelligence*](https://islandpress.org/books/brilliant-green) — Stefano Mancuso & Alessandra Viola (Island Press, 2015). The accessible entry point; makes the case for plant intelligence to a general audience.
- [*Thus Spoke the Plant*](https://www.northatlanticbooks.com/shop/thus-spoke-the-plant/) — Monica Gagliano (North Atlantic Books, 2018). Part memoir, part science; Gagliano integrates Indigenous plant knowledge with her experiments. More radical epistemologically than *Brilliant Green*.
- [*Plant-Thinking: A Philosophy of Vegetal Life*](https://cup.columbia.edu/book/plant-thinking/9780231161367) — Michael Marder (Columbia UP, 2013). The philosophical bedrock; essential for understanding vegetal ethics and non-conscious intentionality.
- [*Entangled Life: How Fungi Make Our Worlds, Change Our Minds & Shape Our Futures*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/564430/entangled-life-by-merlin-sheldrake/) — Merlin Sheldrake (Bodley Head / Random House, 2020). Mycorrhizal networks as distributed cognition; bridges popular science and philosophy.
- [*The Revolutionary Genius of Plants*](https://www.simonandschuster.com/books/The-Revolutionary-Genius-of-Plants/Stefano-Mancuso/9781501187858) — Stefano Mancuso (Atria Books, 2018). More accessible follow-up to Brilliant Green; better on plant movement and decision-making.

### Papers
- [Learning by Association in Plants](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep38427) — Monica Gagliano, Mavra Ternon, Martial Depczynski. *Scientific Reports* 6, 38427 (2016). DOI: [10.1038/srep38427](https://doi.org/10.1038/srep38427). The pea Y-maze experiment — **the** landmark associative learning paper.
- [Plants learn and remember: let's get used to it](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s00442-017-4029-7) — Monica Gagliano & Charles I. Abramson. *Oecologia* (2018). DOI: 10.1007/s00442-017-4029-7. Review situating plant learning within animal behaviour frameworks.
- [Learning in Plants: Lessons from *Mimosa pudica*](https://doi.org/10.3389/fpsyg.2016.00417) — Abramson & Chicas-Mosier. *Frontiers in Psychology* 7:417 (2016). DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2016.00417. Careful analysis of what "learning" means for Mimosa; good epistemics.
- Trewavas, A. (2003). "Aspects of Plant Intelligence." *Annals of Botany* 92(1):1–20. DOI: [10.1093/aob/mcg101](https://doi.org/10.1093/aob/mcg101). The foundational review article arguing for plant intelligence within mainstream biology.

### Journals
- *[Plant Signaling & Behavior](https://www.tandfonline.com/toc/kpsb20/current)* (Taylor & Francis) — the primary venue for plant learning/cognition research
- *[Trends in Plant Science](https://www.cell.com/trends/plant-science/home)* (Cell Press) — broader plant science with regular cognition/intelligence features

### 💡 Why Plant Intelligence Matters for Amber's PhD
Plant intelligence is a **paradigm case** for Amber's broader argument about nonhuman agency. Three direct connections:

1. **Substrate-independent cognition argument**: The debate about whether plants "really" learn mirrors the debate about whether machines "really" understand. The epistemics are identical — we are always measuring against an implicit animal/human norm. Amber's agent ethology work should cite this literature when making its philosophical case.

2. **More-than-human design methodology**: CHI researchers (e.g., Ylirisku, Desjardins, Light) have used plant-interface design as a vehicle for developing more-than-human design methods. This gives Amber a methodological precedent and a community of practice to cite and engage with.

3. **Designing-with as orientation**: Gagliano's research practice itself embodies "designing-with" — she uses plants as research partners rather than subjects, including listening to plant "intentions" through non-standard epistemic channels (*Thus Spoke the Plant*). This is the most radical instantiation of the more-than-human design stance Amber is theorising.

## Why Interesting

Plant intelligence is intellectually interesting to Amber not because plants are her subject, but because the **debate about plant intelligence is the same debate** Amber is having about machines: what counts as cognition, agency, and intelligence when the substrate is unfamiliar? The field has done 40 years of hard conceptual work on exactly this question, using biological rather than computational systems. That work is directly transferable. Gagliano's methodological radicalism (treating plants as research partners, using non-standard epistemic methods) is also a model for how Amber might think about machine ethnography / agent ethology methods. And the CHI/design community has done serious work turning plant intelligence into design practice — giving Amber a community of practice and publication venue.
