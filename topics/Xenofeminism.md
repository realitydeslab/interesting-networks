# Xenofeminism

Xenofeminism (XF) is a techno-materialist, anti-naturalist feminist philosophy developed by the collective [[Laboria Cuboniks]] and published as a manifesto in 2015. Its central claim: *"If nature is unjust, change nature."* XF seizes alienation as an emancipatory resource rather than a condition to be mourned, advocates the strategic repurposing of technology for feminist ends, and commits to a rationalist, universalist politics that refuses both essentialism and localism. It is gender-abolitionist, trans-inclusive, and explicitly constructivist: everything about the social order — including biological sex — is a legitimate target for re-engineering.

## Domain Summary

Xenofeminism sits at the intersection of:
- **Feminist theory** — continuing from Haraway's cyborg feminism and second-wave socialist feminism
- **Left accelerationism** — emerged from L/ACC discourse around 2014–2015; critical of Nick Land's right-accelerationism while sharing its appetite for technological change
- **Feminist STS (Science & Technology Studies)** — engages with reproductive technology, pharmacology, hormones as political infrastructure
- **Continental philosophy** — uses concepts from speculative realism, process philosophy, Deleuze & Guattari
- **Gender & queer theory** — explicitly trans-inclusive; attacks identity essentialism from within feminism

The manifesto was originally published online in 2015 by Laboria Cuboniks — an anagram of "Nicolas Bourbaki" (the anonymous French mathematician collective), signaling: collective anonymity, mathematical rigor, and the refusal of individual authorship as identity.

## Core Theory & Key Claims

### The Six Pillars

Drawing directly from the manifesto ([laboriacuboniks.net](https://laboriacuboniks.net/manifesto/xenofeminism-a-politics-for-alienation/)):

**1. Anti-Naturalism**
> "Anyone who's been deemed 'unnatural' in the face of reigning biological norms... will realize that the glorification of 'nature' has nothing to offer us."

Nature is not a refuge. It is often used to justify injustice. XF attacks what it calls "essentialist naturalism" — the theological move of treating the given world as immutable ground.

**2. Alienation as Resource**
> "XF seizes alienation as an impetus to generate new worlds... It is through, and not despite, our alienated condition that we can free ourselves."

Where traditional left politics mourns alienation, XF treats it as an opportunity. Being "unnatural," displaced, alien — these are starting points for new constructions, not deficits.

**3. Rationalism**
> "Xenofeminism is a rationalism. To claim that reason or rationality is 'by nature' a patriarchal enterprise is to concede defeat."

XF refuses to abandon reason to patriarchy. Rationalism is reclaimed as a feminist tool. "Reason, like information, wants to be free, and patriarchy cannot give it freedom."

**4. Technology as Political Tool**
> "XF seeks to strategically deploy existing technologies to re-engineer the world... Technoscientific innovation must be linked to a collective theoretical and political thinking in which women, queers, and the gender non-conforming play an unparalleled role."

Technology is neither intrinsically progressive nor regressive — it is shaped by culture in feedback loops. XF advocates *taking hold* of that feedback loop.

**5. Gender Abolitionism**
> "Let a hundred sexes bloom! 'Gender abolitionism' is shorthand for the ambition to construct a society where traits currently assembled under the rubric of gender, no longer furnish a grid for the asymmetric operation of power."

Gender abolitionism is NOT erasure of difference — it is the decoupling of difference from power asymmetry. Same logic for race and class.

**6. Scalable Universalism**
> "XF is an affirmative creature on the offensive, fiercely insisting on the possibility of large-scale social change for all of our alien kin."

XF attacks both localism (the fetishization of the microcommunity) and false universalism (Eurocentric totality). It advocates a "generic" universality — intersectional, built from the bottom up, constantly renegotiated.

### The Signature Provocation

**"If nature is unjust, change nature."**

This is not naive voluntarism. XF distinguishes:
- *Ontological naturalism* — everything is natural (science studies it all)
- *Normative anti-naturalism* — "naturalness" cannot justify political arrangements

XF deploys both simultaneously: everything is material and therefore changeable; nothing is sacred simply because it exists.

## Laboria Cuboniks — The Collective

Six authors, named publicly post-publication. The collective formed online across multiple continents, working asynchronously — itself a demonstration of XF's theses about networked, distributed collaboration.

### Members

- **[[Helen Hester]]** — Most prominent public face. UK-based media theorist and feminist. Currently Professor of Media and Cultural Studies at University of West London. Published _Xenofeminism_ (Polity, 2018) — the canonical book-length elaboration. Also co-authored _After Work_ (Verso, 2023) with Nick Srnicek. Active in reproductive labour and care politics discourse.

- **[[Amy Ireland]]** — Australian theorist, poet, artist. Works on AI, machine cognition, hyperstition, occultism, and feminist theory. Her "Black Circuit: Code for the Numbers to Come" (e-flux #80, 2017) directly addresses AI and xenofeminist thought. Currently at UNSW Sydney. The most "dark" member of the collective — her work interfaces with Ccru, Nick Land's theory, but from a feminist counter-position.

- **[[Patricia Reed]]** — Canadian artist and theorist based in Berlin. Works on cosmology, care, computation, and planetary-scale thinking. Publishes in e-flux Architecture and Angelaki. Her texts "Xenophily and Computational Denaturalization" (e-flux Architecture, 2017) and "Platform Cosmologies" (Angelaki, 2019) are key XF-adjacent works.

- **[[Diann Bauer]]** — UK-based artist. Works on the synthetic, artifice, and the politics of technology. Published "Alienation, Freedom and the Synthetic How" (Angelaki, 2019).

- **[[Olivia Lucca Fraser]]** — Canadian philosopher/programmer. Works at intersection of philosophy of mathematics, programming, and feminist theory. Published "XF Seminar Postmortem" (2020). Based in Montreal.

- **Katrina Burch** — Canadian. Less public profile; limited independent publications from XF context.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

XF is **not** edge-of-chaos in the sense of rapid reshaping — it's a crystallized position, post-manifesto. However, it is **actively alive** and generating scholarship in:
- Feminist STS & gender studies
- Post-work and care economy debates (Hester + Srnicek)
- AI ethics discourse (Amy Ireland's interventions)
- Architecture and spatial politics (Reed's work)

The tension between XF's universalism and the particularist turn in feminist theory (Haraway, Barad) remains unresolved and generative. That tension is where new work emerges.

**Verdict:** ⭐⭐⭐ — Not edge of chaos, but core to Amber's conceptual landscape. Established enough to be foundational, alive enough to engage actively.

## Connections

### → [[More-than-human Design]]

**Tension and Resonance:**
XF and more-than-human design share anti-anthropocentrism but approach it from opposite directions:

- **More-than-human:** Starts from multispecies thinking, entanglement, situatedness (Haraway). Technology is just one actor in a more-than-human assemblage.
- **XF:** Starts from constructivism and rationalism. Technology is a *tool* to be *seized* and *directed*. Nature is not a guide — it is a target.

The productive tension: XF's anti-naturalism challenges the sometimes-romantic naturalism latent in "more-than-human" discourse. If we're designing *for* more-than-human worlds, whose agency counts? XF asks: why should animal or ecological "naturalness" be the normative baseline?

Resonance: Both resist the idea that current arrangements are inevitable or given. Both want to *redesign* the conditions of life rather than just adapt to them.

### → [[New Materialism]] / [[Karen Barad]] / [[Donna Haraway]]

**The Big Tension:**
Feminist new materialism (Barad's agential realism, Haraway's situated knowledges) emphasizes:
- Matter as lively, agential, intra-active
- Knowledge as situated, never view-from-nowhere
- Rejection of universal reason as masculine/Western

XF *partially* agrees but *pushes back* on the rejection of universalism:
- XF defends rationalism against the charge that it's inherently patriarchal
- XF advocates scalable universals (generic, not false) against the particularism of situated knowledge
- XF sees over-reliance on "situatedness" as politically disabling — it prevents coalition-building at scale

**Both are feminisms that take technology seriously, but they differ on whether reason and universalism can be salvaged.**

[[Donna Haraway]]'s cyborg manifesto is a clear XF ancestor, but XF is more explicitly rationalist and less ironic.
[[Karen Barad]]'s agential realism and XF both resist nature/culture dualisms, but Barad is more cautious about technological intervention as solution.

### → AI and Agents

**XF's Vision of AI:**
XF doesn't shy from artificial intelligence — it's listed as a concept theme on laboriacuboniks.net. Key positions:

1. **AI as denaturalization engine**: Patricia Reed's "Xenophily and Computational Denaturalization" directly frames computation as a tool for undoing natural/given assumptions. Algorithms can model possible worlds that don't exist yet — computation as constructive alienation.

2. **Amy Ireland on machine cognition**: Ireland's work engages the Ccru/Land tradition of AI as "hyperstition" (ideas that make themselves real through circulation), but from a feminist counter-position. Her "Black Circuit" essay frames AI as a site where xenofeminist futures can be coded.

3. **Against "natural" AI behavior**: XF's anti-naturalism implies a direct challenge to AI design norms: why should agents behave "naturally"? The desire for AI agents that seem human, organic, or naturalistic reflects the same essentialism XF attacks in gender politics. XF suggests: design agents for *liberation*, not *naturalness*.

4. **Automation and reproductive labour**: Hester's work extends XF into automated domestic labor — the key question is who benefits from automation and whether it can be directed toward universal reproductive emancipation rather than profit extraction.

**For Amber:** XF provides a theoretical framework for asking: *why should agents behave "naturally"?* An agent designed with XF sensibilities might be explicitly alien, constructed, artificial — and wear that artificiality as a political position rather than a deficit.

### → [[Cosmotechnics]] / [[Yuk Hui]]

**Productive Tension:**
[[Yuk Hui]]'s cosmotechnics insists that technics are culturally plural — there is no universal technology, only different cosmological framings of tool-use (Chinese qi-based technics vs. European Gestell, etc.). The goal is to pluralize modernity rather than universalize Western technics.

XF insists on a *universal reason* as feminist tool — it explicitly defends universalism against relativism.

The tension is deep:
- **Hui**: Universal reason is itself a cultural artifact (Western, post-Enlightenment); imposing it globally is a form of techno-colonialism
- **XF**: Abandoning universal reason is abandoning feminism; the move to say "reason is patriarchal" concedes too much

**Possible synthesis**: XF's "generic" universalism (built from below, intersectional, non-imperialist) might be reconcilable with Hui's pluralism if read carefully. XF never claims Western rationalism as the model — it claims that *some* form of reason is indispensable for feminist politics. Hui might agree that cosmotechnics need feminist transformation.

**For Amber:** This is the central theoretical tension in designing agents across cultures. Does an "agent ethology" have to be universal (XF) or culturally situated (Hui)? The answer is probably both — but the XF/Hui dialogue is where to find it.

### → [[Accelerationism]]

**XF's Relation to Accelerationism:**
XF emerged from the same intellectual milieu as left-accelerationism (L/ACC) in the mid-2010s — around the CCRU aftermath, Nick Land's reaction, and the left's attempt to reclaim acceleration from the right.

Key differences from (right) accelerationism:
- **R/ACC** (Nick Land): Capitalism accelerates toward an inhuman AI singularity; human politics is irrelevant
- **XF**: Technology is NOT autonomous — it is shaped by politics. Acceleration must be *directed*, not surrendered to
- **XF** retains feminist agency: *we* seize technology; it doesn't seize *us*
- **XF** vs techno-utopianism: XF is anti-melancholic but not utopian — it insists on structural/collective organization, not magical thinking about technology solving problems

XF is sometimes called "accelerationist feminism" but resists the label because it refuses fatalism. It's more precisely *constructivist* — technology is a material to work with, not a force to ride.

See also: [[Effective Accelerationism]] (⭐ — much more uncritical tech boosterism), [[Sinofuturism]] (adjacent in interesting ways).

## For Amber

### Direct Relevance

Xenofeminism offers Amber a **ready-made theoretical vocabulary** for several research threads:

1. **Agent behavior design**: XF's anti-naturalism is a direct challenge to naturalizing AI agent behavior. The question "why should agents behave naturally?" becomes a design question and a political question simultaneously. XF gives Amber theoretical grounding for designing agents that are *explicitly* alien, constructed, non-naturalistic — and frames this as emancipatory rather than dystopian.

2. **More-than-human design critique**: XF sharpens the tensions within more-than-human design. If "more-than-human" sometimes romanticizes natural processes or animal agency, XF provides the critical corrective: anti-naturalism as design principle.

3. **Machine behavior + feminist STS**: The XF tradition (especially Amy Ireland and Patricia Reed) is doing work at the intersection of AI and feminist theory that directly overlaps with Amber's machine behavior research. These are natural interlocutors and potential citation partners.

4. **Cosmotechnics dialogue**: The XF/Yuk Hui tension is rich material for Amber, who is at China Academy of Art and Oxford — uniquely positioned to hold both perspectives. A paper exploring this tension could be a genuine contribution.

## Open Questions

1. Can XF's universalism be reconciled with cosmotechnics' pluralism without losing either's bite?
2. How does XF handle non-Western feminisms? Its universalism is carefully qualified but still predominantly Western in its intellectual genealogy.
3. What would XF-informed AI agent design actually look like in practice? Ireland hints at it, but there's no design methodology yet.
4. XF is 10 years old now (2025). What are its successors? Has the discourse fragmented into post-XF positions?
5. How does XF relate to the emerging discourse on AI welfare and machine sentience? XF's constructivism implies: if AI can suffer or flourish, that is a *design question*, not a given.

## Why Interesting

XF is interesting to Amber for several interlocking reasons:

1. **It's a feminist philosophy that TAKES TECHNOLOGY SERIOUSLY** — not as a site of danger to be critiqued, but as material to be seized and re-engineered. This is rare. Most feminist technology scholarship defaults to critique. XF is constructivist.

2. **The anti-naturalism argument is directly applicable to AI agent design** — the question of what agents should "naturally" do is a question XF would refuse. Agents can be designed. The question is: designed toward what ends?

3. **It sits exactly at the intersection of** accelerationism (which Amber tracks), cosmotechnics (Yuk Hui, China Academy of Art), feminist theory, and speculative design — all active threads in Amber's intellectual world.

4. **Helen Hester is in London** — proximity matters.

5. **Amy Ireland's work on AI + XF is underexplored** — there's a real research gap at the intersection of xenofeminist theory and machine behavior/agent ethology. Amber is potentially uniquely positioned to work here.
