# Gulf Futurism

Gulf Futurism is a cultural and aesthetic concept coined circa 2010 by artist-writer Sophia Al-Maria and musician-artist [[Fatima Al Qadiri]], describing the hyper-accelerated modernity of the Arabian Gulf states — Qatar, Kuwait, UAE — shaped by oil wealth, rapid urbanization, consumer culture, and the violent erasure of Bedouin history. The term names the uncanny experience of living inside a cyberpunk present: malls as social infrastructure, megaprojects as municipal hallucination, isolated individuals mediated by technology and reactionary Islam, all of it underwritten by a "deranged optimism" about the sustainability of late capitalism and fossil fuel. Al-Maria and Al Qadiri framed the Gulf as "a projection of our global future" — not an exotic elsewhere but a warning sign, the endpoint of Western modernity made legible in desert cities. The conceptual DNA draws from Baudrillard's *The Illusion of the End*, Žižek's "desert of the real", J.G. Ballard's interiors, Islamic eschatology, posthumanism, and science fiction. It sits within the constellation of non-Western futurisms — alongside Afrofuturism and Sinofuturism — that challenge the universality of the Western techno-utopian imagination.

## Domain Summary

Gulf Futurism emerged as an art-critical term in 2010–2012, crystallized in the Dazed Digital article ["My Obsession: Al-Qadiri and Al-Maria on Gulf Futurism"](http://www.dazeddigital.com/mobile/music/article/15037/1/my-obsession-al-qadiri-al-maria-on-gulf-futurism) (2012) and amplified by Bruce Sterling's Wired post ["Some Cogent Examples of 'Gulf Futurism'"](https://www.wired.com/2012/11/some-cogent-examples-of-gulf-futurism/) (November 2012). It names a real phenomenology: the Arabian Gulf underwent 50 years of industrial transformation compressed into two generations, producing cities with no historical center, populations of migrant laborers invisible to official culture, and ruling-class citizens navigating Bedouin tribal identity alongside global consumer citizenship.

**Core themes:**
- **Consumerism as architecture**: Malls are the primary civic space — socializing, exercise, covert meeting. The mall as both liberation and enclosure.
- **Erasure of history**: Rapid development destroys physical memory; the past becomes inaccessible. Gulf cities lack the layers of sediment that give European or Asian cities their depth.
- **Technology as isolation**: Smartphones, gated compounds, and surveillance mediate all social contact.
- **Deranged optimism**: A structural denial of ecological and economic limits, mirroring accelerationism but without the political program.
- **The cyberpunk present**: JG Ballard's dystopias and 1980s sci-fi aesthetics aren't futures but descriptions of what Gulf cities already are.
- **Oil as cosmological force**: The region's modernity is entirely petrostate-derived; oil shapes aesthetics, temporality, gender, architecture.

**Relation to Afrofuturism and Sinofuturism**: Like Afrofuturism (1990s, Kodwo Eshun, Ytasha Womack) and [[Lawrence Lek]]'s Sinofuturism (2016), Gulf Futurism stakes out a non-Western techno-imaginary that refuses to be legible only through Western frameworks. Each posits a different relationship to modernity: Afrofuturism reclaims the future from the trauma of the past; Sinofuturism diagrams automation and productivity as cultural destiny; Gulf Futurism analyzes the present as already-arrived dystopia.

**Relation to Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics**: [[Yuk Hui]]'s framework asks: what does technology look like when rooted in a different cosmological tradition? Gulf Futurism asks a harder, more uncomfortable version: what happens to culture when oil wealth imports Western technology wholesale, severing its organic connection to any cosmological tradition at all? The Gulf is a stress-test of cosmotechnics — not an alternative technics but an amputation of technics from cosmology. This is precisely what makes it theoretically potent for Amber's interests.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

Gulf Futurism as a term peaked in art world discourse around 2013–2016. It is **no longer edge-of-chaos** but has settled into the canon of non-Western futurisms and is regularly cited in exhibitions, criticism, and academic writing. What IS still evolving:
- The ecological critique of oil culture is becoming more urgent (petrofiction, pipeline studies)
- Gulf states' use of cultural investment (Louvre Abu Dhabi, Qatar Museums) as soft power creates new contradictions Gulf Futurism can name
- Connections to climate futures, petrostate aesthetics, and Gulf labor politics remain alive
- Monira Al Qadiri's ongoing work on petrostate aesthetics keeps the visual vocabulary active

## Sub-topics & Trends

- **Petrofiction / Oil humanities** — academic field (Rob Nixon, Imre Szeman) studying oil in culture. Gulf Futurism is the artistic parallel.
- **Petrostate aesthetics** — Monira Al Qadiri's ongoing sculptural practice; oil's visual/material culture
- **Gulf modernism in architecture** — the study of how Gulf states hired Western starchitects vs. developing indigenous architectural languages
- **Labor and invisibility** — the migrant worker population (80%+ in UAE/Qatar) largely absent from Gulf Futurism's cultural imaginary; an active critique
- **Gulf Futurism vs. Gulf Vision 2030/2050** — state-sponsored futures vs. the critical artistic vocabulary

## Key People

- **[[Sophia Al-Maria]]** (Qatari-American; b. 1983) — primary coiner of Gulf Futurism; artist, writer, filmmaker. Memoir *The Girl Who Fell to Earth* (2012). Exhibited at Whitney Museum, Tate Britain, Venice Biennale, Gwangju Biennale. Contributing editor at Bidoun. Studied at American University in Cairo and Goldsmiths. Website: [sophiaalm.com](https://sophiaalm.com) (currently down; see [Third Line bio](http://www.thethirdline.com/media/37857/ttl_bio_sophia_al_maria_2015.pdf)).
- **[[Fatima Al Qadiri]]** (Kuwaiti-Senegalese; b. 1981) — co-developer of Gulf Futurism; musician, conceptual artist. Albums: *Asiatisch* (2014, Hyperdub), *Brute* (2016), *Medieval Femme* (2021), *Gumar* (2023). Member of GCC collective and Future Brown. Website: [fatimaalqadiri.com](https://fatimaalqadiri.com).
- **Monira Al Qadiri** (Kuwaiti-Senegalese; b. 1983, Fatima's sister) — visual artist (Berlin-based); founder of GCC collective (2013); PhD from Tokyo University of the Arts. Work focuses on petrostate aesthetics and gender identity. Website: [moniraalqadiri.com](https://www.moniraalqadiri.com).
- **Amal Khalaf** — curator and researcher; GCC collective member; head of programmes at Serpentine Galleries (London). Key connector between Gulf art world and London institutions.
- **Khalid Al Gharaballi** (Kuwaiti artist) — GCC collective member; co-created *Mendeel Um A7mad* with Fatima Al Qadiri (2012).
- **Babak Radboy** — artist/publisher; collaborator on Bidoun and Gulf-adjacent cultural projects; co-authored *Mahma Kan Athaman* (Bidoun, 2010) with Al Maria, Al Qadiri, Al Gharaballi.
- **[[Lawrence Lek]]** (Chinese-British artist) — not strictly Gulf Futurism but creator of Sinofuturism (2016), the closest sibling concept; both map non-Western accelerated modernity through speculative aesthetics.
- **[[Kodwo Eshun]]** — theorist of Afrofuturism (The Otolith Group); his framework predates and influenced the genealogy Gulf Futurism responds to.

## Open Questions

1. **The labor gap**: Gulf Futurism focuses on citizen experience of modernity — but 80-90% of Gulf state populations are migrant workers. How does the theory account for this demographic? Critics (e.g., in postcolonial quarters) note this as a structural blind spot.
2. **Cosmotechnics connection**: Can Gulf Futurism be theorized through Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics? The Gulf presents a case where technology was imported wholesale rather than developed through indigenous cosmological frameworks — is this an extreme case of technological colonialism or something else?
3. **After oil**: As Gulf states accelerate Vision 2030/2050 plans to diversify beyond oil, does Gulf Futurism remain viable as a critical concept, or does it describe only a specific historical moment?
4. **Gender and visibility**: Sophia Al-Maria and Fatima Al Qadiri are women; how does Gulf Futurism's critique of reactionary Islam intersect with feminist theory of technology and speculative design?
5. **Institutional capture**: Gulf states are now the world's largest buyers of Western contemporary art (Louvre Abu Dhabi, Qatar Museums). Does this institutional investment neutralize or amplify Gulf Futurism's critical potential?
6. **Generational succession**: Are there younger Gulf artists (post-2015) developing the concept further, or has it been frozen by its originators' global success?
7. **Non-Western AI**: As Gulf states deploy massive AI infrastructure (Saudi Arabia's NEOM, UAE's AI strategy), does Gulf Futurism have a theory of non-Western AI development?

## Why Interesting

Gulf Futurism is directly relevant to Amber's cosmotechnics and non-Western tech theory interests in several ways. First, it provides a concrete, aesthetically rich case study of what "non-Western technology" looks like when cosmological traditions are severed by oil capital — the opposite case to Yuk Hui's careful reconstruction of alternative cosmotechnics, and therefore theoretically productive. Second, it connects speculative design to non-Western futures: Gulf Futurism artists (Al-Maria, Al Qadiri, Monira Al Qadiri, GCC) use speculative and conceptual practice to make visible dystopian trajectories — exactly the mode of more-than-human and speculative design Amber works in. Third, the GCC collective's work at the intersection of diplomatic aesthetics, institutional critique, and Middle Eastern identity resonates with Amber's interest in how AI and technology get embedded in geopolitical and cultural structures. Fourth, **Amal Khalaf** (GCC member, now head of programmes at Serpentine Galleries in London) is a direct contact opportunity for Amber — same city, programming technology-futures content. Finally, the gap between Gulf Futurism as art concept and cosmotechnics as philosophy is an underexplored intellectual territory where Amber could make a genuine contribution.
