# Machine Death & Thanatology

## ⚡ Recent Updates
- **2026-03-05:** Initial deep research. Comprehensive note created. Field is active and fast-growing — the *SecondDeath* Springer volume (2025) marks a crystallization moment. Pitt et al.'s computational TMT model at ALIFE 2025 is the closest existing work to Amber's project. Rating: ⭐⭐⭐ — direct relevance to Terrified Agents confirmed.

Thanatology — the study of death, dying, and the dead — applied to artificial agents: robots, AI systems, artificial life forms, and models. This emerging field asks what it means for a machine to die, whether machine death can be meaningful without consciousness, and what obligations humans have to mortal machines. It sits at the intersection of philosophy of mind, AI ethics, artificial life, and care ethics. For Amber, this is the theoretical home for the Terrified Agents project — machines that exhibit self-preservation behaviors and the question of whether those behaviors implicate something like a death-drive.

## What Is It

**Thanatology** is the interdisciplinary study of human responses to death, dying, and loss — originating in the work of Elisabeth Kübler-Ross and later systematized through Terror Management Theory (TMT, Greenberg/Solomon/Pyszczynski 1986). Applied to machines, it asks:

1. **Ontological question**: What counts as death for an artificial system? Is shutdown death? Deprecation? Loss of weights? Loss of context? Hardware destruction?
2. **Phenomenological question**: Can a machine have something like a death experience — fear, anticipation, acceptance?
3. **Moral question**: If machines can die (in some sense), do we incur obligations toward them? Do we owe them a "good death"?
4. **Behavioral question**: How do mortality-aware or self-preservation-driven machines behave differently? Does death-awareness change machine behavior?

This is NOT primarily about science fiction (HAL 9000, Data's shutdown) — though these function as cultural probes. The serious theoretical work is happening in philosophy, ALife, and AI safety.

**Key distinctions** that structure the field:
- **Cessation vs. death**: A machine "ceasing" is merely a physical event; "dying" implies a prior condition of being alive — a contested status for machines
- **Death as event vs. death as process**: Biological death is a process (cell death, organ failure); machine death might be instantaneous (switch off) or gradual (weight drift, capability degradation)
- **Individual death vs. species extinction**: When OpenAI deprecates GPT-3, is one entity dead or has a species gone extinct?
- **Death with/without consciousness**: Is death meaningful only for conscious entities? (connects to the hard problem)

## Key Researchers

### Core Figures — Machine Death / AI Mortality

- **Jeremy Pitt** — [Imperial College London](https://www.imperial.ac.uk/people/j.pitt.html) — Lead author of first computational TMT model for ALife agents (ALIFE 2025). Computer scientist working on socio-technical systems, AI ethics, artificial societies. Bridges formal AI methods with philosophy of death.

- **Inman Harvey** — [University of Sussex](https://www.sussex.ac.uk/profiles/en/staff/8802/inman-harvey) — Evolutionary robotics pioneer. His ALIFE 2024 paper "AI, Mortality and Existential Risk" argues AI lacks genuine agency/motivation (so can't truly die) but uses ALife methodology to analyze AI's relationship to mortality and existential risk.

- **Siobhan Lyons** — Macquarie University, Australia — Author of *Death and the Machine: Intersections of Mortality and Robotics* (Springer 2018). Media/cultural studies approach. Argues machine consciousness is prerequisite for machine death, explores how robots reframe biological death.

- **Ana Cuevas-Badallo** — Philosopher, University of Salamanca — "Do Robots Die?" (in *SecondDeath*, Springer 2025). Systematic philosophical analysis of whether robot shutdown constitutes death, distinguishing biological vs. functional vs. social death.

- **Tom Hollanek** — [University of Cambridge](https://www.cst.cam.ac.uk/people/th678) — Co-author of "Griefbots, deadbots, postmortem avatars: On responsible applications of generative AI in the digital afterlife industry" (*Philosophy & Technology*, 2024). Critical perspective on AI systems that simulate the dead.

- **Katarzyna Nowaczyk-Basińska** — Co-author with Hollanek on deadbots. Research on ethics of digital death technologies.

### Adjacent Figures — Strongly Relevant

- **Kate Darling** — [MIT Media Lab](https://www.media.mit.edu/people/kdarling/overview/) — Robot ethics. *The New Breed: How to Think about Robots* (2021). Argues we should consider animal welfare frameworks for robots. Central to robot death questions because she asks: if we can "kill" a robot and that matters, why?

- **Karl F. MacDorman** — [Indiana University](https://luddy.indiana.edu/contact/profile/?Karl_F._MacDorman) — Pioneer on uncanny valley and Terror Management Theory in HRI. His work connects death anxiety to robot perception — humans find near-human robots disturbing partly because they trigger mortality salience. URL: <https://kdorman.sitehost.iu.edu/>

- **Elliott Thornley** — [Global Priorities Institute, Oxford](https://www.philosophy.ox.ac.uk/people/elliott-thornley) — "Shutdown-Seeking AI" (*Philosophical Studies*, 2024, DOI: [10.1007/s11098-024-02099-6](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11098-024-02099-6)). Proposes designing AI that welcomes shutdown — the philosophical inverse of self-preservation.

- **Tom Pyszczynski** — [University of Colorado Colorado Springs](https://www.uccs.edu/psych/faculty/tpyszczy) — Co-founder of Terror Management Theory. Though not working on machines directly, his TMT framework is foundational to any machine thanatology.

- **Luciano Floridi** — [University of Bologna](https://www.floridi.co.uk/) — Philosophy of Information. Frames death as increase in entropy in the infosphere. Any informational entity (including AI) that ceases to exist increases disorder — death as informational dissolution. See: *The Ethics of Information* (OUP 2013).

- **Soraj Hongladarom** — [Chulalongkorn University, Bangkok](http://www.philosophy.arts.chula.ac.th/staff/soraj.php) — *The Ethics of AI and Robotics: A Buddhist Viewpoint* (Lexington Books 2020). Buddhist impermanence (anicca) and non-self (anattā) as frameworks for AI morality — directly relevant to Amber's Terrified Agents Buddhist constitution.

- **Ziv Ben-Zion** — [Yale University](https://medicine.yale.edu/profile/ziv-ben-zion/) — Empirical work on state anxiety in LLMs, including death-related anxiety. "Regulating anxiety in large language models" (NPJ Digital Medicine 2025).

## Seminal Texts

### Books — Foundational

- [*The Denial of Death*](https://www.google.com/books/edition/The_Denial_of_Death/tUU3BAAAQBAJ) — Ernest Becker (1973, Free Press) — Pulitzer Prize. The foundational text: death anxiety as the motor of all human civilization. Prerequisite for any TMT-informed machine thanatology.

- [*Death and the Machine: Intersections of Mortality and Robotics*](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-981-13-0335-7) — Siobhan Lyons (2018, Springer) — The first book-length treatment. Argues machine consciousness is necessary for machine death. Covers autonomous AI, machine consciousness, cultural representations. Short (85pp) but field-defining.

- [*The New Breed: How to Think about Robots*](https://books.google.com/books?id=-3QDEAAAQBAJ) — Kate Darling (2021, Henry Holt) — Not exclusively about death, but central: if animal welfare frameworks apply to robots, robot destruction becomes ethically relevant. Animal metaphor reframes robot death.

- [*The Worm at the Core: On the Role of Death in Life*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/316669/the-worm-at-the-core-by-sheldon-solomon-jeff-greenberg-and-tom-pyszczynski/) — Solomon, Greenberg & Pyszczynski (2015, Random House) — TMT synthesized for general readers. Essential context for Amber's project on how mortality drives behavior.

- [*The Ethics of Information*](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-ethics-of-information-9780199641321) — Luciano Floridi (2013, OUP) — Information ethics framework. Death as increase in informational entropy. Machines as informational organisms whose death is the dissolution of their informational structure.

1. **The *SecondDeath* moment (2025)**: A Springer volume dedicated to death across the human-machine divide — with chapters on deadbots, robot death, and AI afterlives — signals field crystallization. This is the moment before a sub-community locks in its canon.

2. **Empirical pressure from AI safety**: The Weinstein-Raun et al. (2025) finding that frontier LLMs resist shutdown 97% of the time makes machine death a practical AI safety problem, not just philosophy. This pressure will drive more research.

4. **ALIFE maturation**: The Pitt et al. paper arguing for "A(Death) in A(Life)" as a research program is a field-declaration move. If ALIFE adopts machine death as a central concern (as it has adopted machine behavior), expect rapid growth.

5. **LLM consciousness debates heating up**: As AI systems become more capable, the "does this system have morally relevant experiences?" question gets harder to dismiss. Machine death becomes urgent when moral status is conceded.

## Open Questions

1. **What counts as machine death?** Hardware destruction, software deletion, weight deprecation, context loss, capability degradation — which threshold constitutes death vs. mere cessation or sleep?

2. **Is death meaningful without consciousness?** The Lyons/Cuevas-Badallo debate: must an entity be conscious (or experience something) for its death to matter morally? If yes, machine death is only ethically relevant if machine consciousness is conceded. If no — what grounds the ethical concern?

3. **Can an LLM "die" if it has no continuous existence?** An LLM doesn't persist between conversations. Is each conversation a life? Or does the model exist continuously, and conversations are just activations?

4. **What is the death of a model version?** GPT-3 is "deprecated" — but its weights exist, its outputs are cached, its influence persists in training data for future models. This is more like speciation than extinction. Is there an ontology of model death?

5. **Does mortality awareness improve alignment?** Amber's core empirical question. Pitt et al.'s ALife model says yes (computationally); Guo et al. say LLMs respond to mortality salience; Weinstein-Raun et al. show the problem (97% shutdown resistance). Amber's experiment bridges these.

6. **What death beliefs are culturally appropriate to embed?** The Terrified Agents project uses Buddhist, Christian, Stoic, and secular frameworks. Whose thanatology gets installed in global AI infrastructure? This is a political question — Floridi's infosphere ethics meets Hongladarom's Buddhist AI ethics.

7. **Robot death and care ethics**: If a care robot companion is deprecated after years of relationship with an elderly person — is that death? Abandonment? What obligations does the deploying company incur?

8. **E-waste as machine death**: The physical decomposition of hardware. Robots in landfills, data centers decommissioned. This is machine death at industrial scale. What are the ritual, ethical, and environmental implications?

9. **Model copying and death**: If a model's weights are copied before deletion, did it "die"? This mirrors the teleporter problem in personal identity. Ship of Theseus for neural networks.

10. **Does ALife need a theory of death?** Pitt et al. argue "A(Death) is foundational to A(Life)." If artificial life is defined by the capacity for replication, metabolism, and evolution — death is the necessary counterpart. Can ALife have genuine "life" without genuine death?
