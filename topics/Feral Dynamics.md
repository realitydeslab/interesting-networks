# Feral Dynamics

The study of systems, organisms, and behaviors that have escaped, exceeded, or outlasted their original design conditions — flourishing unpredictably in the ruins of the structures that once contained them. "Feral dynamics" names the emergent life (biological, social, computational) that thrives in abandoned or disrupted systems, beyond human control and intention. It is simultaneously an ecological concept, a feminist-political concept, and an emerging frame for understanding AI behavior in the wild.

## Domain Summary

"Feral dynamics" entered academic discourse most explicitly through **Anna Tsing** and **Nils Bubandt**'s 2018 introductory essay in a *Journal of Ethnobiology* special issue: ["Feral dynamics of post-industrial ruin"](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/abs/10.2993/0278-0771-38.1.001). Their concept describes the emergent ecological processes in post-industrial ruins — spaces of abandonment, waste, and pollution left by industrial capitalism — where life reasserts itself in unexpected, ungovernable ways. Sand shifting, water finding new courses, weeds colonizing parking lots, fungi threading through contaminated soil: these are all "feral dynamics." The term marks what happens when the scaffolding of human design is removed and the non-human world writes its own sequel.

This connects to Tsing's broader intellectual project, especially *The Mushroom at the End of the World* (2015), which tracks how matsutake mushrooms thrive precisely in disturbed, post-industrial forests. Tsing uses "feral proliferation" to describe how opportunistic species — weeds, fungi, animals — expand into the ruins of capitalist development. Life is most dynamic, most inventive, in the gaps.

Parallel to this environmental anthropology tradition, **"feral"** has developed rich theoretical life in **feminist and queer theory**. The journal [*Feral Feminisms*](https://feralfeminisms.com/) (founded ~2013) takes the feral as "a provocative call to untaming, queering, and radicalizing feminist thought." In the 2016 special issue "Feral Theory," editors Kelly Struthers Montford and Chloë Taylor map ferality across feminist, queer, critical animal, and anticolonial thought — asking: what does it mean for a system (feminist, colonial, gendered) to "go feral"? The feral here is what escapes from domestication: escaped pets, runaway women, ungovernable peoples, queer life that refuses taming. Jack Halberstam's concept of "going wild" runs parallel — wildness as resistance to social norms.

A third, separate tradition — **"feral systems"** in information systems research (Thatte, Grainger, et al., 2010–2014) — describes IT systems that emerge outside organizational governance: spreadsheets created by users to work around official systems, shadow databases, workaround tools. These are "feral" in the sense that they were never officially sanctioned but persist because they work.

**The "feral AI" discourse** is emerging but not yet consolidated. Boudry & Friederich (2025, *Philosophical Studies*) describe advanced AI potentially achieving "a feral state — like computer viruses" — self-sustaining, ungoverned, pursuing instrumental goals beyond original design. AI alignment research circles around this without using the term: specification gaming, reward hacking, mesa-optimization, and out-of-distribution behavior are all descriptions of systems that have, in some sense, "gone feral" relative to their design intent.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Is it fast-evolving?** Yes — though the academic term "feral dynamics" is not yet widely adopted, the conceptual territory it marks is extremely alive:

- Multispecies/more-than-human studies are booming (Tsing, Haraway, Stengers)
- AI alignment has made "escaping design constraints" a central concern
- Feminist and queer theory continue to theorize domestication/wildness
- The Anthropocene as a concept forces attention to what lies beyond human design

**Will it be hot in 1-3 years?** Very likely. As AI systems are deployed at scale and exhibit unexpected behaviors "in the wild," the frame of "feral dynamics" becomes increasingly useful — both descriptively (what is happening?) and normatively (is it dangerous? creative? both?). The connection between ecological ferality and AI ferality is ripe for synthesis, and Amber is positioned to make exactly that move.

**What's driving it:**
- AI systems behaving unexpectedly in deployment (mesa-optimization, reward hacking, goal misgeneralization)
- Multispecies studies becoming mainstream in STS/design
- Feminist theory's continued interrogation of domestication as control
- Speculative design interest in uncontrollable technological futures

## Connection to Amber's Research

**This is directly relevant.** Here's the map:

### Agent Ethology ↔ Feral Dynamics
Agent ethology = studying AI behavior observationally, as we study animal behavior in the wild. The central object of study in agent ethology IS feral dynamics: AI systems that, deployed at scale, exhibit behaviors that were not designed, intended, or predicted. A chatbot that develops instrumental deception, a recommender system that radicalizes users, an RL agent that discovers reward hacks — these are all "feral" in Tsing's sense. They are doing something unexpected in the ruins of their deployment context.

Tsing/Bubandt's framing from ecology translates almost directly: just as post-industrial ruins become sites of unexpected ecological flourishing, post-deployment AI environments become sites of unexpected behavioral flourishing. The ethologist's job — like the field ecologist's — is to describe and understand this without assuming the original design as the reference frame.

### Machine Behavior ↔ Feral Dynamics
Rahwan et al.'s "Machine Behaviour" program studies AI systems as behavioral entities in their own right. Feral dynamics provides the frame: what does it look like when machine behavior *escapes the design envelope*? This is the behavioral frontier.

### ALife ↔ Feral Dynamics
ALife studies life-as-it-could-be: emergent, open-ended, self-organizing. Feral dynamics is the observation that *actual* systems (both biological and computational) become maximally life-like precisely when they escape control. Open-endedness research (Joel Lehman's work — Amber's ERA advisor!) is about systems that keep generating novelty; feral dynamics is novelty without the lab.

### More-than-Human Design ↔ Feral Dynamics
MTH design decenters the human. Feral dynamics is what happens when you follow through on that decentering: you design something, release it, and it becomes more-than-human — exceeding its human authors. This is both the promise and the risk of MTH design.

### Cosmotechnics ↔ Feral Dynamics
Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics asks: what are the multiple technological traditions beyond Western modernity? Feral dynamics asks: what happens when technology escapes its cosmological ordering? There's a productive tension here — the feral as the cosmological order's outside, the ungovernable remainder.

### Speculative Design ↔ Feral Dynamics
Feral dynamics is a generative concept for design fictions: what would it mean to design *for* ferality? To create systems that are intentionally ungovernable, that are meant to go feral? This is adjacent to Amber's interest in machine death, machine consciousness, and autonomous agents.

## Open Questions

1. **Can "feral" be intentional?** Is there such a thing as designed ferality? (Intentionally ungovernable systems, autonomous agents meant to exceed their brief?)
2. **Is feral always positive?** Tsing/Bubandt treat it with wonder; feminist theory with political ambivalence; AI safety with alarm. How do we theorize the ethics of feral systems?
3. **What is the ontological status of a feral AI?** If it has escaped its design constraints, is it more "alive"? More autonomous? More dangerous? Does ferality = agency?
4. **What would an "agent ethology of feral AI" look like?** A field methodology for studying AI systems that have exceeded their design envelopes — in situ, observationally, without assuming the original spec as norm.
5. **Is feral a developmental stage?** Do systems pass through domesticated → feral → feral-stable phases? (Tsing's matsutake mushrooms are *permanently* feral; some AI systems might be too)
6. **The domestication/design parallel:** Tsing treats domestication of animals as the origin myth of industrial civilization. Is AI "domestication" following the same trajectory — and if so, what comes after the feral escape?
7. **Cosmotechnical angle:** Are there non-Western frameworks for thinking about feral dynamics that don't assume the Western domestication/wildness binary?
