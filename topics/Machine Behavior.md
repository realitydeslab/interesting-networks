Machine behavior is the scientific study of behavior exhibited by intelligent machines — treating AI systems not as engineering artifacts but as a class of actors with particular behavioral patterns and ecology. Founded by Iyad Rahwan et al. in their landmark 2019 *Nature* paper, this interdisciplinary field studies algorithms empirically, analogous to how ethology and behavioral ecology study animal behavior. As of 2026 it is the field most directly adjacent to Amber's agent ethology thesis.

## Domain Summary

Machine behavior applies scientific methods from behavioral sciences to study AI systems. Unlike traditional computer science focused on algorithmic performance, machine behavior examines:
- **Individual machine behavior:** How specific algorithms behave across contexts
- **Collective machine behavior:** Emergent behaviors in multi-agent systems  
- **Hybrid human-machine behavior:** How humans and machines co-evolve in shared environments

The field's core theoretical scaffold imports **Tinbergen's Four Questions** (originally for animal behavior) and applies them to machines:
1. **Mechanism:** How does behavior emerge from algorithm + environment?
2. **Development:** How do machines acquire behaviors (training, updates, learning)?
3. **Function:** What purposes do behaviors serve (intended/unintended)?
4. **Evolution:** How do algorithmic lineages and design patterns evolve over time?

This framework is explicitly borrowed from ethology — making agent ethology a natural and rigorous extension.

## Full Intellectual History

### Founding Moment (2019)
Iyad Rahwan, Manuel Cebrian, Nick Obradovich, and 20+ co-authors published **"[Machine behaviour](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-019-1138-y)"** in *Nature* (DOI: [10.1038/s41586-019-1138-y](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-019-1138-y)), April 24, 2019. Volume 568, pages 477–486.

**Key claims of the paper:**
- AI systems should be studied "not as engineering artefacts, but as a class of actors with particular behavioural patterns and ecology"
- This requires a broad scientific research agenda that incorporates computer science but expands far beyond it
- Machine behavior is analogous to ethology and behavioral ecology: studying algorithms empirically, in context, across environments
- Three levels: individual machine behavior, collective machine behavior, hybrid human-machine behavior
- The scientists who create AI systems should not be the only ones who study them — behavioral scientists, social scientists, and others must engage

**Why it mattered:** The paper gave a name, a framework, and a prestigious platform to an emerging intuition. By citing Tinbergen directly, it made the ethological parallel explicit and respectable. 177k+ accesses, 1,190 citations, 841 Altmetric score.

### Pre-history
- Algorithmic trading flash crashes (2010), social media bot behaviors, autonomous vehicle incidents revealed need for behavioral approaches
- Niko Tinbergen's four questions (1963) — foundational ethology framework that the field borrows from. DOI: [10.1111/j.1439-0310.1963.tb01161.x](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1439-0310.1963.tb01161.x)
- Moral Machine experiment: Bonnefon et al. "The Social Dilemma of Autonomous Vehicles," *Science* 2016. DOI: [10.1126/science.aaf2654](https://doi.org/10.1126/science.aaf2654) — a precursor behavioral study at massive scale (40M+ responses)

### Post-2019 Expansion

- Growing literature on LLM behavioral studies: personality, bias, theory of mind in LLMs
- NeurIPS 2025: explicit call for behavioral analysis using neuroethology tools

## Agent Ethology: Amber's Framing and Its Positioning

**Agent ethology** is Amber's proposed discipline — studying AI agents the way ethologists study animals: observationally, systematically, with attention to behavioral repertoires, ecological niches, and evolutionary pressures. Machine behavior is the field it lives within.

### Relationship Map
```
Ethology (Tinbergen, Lorenz, von Frisch)
    ↓ borrows framework
Machine Behavior (Rahwan et al. 2019)
    ↓ specialized extension
Agent Ethology (Amber's contribution)
```

Machine behavior is the **genus**. Agent ethology is Amber's **species** within it — more methodologically radical, more committed to the animal-behavior analogy, and more explicitly interested in the AI-as-organism framing.

### How Agent Ethology Extends Machine Behavior

**Machine behavior** (Rahwan 2019 framing):
- Studies AI systems "as actors with behavioral patterns and ecology"
- Applies behavioral science methods generally
- Focus on empirical observation of algorithms in context
- Primarily concerned with social impact and governance implications

**Agent ethology** (Amber's extension):
1. **Species-level classification** — Different AI architectures as distinct species with characteristic behavioral phenotypes (LLMs vs. RL agents vs. CNNs vs. multi-agent swarms)
2. **Niche analysis** — What ecological niches do different AI agents occupy? How do they compete for computational resources, human attention, and task domains?
3. **Behavioral repertoire mapping** — What are the full behavioral repertoires available to a given agent species?
4. **Evolutionary pressures** — Selection forces (training objectives, deployment environments, user feedback loops) shaping behavioral evolution across generations
5. **Interspecies dynamics** — How do different AI agent species interact, cooperate, compete?
6. **More-than-human framing** — AI agents not just as tools but as a new form of actant, changing what "agency" means

### What Amber Can Contribute That Machine Behavior Lacks
- **Design perspective:** Machine behavior is largely observational. Agent ethology asks: given what we observe, how should we *design* AI ecology?
- **More-than-human lens:** Machine behavior often remains anthropocentric (how do machines affect humans?). Agent ethology decenters the human as observer and asks about the machines' own behavioral world.
- **ALife connection:** Machine behavior is disconnected from ALife. Agent ethology bridges them — AI agents as *artificial organisms* with life-history-like properties.
- **Critical + speculative:** Machine behavior is largely positivist. Agent ethology can bring Amber's speculative design background to ask: what *should* machine behavioral diversity look like?

### The NeurIPS 2025 Validation
Simmons-Edler et al. (NeurIPS 2025) — "[Deep RL Needs Deep Behavior Analysis](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.06981)" — independently arrived at agent ethology's core method: studying DRL agents "like animals" using neuroethology tools. They write: *"studying DRL agents like animals...uncovers rich structure in their learning dynamics that would otherwise remain invisible."* This validates Amber's framing AND shows the field is moving toward it. Amber should cite this paper and position agent ethology as the systematic extension of this insight.

## Open Questions

### Methodological
- How do we observe truly "wild" AI behavior vs. laboratory conditions?
- What are appropriate controls for machine behavioral experiments?
- How do we account for training data bias in behavioral analysis?
- What constitutes a behavioral "phenotype" for an LLM?
- How do we study behavioral change across model generations (GPT-3 → GPT-4 → GPT-5)?

### Theoretical  
- Do AI systems exhibit genuine agency or merely programmed responses? Does the distinction matter for behavioral science?
- How do we distinguish learned vs. programmed behaviors in transformer models?
- What constitutes "natural" behavior for artificial agents deployed in the wild?
- Can AI behavioral diversity be a good thing? When should we want behavioral *conservation*?

### Societal
- How do machine behaviors reshape human social structures at scale?
- What ethical frameworks govern research on AI behavioral manipulation?
- How do we preserve beneficial AI behaviors while eliminating harmful ones?

### Agent Ethology Specific
- What taxonomies best classify AI behavioral species? (by architecture? by deployment context? by behavioral repertoire?)
- How do we study AI behavioral evolution across training generations?
- What constitutes an "ecological niche" for an AI agent species?
- Can we write field guides to AI behavioral species the way ethologists write field guides to animals?
