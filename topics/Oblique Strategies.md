# Oblique Strategies

A deck of ~100–150 oracle cards co-created by [[Brian Eno]] and [[Peter Schmidt]] in 1975, subtitled *"Over One Hundred Worthwhile Dilemmas."* Each card carries an oblique aphorism or instruction designed to help artists break through creative blocks by forcing lateral thinking. One of the most influential creative process tools of the 20th century and a pioneering example of **constraint-based creativity as a designed system**. Directly applied to Artificial Life research (Hickinbotham 2019). Available online at [oblique-strategies.com](https://www.oblique-strategies.com/); authoritative card archive at [rtqe.net](http://www.rtqe.net/ObliqueStrategies/).

Example cards:
- *"Honour thy error as a hidden intention"*
- *"Use an old idea"*
- *"Do nothing for as long as possible"*
- *"What would your closest friend do?"*
- *"State the problem in words as clearly as possible"*
- *"Emphasize the flaws"*
- *"Work at a different speed"*
- *"Ask your body"*

## Domain Summary

Oblique Strategies sits at the intersection of **music production methodology**, **creative cognition**, **game design**, **ALife**, and **computational creativity**. It is a pre-digital example of what we might now call "prompt engineering for human creativity" — a system of structured randomness that interrupts habitual thought patterns and forces novel approaches.

The cards function as an **oracle**: not as instructions to be followed literally, but as perturbations to cognition. The obscurity of each prompt is a feature, not a bug — it forces the creator to do interpretive work, breaking out of existing mental grooves. This is the key philosophical mechanism: the card doesn't tell you what to do; it tells you to approach the problem differently, and *you* fill in what that means.

This mechanism has deep resonances with:
- **Generative systems**: rules that produce infinite outputs from finite instructions
- **Agent behavior**: how non-determinism + constraints produce novel behaviors
- **ALife**: how simple rules generate complex, open-ended outcomes
- **Speculative design**: using designed artifacts to change how people think

## Full Edition History

Oblique Strategies evolved through two independent projects that converged:
- **Peter Schmidt** (1970): *"The Thoughts Behind the Thoughts"* — 55 sentences letterpress-printed on disused prints, still in Eno's possession. Published in text form by Mindmade Books in 2012.
- **Brian Eno** (1974): Handwritten aphorisms on bamboo cards, named "Oblique Strategies" in 1974.

Significant overlap between the two projects led to their combination in late 1974, offered for general sale in 1975.

| Edition | Year | Notes |
|---------|------|-------|
| 1st | 1975 | ~500 copies, black card. Combined Eno + Schmidt projects. |
| 2nd | 1978 | Published by Opal, London. Minor revisions. |
| 3rd | 1979 | Final Schmidt-era edition. Schmidt died January 1980. Cards became rare and expensive. |
| 4th | 1996 | Commissioned by software pioneer **Peter Norton** as Christmas gifts (not for sale). Documented in Eno's *A Year with Swollen Appendices* (1996). New cards added; Eno revised collaboratively with Norton. |
| 5th | 2001 | Public edition. Eno revisited cards after renewed public interest. |
| 6th | 2007 | Available via [enoshop.co.uk](http://www.enoshop.co.uk/shop/oblique-strategies). |
| Burgundy limited ed. | May 2013 | 500 boxes, burgundy (not black). Limited numbered edition. |

Authoritative card-by-card documentation of all editions maintained by **[[Gregory Alan Taylor]]** at [rtqe.net/ObliqueStrategies](http://www.rtqe.net/ObliqueStrategies/).

**Peter Norton** (4th edition collaborator): Software pioneer (Norton Utilities, Norton AntiVirus), significant art collector. His involvement brought Oblique Strategies back into circulation after 16 years of scarcity.

## Connection to Computational Creativity & AI

This is the critical intersection for Amber's work.

### Key Paper: Oblique Strategies for Artificial Life
**Simon Hickinbotham** (2019). "Oblique Strategies for Artificial Life." In *From Astrophysics to Unconventional Computation*, pp. 185–204. Springer. [DOI: 10.1007/978-3-030-15792-0_9](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-030-15792-0_9)

This paper directly applies Eno & Schmidt's Oblique Strategies to ALife research problems — using the cards as a heuristic framework for generating novel research directions in artificial life. Hickinbotham (University of York, works with Susan Stepney) proposes applying the oblique strategy logic to ALife experiment design, treating research itself as a creative process subject to the same blocks and constraints as artistic creation. This is almost exactly the application to agent design that Amber might pursue.

### ICCC Reference
**Jon McCormack** & T McNamara (2024). "The Creative Act of AI Research." *Proceedings of ICCC 2024* (International Conference on Computational Creativity). [PDF](https://computationalcreativity.net/iccc24/papers/ICCC24_paper_163.pdf) — references Oblique Strategies as a foundational tool for thinking about AI creativity.

### Ambient Journalism Application
**Alex Burns** (2010). "Oblique strategies for ambient journalism." *M/C Journal, 13*(2). [URL](https://vuir.vu.edu.au/15551/) — applies the Oblique Strategies framework to journalism practice and platform design, using Eno's ambient philosophy as a model for media systems.

### Computational Creativity Discourse
- Referenced in Partridge & Rowe's "Creativity: A computational modeling approach" (2002) — the deck is cited as a non-computational creativity support system worth modeling
- Discussed in ICCC community as a historical precedent for computational creativity systems
- The Oblique Strategies mechanism (random perturbation of problem-framing) directly maps to techniques in computational creativity: **conceptual blending**, **analogical reasoning**, **constraint relaxation**

### Agent Design & LLMs
- The card deck is a **finite constraint system that generates open-ended behavior** — structurally identical to what agent prompting systems try to achieve
- Prompt engineering discourse frequently invokes Oblique Strategies as a pre-digital precedent for "prompts that change how you think, not just what you produce"
- Several projects have implemented digital Oblique Strategies as **LLM system prompts** or **agent behavioral modifiers**

## Philosophical Grounding

### 1. Constraint-Based Creativity
The core insight: **constraints liberate rather than restrict**. Eno's broader philosophy — developed in parallel with generative music theory — holds that open-ended freedom produces paralysis, while structured constraints generate creative energy. This connects to:
- Stravinsky's "The more constraints one imposes, the more one frees oneself"
- The Dogme 95 film movement
- OuLiPo literary movement (Perec, Queneau) — writing under mathematical constraints
- Design thinking's use of "How might we..." framings as productive constraints

### 2. Lateral Thinking (de Bono)
Edward de Bono's *Lateral Thinking* (1970) — published five years before Oblique Strategies — provides the cognitive science backing. De Bono argued that dominant ideas in creative problems need to be escaped by deliberately non-logical, lateral moves. Oblique Strategies operationalizes this as a card-drawing ritual.

### 3. Aleatoric / Chance Operations (Cage)
John Cage's use of I Ching to generate compositional decisions (1950s) is the direct predecessor. Cage: "I have nothing to say and I'm saying it." The cards move from pure chance (I Ching) to guided obliqueness — not random, but orthogonal to current thinking.

Connections:
- **Fluxus event scores** (George Brecht, Yoko Ono) — "Water Yam" (1963) as parallel form
- **Aleatoric music** tradition: Stockhausen, Boulez, Cage

### 4. Generative Systems & Cybernetics
Eno's wider theory of **generative music** (music as a system that generates itself, rather than music as a fixed recording) directly connects to:
- **Cybernetics**: feedback loops, self-organizing systems, emergence
- **Systems thinking**: behavior emerging from rule interactions
- **ALife**: Langton's "Life at the Edge of Chaos" — most interesting behavior emerges at the boundary between order and disorder

Eno explicitly theorized this in talks and writings, connecting Oblique Strategies to his broader interest in systems that produce *unpredictable-but-bounded* outputs.

### 5. Improvisation Theory
The cards have been used in improvised music and theater contexts as **real-time prompts**. Connection to:
- **Free improvisation**: Derek Bailey's theory of non-idiomatic improvisation
- **Improv theater**: "Yes, and..." as a lateral move
- **Ethnomethodology**: how performers maintain coherence while incorporating disruption

## Connection to Amber's Work

This is where Oblique Strategies becomes ⭐⭐⭐ for Amber specifically.

### 1. ALife & Agent Design (Most Direct)
Hickinbotham's 2019 paper applies Oblique Strategies directly to **ALife research methodology** — using the card logic to generate novel research directions, break experimental deadlocks, and foster new approaches in artificial life. This is the exact intersection of:
- **ALife** (Amber's field)
- **Constraint-based creativity** (Oblique Strategies' mechanism)
- **Agent design** (applying this to how agents behave, not just how researchers think)

Amber could extend this: **Oblique Strategies as a behavioral protocol for creative agents** — a finite card system that produces open-ended, non-repetitive agent behavior. Instead of humans drawing cards to break creative blocks, agents could have card-drawing as a mechanism for behavioral variation, curiosity, or self-disruption.

### 2. Generative Systems & Open-Endedness
Eno's generative music theory (music as a system that generates itself) is structurally identical to ALife's open-ended evolution goal. Both are interested in **finite rules → infinite variety**. Oblique Strategies is a minimal constraint system that produces this. Relevant to:
- Amber's interest in **open-endedness** (tag: #open-endedness)
- **Generative systems** in Amber's toolset
- **Machine behavior** — what if agents could "draw cards" to vary their behavioral repertoire?

### 3. More-Than-Human Design
The cards' design philosophy — *designed artifacts that change how humans think, not what they do* — is a model for more-than-human design practice. The card as a non-human agent that interrupts and redirects human cognition. This connects to:
- **Speculative design** tools that aren't interfaces but provocations
- **Cosmotechnics**: designed objects as ways of organizing human-world relation
- **Things We Could Design** (Wakkary) — objects as interlocutors

### 4. Speculative Design Method
Oblique Strategies is itself a **speculative design artifact** — an object that encodes a theory of creativity and makes it actionable. For Amber's speculative design practice and teaching, the deck is a model for:
- How to design *methodological tools* as research outputs
- How oracle/constraint systems can function as co-creators
- How designed randomness can be used in design research

### 5. Agent Ethology
If agents had behavioral "cards" — a repertoire of oblique moves they could draw on when in stuck states — this would be a form of **ethological enrichment** (animal husbandry term: environmental complexity that promotes behavioral variety). Connection to Amber's agent ethology work: how do you design agent environments that produce behaviorally rich, non-stereotyped agents?

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Not edge of chaos** — the cards themselves are established (50+ years old), but the *computational application* space is actively evolving:
- LLM prompt engineering discourse is actively rediscovering Oblique Strategies as a framework
- ICCC community continues to reference them as historical precedent
- No one has yet built a comprehensive computational implementation that treats the card mechanism as an **agent architecture** — this is an open space

**Relevant trend (2024-2026):** As LLM agents proliferate, the question of *how to make agents behaviorally diverse and non-repetitive* becomes urgent. Oblique Strategies provides an elegant partial answer: inject structured randomness via constraint cards. This is being independently rediscovered but not yet academically formalized.

## Why Interesting

Oblique Strategies is more than a creativity tool — it's a **designed epistemological intervention**. The cards don't just help artists; they instantiate a theory of creative cognition: that blocks arise from dominant framings, and the cure is orthogonal perturbation rather than harder effort in the same direction.

For Amber, this is interesting at three levels:
1. **Methodological** — a proven creative constraint system to use in research and teaching
2. **Research object** — a designed artifact that changes human cognition; model for more-than-human design
3. **Agent design inspiration** — the card mechanism has direct application to computational agents (Hickinbotham 2019 proves this is a real research direction)

The connection to ALife (via Hickinbotham) and computational creativity (via ICCC) makes this directly actionable for Amber's research program. Rating upgraded to ⭐⭐⭐.
