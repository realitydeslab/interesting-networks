# Data Feminism

A framework and intellectual movement for rethinking data science and data ethics through the lens of intersectional feminism. Anchored in Catherine D'Ignazio and Lauren Klein's canonical 2020 book *Data Feminism* (MIT Press), the field asks who makes data, who data is made about, who benefits, and who is harmed — treating data science as inherently political rather than neutral. Connects to a broader critical data studies ecosystem including Safiya Umoja Noble's algorithmic oppression work, Virginia Eubanks's automation critique, and Ruha Benjamin's "New Jim Code" framework.

## Domain Summary

Data Feminism is both a book and a broader intellectual project. The 2020 book by Catherine D'Ignazio and Lauren Klein argues that data science is never neutral — it encodes the power structures of the societies that produce it. Drawing on intersectional feminist theory (particularly Patricia Hill Collins's matrix of domination and Kimberlé Crenshaw's intersectionality), the book proposes 7 principles for doing data science in ways that actively challenge rather than reproduce inequality.

The field emerged at the confluence of:
- **Critical algorithm studies** (algorithmic fairness, bias, accountability)
- **Feminist STS** (science and technology studies with feminist epistemology)
- **Data justice** (data rights, surveillance capitalism, data sovereignty)
- **Feminist pedagogy** (who teaches data, to whom, in what contexts)
- **Participatory design** (community-centered data collection)

Data feminism is distinct from "AI fairness" in being explicitly political and intersectional — it's not about making algorithms less biased while leaving power structures intact, but about challenging who has power over data in the first place.

### The 7 Principles of Data Feminism

D'Ignazio and Klein articulate seven principles, each a chapter:

1. **Examine Power** — Data science always reflects power differentials. Who controls the data? Who benefits from analysis? Who is left out?

2. **Challenge Power** — Not just examining but actively working to dismantle unjust power structures using data as a tool.

3. **Elevate Emotion and Embodiment** — Traditional data science prizes "objectivity" and suppresses emotion. Feminist epistemology argues these are false dichotomies; emotion and embodied knowledge are valid epistemic resources.

4. **Rethink Binaries and Hierarchies** — Binary classification systems (male/female, normal/abnormal) embed ideological assumptions. Challenge these classification schemes.

5. **Embrace Pluralism** — Prioritize local, situated, and experiential knowledge alongside data-driven knowledge. Multiple perspectives and datasets.

6. **Consider Context** — Data is never "raw"; it is always collected in specific historical, political, and institutional contexts that shape what can be known.

7. **Make Labor Visible** — The human labor behind data systems (data labelers, data cleaners, content moderators) is systematically made invisible. Make it visible.

Each principle is illustrated with data visualization examples, historical cases, and contemporary tech critique.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Edge of chaos: Low-medium.** Data Feminism as a named framework is well-established (2020) and has been thoroughly absorbed into FAccT, CSCW feminist tracks, and critical data studies curricula. The core principles are no longer controversial — they've become standard vocabulary in critical AI discourse. However:

- **Still evolving in application**: D'Ignazio's *Counting Feminicide* (2024) shows how the framework is being taken into data activism outside the US/Anglophone world (Latin America, Indigenous contexts). This transnational turn is genuinely new.
- **Tension with AI generative systems**: The emergence of large language models raises new questions about data feminism principles — who is in the training data, whose voices are amplified/suppressed in RLHF, etc. This application of the framework is still being worked out.
- **Intersection with agent systems**: Data feminism hasn't yet fully grappled with autonomous agents — systems that not only process data but act on it. This is a gap Amber could potentially fill.

Not on the fastest-moving edge, but intellectually generative. More useful as a **foundation** than as a frontier.

## Open Questions

1. **What does data feminism look like for agent systems?** The 7 principles were designed for data science. How do they apply when the data subject is an autonomous agent, not a person? Who counts as a data subject when the agent itself can be a harm-doer?

2. **Data feminism and non-Western epistemologies** — the framework is strong on intersectionality but rooted in US/Anglophone feminist tradition. How does it encounter indigenous data sovereignty, African feminist data activism, or Chinese feminist STS? Tension with cosmotechnics is underexplored.

3. **From critique to design** — Data feminism is excellent at diagnosis; its prescription is less clear. D'Ignazio's *Counting Feminicide* moves toward practice. What does feminist system design actually look like beyond participatory methods?

4. **Agent ethology as feminist data practice** — Amber's angle: studying how agents behave in the wild could be framed as a data feminist project — whose behavior matters, who gets to study it, whose knowledge about agent behavior counts?
