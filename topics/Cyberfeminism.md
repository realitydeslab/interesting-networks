# Cyberfeminism

Cyberfeminism is a feminist philosophy, art practice, and critical methodology that foregrounds the entanglement of gender, power, and digital technology. Coined in 1991 by Australian collective VNS Matrix and theorized in parallel by UK philosopher Sadie Plant, it emerged as a counter-movement to the "boys' toys" culture of early internet — not to exclude women from tech, but to rethink the entire technological imaginary from a position of feminist critique and embodied practice. From its punk-art origins in Adelaide through the institutionalized critical theory of the 2000s to the speculative radicalism of Xenofeminism (2015) and Glitch Feminism (2020), cyberfeminism traces a lineage of feminist refusal and re-imagination of digital machines, AI, cyberspace, and code.

## Domain Summary

Cyberfeminism spans philosophy, art practice, net art, HCI criticism, and STS. Its historical arc:

**Wave 1 — Utopian Cyberfeminism (1991–1997)**
- VNS Matrix's *A Cyberfeminist Manifesto for the 21st Century* (1991): punk-art provocation declaring "the clitoris is a direct line to the matrix." Technology as liberation from patriarchal social order.
- Donna Haraway's "[A Cyborg Manifesto](https://doi.org/10.2307/3174105)" (1985, *Socialist Review*; republished in *Simians, Cyborgs and Women*, 1991): the foundational text. Cyborg as figure for breaking down human/machine, nature/culture, male/female binaries. Haraway didn't coin "cyberfeminism" but provided its philosophical spine.
- Sadie Plant's *Zeros + Ones: Digital Women and the New Technoculture* (1997): argued femininity and computing share a structural history — both subordinated, both now converging to escape "the meat" (masculine biological substrate).
- Early utopian vision: cyberspace as post-gendered space, technology as liberatory from embodied hierarchy.

**Wave 2 — Critical Turn (1997–2008)**
- Old Boys Network (OBN), founded 1997 by Cornelia Sollfrank: institutionalized cyberfeminism as critical practice. Published the "100 Anti-Theses" — 100 things "cyberfeminism is not" — to prevent the movement from being defined/contained.
- First Cyberfeminist International, documenta X, Kassel, 1997. Five subsequent gatherings.
- Critical turn: recognizing early net utopia was itself gendered, raced, classed. Cyberspace was not gender-neutral — it reproduced existing hierarchies.
- Shift toward critique of techno-determinism and "digital divide."

**Wave 3 — Proliferation and Mutation (2008–present)**
- The name "cyberfeminism" largely dissolved into sub-fields: **feminist STS**, **critical algorithm studies**, **feminist HCI**, **data feminism**, **glitch feminism**, **xenofeminism**.
- Each inherits specific aspects: xenofeminism takes the anti-naturalism + tech repurposing; glitch feminism takes the body/code interface; data feminism takes the structural power critique.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Not edge of chaos as a named field.** Cyberfeminism as a unified term is historically established and academically stable — it's been canonized (course syllabi, encyclopedia entries, museum shows). The *energy* has migrated into:
- **[[Xenofeminism]]** — still alive philosophically (edge of chaos: low, but intellectually dense)
- **[[Glitch Feminism]]** — active, especially in digital art and identity politics
- **[[Data Feminism]]** — very active in critical AI studies and FAccT-adjacent work
- **Feminist AI criticism** — fast-evolving, highly relevant to Amber's machine behavior work

Worth tracking as a genealogy and for how its ideas are *being activated* in current AI/tech discourse, not as a living movement per se.

## Sub-topics & Trends (last 3–5 years)

- **Feminist AI Criticism** — algorithmic bias, gendered training data, feminist critique of LLMs. Abeba Birhane, Joy Buolamwini, Timnit Gebru. Active at FAccT.
- **Glitch Feminism** ([[Glitch Feminism]] note) — Legacy Russell's 2020 book. The "glitch" as site of disruption to normative identity online. Very active in art-theory crossover.
- **Xenofeminism** ([[Xenofeminism]]) — Laboria Cuboniks' 2015 manifesto. Tech-accelerationist feminist politics. See full note.
- **Data Feminism** ([[Data Feminism]]) — Catherine D'Ignazio & Lauren Klein's 2020 book. Power analysis of data collection, visualization, classification. Active in data science + STS communities.
- **Feminist HCI / Participatory Design** — decentering the "neutral user," designing for marginalized bodies. Growing subfield at CHI.
- **Cyborg Anthropology / STS** — Karen Barad's agential realism as neo-cyberfeminist ontology; Donna Haraway's *Staying with the Trouble* (2016) shifting from cyborg to "sympoiesis" and multispecies kinship.
- **VNS Matrix Revival** — still active; recent performances and exhibitions (2023–2024), particularly in Australia and European net art spaces.

## Key People

- **[[Donna Haraway]]** — *A Cyborg Manifesto* (1985). The philosophical mother of the whole tradition. Shifted from cyborg → sympoiesis in *Staying with the Trouble* (2016). Feminist STS, UC Santa Cruz.
- **Sadie Plant** — *Zeros + Ones* (1997). UK philosopher; argued femininity and computing structurally converge. No institutional affiliation now; highly cited.
- **VNS Matrix** — Australian art collective (Virginia Barratt, Julianne Pierce, Francesca da Rimini, Sandy Stone). Founded 1991 Adelaide. Still active (2024). Website: [vnsmatrix.net](https://vnsmatrix.net/)
- **Cornelia Sollfrank** — Old Boys Network founder, net.artist, theorist. Based in Berlin/Hamburg. Still active in net art criticism.
- **[[Legacy Russell]]** — *Glitch Feminism: A Manifesto* (Verso, 2020). Executive Director of The Kitchen, NYC. Contemporary heir to cyberfeminist performance art + theory.
- **Laboria Cuboniks** — Collective of 6 (incl. Helen Hester, Amy Ireland, Lucca Fraser). Published [Xenofeminist Manifesto](https://laboriacuboniks.net/manifesto/xenofeminism-a-politics-for-alienation/) (2015). See [[Xenofeminism]] and [[Laboria Cuboniks]] notes.
- **Helen Hester** — Laboria Cuboniks, wrote *Xenofeminism* (Polity, 2018). Professor at University of West London.
- **Amy Ireland** — Laboria Cuboniks, writes on AI and machine cognition from a xenofeminist perspective. Based in Australia/Berlin.
- **Abeba Birhane** — ([[Abeba Birhane]] in graph) — contemporary feminist AI criticism, algorithmic harm.
- **Kate Crawford** — *Atlas of AI* (2021). Power/materiality critique of AI systems. Highly cited at FAccT.
- **Joy Buolamwini** — Algorithmic Justice League founder. Feminist critique of facial recognition and biased AI.
- **Virginia Eubanks** — *Automating Inequality* (2018). Feminist/critical race critique of automated systems.
- **Karen Barad** — *Meeting the Universe Halfway* (2007). Feminist new materialism / agential realism. Foundational for posthuman feminist ontology.

## Why Interesting

Cyberfeminism is the genealogical archive for much of Amber's theoretical vocabulary. The cyborg (Haraway) → the posthuman design stance; the xenofeminist anti-naturalism → the refusal of "natural" AI behavior; the feminist STS observation method → agent ethology's scientific gaze. This isn't a direct intellectual home for Amber, but it's the intellectual *ancestry* of several of her core moves. Understanding this genealogy helps Amber position her work accurately and find allies she might not know are thinking along the same lines. The most live connection is through Haraway's *Staying with the Trouble* — multispecies kinship, sympoiesis, tentacular thinking — which is the most cited theoretical foundation in more-than-human design. Also: as feminist AI criticism grows (FAccT, DAIR, AJL), Amber's machine behavior work will increasingly encounter these communities, and knowing the theoretical heritage will help her navigate the conversations.
