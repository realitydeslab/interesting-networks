# Existential Risk

Existential risk (x-risk) is the study of scenarios in which catastrophic events could either destroy humanity's long-term potential or cause permanent civilizational collapse. The field originated in philosophy and decision theory — primarily at Oxford's Future of Humanity Institute — before expanding into an applied research agenda spanning AI, biosecurity, nuclear risk, and climate. Today, AI-related x-risk dominates the field's attention and funding, and the ecosystem has professionalized rapidly: dedicated labs, fellowships, policy institutes, and a global talent pipeline now constitute a formalized discipline. But the field is also contested: critics argue its longtermist assumptions, demographic homogeneity, and institutional entanglements reproduce harms rather than prevent them.

**Amber is a current ERA Fellow (Winter 2026, Cambridge, Feb–Mar), advised by [[Joel Lehman]] — placing him inside one of the field's key talent pipelines at the exact moment AI x-risk is becoming institutionally dominant.** He approaches x-risk not as a philosopher or ML engineer but as a creative technologist and ALife researcher — an angle that is genuinely rare inside the field.

## What Is It

Existential risk, as a formal field, was shaped by three foundational moves:

**1. The philosophical foundation (Oxford, 2000s)**
Nick Bostrom's [Existential Risks: Analyzing Human Extinction Scenarios](https://www.nickbostrom.com/existential/risks.html) (2002, *Journal of Evolution and Technology*, URL: https://www.nickbostrom.com/existential/risks.html) introduced the taxonomy: extinction events, permanent civilizational collapse, "astronomical" value at stake. The Future of Humanity Institute (FHI, Oxford) became the intellectual home. The argument: because the long-run future is so large, small probabilities of catastrophe deserve enormous priority (Pascal's mugging as policy).

**2. The AI-specific threat narrative (2014–2023)**
Bostrom's [*Superintelligence*](https://www.amazon.com/Superintelligence-Dangers-Strategies-Nick-Bostrom/dp/0198739834) (2014) crystallized the AI x-risk argument: sufficiently capable AI with misaligned goals could rapidly defeat human containment, leading to irreversible civilizational takeover. MIRI (Machine Intelligence Research Institute, Yudkowsky et al.) took this seriously from ~2000; Bostrom's book gave it mass reach. Stuart Russell's [*Human Compatible*](https://www.penguinrandomhouse.com/books/566677/human-compatible-by-stuart-russell/) (2019) provided a technically grounded alternative framing. Toby Ord's [*The Precipice*](https://www.penguin.co.uk/books/435/435867/the-precipice/9780141987002.html) (2020, Penguin) broadened to all x-risks, rated AI as the #1 risk in the 21st century at 10% probability.

**3. The Effective Altruism (EA) alignment**
The EA movement (William MacAskill, Toby Ord, Peter Singer) adopted x-risk as a defining cause area via "longtermism" — the view that expected value of the long-run future dominates present-day obligations. This gave x-risk an activist funding base (Open Philanthropy, FTX Future Fund before the collapse) and a career pipeline (EA Global, 80,000 Hours). The EA-x-risk alignment also created the field's most controversial political feature: enormous resource concentration around relatively speculative long-run scenarios.

**The current moment (2024–2026):**
- FHI has closed. CSER (Cambridge), the Leverhulme Centre for the Future of Intelligence (CFI, Cambridge), GovAI (Oxford), and a cluster of independent orgs (Anthropic, MIRI, ARC, CAIS) are the main institutional nodes.
- ERA, MATS, PIBBSS are the talent pipeline organizations.
- AI governance has partially merged with x-risk discourse — the EU AI Act, AISI (UK), and international summits are x-risk policy in practice.
- The field is undergoing a critique moment: demographic, political, and philosophical challenges to longtermism from multiple directions.

## The Debate

X-risk is one of the most politically contested areas in contemporary AI discourse. Several distinct critiques:

### 1. The Prioritization Critique (Critical AI Studies)
**From:** Timnit Gebru, Emily Bender, Ruha Benjamin, Alex Hanna, Kate Crawford.
**Argument:** Speculative existential risk discourse functions to redirect attention from present, documentable harms — algorithmic discrimination, labor exploitation, surveillance infrastructure, climate costs of compute — toward hypothetical futures. The people most harmed by current AI systems are not the ones writing the x-risk manifestos.
**Key text:** [On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots](https://doi.org/10.1145/3442188.3445922) (Bender et al., 2021).
**The deeper charge:** X-risk is a PR strategy that labs like OpenAI and Anthropic use to appear safety-conscious while accelerating capabilities development and avoiding near-term regulation.

### 2. The Demographic / Power Critique
**From:** Émile Torres, David Thorstad, feminist science studies scholars.
**Argument:** Longtermism and x-risk are overwhelmingly produced by a narrow demographic (white, male, Western, EA-adjacent) and reflect that demographic's fears about which futures are worth protecting. The imagined "humanity" whose future is at stake is not neutral.
**Key text:** Émile Torres and Gebru's "TESCREAL bundle" analysis — critiquing Transhumanism, Extropianism, Singularitarianism, Cosmism, Rationalism, Effective Altruism, Longtermism as an ideological cluster. URL: <https://www.truthdig.com/articles/the-new-tech-order/>
**The deeper charge:** Longtermist x-risk advocacy concentrates power with exactly the institutions posing the alleged risk.

### 3. The Longtermism vs. Neartermism Debate (Inside EA)
Within the EA movement itself: is future value really so large that speculative x-risk should dominate present-day cause prioritization? Critics like Will Crouch argue near-term poverty alleviation has more certain expected value. Defenders argue the math is clear.
**Key text:** [The Case Against Longtermism](https://aeon.co/essays/why-longtermism-is-the-worlds-most-dangerous-secular-credo) (Torres, Aeon 2021). URL: https://aeon.co/essays/why-longtermism-is-the-worlds-most-dangerous-secular-credo

### 4. The "Racing to the Brink" Critique
**From:** Within safety community (e.g., Jan Leike, Ben Garfinkel).
**Argument:** Labs claiming safety leadership use that framing to justify building more powerful systems ("we're the safety-conscious ones who should get there first"). This may actually accelerate x-risk rather than mitigate it.
**Key text:** Jan Leike's resignation letter from OpenAI (May 2024) — public statement that safety was being deprioritized at OpenAI despite safety-forward branding. URL: <https://x.com/janleike/status/1792206439190958558>

### 5. The Cosmotechnics Alternative
**From:** Yuk Hui, connecting to Amber's own philosophical neighborhood.
**Argument:** AI x-risk discourse assumes a universal technological rationality — one trajectory, one catastrophe, one future humanity. But Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics argues that technology is always locally embedded in particular moral-cosmological orders. There is no "one AI" — there are Chinese AI, European AI, Indigenous AI. The x-risk framing is already the answer to a question posed within a particular cosmotechnics.
**Amber's angle:** This is where his work on more-than-human design, Sinofuturism, and the China Academy of Art connection is genuinely relevant. The cosmotechnics critique of x-risk is underexplored and intellectually distinctive.

### 6. What's NOT Contested
Even critics generally accept that: (a) very powerful AI systems pose novel governance challenges; (b) some forms of catastrophic risk from AI are real (not necessarily extinction, but serious); (c) we need more safety research, not less. The disagreement is about *framing*, *priorities*, and *power dynamics* — not the existence of any risk.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Is x-risk fast-evolving?** Yes — and it's entering a transition moment:

- **Empirical safety results are arriving** (alignment faking, sleeper agents) that give concrete x-risk mechanisms beyond hypotheticals.
- **Institutional proliferation** — ERA, MATS, PIBBSS, national AI Safety Institutes — means the talent pipeline is now real and producing researchers.
- **The FHI closure is a generational shift.** Bostrom's institution is gone; the torch passes to a more distributed ecosystem (CSER, ERA, GovAI, ARC).
- **The EA collapse** (FTX, 2022) destabilized major funding, causing reorientation. Less EA-specific, more mainstream ML framing of x-risk.
- **AI capabilities are making x-risk concerns more immediate.** GPT-4, Claude 3.5, DeepSeek — systems visible enough that existential risk is no longer purely hypothetical-sounding.
- **Critical discourse is maturing** — the Gebru/Bender/Torres critiques are now serious scholarly positions, not just Twitter controversies. This is good: the field is being forced to defend its assumptions.

**Will it be hot in 1-3 years?** Already central, and hardening into major institutional form (AI Safety Institutes globally). The interesting space in 1-3 years will be:
1. **Technical x-risk research that's empirically grounded** (not just Bostrom-style philosophy)
2. **Non-Western x-risk perspectives** — what does Chinese AI governance mean for x-risk?
3. **ALife-informed x-risk** — Lehman's trajectory — studying open-ended, evolutionary AI dynamics as the actual threat model

**What's driving it?**
- Frontier models becoming genuinely powerful, faster than safety research
- Governments legislating AI (EU AI Act, UK AISI, US EO 14110)
- A generation of researchers trained specifically on x-risk concerns
- The empirical alignment results (sleeper agents, alignment faking) validating the concern

### Amber's Specific Angles

**1. Machine Death as Safety Mechanism → Corrigibility**
Amber's work on machine death (see [[AI Safety]] note, ALIFE 2026 section) is directly relevant to x-risk. The corrigibility problem — how do you build AI that accepts being shut down? — is partly a problem of designing around machine death. Mortality-as-design is biologically grounded in ways that RLHF-based corrigibility is not. For x-risk: an AI that expects to die has different instrumental drives than one designed for indefinite operation.

**2. Agent Ethology as X-Risk Methodology**
X-risk threat models often assume future hypothetical systems (AGI, superintelligence). Agent ethology would say: study the actual behavioral dynamics of current agents empirically, over full lifecycles, to understand what safety-relevant patterns emerge. This is Iyad Rahwan's machine behavior agenda applied to x-risk specifically. Amber's contribution: the lifecycle structure (birth-to-death arc) is analytically essential, and mortality makes behavioral observation possible.

**3. Cosmotechnics × X-Risk**
Yuk Hui's argument that technology is always cosmotechnically embedded applies directly to x-risk. The x-risk field assumes a single global trajectory to catastrophe. But if AI development is cosmotechnically plural — Chinese AI, European AI, etc. — then x-risk itself must be cosmotechnically grounded. Amber's connection to China Academy of Art, Yuk Hui's visiting professorship there, and Sinofuturism as an intellectual current gives him unique standing to make this argument.

**4. ALife × Open-Endedness × Safety (The Lehman Bridge)**
Joel Lehman's [Open-Endedness is Essential for ASI](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.04268) argues that safety implications of open-ended AI are "increasingly fertile and safety-critical." This is the theoretical bridge Amber can extend: if open-endedness is necessary for ASI (Lehman's claim), what are the x-risk implications of *inherently open-ended* AI systems? This is more tractable than Bostrom's recursive self-improvement scenario and directly connected to ALife's empirical tradition.

**5. More-Than-Human Design → Post-Human X-Risk**
The standard x-risk framing is anthropocentric: it's a risk to *humans*. More-than-human design asks: what about non-human entities, ecosystems, multispecies arrangements? An AI that destroys only human civilization while leaving viable ecologies for other forms of life is — by standard x-risk definitions — still a success. Amber's more-than-human lens offers a genuinely distinct framing of what "existential" means.
