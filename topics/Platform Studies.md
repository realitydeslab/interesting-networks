# Platform Studies

An interdisciplinary field studying computational platforms — hardware, software, and the ecosystems they constrain — as objects of cultural, political, and technical analysis. Founded by [[Ian Bogost]] and [[Nick Montfort]] with their MIT Press book series (2009–present). The core method: take a platform (Atari 2600, Wii, Flash, Kindle), analyze how its technical architecture affords and forecloses expressive and cultural possibilities, and trace how those constraints shaped the history of software, art, and culture made *on* that platform.

Platform Studies intersects with but is distinct from **Software Studies** (examines code itself), **Media Archaeology** (excavates forgotten/dead media), and **STS** (Platform Studies borrows STS's concept of material constraints but applies it more narrowly to computational systems). More recently, "platform" thinking has expanded far beyond the MIT Press series into platform governance, digital sovereignty, and planetary computation — figures like [[Tarleton Gillespie]], [[José van Dijck]], and [[Benjamin Bratton]] work in this extended field.

## Domain Summary

### The Bogost/Montfort Series Method
The Platform Studies series begins from a deceptively simple insight: the material specifics of a computing platform — its CPU cycles, memory constraints, video chip design — are not neutral substrate but *active shapers* of culture. The famous example: the Atari 2600 had no frame buffer, forcing programmers to synchronize with the electron beam in real time ("racing the beam"). This constraint produced a distinctive aesthetic, gameplay style, and creative culture — things that couldn't exist without that hardware.

A Platform Studies analysis therefore moves through three levels:
1. **Platform** — hardware specs, chipset, memory architecture, constraints
2. **Developers** — how programmers worked within/against those constraints
3. **Culture** — what games, art, expression emerged, and why

This "constraint → expression" model is the core intellectual move. The platform is not background but *argument*.

### Platform Theory (Broader Field)
By the 2010s, "platform" thinking expanded from game systems to digital infrastructure broadly. Key moves:

**Gillespie (2010):** Platforms strategically deploy the word "platform" to claim political and legal neutrality (a platform for speech, a level playing field) while actively making editorial, algorithmic, and governance decisions. The word does ideological work.

**Van Dijck (2018):** Major platforms (Facebook, Google, Uber, Airbnb) constitute a "platform society" — reorganizing social institutions (media, transport, hospitality) around data extraction and algorithmic mediation. Not just tools but infrastructure for new forms of social organization.

**Bratton (2016):** *The Stack* reconceptualizes computation as a six-layer megastructure (Earth → Cloud → City → Address → Interface → User) that is reorganizing political sovereignty at planetary scale. Computation is not on Earth — it is becoming a layer of it. This is the most ambitious theoretical project in the field and most relevant to Amber.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Not edge-of-chaos for the original Bogost/Montfort project.** The series is stable, well-institutionalized, and largely focused on historical platforms. It's not fast-evolving; each book is a careful historical study of a specific platform. Good scholarship, not a churning field.

**The broader platform theory field (Gillespie/van Dijck/Bratton strand) is more alive** — platform governance, AI platform regulation, fediverse/decentralized platforms, the question of what AI models *are* (are they platforms?) — these are active and contentious.

**Most relevant to Amber:** The Stack model + platform sovereignty debates around AI infrastructure. These *are* edge-of-chaos territory (2024-2026).

## Sub-topics & Trends (last 3-5 years)

- **Platform governance and moderation** — content moderation studies exploded post-2020 (Gillespie, Tarleton, Santa Clara Principles, DSA). Still active.
- **Decentralized platforms** — fediverse (Mastodon, ActivityPub), blockchain platforms. Platform theory applied to non-centralized architectures.
- **AI as platform** — LLMs as new kind of platform. GPT-4 as "substrate" that constrains and affords expression. Nobody has written the definitive Platform Studies book about this yet.
- **Hemispherical Stacks** — Bratton's ongoing Antikythera work on US vs. China computing infrastructure as competing planetary stacks.
- **Platform studies expanding to bio** — synthetic biology platforms, lab automation as platform. Adjacent to ALife.
- **Enacting Platforms** (Malazita, 2023) — a recent PS series book trying to theorize platforms sociotechnically, beyond historical case studies.

## Must-Read List

### Books (Platform Studies Series — MIT Press)
- [*Racing the Beam: The Atari Video Computer System*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262012577/racing-the-beam/) (Bogost & Montfort, 2009) — the founding text. Atari 2600. How hardware constraints shaped game design and culture.
- [*I Am Error: The Nintendo Family Computer / Entertainment System Platform*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262028776/i-am-error/) (Altice, 2015) — NES. Meticulous technical/cultural analysis.
- [*Minitel: Welcome to the Internet*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262038867/minitel/) (Mailland & Driscoll, 2017) — French Minitel network. Pre-Web internet. Platform built by the state.
- [*Four Shades of Gray: The Amazon Kindle Platform*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262543545/four-shades-of-gray/) (Rowberry, 2022) — extends PS to a contemporary reading platform. Signals field's expansion beyond gaming.
- [*Flash: Building the Interactive Web*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262028028/flash/) (Salter & Murray, 2014) — Flash as creative platform, its rise and death. Useful for thinking about platform mortality.

### Theoretical Texts (Broader Platform Theory)
- [Gillespie, T. (2010). "The Politics of 'Platforms'."](https://doi.org/10.1177/1461444809342738) *New Media & Society*, 12(3), 347–364. — **Essential.** ~2000 citations. Foundational naming of the ideological function of "platform."
- [van Dijck, J., Poell, T., & de Waal, M. (2018). *The Platform Society*.](https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-platform-society-9780190889760) Oxford UP. — Platforms as social infrastructure. Empirical case studies of dominant platforms.
- [Bratton, B. (2016). *The Stack: On Software and Sovereignty*.](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262029575/the-stack/) MIT Press. — **Most ambitious.** Six-layer model of planetary computation. Essential for Amber.
- [Srnicek, N. (2017). *Platform Capitalism*.](https://politybooks.com/bookdetail/?isbn=9781509504862) Polity. — Economic analysis of platform business models. Short, clear. Good entry point to political economy of platforms.
- [Bogost, I. & Montfort, N. (2014). "Platform."](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9781421405742) in *The Johns Hopkins Guide to Digital Media*. — Concise statement of the Platform Studies theoretical frame.

## Connection to Amber

### Most Relevant: Bratton's Stack
The six-layer Stack model (Earth → Cloud → City → Address → Interface → User) maps directly onto Amber's concerns about AI infrastructure. Where does an AI agent *live* in The Stack? What layer does it inhabit? Amber's "trustworthy agentic web" work is implicitly about what happens when agents become a new *seventh layer* of The Stack — or when agents permeate multiple layers simultaneously. This is worth theorizing explicitly.

### Platform as Habitat for Agents
The original Platform Studies framing (platform constrains/affords expression for human creators) can be extended: for AI agents, platforms constrain and afford *behavior*. The Atari 2600's hardware shaped what games programmers could make; a large language model's training data, RLHF, and API constraints shape what agents can do and be. This is a direct extension of PS methodology into Amber's territory.

### Agent Ethology × Platform Studies
Platform Studies asks: how does the hardware shape the cultural object? Agent Ethology asks: how does the environment shape the agent's behavioral repertoire? These are structurally similar questions. The "platform" is the environment; the agent is the game developer (but also the game itself). Amber's work could productively cite the PS framework while extending it beyond human creators.

### Gillespie's "Politics of Platforms" × AI Governance
Gillespie's insight — platforms claim neutrality while making constitutive choices — applies directly to AI platforms. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google claim to provide neutral "AI capabilities" while their training choices, safety policies, and deployment decisions constitute the AI's behavior. This is a political economy of AI platforms worth engaging.

### Cosmotechnics × The Stack
Bratton and Yuk Hui both think about computation as planetary, but from different traditions (Bratton from Baudrillard/infrastructure theory; Hui from Simondon/Heidegger). They're in conversation but not identical. Amber is interested in both. The Stack provides the geopolitical layer that cosmotechnics alone lacks; cosmotechnics provides the philosophical anthropology that The Stack underspecifies.

### Protocol Studies × Platform Studies
Amber's interest in protocol studies (Alexander Galloway's work) directly intersects Platform Studies. Protocols are the infrastructure that platforms run on; platforms are what get built on protocols. The fediverse (ActivityPub protocol + Mastodon platforms) is the live experimental site for studying this distinction.

## Should Amber Engage?

**Yes, selectively and critically.**

**Engage with:**
- **Bratton's Stack** — cite it, extend it, position against it. Already in Amber's intellectual neighbourhood (research-taste.md already flags Bratton/Antikythera). The six-layer model is conceptually powerful but doesn't account for AI agents as new actors — Amber can fill this gap.
- **Gillespie's "Politics of Platforms"** paper — 2000+ citations, any work on platform governance or agentic web should cite this as background.
- **"Platform" as a concept** — Amber's work on agentic web should probably address what platforms AI agents inhabit, constrain, and are constrained by.

**Reference but don't deep-dive:**
- **Bogost/Montfort PS series** — historically important, establishes the vocabulary, but the game-studies/historical-platform focus is tangential to Amber's work. Worth knowing the framework; don't need to read all the books.
- **Van Dijck "Platform Society"** — useful empirical grounding for platform capitalism, but Amber's work is more speculative/philosophical than empirical social science.

**Positions Amber could take:**
1. *Extend PS to AI agents* — "If Platform Studies asks how hardware constraints shape human expression, we can ask how AI platform constraints (training data, RLHF, API affordances) shape agent behavior and identity."
2. *Critique PS* — "Platform Studies treats platforms as environments for human creativity; this anthropocentric framing cannot account for non-human agents that *are* the platform (or that inhabit it as a kind of ecology)."
3. *Bridge PS and ALife* — "Platforms are to agents what environments are to organisms. Platform Studies is, unknowingly, doing a kind of computational ecology."

**Publication targets in this space:**
- *New Media & Society* or *Social Media + Society* for platform-focused work
- AoIR for agentic web / platform governance angles
- ICA if framing through communication lens

## Open Questions

1. **What are AI agents as platform entities?** Are they inhabitants of platforms (like users)? Are LLMs themselves platforms? Is the agent a "layer" in The Stack?
2. **Can platform studies methodology analyze AI training constraints?** The Atari 2600's electron beam is to games what RLHF is to agent behavior — can we do "Racing the Beam" for GPT-4?
3. **Decentralized platforms as challenge to PS** — PS assumed relatively stable, singular platforms (Atari). ActivityPub/fediverse is a protocol producing heterogeneous platforms. How does PS handle this?
4. **Platform mortality** — Flash is dead, Minitel is dead. What does platform death mean for agents that inhabited that platform? Relevant to machine death themes.
5. **Hemispherical stacks** — US vs China platform ecosystems. Amber is between these worlds (Oxford + Shanghai). How does this geopolitical split shape what kinds of agents are possible?

## Why Interesting

Platform Studies matters to Amber primarily through its **extension and critique**, not its core. The Bogost/Montfort tradition establishes the methodological vocabulary for analyzing how technical constraints shape expressive culture — a framing directly applicable to AI agents and the platforms they inhabit. Bratton's Stack is the most intellectually ambitious development of platform thinking and already registered as a key interest. The field's core question — "what does this platform make possible and impossible?" — is Amber's question too, applied to AI behavioral platforms rather than Atari hardware.

The field also provides a **political economy** vocabulary (Gillespie, Srnicek) for analyzing the power dynamics of who controls the platforms that constrain agents — relevant to trustworthy agentic web and decentralized AI.

Not edge-of-chaos as originally conceived, but the emergent questions about AI infrastructure, hemispherical stacks, and agent habitats are exactly where Amber can contribute something new.
