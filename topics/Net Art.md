# Net Art

Net Art (also written net.art or Internet art) is a practice and genre in which the internet — its protocols, interfaces, browsers, social structures, and politics — is both the medium and the subject of the artwork. Emerging in the early-to-mid 1990s when the World Wide Web became publicly accessible, net art radically circumvented the traditional gallery system: works existed natively online, often interactive, participatory, and deliberately ephemeral. Unlike art digitized for online viewing, net art only exists *as* the internet — exploiting HTTP, HTML, browser rendering quirks, hyperlinks, email, bots, and network topology as its raw materials. The foundational generation (Vuk Ćosić, JODI, Olia Lialina, Alexei Shulgin, Heath Bunting, 1994–2001) treated the early web as a genuinely new medium — anarchic, unmonetized, structurally weird — and made work that would become illegible as browsers evolved, raising foundational questions about preservation, mortality, and what it means for a medium to die. These questions are now central to digital art preservation and have unexpected resonance with Amber's work on machine death.

## Domain Summary

Net Art emerged 1993–1996 from a loose international community connected by email lists (particularly **nettime**, founded 1995) and early web experimentation. The term "net.art" was itself an accident — a fragment of garbled ASCII code in a 1995 email received by Vuk Ćosić from an anonymous sender, which he adopted as a name for the emerging practice. The foundational circle — Ćosić (Ljubljana/Slovenian), Joan Heemskerk & Dirk Paesmans as JODI (Dutch/Belgian, based in Barcelona), Olia Lialina (Moscow), Alexei Shulgin (Moscow), Heath Bunting (Bristol/UK) — met at Ars Electronica and circulated on nettime. They were anti-commercial, anti-institutional, and deeply aware that the web was changing beneath their feet.

Key moments:
- **1994–96:** First works appear — Lialina's *My Boyfriend Came Back from the War* (1996), Bunting's *King's Cross Phone-In* (1994), Shulgin's *Form Art* (1997)
- **1996:** Rhizome founded by Mark Tribe as email list at Ars Electronica
- **1997:** Net art enters documenta X (Kassel) — first major museum legitimation
- **1999:** Rhizome ArtBase archive launched — first attempt to preserve net art
- **2000:** Whitney Biennial includes net art as dedicated category (Mark Amerika, etoy, ®™ark)
- **2001:** 49th Venice Biennale features *Biennale.py* (Eva & Franco Mattes) — a computer virus released on opening night, spreading worldwide
- **2001–onwards:** "Post-Internet art" begins displacing net art — the term coined by Marisa Olson in 2008 marks the shift from art *native to* the internet to art *influenced by* internet culture
- **2015+:** Preservation crisis deepens; Rhizome launches Webrecorder/Conifer for archiving dynamic web content
- **2017:** Rhizome launches Net Art Anthology — 100 landmark works in digital preservation context
- **2020s:** Renewed interest via NFT/blockchain art (debates about what net art "ownership" means), and AI-generated web art raising old net.art questions in new form

**Current state (2026):** Net art as a distinct movement is historical, but its questions — autonomy, browser as medium, network as material, agent behavior online, digital preservation — are more live than ever. Rhizome continues as the primary institutional home. Academic journals like Leonardo and conferences like ISEA continue to publish net art scholarship. The field connects directly to current debates about AI agents on the web, browser fingerprinting as political act, and what it means for digital works to "die."

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

Net art as a movement is **established and historical** — the classic 1994–2001 period is well-documented and canonized. However, several sub-questions are still live and fast-evolving:

- **Digital preservation** — actively developing (Rhizome, Internet Archive, emulation technology)
- **AI agents as net art** — autonomous web agents, generative bots, GPT-driven "performances" as successors to 1990s bot art — very much alive
- **Post-post-Internet** — what comes after Post-Internet in an era of surveillance capitalism and AI-generated content?
- **Machine death and net art mortality** — as browsers lose Flash, Java applets, early HTML standards, whole bodies of work cease to function — this is literally machine death at scale

Edge of chaos: **partially** — the historical period is settled, but the *questions it raises* are reactivating.

## Sub-topics & Trends (last 3–5 years)

- **AI art revisiting net.art genealogy** — artists like Hito Steyerl, Ian Cheng referencing early net art aesthetics
- **Web archiving and emulation** — Rhizome's Webrecorder, Internet Archive, MAME-based emulation projects for software art
- **Bots as artwork** — successor lineage to net art: autonomous Twitter bots, GPT personas, agent-driven performances (see: Sam Lavigne, Lauren Lee McCarthy's conversational AI art)
- **Crypto/NFT and net art legacy** — debated whether blockchain art inherits or betrays net art's anti-institutional ethos
- **Net art and sovereignty** — Heath Bunting's *StatusProject* (mapping identity through documentation) directly anticipates debates about AI agent identity

## Open Questions

1. **Is net art preservation possible?** Can archived, emulated net art be the "same" work as the original — or does preservation produce something categorically different (a kind of copy-death)?
2. **AI agents as net art successors?** When GPT-powered bots navigate, comment, and create online — are they a new generation of net art or something fundamentally different?
3. **What did net art get right about the internet that we forgot?** The early net art community saw the web as anarchic commons; what happened to that vision, and can it be recovered?
4. **Machine death in net art:** Works dependent on Flash, Java, early HTML die as browsers change. This is involuntary machine death at scale — what does it mean for a medium's corpus to die gradually?
5. **Net art and the non-Western internet:** The canonical net.art was almost entirely Euro-American. What were Chinese, Latin American, and other internet artists doing simultaneously? (Relevant: [[Cao Fei]] developed RMB City in Second Life)
6. **Post-Post-Internet:** In an era of surveillance capitalism and AI-generated content, what is the genuine successor to net art? Can art still be "native" to the current web?

## Why Interesting

Net art sits at an unexpected intersection of Amber's most specific interests:

**1. Bots and autonomous agents as art:** Early net art experimented extensively with automated agents — web crawlers repurposed as art, spam bots, FloodNet's distributed virtual protest (1998) where thousands of users' browsers simultaneously pinged a target website. This is literally agent ethology avant la lettre — studying what machines *do* on the network, but as artistic practice. The link from net art's bot-art to contemporary machine behavior science is direct.

**2. Machine death and digital mortality:** Net art is one of the few art forms where *the medium itself dies*. As browsers dropped support for Java, Flash, specific HTML behaviors — whole bodies of net art became literally inoperable. This involuntary, medium-level death (not the death of individual works but of an entire substrate) is a philosophically rich site that resonates deeply with Amber's machine death and care ethics research. Preservation is a form of care for dying machines.

**3. The browser as behavior space:** JODI's work was specifically about using the browser as a behavioral environment — their work appeared broken, exploited rendering bugs, made the interface itself the subject. This is essentially ethological observation of a computational system turned into artistic practice.

**4. Olia Lialina's "Turing Complete User":** Her 2012 essay argues for the user as an active computational agent, not a passive consumer — which connects to Amber's more-than-human design framework and the question of what "agency" means at the human-computer interface.

**5. Network topology as political fact:** Heath Bunting's *StatusProject* mapped identity through bureaucratic documentation networks — an early example of what we'd now call "infrastructural critique" of the kind Amber's collaborators practice.

**6. Institutional critique through protocol:** Net art was the first art form to use internet protocols themselves as a medium of critique — exploiting HTTP, HTML, TCP/IP as expressive material. This is protocol studies avant la lettre.
