# Object-Oriented Ontology

Object-Oriented Ontology (OOO) is a 21st-century metaphysical movement that rejects the privileging of human existence over nonhuman objects. Founded by philosopher Graham Harman in 1999, OOO argues that all objects — human, nonhuman, natural, artificial — exist independently of each other and of human perception, each possessing a withdrawn, inexhaustible reality that can never be fully accessed by any other entity. Its core challenge: philosophy since Kant has been obsessed with the human-world correlation ("correlationism") at the expense of the vast majority of reality. OOO is a subset of [[Speculative Realism]], and its influence has spread across ecology, design, media studies, art criticism, and cultural theory.

## Domain Summary

OOO's central claims:

1. **Anti-correlationism** — objects exist independently of human thought or perception; reality is not reducible to what humans can access
2. **Withdrawal** — every object has a hidden interior reality that can never be fully exposed in any relation; even rocks relating to other rocks encounter only "caricatures" of each other
3. **Flat ontology** — all objects (quarks, armies, corporations, ideas, hammers, glaciers) have equal ontological status; there is no privileged level of reality
4. **Anti-reductionism** — objects cannot be "undermined" (reduced to smaller components) or "overmined" (reduced to their effects and relations)

### Graham Harman's Four-Fold Object
Harman distinguishes: real objects (withdrawn, never fully accessible) vs. sensual objects (as they appear in experience); and real qualities vs. sensual qualities. This yields four "tensions": essence (RO-RQ), space (RO-SQ), eidos (SO-RQ), time (SO-SQ). Causation is always "vicarious" — objects only meet inside a third object.

### Levi Bryant: Onticology
Bryant's variant ("onticology") centers on the **Ontic Principle** ("there is no difference that does not make a difference") and the **Principle of the Inhuman** (difference-making is not exclusive to humans). He proposes a "wilderness ontology" and a typology of objects: bright (strongly manifest), dim, dark (fully withdrawn), rogue (wander between assemblages). Key book: *The Democracy of Objects* (2011).

### Timothy Morton: Hyperobjects
Morton brought OOO into ecological theory. **Hyperobjects** are entities so massively distributed in time and space that they transcend any local manifestation — global warming, radioactive plutonium, styrofoam, the biosphere, the internet. Hyperobjects are: viscous (they stick to everything), molten (they warp spacetime), nonlocal (you only see local manifestations), phased (they exist in higher-dimensional spaces), interobjective (formed by relations). Hyperobjects was a hugely influential concept in climate humanities.

### Ian Bogost: Alien Phenomenology
Bogost developed an "applied" OOO through **alien phenomenology**: asking what it's like to be any object. His three modes: **ontography** (revealing object relations), **metaphorism** (speculating on inner lives of objects), **carpentry** (making artifacts that embody object perspectives). His approach shaped "platform studies" and game studies.

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

OOO's peak influence was roughly **2009–2015**. The first OOO symposium at Georgia Tech (2010) was a flashpoint — organized by Bogost, including Bryant, Harman, Shaviro, DiSalvo. The Speculative Turn anthology (2011) marked the movement's crystallization. Since ~2016, the field has fragmented:

- OOO's vocabulary is now embedded in design, ecology, art criticism, and media studies — **permanent infrastructure**
- Philosophical debates have largely moved on to competing frameworks (new materialism, process philosophy, speculative pragmatism)
- Harman remains active (writing, SCI-Arc faculty); Morton's hyperobjects concept continues to propagate in climate humanities
- 2024-2026: OOO is no longer cutting-edge philosophy but is **actively generative** in design theory and multispecies humanities

**edge_of_chaos: false** — established, not actively churning. But its infrastructure (vocabulary, concepts) keeps spreading.

## Design Implications

OOO has had substantial influence on design theory, particularly in:

1. **Thing-centered design** — designing *for* objects, not just *with* objects; what would a design process look like that takes the perspective of the artifact seriously?
2. **Carpentry as method** — Bogost's concept of "making things that explain things" — building artifacts as a form of philosophical inquiry. Resonates strongly with practice-based research.
3. **More-than-human design** — OOO provides the philosophical foundation for why nonhuman entities should be "co-designers" or at least "stakeholders" — they have their own withdrawn reality that designs disturb.
4. **Nonhuman-centered design** — Carl DiSalvo and others have used OOO to justify adversarial and more-than-human design approaches; the CHI community has picked this up.
5. **Design fiction and speculative objects** — OOO's interest in the inner lives of objects resonates with design fiction's fascination with what objects mean from their own perspective.
6. **Architectural applications** — Harman at SCI-Arc; OOO has a dedicated architectural theory following (Michael Young, Tom Wiscombe).

## Connection to Amber

OOO is philosophically adjacent to almost every one of Amber's core threads:

### 1. Cosmotechnics and Yuk Hui
Yuk Hui explicitly critiques OOO as a form of Western universalism that proposes a single flat ontology for all objects. In *Recursivity and Contingency* (2019), Hui argues that OOO's "withdrawal" is still a Western metaphysical move — it universalizes a particular relation to things. **Cosmotechnics** (Hui) is in productive tension with OOO: both ask about the relation between humans and technology/objects, but Hui insists that this relation varies across cultures and cosmologies. For Amber, this tension is generative — OOO gives you the "objects matter" argument; cosmotechnics gives you the "but HOW they matter is culturally specific" corrective.

### 2. More-than-Human Design
OOO is arguably the most influential philosophical framework behind more-than-human design. The insistence that nonhuman objects have their own reality — not just as instrumental extensions of human will — is the philosophical license for designing *with* rather than *for*. Wakkary's *Things We Could Design* references this lineage. Amber's work is directly in this stream.

### 3. Agent Ethology
OOO's concept of withdrawal is fascinating for AI agents: AI agents might be understood as "objects" with a withdrawn interior — they are never fully exposable through their behavior. What does it mean to study AI ethologically (from behavior) if OOO is right that behavior never exhausts being? The withdrawal thesis suggests machine behavior is always a *partial* manifestation of something larger and hidden. This creates a productive philosophical challenge to behavioral approaches.

### 4. Nonhuman Agency
OOO (especially via Bennett's adjacent *Vibrant Matter*) underpins arguments for nonhuman agency — the "thing-power" of objects. For ALife and machine behavior research, this is useful: it grants philosophical legitimacy to studying what machines *do* independent of what humans intend them to do.

### 5. Hyperobjects and Ecological Design
Morton's hyperobjects concept is relevant to Amber's climate-adjacent interests — designing with/against entities that can't be localized (global warming, the internet, AI infrastructure) requires thinking at scales OOO licenses.

## Critiques

### Philosophical critiques

- **Rein Raud** — OOO fails its own anti-anthropocentrism goal: the "objects" it discusses are always those the human cognitive apparatus can readily perceive or conceptualize. The universalism is illusory. URL: Raud, *Being in Flux* (2021), https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Being+in+Flux%3A+A+Post+Anthropocentric+Ontology+of+the+Self-p-9781509540327
- **Steven Shaviro** — OOO too dismissively rejects process philosophy (Whitehead, Simondon, Deleuze). These thinkers actually explain *how* objects come into existence through processes, which OOO ignores. Shaviro's *The Universe of Things* (2014) is the key text.
- **Alexander Galloway** — OOO mirrors and naturalizes **computational/capitalist logic** — its language of objects, properties, and classes mirrors object-oriented programming; its flat ontology maps onto post-Fordist organization. OOO is ideologically suspicious for being historically situated while claiming universality. Galloway, ["The Poverty of Philosophy: Realism and Post-Fordism"](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/668529) (2013), *Critical Inquiry* 39(2).
- **David Berry** — similar critique; OOO's ontology is structured by computational metaphors; it's a philosophy *of* digital capitalism dressed as neutral metaphysics.

### Feminist / political critiques

- **Karen Barad / New Materialism** — OOO's "objects" are too static and pre-given; Barad's agential realism insists on *intra-action* (objects are constituted *in* relation, not prior to it). OOO's flat ontology evacuates the political stakes of specific configurations of power, gender, race.
- **Donna Haraway** — the implicit politics of a philosophy that places human and bacterium and corporation on equal ontological footing is troubling — it can flatten power and accountability.
- **Leftist flat ontology critique** — if everything is equally an object, what happens to domination, exploitation, structural inequality? Galloway and others argue OOO's egalitarian ontology is politically regressive.

### OOO's own counterclaim
OOO distinguishes *ontological equality* (everything exists) from *ethical equality* (everything matters equally). Bogost: "Ontological egalitarianism doesn't mean moral egalitarianism." But critics find this too easy a dodge.

## Why Interesting
