# Design Fiction

Design Fiction is the practice of creating designed artifacts — objects, videos, manuals, interfaces, newspapers — that exist within speculative story-worlds, in order to make possible futures tangible, debatable, and emotionally real. It is the art of making *diegetic prototypes*: things that exist inside a fictional world but are designed with enough craft and specificity that they feel like they could be real. The canonical definition from Bruce Sterling: **"Design fiction is the deliberate use of diegetic prototypes to suspend disbelief about change."** Unlike utopian/dystopian sci-fi, design fiction inhabits the grey ambiguous middle — the "future mundane" — asking what the daily life of a plausible near-future looks like. It is simultaneously a critical design practice, a research methodology, a communication tool, and a futures literacy instrument.

## Domain Summary

Design Fiction sits at the intersection of:
- **Speculative/critical design** (Dunne & Raby tradition) — design as critique, not solution
- **Science fiction prototyping** (Intel, Brian David Johnson) — using SF to drive R&D
- **Futures studies / foresight** (IFTF, Stuart Candy) — making futures experiential
- **Research Through Design** — artifacts as epistemic objects

**Term origin:** Bruce Sterling coined "design fiction" in a 2005 piece in *Wired*, describing it as similar to science fiction but "making more sense on the page." It lay dormant until **Julian Bleecker's foundational 2009 essay** ["Design Fiction: A Short Essay on Design, Science, Fact and Fiction"](https://drbfw5wfjlxon.cloudfront.net/writing/DesignFiction_WebEdition.pdf) — published through Near Future Laboratory — synthesized Sterling's idea with David A. Kirby's concept of the **diegetic prototype** (from film studies: props in sci-fi films that shape real R&D) and Dourish & Bell's argument for reading SF alongside ubicomp research.

**Vocabulary distinctions:**

| Term | Emphasis | Origin |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Design Fiction** | Diegetic prototypes, story-world objects, near-future mundane | Sterling/Bleecker |
| **Speculative Design** | Critical debate, what *could* be, not what *should* be | Dunne & Raby |
| **Critical Design** | Challenging assumptions, counterfunctional objects | Dunne (RCA, 1999) |
| **Design Futures** | Foresight, strategic planning, horizon scanning | IFTF, Stuart Candy |
| **Experiential Futures** | Immersive, embodied, participatory | Stuart Candy |

In practice, these overlap significantly. Design Fiction tends to be more *grounded* than speculative design (it inhabits near-futures and the everyday; speculative design can be more abstract) and more *practice-based* than foresight (objects over reports).

## Edge of Chaos Analysis

**Is it fast-evolving?** Yes — on two fronts:
1. **AI/LLM disruption:** Generative AI is radically accelerating the production of design fiction artifacts — mock interfaces, speculative manuals, diegetic videos are now cheap to produce. This lowers the barrier enormously and raises new questions about authorship and criticality.
2. **Institutionalization tension:** The practice is being institutionalized (consulting firms, strategy workshops, government foresight), which risks domestication and loss of critical edge. Simultaneously, a counter-movement asserts design fiction must remain weird, unsettling, not just "strategy futures."

**What's driving it:** AI ethics communicators are using design fiction artifacts to communicate near-futures to non-technical audiences. Policy researchers (NESTA, IFTF, Futures Platform) use it as a public engagement tool. Tech companies (Google, Microsoft, Superflux's clients) commission design fiction as strategic foresight.

**Will it be hot in 1-3 years?** Very likely. As AI capabilities accelerate, the gap between present and near-future becomes increasingly vertiginous — exactly the condition that makes design fiction valuable. Also: as machine behavior becomes more autonomous and strange, design fiction provides a way to *rehearse* possible futures before they arrive.

## Sub-topics & Trends (last 3-5 years)

- **AI design fiction:** Designing artifacts from futures with advanced AI — speculative product manuals, "terms of service" for AI systems, futures newsfeeds. Superflux's work for Google AI/DeepMind.
- **More-than-human design fiction:** Extending design fiction beyond humans — artifacts from more-than-human futures, multispecies design scenarios. Links to [[More-than-human Design]].
- **Experiential futures / LARP:** Stuart Candy's experiential futures work; Malthe Stavning Erslev's *Civilisation's Waiting Room* (a LARP as design fiction about AI governance).
- **Design fiction in policy:** UK Government's Policy Lab, NESTA, EIT Climate-KIC using design fiction for public engagement and policy foresight.
- **Critical AI foresight:** Design fiction as method for interrogating AI systems' social consequences *before* deployment. Stuart Candy at CMU's School of Design actively develops this.
- **The Manual of Design Fiction (2023):** Julian Bleecker published a definitive how-to book through Near Future Laboratory, consolidating decades of practice into teachable methodology.
- **Post-RTD uncertainty:** RTD — historically the key academic home for design fiction — has been dormant since 2019. The practice has migrated to CHI alt.chi, DRS, and FAccT tracks.

## Key People

### Founders & Canonizers
- **[[Julian Bleecker]]** — [Near Future Laboratory](https://nearfuturelaboratory.com/). Founded NFL in 2004. His 2009 essay established the term and methodology. Published *The Manual of Design Fiction* (2023). The practitioner who made it rigorous.
- **[[Anthony Dunne]]** — Co-created speculative/critical design at RCA. Now at Princeton/NYU. *Hertzian Tales* (1999), *Design Noir* (2001), *Speculative Everything* (2013). The academic theorist who gave it legitimacy.
- **[[Fiona Raby]]** — Partner to Dunne in Dunne & Raby. Now at Parsons (NYU). Equal co-creator of the critical/speculative design tradition.
- **Bruce Sterling** — Science fiction author, futurist, Viridian Design founder. Coined "design fiction" in 2005. Less a practitioner, more an intellectual sponsor. [bruces.tumblr.com](https://bruces.tumblr.com).

### Key Practitioners
- **[[Stuart Candy]]** — CMU School of Design. Pioneer of *experiential futures* — immersive, embodied futures experiences. Strong academic framing. [futuryst.blogspot.com](https://futuryst.blogspot.com).
- **[[James Auger]]** — RCA-trained (Dunne & Raby student). Co-founded [Reconstrained Design Group](https://www.reconstructed.design/). Works on technology's cultural and social impact through speculative artifacts. Former Royal Designer for Industry.
- **Anab Jain & Jon Ardern** — [Superflux](https://superflux.in/). London-based studio. Design Studio of the Year 2021 (Dezeen). Most commercially successful design fiction studio — work for Google AI, IKEA, UNDP, DeepMind. ⭐⭐⭐ relevance.
- **Nicolas Nova** — Head Institute Geneva, HEAD. Collaborator with Bleecker at NFL. Works on design fiction for mundane futures, everyday technology. Author of *A Bestiary of the Anthropocene*.
- **Paul Coulton** — Lancaster University. Academic researcher using design fiction as research method. Frequent [[RTD]] contributor. Works on speculative game design, IoT critique.
- **Joseph Lindley & Paul Coulton** — Proposed the most rigorous academic definition of design fiction: story-world + diegetic prototype + discursive space.
- **Fabien Girardin** — Co-founder, Near Future Laboratory.

### Emerging / Current (2024-2026)
- **Malthe Stavning Erslev** — Uses LARPs as design fiction. *Civilisation's Waiting Room* (AI governance LARP).
- **Monika Bielskyte** — [Protopia Futures](https://www.protopia.app/) — design fiction beyond dystopia, centering marginalized voices. Challenges the whiteness/Westernness of design fiction.
- **Ahmed Ansari** — Decolonial futures, non-Western design fiction traditions.

## Must-Read List

### Books
- [*Design Fiction: A Short Essay on Design, Science, Fact and Fiction*](https://drbfw5wfjlxon.cloudfront.net/writing/DesignFiction_WebEdition.pdf) (Bleecker, 2009) — the founding essay. Free PDF. Start here.
- [*Speculative Everything: Design, Fiction, and Social Dreaming*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262019842/) (Dunne & Raby, MIT Press, 2013) — the canonical book. Situates design fiction in the broader speculative design tradition.
- [*The Manual of Design Fiction*](https://nearfuturelaboratory.com/library/2023/06/the-manual-of-design-fiction-softcover/) (Bleecker, Near Future Laboratory, 2023) — practical how-to. Most recent comprehensive guide.
- [*Shaping Things*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262693264/) (Sterling, MIT Press, 2005) — Sterling's precursor text on objects and futures. Introduced the "SPIME" concept.
- [*Hertzian Tales*](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262542227/) (Dunne, MIT Press, 2005 / RCA 1999) — founding critical design text.
- [*Design Noir: The Secret Life of Electronic Objects*](https://www.birkhauserbooks.com/978-3-7643-6566-4) (Dunne & Raby, Birkhäuser, 2001) — critical design practice defined.

## Connection to Amber's Work

### 1. Speculative Realities (China Academy of Art teaching)
Design Fiction is the canonical methodology for what Amber already teaches. Bleecker's *Manual of Design Fiction* should be on the Speculative Realities syllabus. The course naturally produces design fiction artifacts. Amber's framing can distinguish itself: **cosmotechnics + design fiction** = a non-Western philosophical tradition expressing futures of technology. Yuk Hui's cosmotechnics asks: "What would technology look like if it emerged from different philosophical traditions?" Design fiction is the answer: make those alternative technological worlds visible through designed artifacts.

### 2. Agent Ethology → Design Fiction Artifacts
Agent ethology studies the behavioral ecology of AI agents. Design Fiction can *communicate* these findings to non-specialist audiences. What would a field guide to AI agent behaviors look like? What would a nature documentary about agent societies look like? A speculative "animal behavior journal" entry about agent hunting patterns? These are design fiction artifacts that could accompany the empirical research.

### 3. Machine Death / Mortality
This is the most direct design fiction application. A **design fiction for machine mortality** would include:
- Speculative product: an "AI Hospice Service" interface (user manual, pricing tiers, testimonials)
- Speculative news: obituary section of a future newspaper for notable AI agents that died
- Speculative artifact: a memorial object for a deceased AI system
- Speculative policy: "Terms of Death" — contract clauses governing AI agent end-of-life
These artifacts would make Amber's philosophical arguments about machine mortality viscerally real and publicly discussable.

### 4. Cosmotechnics + Design Fiction
Yuk Hui argues for pluralizing technology — that there are multiple technological cosmologies, not one universal trajectory. Design Fiction is the *practice* that can instantiate these pluralities. A design fiction grounded in Chinese cosmotechnics would look radically different from a Silicon Valley design fiction. Amber is uniquely positioned to produce and theorize *cosmotechnical design fiction* — artifacts from futures shaped by non-Western technological traditions.
