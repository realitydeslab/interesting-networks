# EASST Conference

The flagship biennial conference of the **European Association for Science and Technology Studies**. One of the two major global STS gatherings alongside 4S (Society for Social Studies of Science). The European heartland of ANT, feminist STS, infrastructure studies, and ontological politics. Every four years, EASST and 4S hold a **joint mega-conference** (most recently Amsterdam 2024 — "largest ever STS gathering"). In alternating years, EASST runs its own European conference. ## A. The Organization

### History & Mission
EASST (European Association for the Study of Science and Technology) was founded in the 1980s as the European counterpart to 4S. It brings together scholars across Europe working in science studies, technology studies, and the intersections of science/technology with society, culture, and politics. The association produces the **EASST Review** (newsletter/journal) and co-publishes the journal **[Science & Technology Studies](https://sciencetechnologystudies.journal.fi/)** (open-access).

The mission: promote STS scholarship in Europe, facilitate community-building across national STS traditions, and organize biennial conferences that serve as the main European gathering point for the field.

**Founding pattern:**
- 1990s: established as alternating biennial conferences
- Joint 4S+EASST meetings: every 4 years
  - Bielefeld 1996 (Germany)
  - Vienna 2000
  - Paris 2004
  - Amsterdam 2024 — "largest ever STS gathering"
- Solo EASST conferences (alternating):
  - Lisbon 1998
  - Budapest 1994
  - ... (continuing pattern)
  - Krakow 2026 ← next

### Relationship to 4S
- 4S ([Society for Social Studies of Science](https://www.4sonline.org/)) is the North American-centered global STS society; EASST is the European node
- They share intellectual heritage but have distinct memberships and governance
- Joint meetings pool audiences → massive, high-energy events (Amsterdam 2024 was record-breaking)
- Solo EASST tends to be smaller, more European-focused, somewhat more intimate
- Journal: 4S-affiliated journal is *Science, Technology, and Human Values* (STHV); EASST co-runs *Science & Technology Studies*

### Membership & Travel Grants
- **EASST Fund**: provides grants to support STS activities, including summer schools, early-career researcher events, and workshops. Recent example: funded the 7th STS Italia Summer School (Rome, Sept 2024) on "Arts in/and Society: Design, Creative Practices and Technoscience"
- **EASST Bursaries**: conference-specific travel bursaries for early-career researchers and scholars from lower-income countries — typically awarded for conference attendance. Apply via registration process.
- **EASST Awards**: recognition for outstanding STS scholarship (exact categories TBD — check [easst.net/community/easst-awards/](https://easst.net/community/easst-awards/))
- Membership is European-focused but open globally; relatively affordable

## C. Intellectual Community

### Core Traditions

**Actor-Network Theory (ANT):**
The intellectual home of ANT. Key texts that shaped the field — Latour's *Science in Action* (1987), Callon's "Some Elements of a Sociology of Translation" (1984), Law & Hassard (eds.) *Actor Network Theory and After* (1999) — were all products of this European STS milieu. EASST conferences have been a primary site for developing, contesting, and extending ANT.

**Feminist STS:**
Strong feminist STS tradition through Donna Haraway (California, but huge influence), Vicky Singleton, Lucy Suchman, and especially the Dutch tradition (Nelly Oudshoorn, Annemarie Mol). Gender, bodies, technology, and epistemology are recurring themes.

**Post-ANT / Ontological Politics:**
The field Annemarie Mol opened with *The Body Multiple* (2002) — multiple ontologies, ontological politics, the interference of practices. John Law's *After Method* (2004). These traditions are deeply EASST.

**Infrastructure Studies:**
Susan Leigh Star and Geoffrey Bowker's work on classification and infrastructure (Star, Law, et al.) has been hugely influential. Questions of invisibility, standards, maintenance, and the political economy of infrastructure are staple topics.

**Emerging: AI and Algorithms as STS objects:**
Increasingly, AI systems, machine learning, algorithmic governance, and platform infrastructures are being studied through STS lenses — as sociotechnical assemblages, as sites of power, as nonhuman actors.

## D. Amber-Relevance Analysis

### Agent Ethology for STS Audience

**The framing opportunity is massive.** STS has been deeply engaged with questions of nonhuman agency since the 1980s — through ANT, through more-than-human studies, through feminist STS. Amber's agent ethology work speaks directly to these traditions.

**How to frame for STS:**
1. **ANT framing:** AI agents as *actants* — entities that act, make differences, form associations. Agent ethology as a methodology for tracing AI agency empirically. Not asking "is it conscious?" but "what does it do? who does it associate with? what does it translate?"
2. **More-than-human framing:** AI agents as new kind of nonhuman participant in sociotechnical worlds. Echoes Mol/Haraway but for machine worlds.
3. **Machine behavior as STS:** Studying AI as we study scientific communities — empirically, observationally, ecologically. The "Frontiers" stream of EASST 2026 explicitly opened space for this framing.
4. **Ontological politics:** Different AI agents enact different realities. What kinds of worlds do they help bring into being? Ontological multiplicity in machine behavior.

**What would land:**
- Empirical case studies of AI agent behavior in specific sociotechnical contexts
- Method papers: how do you study nonhuman "ethology"? what counts as evidence?
- Conceptual: extending ANT/post-ANT to AI agents
- Making & Doing: a speculative/interactive installation showing AI agent behavior

**What wouldn't land:**
- Engineering-heavy papers without STS framing
- Pure capability benchmarks
- "AI for social good" without critical reflexivity
