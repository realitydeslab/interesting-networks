# Manifesta — European Nomadic Biennial of Contemporary Art

Nomadic European biennial founded in 1994 by Dutch art historian **Hedwig Fijen** and owned by the [International Foundation Manifesta](https://manifesta.org/) (IFM, Amsterdam). Unlike Venice Biennale or Documenta, Manifesta moves to a different European city every two years — making site, urban context, and European geopolitics central to each edition. Known for choosing peripheral, post-industrial, or politically charged cities rather than cultural capitals. Strong tradition of institutional critique, migration, and pan-European identity politics. **No open calls for artists** — fully curated by invitation.

## Manifesta 15 (2024 — Barcelona)
**Website:** [manifesta15.org](https://www.manifesta15.org/)
**Period:** September–November 2024
**Curators:** Sergio Pardo López and Filipa Oliveira
**Three curatorial threads:**
- "Balancing Conflicts" — social tensions, urban conflict, coexistence
- "Cure and Care" — health, ecology, healing
- "Imagining Futures" — speculative, futures thinking, city transformation

Barcelona was chosen for its metropolitan complexity — housing crisis, tourism gentrification, Catalan independence tensions, urban commons movements. The biennial spread across multiple neighborhoods rather than a single institution. Notably engaged with the city's ongoing struggle to remain livable. No direct technology/media art angle.

The Ruhr is a historically significant choice — it echoes Manifesta 9 in Genk (post-coal Belgian mining region). The Ruhr's post-industrial transformation (from coal/steel to culture: Essen Capital of Culture 2010, Zeche Zollverein UNESCO site) makes it a natural Manifesta city. Josep Bohigas as an architect signals likely urban/infrastructure focus.

## Manifesta Journal
**Website:** [manifestajournal.org](https://www.manifestajournal.org/) (now redirects to manifesta.org)

The *Manifesta Journal* was a peer-reviewed / critical theory publication focused on **international contemporary art exhibitions, biennials, and curatorial practice**. It ran for approximately 10 issues and was discontinued / integrated into Manifesta's main web presence. It was **not** a typical academic submission journal — it was a curated critical publication commissioned by Manifesta, not a venue for open submissions. It was important as a discursive space for biennial theory and critique.

## Recurring Themes in Manifesta

- **Post-industrial urbanism** — Genk, Ruhr, Pristina: former industrial cities in transition
- **Migration and the Mediterranean** — especially Manifesta 12 (Palermo) and 13 (Marseille)
- **European geopolitical edges** — Russia, Cyprus, Kosovo, post-colonial port cities
- **Institutional critique** — often the biennial itself is the subject
- **Ecological and more-than-human** — Manifesta 12's "Planetary Garden" most explicit
- **Labor and work** — Manifesta 11 ("What People Do for Money"), Manifesta 9 (coal mining)
- **Urban commoning** — participatory, community-led, site-specific

## Education & Mediation Programme
Manifesta runs a formal **Education and Mediation Programme** at each edition, beginning early in the host city:
- Extensive field research and sociocultural mapping before the biennial opens
- Collaborative work with local artists and associations
- Research-and-practice-based programmes focused on local knowledge
- Discursive mediation and critical programming open to the public
- This is Manifesta's most participatory / open strand — not a typical open call but may offer collaborative opportunities for researchers/educators

---

## How Does One Get Involved?
- **Artists:** No open call. Curators are appointed by IFM; artists are invited directly.
- **Educators/researchers:** Education programme is developed in the host city, with local institutions and associations. External researchers may engage collaboratively in the host city context.
- **Manifesta Journal:** No longer accepting submissions (discontinued as standalone publication).
- **Institutional proposals:** Cities bid to host Manifesta — an urban/political-level process.

---

## Fellowships & Programmes
No documented open fellowships or residencies associated with Manifesta for outside researchers. Manifesta partners with local institutions in each host city — check host city arts councils for affiliated programmes.

## Why Interesting
Manifesta is significant as a barometer of European art-political discourse — it consistently chooses geopolitically charged locations and frames them through contemporary critical art theory. Its best editions (M7, M9, M12, M14) are theoretically sophisticated, site-responsive, and politically uncomfortable. The Manifesta 12 "Planetary Garden" theme and the Manifesta 7 animism-adjacent curatorial framework (Anselm Franke, Raqs) come closest to Amber's more-than-human and cosmotechnical concerns. However, it remains a traditional art biennial — curated, invitation-only, no technology or ALife dimension — and is not a venue where Amber can participate directly.
