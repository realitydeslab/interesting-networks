# Isis (History of Science Society)

The oldest and most prestigious journal in the history of science — founded 1913 by George Sarton, published by [University of Chicago Press](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/toc/isis/current) for the [History of Science Society (HSS)](https://www.historyofscience.org/). Quarterly. Over 110 years of continuous publication. This is the flagship — publishing in *Isis* marks entry into the scholarly history of science community.

Its companion journal *[Osiris](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/loi/osiris)* publishes annual thematic volumes. The *Isis Current Bibliography* is the field's largest and oldest bibliography (freely available via Chicago Press and [IsisCB Explore](https://isiscb.org/)).

**Sections per issue:**
- **Research Articles** — peer-reviewed original research (the main event)
- **Focus** — thematic mini-sections on a specific question (open access)
- **Viewpoint** — short opinion/perspective pieces (open access)
- **Second Look** — revisiting classic arguments in the field (open access)
- **Book Reviews** — extensive review culture; a major part of how the field talks to itself

## Topics

*Isis* covers all of history of science, technology, and medicine. Current editorial priorities under Mukharji & Burton signal:
- **Postcolonial and global history of science** (decentering Western canon)
- **Feminist and queer STS**
- **History of computing, AI, and digital technologies** (growing area)
- **Material and embodied approaches**
- **History of environmental and climate science**
- **Science and race, science and colonialism**

The Focus/Viewpoint/Second Look sections allow shorter, more experimental forms — including historiographical arguments, methodological debates, and engaged responses.

## Submission Guidelines Summary

*(Based on Chicago Press author guidelines; verify at journal homepage before submitting)*

- **Original research articles**: No fixed word limit specified; typical length 8,000–12,000 words including notes
- **Focus contributions**: Shorter (~3,000–5,000 words); often solicited but open submissions accepted
- **Viewpoint pieces**: ~2,000–3,000 words; argument/position pieces
- **Book reviews**: Commissioned; contact review editor
- **Language**: English only (since the 1920s)
- **Style**: Chicago Manual of Style; footnote citation format
- **Submission**: ScholarOne manuscript system via journal homepage
- **Simultaneous submissions**: Not permitted
- **Pre-registration/preprints**: No specific policy (humanists rarely preprint)

**Practical note:** *Isis* is a competitive journal. The history of science is a small field, and a first publication here typically requires either (a) grounding in historical method (archival research), or (b) an exceptionally theorized argument that speaks to ongoing debates in the field. Dick's path — Harvard SoF → carefully placed Isis papers — is the model.

## Audience

Historians of science, STS scholars, science historians in history departments and HPS programs worldwide. Small but highly engaged field — around 3,000 HSS members globally. *Isis* is read by everyone in the field. A publication here is known universally within HoS.

### Amber-Relevance Assessment

**Could Amber publish here? Yes — but it requires positioning.**

*Isis* is a historian's journal. It expects archival depth, engagement with historiography, and situating claims within ongoing scholarly debates. Amber is a designer-researcher, not a trained historian. The route in requires:

1. **Frame work as history of science/technology**, not as design research or HCI. The object of study (AI agents, machine behavior) must be treated historically — with archival sources if possible.

2. **Best angle for Amber: "Focus" or "Viewpoint" pieces** — shorter, more essayistic, less demanding of traditional archival depth. A theorized argument about machine behavior as a category, drawing on STS and history of science frameworks, could work in Viewpoint.

3. **Realistic pathway:** Amber's agent ethology work has a historical argument at its core — the idea that we should study AI systems the way we study animals (ethology as method). This has deep connections to the history of animal studies, cybernetics, and behavioral science. Situating agent ethology within that history — engaging with figures like Norbert Wiener, Jakob von Uexküll, the ethology-computing debates of the 1960s–80s — could produce a genuine *Isis* contribution.

4. **Collaboration route:** Co-authoring with a trained historian of science (e.g., through contacts in the Oxford history of science community) would significantly increase success odds.

5. **Indirect route:** Publish first in *Technology and Culture* (the History of Technology flagship, slightly more applied/STS-friendly) or in *Osiris* (thematic volumes, often invite critical/theoretical work). *Isis* may follow.
