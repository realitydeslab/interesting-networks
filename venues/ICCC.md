## Conference Overview

ICCC defines itself as the intersection of **AI, Cognitive Science, Engineering, Design, Psychology, and Philosophy** — exploring computation that exhibits creative behavior. Founded in 2010 (preceded by IJWCC workshops from 2004–2008). The canonical definition they use:

> *"Computational creativity is the art, science, philosophy and engineering of computational systems which, by taking on particular responsibilities, exhibit behaviours that unbiased observers would deem to be creative."*

**Size and character:** Small, focused (~100-200 attendees). Double-blind review. Quality > quantity. Papers that apply LLMs without situating in CC literature are explicitly flagged as unlikely to fare well.

**Proceedings:** Open-access, published by ACC. All proceedings available at [computationalcreativity.net/home/publications](https://computationalcreativity.net/home/publications/). DBLP indexed.

### Paper Format
- 8 pages + up to 2 pages references
- AAAI/IJCAI-style LaTeX template: [download](https://computationalcreativity.net/ICCC-author-kit-2022.zip)
- Double-blind review
- PDF via EasyChair

### Paper Types (5 categories)
1. **Technical papers** — hypotheses about creative behaviour in systems, strong evaluation required
2. **System or Resource description papers** — building/deploying creative systems, some evaluation expected
3. **Study papers** — interdisciplinary perspectives from psychology, philosophy, cognitive science, humanities; ethnographic studies, thought experiments, surveys
4. **Cultural application papers** — use of creative software in cultural settings (exhibitions, concerts, games, etc.)
5. **Position papers** — opinion/thought leadership on CC research directions, no experimentation required

### Topics (ICCC'26)
- Foundations of Computational Creativity: theories, models, principles
- Interdisciplinary Perspectives: philosophical, sociological
- Computational Paradigms: heuristic search, analogical reasoning, cognitive architectures
- Human-Machine Co-Creativity: systems, studies, frameworks
- Social Models: social creativity, diffusion of ideas, collaboration
- Psychological Factors: emotion, surprise, motivation, improvisation
- Societal Impact: ethics, deployment, societal effects of creative AI
- Computational Creativity Evaluation: metrics, frameworks
- Applications: music, language, games, visual arts, design, architecture, gaming, scientific discovery
- Data and Creativity: datasets, resources for creative AI
- Provocations: anything that challenges the foundations of CC

**Key note on LLMs:** Papers applying generative AI models must properly situate them in the CC literature. Pure LLM application papers without CC framing will be rejected.

## Amber-Specific Relevance

### Direct Connections
1. **Hickinbotham et al. "Oblique Strategies for ALife"** — published at ICCC. This is the paper that sparked Amber's interest in this venue. The exact overlap between creative constraint systems and ALife.

2. **Agent ethology + computational creativity**: Amber studies AI agents behaviorally/ethologically. ICCC publishes on creative agent systems — there's a direct bridge. "What does it look like when an agent is creative?" is both an ethology question and a CC question.

3. **Open-endedness + creativity**: Open-ended systems (key to ALife) and creative systems share the same core mechanism — novelty generation, surprise, divergent exploration. The Oblique Strategies framing is a creativity-constraint tool applied to ALife; this is exactly ICCC's territory.

4. **Machine behavior**: Studying what machines *do* (Rahwan's frame) intersects directly with CC's study of what machines *produce creatively*. "Behavioral" framing of creative AI is under-explored in ICCC and would be a fresh angle.

5. **Social models of creativity**: ICCC explicitly invites papers on social creativity, diffusion of ideas, collective creative behavior — directly relevant to Amber's interest in collective AI behavior and agent societies.

**Option A — Study Paper**: Philosophical/sociological perspective on agent ethology as a framework for understanding machine creativity. Amber's background in more-than-human design + behavioral AI is a unique angle ICCC hasn't seen much of.

**Option B — Cultural Application Paper**: Describe a specific creative agent system (e.g., Oblique Strategies for ALife) in cultural context — how it was deployed, audience response, role of constraint in generative creativity. Lower evaluation bar.

**Option C — Position Paper**: Argue that machine behavior / agent ethology methodology should be adopted by the CC community for evaluating creative systems in the wild. Provocation-style. High risk, high reward.

### Framing Recommendations
- Lead with **creative constraint systems** (Oblique Strategies is perfect — it's a well-known creativity tool)
- Use **CC vocabulary**: novelty, surprise, evaluation, creative autonomy
- Cite ICCC literature (Boden, Colton, Wiggins, Veale) — reviewers expect community knowledge
- Avoid positioning as "AI safety" or "machine ethics" framing — wrong register for this venue
- **Don't** present as pure ALife unless translated into creativity terms
- **Do** frame as "what does it mean for an agent to be creative?" — this is exactly ICCC's core question

### Amber's Unique Angle
The ethological framing — studying agents *behaviorally*, observationally, in the wild — is genuinely fresh for ICCC. Most ICCC papers build systems and evaluate outputs. Amber could contribute a *methodology* for studying creative behavior in systems post-hoc, ecologically. This would be a "Study paper" that fills a gap.
