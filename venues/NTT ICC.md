## Vibe
The flagship institution for Japanese media art, backed by telecom infrastructure money and intellectual ambition. ICC occupies the 4th floor of the Tokyo Opera City Tower — sharing a building with Tokyo Opera City Concert Hall and Tokyo Opera City Art Gallery, which creates a unique cross-pollination of contemporary art, music, and technology. The institution carries a genuine research mandate (NTT's own R&D labs exhibit here as "Research Complex NTT R&D @ICC"), making it simultaneously a gallery, a research facility, and a public cultural institution. Less overtly "festival" than Ars Electronica; more deliberately slow, archival, curatorial.

The soul of ICC is the long-duration relationship: artists return over decades, curators develop works with artists over years, and the archive (2,200+ participants documented in HIVE) is treated as primary intellectual output. This is an institution that takes media art *seriously* as a historical project.

## Mission & Scope
Founded to commemorate the **100th anniversary of telephone service in Japan (1990)**. ICC's preparatory phase began in 1990 — seven years of events, publications, and experimentation before the physical facility opened in April 1997. The founding journal *InterCommunication* (published from the preparatory phase) set the intellectual agenda: art, science, and technology in dialogue around the radical potential of communication networks.

Core programs:
- **Exhibitions**: Long-term "Open Space" annual exhibition (free admission lobby area) + paid special exhibitions
- **ICC Annual**: The annual flagship exhibition with a thematic focus
- **Kids Programs**: Summer holiday interactive programs (ongoing since founding)
- **Open Salons**: Public talks, symposia, performances with invited artists and scientists
- **Research Complex NTT R&D @ICC**: Showcase of NTT's own research in haptics, sensory systems, communication technology (ongoing since 2017)
- **HIVE**: Video archive (Creative Commons licensed) of 400+ videos — artist talks, symposia, live performances, video art collection — covering 1997–present
- **Hyper ICC**: Online program / virtual programming launched during COVID, continuing with "Garden Theater" (2025)
- **Channel ICC**: Podcast and blog

## Relationship to Japanese Media Art Scene
ICC is the *institutional anchor* of the Japanese media art scene — not its sole institution but its most prestigious:

- **Compared to [[YCAM]]** (Yamaguchi Center for Arts and Media): YCAM is more production/residency-focused, rural, collaborative. ICC is curatorial, urban, archival. Both commission major works. Many artists (Mikami, Manabe) work across both.
- **Compared to [[Japan Media Arts Festival]]**: The Festival is competitive and annual (prizes). ICC is a year-round institution with long-duration relationships and commissions.
- **Compared to [[Ars Electronica]]**: Ars is a global mega-festival. ICC is intimate, Tokyo-specific, with a 30-year institutional memory.
- **NTT funding logic**: NTT is a telecommunications giant. ICC's mission — "dialogue between art, science, and communication technology" — directly serves NTT's brand and legitimacy as cultural infrastructure. This gives ICC genuine resources for ambitious commissions.
- **The telecom-art connection**: ICC was conceived when the Internet was still forming (1990). Its founding intellectual project — what does networked communication mean for art, culture, self? — remains deeply contemporary.
- **Global role**: ICC has been a bridge between the Japanese media art world and international circuits (Ars Electronica, ZKM, transmediale). International artists regularly show alongside Japanese artists. The HIVE archive is CC-licensed and globally accessible.

## Why Interesting
ICC is not just a venue — it's the institutional memory of Japanese media art. With 2,200+ documented participants and a HIVE archive spanning 1997–present, it's the only institution that has been consistently holding space for rigorous art-technology dialogue in Japan over nearly three decades. It's simultaneously a model institution and an ongoing question: what does it mean to take "communication" as the founding concept of an art institution?
