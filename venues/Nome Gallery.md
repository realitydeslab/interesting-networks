# Nome Gallery

Contemporary art gallery in Berlin working at the intersection of art, technology, and politics. Founded 2015. Small, focused, politically committed — strong curatorial identity built around power, representation, and technological critique.

## Overview / Vibe

NOME is not a digital art gallery in the flashy sense — it's a **politically engaged gallery that uses digital and technology-based practices as a critical lens**. The program consistently asks: who has power over representation, surveillance, narrative, data? Founded by **Luca Barbeni** in 2015, it operates at a smaller scale with a tight curatorial identity: about 4–5 exhibitions per year, numbered sequentially (currently at No. 56 as of late 2025).

The vibe: serious, politically aware, internationally connected, critical of technological systems rather than celebrating them. Artists here are not building cool AI tools — they're examining **what AI, surveillance, colonialism, and data governance do to people**. Adjacent to James Bridle's worldview: opacity, power, counter-narrative.

Address: [Potsdamer Str. 72, 10785 Berlin](https://nomegallery.com/address/) — open Thu–Sat, by appointment.

## Submission / Relationship Path

NOME does **not** have open calls, residencies, or submission portals. It is a **director-run gallery with an invitation-only program**. Artists get in through:

1. **Sustained relationship with Luca Barbeni** — he programs based on personal research and curatorial vision
2. **Being in the international art-tech circuit** — artists shown at Transmediale, HeK Basel, Ars Electronica, Serpentine tend to cross into NOME's radar
3. **Artist referrals** — existing NOME artists (especially [[James Bridle]]) likely introduce new names
4. **Art fairs** — NOME participates in fairs ([nomegallery.com/fairs/](https://nomegallery.com/fairs/)); meeting Barbeni at fair openings is a realistic entry point

**Practical path for Amber:** Attend a NOME opening during Berlin Art Week (September) or gallery season. Email [info@nomegallery.com](mailto:info@nomegallery.com) to introduce work. James Bridle connection is the most direct bridge — Bridle is NOME's longest relationship (since 2015) and has curated for them.

## Network Position

NOME operates in Berlin's **critical digital art ecosystem**, not the mainstream gallery circuit. Key connections:

- **[[Transmediale]]** — thematic overlap; artists like Paolo Cirio appear in both
- **HeK Basel** (House of Electronic Arts) — overlapping artist roster; similar critical-tech positioning
- **Ars Electronica** — some artist crossover (Quayola, Joris Strijbos)
- **Serpentine Galleries** (London) — James Bridle's UK counterpart gallery; NOME is his Berlin home
- **Berlin Art Week** — NOME regularly participates (confirmed 2025: No. 56 opened during Berlin Art Week)
- **Art fairs** — likely LISTE, abc Berlin, possibly NADA

NOME is **not** connected to the ZKM/academic research circuit directly, but occupies a similar cultural position (critical tech art) from a commercial gallery angle. It is small-to-mid tier commercially but punches above its weight curatorially.

## Why Interesting for Amber

**Alignment:**
- [[James Bridle]] is Amber's primary connection — *Ways of Being* (2022) is directly relevant to more-than-human design and non-human agency. Bridle's work on machine perception ("Failing to Distinguish...") touches Amber's agent ethology interests.
- The **AGENCY** show (2018) explicitly addressed individual agency vs. opaque computational governance — the philosophical core of Amber's machine behavior research.
- **Computational creativity / generative systems**: Quayola's show (2022–23) demonstrates NOME occasionally programs pure computational aesthetics work. This is the opening for Amber's type of practice.
- **"Accurate Misreadings"** (2025) included works engaging algorithmic codes and bureaucratic documents — adjacent to critical computing.

**Misalignment:**
- NOME's current roster is **more politically oriented** (decolonial, surveillance, Black/Indigenous counter-narratives) than Amber's core interests (ALife, agent ethology, somaesthetics). Amber's work is more speculative-science than activist-political.
- No ALife, no emergent systems, no multispecies/more-than-human framing in the current program.
- NOME does not show interactive or experiential work — it's primarily wall-based, print, video, performance documentation.

**Verdict:** NOME is a **tangential but real** connection — the James Bridle relationship is genuine, and if Amber's work develops a critical/political dimension alongside its speculative-science dimension, NOME becomes more relevant. Right now: monitor, don't chase. If Bridle ever curates at NOME again, that's the moment.

**Rating: ⭐⭐** — influential gallery for critical tech art, but not Amber's primary lane. Worth knowing, not worth actively pursuing right now.
