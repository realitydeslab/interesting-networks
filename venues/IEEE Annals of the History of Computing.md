# IEEE Annals of the History of Computing

The flagship peer-reviewed journal for the history of computing. Published by [IEEE Computer Society](https://www.computer.org/csdl/magazine/an) since 1992 (originally *Annals of the History of Computing* from 1979, when it was founded by the American Federation of Information Processing Societies with Bernard Galler as first editor-in-chief). Quarterly.

This is where computing historians publish. It has evolved significantly over 45 years — from a technical/institutional focus (hardware, architectures, corporate histories) toward a broader, more socially and politically conscious history of computing that incorporates STS, feminist, labor, and critical race perspectives. [[Stephanie Dick]] and colleagues are part of the generation pushing this shift.

**Article types include:** Scholarly articles, interviews, "think pieces," and memoirs by computer pioneers — broader format palette than a traditional research journal. This makes it accessible for practice-based researchers.

## Topics

The journal covers the history of computing broadly — but has a distinctive mixture of:

**Traditional (still core):**
- Hardware architecture and systems history (Babbage → ENIAC → transistor revolution)
- Institutional histories (Bell Labs, IBM, PARC, government computing programs)
- Computer pioneer biographies and oral histories
- Programming language history

**Emerging (increasingly prominent):**
- **Labor and gender in computing** — who actually built it; women programmers, operators, coders
- **Software infrastructure and maintenance** — the invisible work that keeps systems running (Dick & Volmar's "DLL Hell" is exemplary)
- **Computing and social control** — databases, surveillance, policing (FBI's NCIC, welfare surveillance)
- **STS approaches** — treating computing as sociotechnical system, not just technical artifact
- **AI and machine learning history** — growing rapidly as a subfield
- **Global and non-Western computing history** — expanding beyond US/UK origin stories

## Stephanie Dick's Role & Award

[[Stephanie Dick]] won the journal's **2018 Best Paper Award** with Daniel Volmar for ["DLL Hell: Software Dependencies, Failure, and the Maintenance of Microsoft Windows"](https://ieeexplore.ieee.org/document/8509170) (*IEEE Annals* 2018). DOI: 10.1109/MAHC.2018.042462

This paper — a labor and infrastructure history of Windows' catastrophic dependency system — is exactly the kind of work the journal is now rewarding: socially engaged, technically precise, historically rigorous, and politically consequential. It signals what the journal values in the current moment.

Dick also serves on the editorial board, which means she shapes what gets published and how the field evolves.

## Submission Guidelines Summary

*(Based on IEEE Annals editorial policies; verify at [computer.org/csdl/magazine/an](https://www.computer.org/csdl/magazine/an) before submitting)*

- **Research articles**: 5,000–8,000 words typical (broader range than many journals)
- **Think pieces**: Shorter, more speculative/analytical; ~3,000–5,000 words
- **Interviews**: With computer pioneers, historians; editorial discretion
- **Memoirs/Retrospectives**: First-person accounts by computing pioneers; editorial commission
- **Style**: Chicago Manual or IEEE style; footnotes acceptable
- **Submission**: Via ScholarOne (IEEE manuscript system)
- **Peer review**: 2–3 reviewers; double-blind
- **Turnaround**: 3–5 months typical
- **OA option**: IEEE Author-Pay OA available (fee required)

**Practical note:** The journal is more accessible than *Isis* for Amber's kind of work — it values "think pieces" alongside traditional research, and its STS turn means practice-based and theoretically-oriented pieces are increasingly welcome. The IEEE pedigree also means it's taken seriously by engineering-adjacent communities (CS, HCI, ML) who would never read *Isis*.
