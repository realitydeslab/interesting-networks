## ⚡ Recent Updates

Formal/mathematical approaches to consciousness. Where physics, philosophy, mathematics, and AI consciousness converge. Surprisingly open: "contributed talk applications are open to ANY topic in the field." Not a closed neuroscience club — AI consciousness, machine sentience, and artificial cognition are recurring core themes. Running since 2019, small (~100–200 people), intimate discussion format. **Oxford-connected**: OMCAN (Oxford Mathematics of Consciousness and Applications Network) co-organizes; Yakov Kremnitzer (Oxford Mathematical Institute) on Advisory Board.

## Topics

MoC's scope has three stable recurring axes, plus an open "anything goes" contributed talks track:

1. **Philosophy of Consciousness & Mathematized Phenomenology** — formal phenomenology, structuralism, quality spaces, qualia structure, IIT
2. **Quantum Approaches to Consciousness** — Orch OR (Penrose-Hameroff), quantum cognition, local friendliness
3. **Research on Artificial Consciousness** ← **most relevant to Amber** — AI sentience, synthetic phenomenology, consciousness in LLMs, digital minds, machine consciousness frameworks

Key theories discussed across editions:
- **IIT (Integrated Information Theory)** — Tononi et al.
- **Global Workspace Theory (GWT)** — Baars, Dehaene
- **Predictive Processing / Active Inference** — Friston, Wiese
- **Enactivism / 4E Cognition** — Froese, Varela tradition
- **Minimal Phenomenal Experience (MPE)** — Metzinger, meditation-as-window
- **Process philosophy** — Whitehead tradition (Prentner's work)
- **Category theory approaches** — Lorenz, Taguchi

## Why Interesting

MoC occupies a rare niche: it is rigorous (mathematical, formal) AND genuinely interested in machine consciousness as a first-class problem. Most HCI/AI venues treat consciousness as a metaphor. Most consciousness science venues are purely neuroscience. MoC is the overlap zone where "what if AI is actually conscious?" is taken seriously with mathematical tools.

For Amber, working on machine death, machine behavior, and the ethics of potentially-conscious AI systems, this is a natural congregation point. The questions MoC asks — what makes a system conscious? what would we even measure? — are prerequisites for the moral and design questions Amber's work raises.

The community is also notably non-dogmatic (AMCS explicitly calls for "open-minded, unprejudiced, and non-dogmatic research") — rare in consciousness science, which has historical IIT vs. GWT factions. This openness extends to format: no proceedings, just talks, which is low-barrier for experimental/design-forward work.

MoC7 in Shanghai in 2026 is a potentially extraordinary convergence: Amber's research angle, her Shanghai connections, and a venue that would welcome precisely her kind of work.
