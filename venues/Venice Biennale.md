# Venice Biennale (La Biennale di Venezia)

The world's oldest and most prestigious art biennial. Founded 1895 in Venice, Italy. The symbolic center of the global contemporary art world — where national cultural diplomacy, curatorial theory, and artistic experimentation collide at maximum visibility. Winning the Golden Lion is the highest honor in contemporary art. Approximately 90 countries maintain national pavilions. Attendance: over 800,000 visitors at the 2022 Art Exhibition alone.

## Structure & Format

La Biennale di Venezia comprises **six disciplines** operating year-round:

| Discipline | Frequency | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Art Biennale** | Even years (since 2021) | Main exhibition + national pavilions. Flagship. |
| **Architecture Biennale** | Odd years (since 2021) | Giardini + Arsenale. Second most prestigious. |
| **Venice Film Festival** | Annual (Aug–Sep) | Est. 1932, the world's oldest film festival. |
| **Biennale Musica** | Annual | Contemporary music. Est. 1930. |
| **Biennale Teatro** | Annual | International theatre festival. Est. 1934. |
| **Biennale Danza** | Annual | Contemporary dance. Est. 1999. |

**Note on scheduling shift:** COVID-19 forced postponements in 2020–2021 that permanently shifted the calendar. Art Biennale now runs in even years; Architecture in odd years.

### Venues
- **Giardini della Biennale (Castello):** ~30 permanent national pavilions built by individual countries. The most prestigious address.
- **Arsenale (Corderie + Artiglierie):** The main international exhibition + overflow pavilions for countries without Giardini buildings.
- **Around Venice:** Additional national pavilions and collateral events in palazzos, churches, and public spaces throughout the city.

## Topics Covered

The Venice Art Biennale's thematic arc reveals what the global art world considers urgent:

- **Decoloniality & Global South** — Enwezor 2015, Pedrosa 2024, Kouoh 2026 ("In Minor Keys" = subaltern voices)

- **Intelligence (natural/artificial/collective)** — Ratti 2025 (most relevant to Amber)
- **African + decolonial futures** — Lokko 2023
- **Housing & collective living** — Sarkis 2021

## Recent Editions (Detailed)

### 61st Art Biennale 2026 — "In Minor Keys" (Koyo Kouoh)
- **Dates:** May 9 – November 22, 2026 (preview May 6–8)
- **Director:** Koyo Kouoh (1967–2025); Cameroonian-Swiss curator, Executive Director of Zeitz MOCAA
- **Posthumous:** Kouoh died unexpectedly on May 10, 2025. La Biennale decided to proceed with her exhibition exactly as she conceived it.
- **Theme:** "In Minor Keys" — evoking subalternity, minor modes, voices outside the dominant register. Politically and aesthetically charged: minor keys in music carry tension, melancholy, complexity; "minor" also invokes marginalized positions.
- **Significance:** A tribute to a major African curator who was reshaping how Western institutions reckon with African contemporary art. The posthumous execution is itself a statement about legacy, care, and institutional responsibility.

### 60th Art Biennale 2024 — "Stranieri Ovunque / Foreigners Everywhere" (Adriano Pedrosa)
- **Dates:** April 20 – November 24, 2024 (preview April 17–19)
- **Director:** [Adriano Pedrosa](https://www.masp.org.br/en) (b. 1965, Brazil); Artistic Director of MASP São Paulo
- **Theme:** "Stranieri Ovunque" (Foreigners Everywhere) — title borrowed from Paris-born, Palermo-based collective Claire Fontaine's neon text works. Explored displacement, diaspora, the outsider as insider.
- **Focus:** Global South, indigenous, queer, and diaspora artists. Featured many artists outside the traditional Euro-American circuit. Notable: Pope Francis visited the Holy See pavilion on April 28, 2024 — first pontiff at the Biennale.
- **Golden Lion (Lifetime):** Anna Maria Maiolino (Brazilian-Italian artist)
- **Golden Lion (National Pavilion):** Australia (Archie Moore — "kith and kin", Aboriginal lineage installation) — over 65,000 names of Aboriginal people traced on blackboard
- **Significance:** Canonized the "global art history" perspective Pedrosa had developed at MASP. Shifted the Biennale toward non-Western art histories as its curatorial center.

### 59th Art Biennale 2022 — "The Milk of Dreams" (Cecilia Alemani)
- **Dates:** April 23 – November 27, 2022
- **Director:** [Cecilia Alemani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cecilia_Alemani); Italian, Chief Curator of High Line Art NYC
- **Theme:** "The Milk of Dreams" — after Leonora Carrington's book of magical tales. Three axes: body/metamorphosis, human-technology relations, body-Earth relations.
- **Feminist + posthuman:** <10% male artists; invokes Silvia Federici and Ursula Le Guin. Nearly half the artists are deceased — recovering underrecognized avant-garde figures (many women, non-Western).
- **Attendance:** 800,000+ visitors
- **Golden Lion (Best Participant):** Simone Leigh (first Black woman to represent the US)
- **Golden Lion (Best National Pavilion):** UK (Sonia Boyce)
- **Significance:** Post-COVID return; strong posthumanist framing aligned with the then-emerging more-than-human discourse.

### 58th Art Biennale 2019 — "May You Live in Interesting Times" (Ralph Rugoff)
- **Dates:** May 11 – November 24, 2019
- **Director:** [Ralph Rugoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Rugoff); British, Director of Hayward Gallery London
- **Theme:** "May You Live in Interesting Times" — the (apocryphal) Chinese curse, deployed amid Brexit/Trump turbulence
- **Golden Lion (Lifetime):** Jimmie Durham (Cherokee-American sculptor)
- **Golden Lion (Best Pavilion):** Lithuania

### Architecture Biennale 2025 — "Intelligens. Natural. Artificial. Collective." (Carlo Ratti)
- **Dates:** May 10 – November 23, 2025
- **Director:** [Carlo Ratti](https://www.carloratti.com/); Italian architect; MIT Senseable City Lab director
- **Theme:** Three forms of intelligence — natural (biological), artificial (computational), collective (social/organizational). 750+ participants from architecture, climate science, AI, philosophy, farming, cooking.
- **Significance:** Most direct overlap with Amber's interests: AI × collective intelligence × ecological thinking. A Venice Biennale explicitly centered on AI and collective behavior.

### Architecture Biennale 2023 — "The Laboratory of the Future" (Lesley Lokko)
- **Dates:** May 20 – November 26, 2023
- **Director:** [Lesley Lokko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lesley_Lokko); Ghanaian-Scottish architect; founder of [African Futures Institute](https://africanfuturesinstitute.com/) in Accra, Ghana
- **Theme:** Architecture as a laboratory for more equitable, optimistic futures. Centered African and Global South perspectives.
- **Significance:** Decolonial futures meets spatial design; connects to speculative design traditions Amber cares about.

## Notable Historical Moments (Curatatorial Significance)

- **1964:** Robert Rauschenberg wins Grand Prize — Venice Biennale imports Pop Art into canon. First American to win.
- **1972:** First thematic edition — "Opera o comportamento" (Work or Behaviour) — shift from survey to argument
- **1980:** Architecture section established; Arsenale opened to the public
- **1993:** Harald Szeemann + Achille Bonito Oliva introduce "Aperto" — section for emerging art
- **1999–2001:** Szeemann expands Asian/Eastern European representation significantly
- **2003 (50th):** Francesco Bonami + 7 co-curators including HUO, Hou Hanru, Catherine David, Massimiliano Gioni — most decentralized curation in Biennale history
- **2009 Mexico Pavilion (53rd):** [[Cuauhtémoc Medina]] curates [[Teresa Margolles]], "¿De qué otra cosa podríamos hablar?" — landmark political art in national pavilion context
- **2015:** Okwui Enwezor — first African director; centers Marx's Das Kapital as score
- **2022:** Simone Leigh — first Black woman to represent the US; Golden Lion

## Fellowships & Programmes

La Biennale does not offer open-call fellowships in the traditional sense — participation is by invitation through national cultural ministries or the artistic director. However:

- **[Biennale College — Arte](https://www.labiennale.org/en/art/2024/biennale-college-arte)** — Workshop + production grant program for emerging artists. Applications open; small grants for new productions. [Application URL](https://www.labiennale.org/en/biennale-college)
- **[Biennale College — Cinema](https://www.labiennale.org/en/cinema/biennale-college-cinema)** — Film development program for emerging filmmakers (feature + VR/AR). Highly competitive, global applications.
- **[Biennale College — Dance, Music, Theatre](https://www.labiennale.org/en/biennale-college)** — Similar programs across disciplines.
- **Accreditation/Press:** Academic researchers can apply for press credentials to attend the opening week — closest thing to direct access for researchers.
- **National Pavilion Route:** Artists are selected by national cultural ministries/institutes. For UK: [British Council](https://www.britishcouncil.org/arts/visual-arts/venice-biennale); For China: Ministry of Culture; etc.

Venice Biennale is the gravitational center of the global contemporary art world. It's not where most of the interesting experimental work happens — but it's where experimental work gets canonized, where curatorial ideas get tested at planetary scale, and where the art-science-politics intersection gets its most visible expression. ## Why Interesting

Venice Biennale isn't primarily a venue for the experimental work Amber does — it's the place where experimental work gets legitimized into the canon. The recent curatorial arc (decolonial, posthuman, non-Western, feminist) closely tracks the intellectual movements in Amber's research taste: more-than-human design, non-Western tech-philosophy, and speculative practice-based research.

The 2025 Architecture Biennale ("Intelligens") was a direct overlap: an exhibition about AI, natural intelligence, and collective intelligence that pulled in architects, coders, farmers, philosophers together. This is the Venice Biennale operating at the intersection of Amber's exact research interests.

The Venice Biennale also teaches something about *scale and framing*: the most impactful curatorial gestures here are not about individual artworks but about *what question gets posed* to 800,000 visitors. Cecilia Alemani asked: what does it mean to be human after posthumanism? Adriano Pedrosa asked: whose art history counts? Koyo Kouoh was going to ask: whose voice is minor?

These are questions Amber's research implicitly asks too — about machines, about non-Western AI, about who defines what agents do. Venice Biennale is worth tracking as a bellwether for how these questions move from academic discourse into cultural mainstream.
