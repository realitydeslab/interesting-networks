# ACM DIS (Designing Interactive Systems)

The premier international venue for design research that takes interaction seriously as a political, aesthetic, and ecological act. DIS is where the field's most critical voices argue about what design is *for*, who it serves, and whether "interaction" is even the right frame anymore. Ranked 12th in HCI globally (Google Scholar), ~25% acceptance rate, Tier A publication venue. Annual, rotating locations.

Not a typical HCI conference. DIS attracts researchers who care about the thing-ness of computing, the politics of design, and the messiness of making — researchers who use words like "posthuman," "somaesthetic," "decolonial," and "ontological turn" and mean them seriously.

## Topics & Contribution Areas

DIS has **five formal subcommittees** — each is essentially a research agenda in itself:

### 1. Critical Computing and Design Theory
The political and philosophical heart of DIS. Reimagining AND critiquing technologies — the ethical, societal, and power dimensions of interactive systems. Epistemologically pluralistic: welcomes feminist, decolonial, queer, postcolonial, and Marxist approaches. Papers that question technological progress itself are welcome.

Key example papers:
- "Designing with Decolonial Intent: Towards a Decolonial Archive in Resistance to Epistemicide" (DIS 2025)
- "Can Smartness Fail? The Charisma of High Tech as Class Politics" (DIS 2025)
- "Designing within Capitalism" (DIS 2022)

### 2. Research Through Design (RtD)
Design as a mode of inquiry — not designing *for* research, but designing *as* research. Annotated portfolios, design experiments, speculative artifacts that carry and generate knowledge. First-class contribution type since DIS introduced it.

### 3. Design Methods and Processes
Documentation, innovation, and advancement of design practice. Participatory design, co-design, generative methods, methods for more-than-human contexts.

### 4. Artifacts and Systems
Building and evaluating actual systems. Tangible interfaces, AR/VR, fabrication, sensing, unconventional interactions, physical-digital entanglement. Strong materiality focus.

### 5. AI and Design
Design *for* AI, design *with* AI, design *of* AI agents and robots. Responsible design of AI, AI implications for design practice. Papers must contribute to *design* knowledge, not just AI metrics.

### Other Track Formats
- **Pictorials** — visual-first publication format (introduced at DIS 2014 in Vancouver). Images lead, text annotates. Max 12 pages. First-class archival publication. Perfect for design-heavy, practice-based, or speculative work.
- **Works-in-Progress (PWiP) / Provocations** — shorter, in-progress ideas. Good for emerging research directions.
- **Workshops** — 2 days pre-conference; can be full-day or half-day.
- **Doctoral Consortium** — PhD students present and get mentorship. Small, intimate (≤12 participants). DC papers published in proceedings.
- **Demonstrations / Interactivity** — hands-on demos of interactive systems.
- **Art Track** — Occasional (appeared at DIS 2025). Artworks as research contributions.
- **Student Design Competition** — design challenges for students.

## Why Interesting

DIS is one of the few venues where "more-than-human" is not a fringe position but the leading edge of the field. Where Anne Haaning can keynote about accelerationism and Silicon Valley's quasi-religious ideology. Where Stacy Alaimo keynotes about posthumanism and deep-sea creaturely aesthetics. Where a paper on "decolonial archives in resistance to epistemicide" is not just accepted but celebrated.

**The 2026 theme "Beyond Interaction" is a paradigm-shift moment.** The community is questioning its own foundational concept — "interaction" itself — and asking whether it's too anthropocentric, too dyadic, too limited. This is edge-of-chaos territory: the field is actively reshaping itself.

Three things make DIS specifically valuable for Amber:

1. **Research Through Design is a first-class contribution type.** Not a tolerance, not a track for art projects that couldn't get into the main venue — a fully peer-reviewed, proceedings-published contribution path that treats making as a way of knowing. Amber's practice-based work belongs here.

2. **Critical Computing track has teeth.** Unlike mainstream CHI where critique can feel performative, DIS Critical Computing welcomes papers that "question or confront technological progress" and take "epistemologically pluralistic approaches." A paper arguing against AI solutionism or for non-Western technology epistemologies would find a serious audience.

3. **Pictorials as format.** A 12-page visual-first publication format, archival quality, same prestige as papers. For speculative design work, installation-derived research, or design process documentation, this is the right format. Most other venues don't have this.

The Pirate Paper Night (DIS 2025) — an unofficial event celebrating rejected papers — reveals something important about this community: they know the field's most interesting ideas sometimes arrive in the wrong format or too early for consensus.

DIS is where the design research community's most interesting edge lives. Amber should be publishing here.
