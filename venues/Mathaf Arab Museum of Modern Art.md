# Mathaf: Arab Museum of Modern Art

The world's largest collection of modern and contemporary Arab art — 9,000+ works spanning the 1840s to now — housed in a transformed school building in Doha's Education City. Part of the [Qatar Museums](https://qm.org.qa/) network, Mathaf is simultaneously a serious scholarly institution, a soft-power instrument of Qatar's National Vision 2030, and a genuinely ambitious site for international contemporary programming. It has hosted [[Raqs Media Collective]], Cai Guo-Qiang, Mona Hatoum, El Anatsui, Shirin Neshat, Kader Attia, and many more — making it the most important museum platform for Arab modern art globally, with strong international crossover.

**Owner:** [Qatar Museums](https://qm.org.qa/) (formerly Qatar Museums Authority, est. 2005)  
**Director:** [Zeina Arida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeina_Arida) (since November 2021; prev. director of Sursock Museum, Beirut)  
**Website:** [mathaf.org.qa](https://mathaf.org.qa/en/)  
**Contact:** mathaf@qm.org.qa | +974 4402 8830

### History

The collection was initiated in the **early 1990s** by Sheikh **Hassan bin Mohamed bin Ali Al Thani**, who began amassing Arab art from the previous 200 years with the explicit aim of building a museum that could represent the full breadth of Arab artistic production. From the 1990s–early 2000s, works were stored in private villas in Madinat Khalifa while the conceptual architecture of what an "Arab perspective on modern and contemporary art and museums" could be was debated.

Founding team member and artist **Sophia Al-Maria** describes the early intention as creating a "twenty-first century 'post-museum'...a fledgling term for transparent, interaction-oriented museum models." This ambition — not just a repository but an experimental institutional form — has inflected Mathaf's programming ever since.

The museum opened in **December 2010** as the first museum in Qatar dedicated to modern and contemporary art. Sheikh Hassan donated the collection to the Qatar Foundation; it was later acquired by the Qatar Museums Authority. **Sheikha Al-Mayassa Al Thani** (chairwoman of Qatar Museums) stated at opening: "We are making Qatar the place to see, explore and discuss the creations of Arab artists of the modern era and of our own time."

Doha was named **Arab Capital of Culture 2010** — the Mathaf opening was the centrepiece event.

Mathaf has consistently shown **video art and media-based practices** within its contemporary programming:

- **Shirin Neshat** — photography and video (Afterwards, 2014–15)

- **Kader Attia** — installation and video (On Silence, 2021–22)
- **"Your Ghosts Are Mine: Expanded Cinemas, Amplified Voices"** (April–Aug 2025) — expanded cinema exhibition, directly engaging with media, film, and voice amplification practices

While not primarily a "new media" museum, Mathaf's contemporary programming consistently includes **time-based media, video installation, and experimental practice** alongside painting and sculpture. The 2025 expanded cinema show signals continued media-art interest.

## Raqs Media Collective Connection

### *Still More World* (2019)
**Exhibition:** Raqs Media Collective: *Still More World*  
**Curator:** Laura Barlow  
**Year:** 2019  
**Venue:** Mathaf: Arab Museum of Modern Art, Doha  
**Source:** [Wikipedia/Mathaf exhibition list](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathaf:_Arab_Museum_of_Modern_Art)

*Still More World* was Raqs Media Collective's **solo exhibition at Mathaf** — a significant institutional endorsement of their practice in the Gulf context. The title is characteristic of Raqs's philosophical register: "still more world" as in the world's ongoing excess, its refusal to be fully accounted for, the surplus that remains after every mapping. Raqs's practice — spanning installation, video, text, and philosophical speculation — was here positioned within the Arab contemporary art circuit, creating a bridge between their New Delhi base and the Gulf.

Raqs (Monica Narula, Shuddha Sengupta, Jeebesh Bagchi) work at the intersection of philosophy, media theory, political imagination, and art. Their choice of Mathaf as a venue for a solo show reflects the museum's ambition to bridge Global South artistic practices — not just the Arab world but diasporic and international contemporary art that resonates with questions of modernity, time, and historical imagination.

### *Alive, with Cerussite and Peppered Moth* (2017)
**Work:** *Alive, with Cerussite and Peppered Moth*  
**Year:** 2017  
**Status:** Permanent collection, Mathaf  

This work entered Mathaf's permanent collection in 2017 — meaning it predates the solo exhibition and signals the museum's sustained relationship with Raqs's practice. Cerussite (a lead carbonate mineral) and the Peppered Moth (famous for industrial melanism — evolution driven by pollution) are both signatures of Raqs's interest in **geological time, evolutionary change, and the entanglement of biology with human industrial history**. Exactly the kind of more-than-human, speculative-scientific framing that characterizes their strongest work.

**Why this matters for the Raqs/Mathaf relationship:** The museum acquired the work in 2017, mounted the solo show in 2019. This is a multi-year institutional relationship — not a one-off invitation. Mathaf made a considered bet on Raqs as a significant voice.

## Gulf Futurism Connection

**[Gulf Futurism](https://www.artangel.org.uk/project/the-future-is-not-what-it-used-to-be/)** is a term coined by **Sophia Al-Maria** (early Mathaf staff member) and artist **Fatima Al Qasimi** to describe the aesthetic and political condition of rapid, oil-funded modernization in the Gulf — the coexistence of hyper-modern infrastructure with deep conservatism, the erasure of history through construction, the alienation of migrant labor, the uncanny quality of Dubai and Doha as cities built on sand by people who will never belong there.

Mathaf sits at the **epicenter of this discourse**:
- It is a product of the same cultural-political project Gulf Futurism critiques
- Yet it has actively hosted artists who critique and interrogate that project (Wael Shawky, Kader Attia, Mounira Al Solh)
- Sophia Al-Maria herself moved from early Mathaf collaborator to critic of Gulf Futurism's conditions
- The museum operates the tension between **legitimizing Qatar's global cultural ambition** and **providing space for critical Arab contemporary practice**

This tension is productive, not paralyzing. Mathaf has shown some of the most important critical artists of the Arab world precisely because it can afford to — the soft-power framing gives cover for uncomfortable content. Compare: how MoMA or Tate can show radical art because institutional prestige absorbs the critique.

**Key theorists of Gulf Futurism:**
- Sophia Al-Maria — [*The Girl Who Fell to Earth*](https://www.harpercollins.com/products/the-girl-who-fell-to-earth-sophia-al-maria) (memoir, 2012)
- Fatima Al Qasimi — visual artist, collaborator with Al-Maria
- Wark McKenzie on Gulf infrastructural aesthetics

## Circuit Significance

Showing at Mathaf signals:
1. **Recognition within the Arab/Gulf art circuit** — the most prestigious Arab modern art institution globally
2. **International art-world validation** — Mathaf operates at the level of Tate, MoMA, Centre Pompidou in terms of budget and ambition; being shown here is comparable prestige
3. **Global South bridge** — Mathaf actively programs non-Arab international artists (Cai Guo-Qiang, [[Raqs Media Collective]], El Anatsui, Mona Hatoum) placing them in dialogue with Arab modernism, creating a non-Western-to-non-Western conversation outside the Euro-American art circuit
4. **Soft-power endorsement** — shows enter the Qatar Museums archive, gain permanent collection consideration, participate in a state-backed visibility infrastructure

For Raqs specifically: a Mathaf solo show places their practice in the context of Arab modernism and positions them as a bridge figure between South Asian critical media practice and the Gulf contemporary scene.

## Why Interesting

Mathaf is interesting for three intersecting reasons:

**1. Institutional ambition meets critical practice.** The museum was conceived as a "post-museum" and has hosted some of the most important critical artists working in/on the Arab world and Global South. It doesn't fit the reductive narrative of Gulf oil money + prestige art = cultural washing — the programming is too rigorous, the curators too serious.

**2. Non-Western art-world axis.** Mathaf represents a Doha–New Delhi–Beirut–Cairo–Casablanca axis of contemporary art that doesn't route through London or New York. For Amber's growing interest in non-Western theoretical traditions (cosmotechnics, sinofuturism), understanding the Gulf contemporary art ecosystem is essential peripheral knowledge.

**3. Raqs Media Collective as the bridge.** Raqs connects Mathaf to everything in Amber's intellectual world: media theory, political imagination, geological time, more-than-human thinking, collective practice. The Mathaf/Raqs relationship is a fact worth understanding in depth.
