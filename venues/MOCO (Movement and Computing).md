# MOCO — International Conference on Movement and Computing

The premier interdisciplinary conference at the intersection of movement and computing. Not a CHI sub-track, not a niche workshop — a dedicated community with its own steering committee, ACM proceedings, and decade of continuity. MOCO is where dancers, choreographers, neuroscientists, HCI researchers, machine learning engineers, and performance artists find common ground in the body as a computational object. Equal parts rigorous and radically open: practice works (performances, installations, workshops) share the stage with academic papers.

Founded 2014 at IRCAM-Centre Pompidou, Paris. Now in its 12th year (MOCO'26 = 10th conference, MOCO is the "initiative" that started in 2014). Co-founded by [[Thecla Schiphorst]] (Simon Fraser University) and [[Frédéric Bevilacqua]] (IRCAM).

## Mission & Scope

> "MOCO aims to gather academics and practitioners interested in the computational study, modelling, representation, segmentation, recognition, classification, or generation of movement information. MOCO is positioned within emerging interdisciplinary domains between art & science."

At its core, MOCO holds a productive tension: computation wants to **abstract** movement into data; the body exceeds all such abstraction. Every edition explores this gap — not to resolve it, but to live productively inside it.

Key intellectual threads:
- Movement analysis & recognition (Laban, machine learning, motion capture)
- Dance and technology (choreographic tools, live performance systems)
- Embodied cognition and interaction design
- Somatic practice and computation
- Gesture-sound-music relationships
- Wearables and movement tracking
- Robotics and artificial agents with movement expressivity
- Critical and philosophical perspectives on embodiment and digitality
- Health applications of movement computation (growing focus)
- Surveillance, biometrics, race and movement computation (growing critical thread)

### MOCO'24 — Beyond Control (Utrecht)
10th anniversary edition. Hosted at Faculty of Humanities, Utrecht University, in collaboration with [SPRING Performing Arts Festival](https://spring-festival.nl/). Theme drew on critical theory (Seb Franklin's *Control: Digitality as Cultural Logic*), posthumanism, and feminist new materialism (Barad, Haraway, Bennett). The humanities turn was explicit — asked: what does control mean in movement computation, and how do we get beyond it?

Conference Chair: **Laura Karreman** (Utrecht University)
Scientific Program Chair: **Maaike Bleeker** (Utrecht University)
Demo/Artistic Chair: **Evelyn Wan** (Utrecht University)
Workshop Chair: **Kim Baraka** (Free University of Amsterdam)

## Submission & Format

MOCO accepts:
1. **Research papers** — long (8pp) or short (4pp), double-blind peer review, published in ACM Digital Library
2. **Practice works** — performances, installations, demos, workshops, games. Peer-reviewed. **No payment for commissions.**
3. **Doctoral Consortium** — PhD/MFA students present work in progress
4. **Open track** — short abstracts (2pp max), not in ACM DL, published via HAL/Zenodo

All proceedings indexed in [ACM Digital Library](https://dl.acm.org/). **Note (2026):** ACM has introduced APCs (article processing charges) for authors not at ACM Open institutions — check your institution's status before submitting.

Submission system: varies by year (EasyChair, Oxford Abstracts, SciencesConf)

MOCO's ethos: "artistic and technical contributions are synergistic and equally valued." This is not lip service — practice works get equal program time.

### SloMoCo (2021) — The Pandemic Experiment
When COVID hit, the MOCO community didn't just move to Zoom. They built [SloMoCo](https://moco21.movementcomputing.org/): a durational, distributed, telematic experiment running across three phases (Spring/Summer/Fall 2021). Micro-residencies, seminars, practice works, paper sessions — all spread over a year. Published via [Manifold](https://manifoldapp.org/) rather than ACM DL. A self-aware meditation on what a conference can be when the body is both the subject and can't be in the room.

## Fellowships & Support

- **Artist Vouchers** — free registration for independent artists without institutional affiliation (introduced MOCO'24, continued MOCO'26). Apply to conference organizers with bio + motivation.
- **Student travel support** — limited grants available each year for accepted student work
- **Doctoral Consortium** — dedicated session for PhD/MFA students; community of practice with field experts
- MOCO has no central funding body — relies on host institution + registration fees. Low-fee ethos.

## Why Interesting

MOCO occupies a unique niche: it insists that **movement is not reducible to data**. The whole conference is built around the irreducibility of bodily experience to computational representation — and yet it also builds those representations, rigorously. This productive tension is exactly Amber's territory.

**Connects to research-taste.md:**
- **Embodied interaction** — this is MOCO's core. Kia Höök's somaesthetics, body-first design.
- **Art-science bridging** — MOCO insists on this structurally. Practice works and papers are equal.
- **Critical computing** — growing thread: race, surveillance, glitch feminism, "beyond control." MOCO'24's theme was explicitly critical-theoretical.
- **More-than-human** — the posthumanist turn is visible in MOCO'24's references (Barad, Haraway, Bennett). This is where MOCO is going.
- **Small over big** — MOCO is intimate (~150-200 people). The vibe is collegial, not overwhelming. This is Amber's kind of venue.
- **Practice-based** — Amber's HoloKit + somaesthetic realities work fits perfectly here as a practice work.

The SloMoCo experiment is also a signal: this is a community that thinks reflexively about what a conference is and should be. They don't just run conferences — they experiment with knowledge production forms.
