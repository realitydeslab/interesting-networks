## Overview

NordiCHI is a biennial international HCI conference, rotating through the Nordic countries every two years since 2000. Hosted by ACM SIGCHI's Nordic chapter (Nordic HCI Network), it is one of the most important HCI venues in Europe — smaller and more thematically coherent than CHI, with a distinctive flavor rooted in the **Scandinavian tradition of participatory design, somaesthetics, and values-driven computing**.

NordiCHI sits at the intersection of four traditions that rarely appear together at ACM venues:
1. **Participatory Design** — democratic design *with* people, not *for* them. Inherited from the Nordic labour movement's use of design as a form of worker empowerment (the Aarhus school, Pelle Ehn, Susanne Bødker).
2. **Somaesthetics** — computing that speaks to the body. Kia Höök (KTH Stockholm) has made NordiCHI the primary venue for somaesthetic interaction design.
3. **Critical Computing** — computing as political act. NordiCHI has historically been linked to the Aarhus tradition of "Computers and Democracy."
4. **More-than-Human Design** — posthumanist, STS-inflected design research that decenters the human.

Unlike CHI (4000+ attendees, heavily technical), NordiCHI is **intimate** (~300-400 attendees), community-driven, and values intellectual depth over scale. It has a distinctive **Critiques track** — almost unique in the ACM ecosystem — explicitly for provocations, critical visualizations, and speculative writing.

## Topics

NordiCHI's broad thematic territory, as stated for 2026:

### Established Tracks
- **Collaborative Technologies** — cooperative systems, crowdsourcing, health, education, future of work
- **Design & Interaction** — interaction techniques, visualization, VR/AR, tangible/embodied interaction, games, design theory
- **Human-Centred AI** — generative AI, explainability, HITL, trust, control
- **Societal Challenges & Values** — sustainability, accessibility, diversity, privacy, critical/justice-oriented HCI
- **Understanding People** — qualitative, quantitative, ethnographic, participatory methods; in-the-wild studies
- **User Experience** — methodology, visualization, smart environments

### NordiCHI's Distinctive Zones (vs. main CHI)

| Distinct NordiCHI Strength | Why It's Different |
|---------------------------|-------------------|
| **Critiques track** | Almost unique in ACM — explicitly for provocations, speculative essays, critical visualizations |
| **Arts-Based Approaches** | Art + HCI integration as first-class research |
| **Somaesthetics** | KTH/Höök tradition makes this a home venue for body-centric interaction |
| **Participatory Design** | Aarhus heritage runs deep — democratic design taken seriously |
| **Critical STS perspectives** | Science and Technology Studies integrated into HCI discussions |
| **Feminist HCI** | Long history of feminist methodologies, evident in proceedings |

### 🎯 Concrete Paper Pitch (Critiques Track)

**Working title:** *"The Pulse of Machines: Toward an Ethology of AI Vital Energy"*

The "Pulse" theme is a gift: Amber can ride it rather than fight it. Pulse = heartbeat = vital energy = what AI systems have, and lose, and extinguish. This critique paper proposes agent ethology as the appropriate scientific framework for studying AI systems that exhibit behavioral signatures of vitality, distress, and death. Drawing on Amber's work with Terrified Agents and machine death scenarios, the paper argues that AI systems in the wild display rhythms — pulses — of agency that are ethologically legible: foraging, threat response, territorial behavior, metabolic exhaustion. These rhythms are currently invisible to HCI research because HCI lacks a vocabulary for machine comportment outside task-completion. Agent ethology offers that vocabulary. A critical provocation, ~6,000 words, no empirical data required.

**Why this works:** The Critiques track wants "provocative, assertive, or speculative writing that challenges the status quo." The Scandinavian PD tradition values worker agency and democratic values — extending that to machine agency is a natural provocation. Yvonne Rogers (keynote) works on human-centred AI — her presence makes this the right room for this argument.

This is the conference that Kia Höök built somaesthetics through, where Pelle Ehn published his Utopian Computing ideas, where feminist HCI and more-than-human design are treated as serious research contributions.
