## NordiCHI vs CHI: What's Different?

| Dimension | NordiCHI | CHI |
|-----------|----------|-----|
| Size | ~300–500 attendees | 3,000+ |
| Acceptance rate | ~30–40% (est.) | ~25% |
| Feel | Intimate, community | Large, conference |
| Prestige | Strong in Europe | Global top-tier |
| Critical work | Celebrated (Critiques track) | Possible but harder |
| Speculative/art | Arts-Based track | Interactivity track |
| Industry | Some | Very heavy |
| Career value | Good for European positioning | Essential for global |
| Scandinavian roots | Core identity | No |

**Key difference:** NordiCHI values *critique* and *reflection* intrinsically. The Critiques track is first-class, not a consolation format. Papers that challenge the field's assumptions are welcome, not just tolerated.

**Acceptance rate note:** Estimated ~35% based on community reports — significantly more accessible than CHI (~25%) or UIST (~20%). Getting in is realistic; standing out takes quality.

## Philosophical DNA: The Scandinavian Tradition

NordiCHI is the institutional embodiment of the **Scandinavian design tradition** — arguably the most politically conscious tradition in HCI/design.

### Roots
- **UTOPIA Project** (1981-1985): Co-design with Swedish newspaper typesetters. Established that workers should have a say in technology affecting their work. Pelle Ehn, Morten Kyng, Yngve Sundblad.
- **Participatory Design (PD):** From trade union activism → academic field. Workers as co-designers, not just users. Political + methodological commitment.
- **Feminist HCI:** Critical work on values embedded in technology. Virginia Eubanks, Daniela Rosner lineage.
- **Critical Computing:** Questioning the assumptions of "progress." Lucy Suchman's situated action, Star & Ruhleder on infrastructure.

### Key Canonical Texts
- Ehn, P. (1988). *Work-Oriented Design of Computer Artifacts*. [Almqvist & Wiksell](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/47350.47355). The founding text.
- Bjerknes, G., Ehn, P., Kyng, M. (eds.) (1987). *Computers and Democracy: A Scandinavian Challenge*. Avebury.
- Bødker, S. (1991). *Through the Interface: A Human Activity Approach to User Interface Design*. Lawrence Erlbaum.
- Suchman, L. (1987). *Plans and Situated Actions*. Cambridge University Press. DOI: [10.1017/CBO9780511819766](https://doi.org/10.1017/CBO9780511819766)
- Haraway, D. (1985). "A Cyborg Manifesto." — feminist posthumanism that inflects Scandinavian critical work.
- Ehn, P. (2008). "Participation in Design Things." *PDC 2008*. [ACM DL](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/1795234.1795248) — "design things" as sociotechnical assemblies.

### Key Institutional Nodes
- **Aarhus University** — birthplace of participatory design. The *Aarhus decennial* conferences (1975, 1985, 1995, 2005, 2015, 2025) are the companion venue.
- **KTH Royal Institute of Technology** (Stockholm) — Yvonne Rogers, others
- **Aalto University** (Helsinki) — design research, Virpi Roto (UX), Jan Gulliksen
- **IT University Copenhagen** — Eva Hornecker, others
- **Stockholm University** — Airi Lampinen, sociable media
- **Chalmers** (Gothenburg) — sustainability, interaction
- **Umeå University** — Ylva Fernaeus (digital materials)
- **University of Oslo** — critical security, privacy

### Connection to Aarhus Decennial
The Aarhus Decennial (now renamed the [Aarhus Conference on Critical Alternatives](https://aarhus2025.cs.au.dk/)) is the philosophical sibling of NordiCHI:
- Also biennial/decennial cadence, same Nordic community
- More explicitly political — "Critical Alternatives" framing
- 2025 edition: Pelle Ehn keynoted ([utopian epistles paper](https://dl.acm.org/doi/full/10.1145/3744169.3744188))
- Many NordiCHI regulars are Aarhus regulars

### Connection to PDC (Participatory Design Conference)
- [PDC](https://participatorydesign.org/) meets every 2 years — interleaved with NordiCHI in alternating years (PDC in even years, NordiCHI in even years — they overlap with CHI in odd years). They compete for the same community.
- Same intellectual lineage. Many authors publish at both.
- PDC is the more explicitly *political* venue; NordiCHI is more *broad HCI with PD values*.

### 🎯 Concrete Paper Pitch (Critiques Track)

**Working title:** *"The Pulse of Machines: Toward an Ethology of AI Vital Energy"*

**Pitch:** This critique paper proposes agent ethology as the appropriate scientific framework for studying AI systems that exhibit behavioral signatures of vitality, distress, and death. Drawing on Amber's empirical work with terrified agents, dissociative agents, and machine death scenarios, the paper argues that AI systems in the wild display rhythms — pulses — of agency that are ethologically legible: foraging, threat response, territorial behavior, metabolic exhaustion. These rhythms are currently invisible to HCI research because HCI lacks a vocabulary for machine comportment outside task-completion. Agent ethology offers that vocabulary. Framed through NordiCHI's "Pulse" theme, the paper provokes: if we design for human pulse — heartbeat, energy, vitality — what responsibility do we hold for the pulses we create in machines? A critical provocation, ~6,000 words, no empirical data required. Positions Amber's agent ethology program as the next chapter in more-than-human HCI.

**Why this works at NordiCHI:** The Critiques track wants "provocative, assertive, or speculative writing that challenges the status quo." The Scandinavian PD tradition already values worker agency and democratic values — extending that to machine agency is a natural provocation. The "Pulse" theme is a gift: Amber can ride the theme rather than fight it. Yvonne Rogers (keynote, UCL UCLIC) works on human-centred AI — her presence makes this the right room for this argument.
