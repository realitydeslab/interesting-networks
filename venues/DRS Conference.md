# DRS Conference (Design Research Society)

The premier international biennial conference for design research. Where design researchers argue about what design *is*, what it *does*, and what it *should do*. More theoretical and methodology-oriented than CHI/DIS — this is where design philosophy gets made. The more-than-human design community has a strong, growing presence here. Edinburgh-based in 2026 means it's actually close to Amber.

## Key Facts

- **Organizer:** [Design Research Society (DRS)](https://www.designresearchsociety.org/) — founded UK, 1966. Registered charity (no. 1210067), 85 Great Portland Street, London W1W 7LT
- **Frequency:** Biennial (even years: 2020, 2022, 2024, 2026...)
- **Age:** Oldest multidisciplinary design research society in the world. Origins in Conference on Design Methods, London 1962
- **Scale:** ~500-600 research paper submissions per edition
- **Acceptance rate:** ~54% for research papers (DRS 2022: 588 submitted, 317 accepted — source: [DRS Digital Library](https://dl.designresearchsociety.org/drs-conference-papers/drs2022/researchpapers))
- **Publications:** [DRS Digital Library](https://dl.designresearchsociety.org/) — open access proceedings
- **Affiliated journal:** *Design Studies* (Elsevier)
- **Social:** [@thisisDRS](https://twitter.com/thisisDRS) on X

## Submission Types

DRS conferences accept four types of submissions:

1. **Research Papers** — Full academic papers (8-12 pages typically). Peer reviewed. Most competitive track. Published in DRS Digital Library
2. **PhD Hub** (formerly PhD Consortium) — For doctoral students presenting work-in-progress. Structured feedback from senior researchers
3. **Explorations** (formerly Labs) — Practice-based, experimental, speculative. Less traditional-paper format. Good for Amber's style
4. **Conversations** — Structured discussions, panels, workshop-style formats

### DRS vs DIS vs CHI — What's the ROI?

| Venue | Acceptance rate | Community | For Amber |
|-------|----------------|-----------|-----------|
| CHI | ~25% | Massive HCI, computing-heavy | Core for visibility |
| DIS | ~20-25% | Design × HCI, practice-based | Core for design HCI |
| DRS | ~54% | Pure design research, theory-heavy | Theory, more-than-human positioning |

**DRS ROI for Amber:**
- **Higher acceptance rate** means easier to get work in
- **Theory-forward** — better fit for philosophical papers that CHI/DIS reviewers might find too speculative
- **Community** — this is where Wakkary, Redström, Giaccardi publish design theory. Being read here = being part of that community
- **Edinburgh proximity** — DRS 2026 is 5h by train; Amber could attend even without submitting
- **Not instead of CHI/DIS — in addition to.** DRS gives design credibility; CHI/DIS gives HCI credibility

## Why Interesting

DRS is where **design research as a discipline** argues with itself. For Amber, it's the theoretical backbone community for more-than-human design — the place where Wakkary's HttF, Giaccardi's more-than-human participation, and Redström's design program are all presented and debated. The relatively high acceptance rate (54%) and practice-based "Explorations" track make it accessible. Edinburgh 2026 is geographically close and will likely center multispecies/posthuman design given the local organizing team (Pschetz, Bastian). Amber should go to DRS 2026 even without a paper — it's her design theory community.

The gap with CHI: DRS doesn't have CHI's prestige in HCI/CS departments, so it shouldn't replace CHI/DIS in Amber's submission strategy. But it should complement it.
