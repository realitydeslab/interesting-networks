## What It Is

The [Barbican Centre](https://www.barbican.org.uk/) is the largest performing arts centre in Europe, located in the Barbican Estate, City of London (EC2). Opened in 1982, designed by Chamberlin, Powell and Bon in brutalist style — itself a remarkable architectural artefact. Owned and funded by the City of London Corporation. Grade II listed.

The **Barbican Art Gallery** is the visual arts arm within the centre, but the term is loosely applied to three distinct spaces:
- **Barbican Art Gallery** — main ticketed gallery, large-scale solo and group exhibitions
- **The Curve** — free, curved corridor space; primarily used for new commissions, often site-specific
- **Sculpture Court** — outdoor space in the estate; used for major outdoor commissions (returned to use in 2026 after 10-year gap with Delcy Morelos)

The Barbican also runs **Barbican Immersive** — a branded arm that produces touring exhibitions (AI: More Than Human, Our Time on Earth, etc.) for international venues. These start at the Barbican and then tour. A significant signal of which exhibitions the Barbican considers its intellectual flagship.

**Scale:** 500,000+ visitors per year for visual arts. One of the most-visited contemporary art venues in the UK.

**Leadership (2026):**
- **CEO:** Abigail Pogson (2026–present; succeeded Claire Spencer who departed 2024)
- **Former Director for Arts and Participation:** Devyani Saltzman (Feb 2024–May 2026)
- **Curator:** Lotte Johnson (confirmed, 2026)
- **Assistant Curator:** Diego Chocano (confirmed, 2026)

## Programming Philosophy

Barbican's visual arts programming operates along several axes:

1. **Large-scale thematic surveys** — group exhibitions that address major intellectual questions: ecology, feminism, AI, postcolonialism, race. These become cultural benchmarks. Re/Sisters, Our Time on Earth, AI: More Than Human all worked this way.

2. **International solo retrospectives** — major surveys of mid-career to established artists who are getting their first major UK institutional recognition. Pattern: often artists from the Global South (Beatriz González, Delcy Morelos, Francis Alÿs), or artists making a historical case. The Barbican frequently *initiates* these shows (as with Noah Davis) rather than just borrowing them.

3. **Site-specific commissions** — The Curve and Sculpture Court are used for new commissions, funded through the **Barbican Commissioning Council** (a philanthropic body launched recently). This is where emerging-to-mid-career artists get a high-profile new work platform.

4. **Cross-disciplinary programme** — the Barbican is NOT just a gallery. Exhibitions happen alongside music, theatre, talks, film. An exhibition like Re/Sisters came with a full programme of events. This means Barbican shows reach a much broader, more diverse audience than a pure gallery context.

5. **Political commitment** — Barbican has gone through significant anti-racism reckoning (2021 Barbican Stories dossier, departure of Nicholas Kenyon). The post-2022 programme reflects a stronger commitment to decolonial, feminist, and Global South perspectives.

## Notable Exhibitions (Amber-Relevant)

### AI: More Than Human (May–Aug 2019)
[barbican.org.uk](https://www.barbican.org.uk/hire/exhibition-hire/ai-more-than-human)
The definitive major institutional survey of AI art. Explored consciousness, agency, human-machine relationships, and AI creativity across science and art. Artists included Marshmallow Laser Feast, TeamLab, Joy Buolamwini, Roman Verostko. Toured internationally and is still available for venue hire via Barbican Immersive — meaning the Barbican considers it a flagship statement exhibition.
**Relevance:** Direct overlap with Amber's AI/machine behavior interests. Benchmark for how a major institution frames AI art.

### Our Time on Earth (May–Aug 2022)
[barbican.org.uk](https://www.barbican.org.uk/hire/exhibition-hire/our-time-on-earth)
Ecology-focused survey asking how we can shift from a human-centred worldview to one that respects all life. Themes: circular economy, indigenous knowledge, rewilding, climate futures. Multispecies and more-than-human framing explicitly. Also available via Barbican Immersive.
**Relevance:** Core more-than-human content. Barbican commissioning this as a flagship shows institutional support for this frame.

### Re/Sisters: A Lens on Gender and Ecology (Oct 2023–Jan 2024)
Major survey of 50 international women and gender non-conforming artists exploring the intersection of gender and ecology. Included feminist science studies perspectives, Indigenous knowledge, ecological justice.
**Academic Lead: [[Kathryn Yusoff]]** — directly in Amber's existing network.
**Relevance:** Strongest existing link between Barbican and Amber's network. Yusoff's involvement means the Barbican's intellectual framing of ecology-feminism is aligned with the Black Shoals tradition that Amber knows.

### Imaginary Institution of India: Art 1975–1998 (2023–2024)
Historical survey of Indian contemporary art from 1975 to 1998 — a critical period of postcolonial cultural production. Artists included Nalini Malani, Vivan Sundaram, Gulammohammed Sheikh, Jangarh Singh Shyam. First major UK showing of this period.
**Relevance:** Non-Western art history, postcolonial critique, non-European tech-culture lineages.

### Francis Alÿs: Ricochets (2024)
[barbican.org.uk/whats-on/2024/event/francis-alys-ricochets](https://www.barbican.org.uk/whats-on/2024/event/francis-alys-ricochets)
Cinematic survey of children's games globally — Mexico, Iraq, Hong Kong, Afghanistan. Multi-screen film installations. Social documentation + play + cultural comparison. Cross-cultural community ethnography through art.
**Relevance:** Agent-like observational method (artist as ethologist), cross-cultural behavioral documentation, play as research object. Aligns with Amber's machine behavior / ethological lens.

### Delcy Morelos (May–Jul 2026) 🌱
[barbican.org.uk/whats-on/2026/event/delcy-morelos](https://www.barbican.org.uk/whats-on/2026/event/delcy-morelos)
New outdoor commission in the Sculpture Court. Colombian artist creates monumental 24-metre circumference installation from soil, clay, and fragrant spices. Inspired by Andean and Amazonian architecture. Sits in dialogue with the Barbican's brutalist concrete.
**Free.** Supported by Barbican Commissioning Council.
**Relevance:** Explicit ecology/land/multispecies framing. Amazonian knowledge systems. Material thinking (soil as medium). Perfect example of more-than-human institutional practice. VISIT when in London May–Jul 2026.

### Yuko Mohri (Oct 2026–Jan 2027) ⚡ MUST-SEE
[barbican.org.uk/whats-on/2026/event/yuko-mohri](https://www.barbican.org.uk/whats-on/2026/event/yuko-mohri)
New site-specific commission in The Curve. Japanese artist whose practice spans kinetic sculpture, sound, film, photography, painting. Creates installations that transform via "unstable elements" — magnetism, electricity, gravity, dust, water. Found/everyday objects repurposed to trigger chains of reaction.
**Key framing from Barbican:** "The unpredictable and transient nature of these installations, affected and altered by the visitors around them, resonate with the ecological and social precarity of our time."
**Free exhibition.**
**Relevance:** This is ALife aesthetics in art form. Emergent systems, transience, environmental responsiveness. The interconnectedness + planetary crises framing mirrors Amber's machine behavior / more-than-human research. Also Japanese artist — interesting cultural context. Should be a direct inspiration for Amber's practice. MARK CALENDAR: Opens Oct 1 2026.

### Julia Phillips: Inside, Before They Speak (Jan–Apr 2026)
[barbican.org.uk/whats-on/2026/event/julia-phillips](https://www.barbican.org.uk/whats-on/2026/event/julia-phillips)
First UK institutional solo for German-American artist. Ceramic fragments pressed against the artist's own body, combined with metal hardware. Body, conception, human connection, attachment. Somatic sculpture.
**Relevance:** Embodied practice, somaesthetics, body-as-medium.

## Open Calls / Commissions

**Barbican Commissioning Council** — new philanthropy-backed body that funds new commissions in The Curve and Sculpture Court. Founded by Anastasia Bukhman/Bukhman Foundation, Stephanie Camu, Aarti Lohia/SP Lohia Foundation, Mark & Dana Strong, and Tia Collection. This is how artists like Yuko Mohri (2026) and Delcy Morelos (2026) were commissioned.
No public open call — works through curator networks and nominations.

**Open Lab** — listed under "Take Part > Emerging & Practising Artists" in the Barbican's navigation menu. Appears to be a programme for emerging artists, but no public details available at time of research (pages not loading; likely in-development). Worth monitoring the Barbican website for Open Lab open calls.
[barbican.org.uk — Take Part section](https://www.barbican.org.uk/)

**Barbican Young Creatives** — youth creative programme (Young Poets, Young Film Programmers). Not directly relevant to Amber but signals the Barbican's investment in the next generation.

**No standard open call for exhibitions.** The Barbican commissions and curates proactively. Showing here requires curatorial relationships or being approached through institutional networks.
