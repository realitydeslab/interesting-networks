# NIME — New Interfaces for Musical Expression

**NIME** (New Interfaces for Musical Expression) is the annual international conference on music-technology interfaces, creative computing, and the aesthetics of human-machine musical interaction. Started in 2001 as a workshop at CHI, it became its own conference from 2002. Proceedings published open-access at [nime.org/proceedings](https://www.nime.org/proceedings/).

NIME sits at the junction of HCI, music, embodied interaction, and critical creative technology. While nominally about "music," its actual scope includes: interactive art installations, body-machine interfaces, somaesthetics, participatory design of instruments, haptics, AI/ML in creative practice, and critical examination of what "new" means in human-computer interaction. The field has increasingly absorbed HCI methodology (especially from CHI) and critical theory.

NIME 2026 keynotes: location at Imperial College London + Loughborough University London; hybrid format.

**Physical interaction & interface design:**
- Novel controllers and gestural interfaces
- Augmented and hybrid instruments (part-acoustic, part-digital)
- Haptic feedback, force feedback, tactile interfaces

- Embodied cognition in musical performance

## alt.nime — The Key Track for Amber

**alt.nime** was introduced at NIME 2026 as a response to the *discontinuation of alt.chi at CHI 2025*. It explicitly carries forward the alt.chi tradition:

> "a forum for controversial, risk-taking, and boundary-pushing research"

alt.nime submissions might:
- "fight with core musical assumptions, explode technological paradoxes, **deconstruct research myths and narratives**"
- "apply **unorthodox methods** that expand NIME boundaries"
- "engage with controversial socio-techno-musical issues through **politically activist or interventionist perspectives** such as whistleblowing, boycotting, hacking, jamming and détournement"
- "consider different ways to disseminate NIME research throughout the conference such as sharing objects, zines, pop-ups interventions or **situated participatory formats**"
- "question what a NIME research contribution is"

**This is EXACTLY Amber's research aesthetic.** Machine death, parasitic agents, terrified agents — all of these could be framed as alt.nime contributions. The "unorthodox methods" framing is identical to what made alt.chi work attractive for practice-based critical researchers.

## Why Interesting

NIME is where the body meets computation in a way that's explicitly aesthetic, not just functional. Unlike CHI (which tends toward utility) or DIS (which frames things through design), NIME asks: what is it like to *be* an interface? What happens when gestures become sounds? This is somaesthetics made technical. For Amber, NIME is adjacent territory worth monitoring — especially because alt.nime is now the new home of the alt.chi spirit, which was Amber's community in HCI (critical, unconventional, practice-based). The London 2026 edition is a gift: attend, network, scope, plan for 2027.
