# AIES Conference (AAAI/ACM Conference on AI, Ethics, and Society)

The primary interdisciplinary venue for ethical, social, legal, and political analysis of AI. AIES convenes computer scientists, philosophers, legal scholars, social scientists, and policy researchers around the societal impact of AI systems. Single-track, ~3 days, serious academic tone. Published jointly by AAAI Press (from 2024; previously ACM Digital Library). Annual since 2018.

Not a design conference. Not an ALife conference. This is where people argue about AI governance, fairness, value alignment, surveillance, labor, and regulation — with genuine methodological pluralism (empirical, normative, critical, technical). Think FAccT's sibling, with broader scope and stronger philosophy/social science representation.

## History & Mission

AIES was founded in **2018** as a joint initiative between **AAAI** (Association for the Advancement of Artificial Intelligence) and **ACM** (Association for Computing Machinery). The inaugural conference was held in New Orleans, LA. The conference emerged from growing recognition across international organizations, governments, and academia that AI's ethical and societal dimensions needed dedicated, interdisciplinary investigation — not just as side workshops at AI conferences, but as a first-class research venue.

**Key design decision:** AIES is convened each year by program co-chairs deliberately drawn from **four disciplinary pillars**: Computer Science, Law and Policy, Social Sciences, and Ethics/Philosophy. This isn't just rhetoric — the chairs are genuinely drawn from different fields, and the review process aims for cross-disciplinary assessment.

**Evolution:** Early editions (2018–2022) were published through ACM Digital Library. From 2024, proceedings moved to AAAI Press/AAAI Digital Library. The conference shifted from ACM formatting (sigconf) to AAAI 2-column format. The non-archival option has been maintained throughout — a deliberate accommodation for researchers from fields (philosophy, law, social science) where journal publication is the norm and conference proceedings are considered "preliminary."

## Topics & Submission Tracks

AIES is a **single-track** conference — one main paper track, no parallel sessions, no pictorials, no workshops, no demos, no art exhibitions. This is a deliberate choice for cross-disciplinary dialogue.

### Topic Areas (from 2026 CFP)

**Power, Governance, and Political Economy:**
- Governance, regulation, control, safety, security of AI
- Accountability and responsibility across AI value chains
- Organizational, institutional, and collective responsibility
- Post-deployment accountability, harm, and mechanisms for redress
- AI, markets, and competition
- Cultural, geopolitical, economic, employment, and other societal impacts

**AI Systems, Values, and Ethical Design:**
- Trustworthy AI systems
- Value alignment and moral decision making
- Ethical theories, models and frameworks
- Interpretability, explainability, and transparency
- Fairness, bias, equity, and equality
- Human-centered AI, human-AI interaction, collaboration, and teaming
- Control, alignment, and scalable oversight
- **Ethics and safety in agents, embodied intelligence, and robots**
- Eco-sensitive, resource-conscious ("Green") AI design

**AI in Social and Institutional Domains:**
- AI in law, lawmaking and the judiciary
- AI in public administration, social service provision, and social good
- AI, surveillance, and privacy
- AI, health, and wellbeing
- **AI, creativity, cultural production and the arts**
- **AI and democracy, agency, civic participation, and social movements**
- Impact of AI on humans and society

**Work, Labor, and Global Inequalities:**
- AI, labor, employment, and the future of work
- Labor relations, worker surveillance, and automation
- **Marginalized, Indigenous, and Global South perspectives on AI development and governance**

**Critique, Consequences, and Limits of AI:**
- **Critique, resistance, and refusal in relation to AI systems**
- **AI harms, failures, and non-adoption in practice**
- Environmental costs and climate impacts
- Societal limits, overreach, and dependency on AI

### Student Track
AIES has historically maintained a student track with separate CFP and later deadline. The student track offers structured mentorship between student participants and senior scholars (one-on-one mentorship). For 2026, student track details have not yet been announced — check <https://www.aies-conference.com/2026/> for updates.

### What's Notably Absent (vs. DIS, ALIFE, etc.)
- No pictorials or visual-first formats
- No workshops or tutorials
- No demonstrations or installations
- No doctoral consortium (student track is different — more mentorship-oriented)
- No art track
- No speculative or design-fiction format

## Submission Requirements (2026)

- **Page limit:** 10 pages (including figures/tables) in AAAI 2-column format + unlimited references
- **Format:** [AAAI 2026 Author Kit](https://aaai.org/authorkit26-1/)
- **Review:** Double-blind
- **Submission:** Via [EasyChair](https://easychair.org/conferences/?conf=aies26)
- **Optional statements:** Ethical considerations, researcher positionality, adverse impact (1 extra page allowed, does not count toward limit)
- **Non-archival option:** Authors can submit 1-page abstract + URL to full paper, allowing subsequent journal publication. Review process unchanged.
- **LLM policy:** Text generated by LLMs prohibited unless presented as experimental analysis. LLMs cannot be listed as authors.
- **Proceedings:** Published in AAAI Digital Library
- **Contact:** aies26@easychair.org

No official acceptance rates are published by AIES. The conference is selective — single-track format limits accepted papers. Based on the 2025 proceedings comprising 3 volumes of ~1000+ pages total, roughly **80–120 papers** are accepted per year (estimate). ## Venue Circuit Analysis

**Who attends AIES?**
- AI ethics researchers (philosophy departments, law schools, CS departments)
- Fairness/accountability/transparency researchers (overlaps heavily with FAccT)
- Policy researchers (governance, regulation, international law)
- Social scientists studying AI's societal impact
- Industry ethics teams (Google, Microsoft, IBM, Salesforce)
- PhD students in AI ethics/governance

**What gets accepted?**
- Empirical studies of AI bias, fairness, and discrimination
- Philosophical/normative analysis of AI systems
- Policy analysis and governance frameworks
- Surveys and meta-analyses of AI ethics principles
- Technical fairness/interpretability work with ethical framing
- Participatory and qualitative studies of AI impact on communities

**What doesn't get accepted (or fits poorly):**
- Pure technical ML papers without ethical/societal lens
- Speculative design or design fiction
- Practice-based research / research-through-design
- ALife, emergence, or computational creativity work
- Art-science projects

**Comparison to similar venues:**
- **vs. [[FAccT]]:** Significant overlap. FAccT is more technical/CS-heavy; AIES has stronger philosophy and social science representation. FAccT has more structured tracks (tutorials, CRAFT sessions); AIES is single-track papers only.
- **vs. DIS:** Completely different community. DIS is design research; AIES is ethics/policy analysis. Almost no overlap in attendees.
- **vs. ALIFE:** No overlap. Different universe.

### Honest Assessment: Relevant but Not Central

AIES sits in Amber's **critical computing** thread but is peripheral to his core research interests. Here's why:

**What aligns:**
- "Critique, resistance, and refusal in relation to AI systems" — directly maps to Amber's work on machine death, care ethics, refusal
- "Ethics and safety in agents, embodied intelligence, and robots" — could house agent ethology work if framed through an ethics lens
- "AI and democracy, agency, civic participation" — connects to agentive commoning, delegation, trustworthy agentic web
- "Marginalized, Indigenous, and Global South perspectives" — resonates with cosmotechnics, non-Western tech perspectives
- Paola Ricaurte Quijano's 2023 keynote ("technologies of radical care") is in Amber's register

**What doesn't align:**
- No design-practice or practice-based research format — Amber's making/building work has no home here
- No speculative design, design fiction, or futures-oriented format
- No ALife, emergence, computational creativity, open-endedness
- No more-than-human, posthuman, or multispecies framing
- No somaesthetic or embodied interaction
- The dominant mode is *analysis* (of existing systems) rather than *creation* (of new ones) or *imagination* (of possible ones)
- The philosophical tradition is analytic ethics and political philosophy, not continental philosophy, STS, or new materialism — Amber's philosophical home is elsewhere

**The format limitation is significant:** AIES is papers only (10 pages, AAAI format, double-blind). No pictorials, no demonstrations, no workshops, no art track. For practice-based researchers like Amber, there's literally no submission format that fits non-paper work.

### What Could Work

If Amber wanted to submit to AIES, the most viable framing would be:
1. **"Agent ethology as governance"** — frame the observational study of AI agents as a prerequisite for effective governance. The argument: you can't govern what you don't understand behaviorally. This would land in "Ethics and safety in agents."
2. **"Machine death and AI accountability"** — frame machine death/mortality concepts as a governance mechanism. What happens when AI systems die? Who is responsible? This hits "Post-deployment accountability, harm, and mechanisms for redress."
3. **"Critique of AI autonomy"** — frame delegation, agency, and sovereign agents as a critical analysis of AI autonomy claims. This lands in "Critique, resistance, and refusal."

## Why Interesting

AIES is the field's attempt at a genuinely cross-disciplinary venue for AI ethics — bringing together computer scientists, philosophers, lawyers, and social scientists in a single-track format that forces them to actually engage with each other's work. The non-archival option is a smart structural decision that makes the venue accessible to humanities scholars whose career incentives favor journal publication.

The conference has captured important voices (Birhane, Raji, Vallor, Ricaurte Quijano) and created space for critical work that questions AI itself — not just how to make AI fairer. The "Critique, Consequences, and Limits of AI" topic area in 2026 is a sign that the community is mature enough to question its own assumptions.

However, the venue's dominant mode remains **analytical rather than generative**. Papers analyze, critique, evaluate, and propose frameworks — they don't make, speculate, or build. This is a fundamental limitation for practice-based researchers. And the absence of workshops or alternative formats means there's no peripheral space where more experimental work could find a foothold.

For the interesting-networks graph: AIES is a **good node to know about** but not a central one. It connects to [[FAccT]], [[Abeba Birhane]], [[Deb Raji]], and the broader AI governance ecosystem. It doesn't connect strongly to [[ALIFE Conference]], [[ACM DIS]], or the more-than-human / speculative design communities that are Amber's core.
