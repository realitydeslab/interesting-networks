Online journal of critical writing on architecture, urbanism, and landscape. One of the most prestigious venues for long-form critical essays at the intersection of design, culture, and theory. Not an academic journal — it's essay-based, pitch-driven, heavily edited, and functions more like a highbrow critical magazine. **Open access, free to read.** [[Shannon Mattern]]'s regular column "Urban Intelligence" (on data, infrastructure, and smart city critique) is one of its most widely-read features. ## Scope & Topics

Places Journal's stated scope: architecture, urbanism, and landscape — but interpreted very broadly. In practice, over the last decade it has expanded significantly toward:

- **Infrastructure critique** — pipes, cables, data centers, utilities, maintenance, repair
- **Technology and cities** — smart cities, algorithmic governance, surveillance urbanism
- **Media theory** — treating buildings, cities, landscapes as media
- **Environmental humanities** — climate, ecology, more-than-human geography
- **Housing and planning** — policy-adjacent but theory-rich
- **Cultural geography** — landscape as cultural text
- **Racial and decolonial urbanism** — space, power, justice

**What it does NOT publish:**
- Empirical research (no datasets, no user studies, no quantitative findings)
- Technical CS/HCI papers
- Conference-style research with evaluation sections
- Heavily footnoted academic papers in the traditional sense

## Format & Process

**NOT peer-reviewed.** Essays are commissioned or solicited through pitch.

- **Pitch-based:** Authors submit pitches (short proposals). Editorial team selects and commissions.
- **Heavily edited:** The editing process is intensive — more like magazine journalism than academic publishing.
- **Essay length:** ~3,000–8,000 words, highly crafted prose
- **Open access:** Free to read, no paywall
- **Publication cadence:** Continuous online publication, typically several essays per month
- **No submission fee**
- **Acceptance rate:** Unknown, but very selective. The journal is curated, not open submission. Cold pitches are accepted but the hit rate for unsolicited pitches is low. Better approach: relationship → commission.

## Intellectual Positioning

Places Journal occupies a unique niche: it's too theoretical and critical for mainstream architecture trade press (Dezeen, ArchDaily), and too accessible and essayistic for traditional academic journals (JSAH, *Grey Room*, *Journal of the Society of Architectural Historians*). It sits between those worlds.

**Comparable venues:**
- **[e-flux architecture](https://www.e-flux.com/architecture/)** — more explicitly leftist/political, more continental philosophy, similar essay format
- **[Log](https://www.anycorp.com/log)** — print journal, shorter essays, more formalist
- **[Metropolis](https://metropolismag.com/)** — more accessible, design industry
- **[Architectural Review](https://www.architectural-review.com/)** — UK equivalent, more historical
- **[Cabinet Magazine](http://www.cabinetmagazine.org/)** — adjacent spirit, more art/culture, less architecture

Places Journal is probably closest in spirit to *e-flux architecture* but with more empirical/social science grounding and less continental theory jargon. Shannon Mattern's column is distinctive for bringing media studies and anthropology into the architectural conversation — that's the niche Amber could inhabit.

**⚠️ Conditional fit.** Places Journal is NOT a direct venue for Amber's typical research output (CHI papers, ALife papers, speculative prototypes). But it IS a viable venue for critical long-form essays if Amber wants to:

1. **Write about AI agents in urban/spatial contexts** — framing agent ethology as a question about how autonomous systems occupy, reshape, and behave in urban space. The "A City Is Not a Computer" lineage creates an obvious opening for a piece asking "What does it mean for a city to host non-human agents?"

2. **Publish a critical essay on more-than-human design and posthuman architecture** — Places Journal already publishes work on multispecies urbanism, environmental sensing, and non-human agency. Amber's angle on machine behavior and agent ethology fits this conversation.

3. **Write on machine death and the temporality of AI systems in urban infrastructure** — the mortality angle could be framed architecturally (infrastructure decay, end-of-life systems in cities, machine cemeteries).

### Why This Matters
Shannon Mattern's column is the proof of concept. She built a career publishing media theory and anthropology at this venue by framing it through an architectural/urban lens. Amber could do the same from a CS/design angle. The precedent exists.

1. **Read 5-10 Mattern essays** to understand the prose register and framing moves she uses — how she enters through the concrete (a repair manual, a sandwich board, a piece of sandpaper) and expands to the theoretical. Amber would need to master this mode.
