## Vibe
Miguel Abreu Gallery is one of New York's most intellectually serious commercial galleries — an art space that doubles as a philosophical salon. Operating out of two lower east side addresses (88 Eldridge Street, the main programming space; 36 Orchard Street, the secondary venue), it sits at the precise intersection of contemporary art, continental philosophy, and the global speculative theory circuit.

The gallery is the de facto NYC home for the **[[Urbanomic]]** / **[[Sequence Press]]** publishing ecosystem — events here have featured Quentin Meillassoux, Alain Badiou, Reza Negarestani, Nick Srnicek & Alex Williams, [[Yuk Hui]], and Jason Bahbak Mohaghegh. But it's not just a lecture venue: the artist roster includes Tishan Hsu (MIT-trained, body-technology-posthumanism pioneer), Pamela Rosenkranz (biochemistry-and-the-body philosopher-artist), Sam Lewitt (technology and material systems), Jean-Luc Moulène (conceptual rigor), and a constellation of artists engaging deeply with philosophical and posthuman questions.

This is the gallery for people who read Collapse journal, appreciate speculative realism, care about xenofeminism, and want their art to do real philosophical work. Not Chelsea corporate art — downtown intellectual seriousness.

## History
- **Founded ~2006** by Miguel Abreu (director). The events archive traces back to 2006 on the website. Located in the Lower East Side, long the home of NYC's more experimental art communities.
- **88 Eldridge Street** — main gallery space, primary lecture/event venue. Lower East Side, Manhattan.
- **36 Orchard Street** — secondary space, used for events and smaller programming.
- Early years established connections with French continental philosophy: Quentin Meillassoux's *The Number and the Siren* book launch (2012) was one of the foundational events in the Urbanomic/Sequence Press collaboration.
- Sequence Press co-founded as the NYC arm of the Urbanomic distribution and publishing network, operating in close association with the gallery. Sequence Press handles US distribution and co-publishing with Urbanomic.
- The gallery developed an artist roster that combines conceptual rigor with philosophical ambition — R. H. Quaytman, Jean-Luc Moulène, Sam Lewitt, Scott Lyall, Blake Rayne — alongside more recent additions like Tishan Hsu (retrospectively), Pamela Rosenkranz, and Nour Mobarak.
- Parallel programming strand: regular lectures, book launches, and philosopher visits that establish the gallery as a locus for the wider art-philosophy circuit in NYC.

### Most Relevant to Amber

**[[Tishan Hsu]]** ⭐⭐⭐ — [miguelabreugallery.com/artists/tishan-hsu/](https://miguelabreugallery.com/artists/tishan-hsu/) — MIT-trained architect (B.S. Environmental Design 1973, M.Arch 1975), turned artist. Since the mid-1980s, has explored cognitive and physical effects of technology on the body. Uses unusual materials, software, and fabrication to make enigmatic paintings/sculptures probing the posthuman body's relationship to screens, networks, and digital systems. Prophetic practice: he was doing "body-as-interface" work before the internet existed. Key exhibitions: *Liquid Circuit* (SculptureCenter/Hammer Museum, 2020), 59th Venice Biennale *Milk of Dreams* (2022), MAMCO Geneva survey (2024), Interface Remix (MOCA Toronto, 2025). Collections: Met, Whitney, MoMA, Centre Pompidou, Tate Modern, M+. **Why for Amber:** Body-technology interface art that predates posthumanism theory. Direct overlap with agent ethology (what does the body become when wired into systems?), machine behavior, and more-than-human design. His MIT training plus philosophical depth is Amber's exact background.

**[[Pamela Rosenkranz]]** ⭐⭐ — [miguelabreugallery.com/artists/rosenkranz/](https://miguelabreugallery.com/artists/rosenkranz/) — Swiss artist (b. 1979). Represents Switzerland at 56th Venice Biennale (2015, *Our Product*). Work investigates biochemistry, pharmaceutical culture, skin, fluids, and the body's relationship to consumer capitalism. Thinks about the human as a biological entity in a world of synthetic substances. Blurs the boundary between biological and industrial, natural and artificial. **Why for Amber:** Her work engages posthumanism and more-than-human thinking through biological/material lens — how is the human body already a chemical interface? Relevant to Amber's mortality and more-than-human interests.

**[[Nour Mobarak]]** ⭐⭐ — [miguelabreugallery.com/artists/nour-mobarak/](https://miguelabreugallery.com/artists/nour-mobarak/) — Artist working with sound, language, and bodily resonance. **Why for Amber:** Language as material, voice and body, possibly relevant to embodied interaction work.

**[[Sam Lewitt]]** ⭐⭐ — [miguelabreugallery.com/artists/lewitt/](https://miguelabreugallery.com/artists/lewitt/) — Conceptual artist working with information systems, materials, and the logic of industrial/technological processes. Engaged with infrastructure, data, and material flows. **Why for Amber:** Critical computing lens applied to material systems; relevant to speculative design and computational systems research.

**[[Rochelle Goldberg]]** ⭐⭐ — [miguelabreugallery.com/artists/goldberg/](https://miguelabreugallery.com/artists/goldberg/) — Works with ecological entanglement, decay, organic and industrial materials interpenetrating. **Why for Amber:** Relevant to more-than-human design and multispecies entanglement.

**[[Jean-Luc Moulène]]** — [miguelabreugallery.com/artists/moulene/](https://miguelabreugallery.com/artists/moulene/) — French conceptual sculptor. Has participated in philosophical conversations alongside Badiou and Negarestani at the gallery. Work involves logic, impossibility, mathematical/philosophical concepts materialized.

**[[R. H. Quaytman]]** — Painter developing a serial, archival, self-referential body of work ("chapters") that treats painting as a system. Recent Sequence Press book launch (2025) with Susan Howe, Yuri Stone, and Felix Bernstein.

**[[Liz Deschenes]]** — Photographer whose work investigates the fundamental conditions of photography, light, and perception. Conceptually rigorous.

**[[Eileen Quinlan]]** — Photographer working with darkroom processes, chance, and materiality. Explores photography's limits.

## Sequence Press / Urbanomic Network

**Urbanomic's Philosophical Lineage:**  
Speculative Realism → Object-Oriented Ontology → Accelerationism → Xenofeminism → Cosmotechnics. From Quentin Meillassoux and Ray Brassier (anti-correlationism, nihilism) through Nick Land (dark accelerationism) to Amy Ireland/Maya B. Kronic (cute accelerationism, xenofeminism), Reza Negarestani (rationalist posthumanism), and Yuk Hui (cosmotechnics, non-Western tech philosophy). This lineage is one of the most generative theoretical strands of the past 15 years.

## Why Amber Should Care

### The Core Overlap
Miguel Abreu Gallery sits at the intersection of **three things Amber cares about deeply**:

1. **Cosmotechnics / non-Western tech philosophy**: Yuk Hui's *Post-Europe* book launch happened here (April 2025). Yuk Hui is visiting professor at Amber's school (China Academy of Art). The Urbanomic catalogue publishes *The Question Concerning Technology in China*. This is the physical space where Amber's intellectual lineage from cosmotechnics gets enacted in New York.

2. **Posthumanism / more-than-human / machine behavior**: Tishan Hsu's prescient body-technology interface work, Pamela Rosenkranz's biological-pharmaceutical posthumanism, Sam Lewitt's infrastructure systems thinking — these are all gallery artists working through the same questions Amber works through, from an art practice side.

3. **Speculative philosophy as research infrastructure**: Reza Negarestani's rationalist posthumanism (intelligence as engineering problem, agency as process), Amy Ireland's xenofeminism, the entire Urbanomic theoretical apparatus — this is the continental theory that feeds into what Amber does. The gallery is where that theory has its NYC home.

Additionally: **Hal Foster** (who participates in gallery events) wrote *The Return of the Real* and is one of the key critical theorists of art and politics. The gallery's convening of art critics (Foster, Joselit, Bois, Lee) for "The Arts at Risk" (May 2025) signals its role as a serious intellectual institution, not just a commercial gallery.

### Specific Intellectual Synergies
- **Reza Negarestani** — His rationalist theory of intelligence (mind as normative process, reason as engineering) is philosophically adjacent to Amber's work on machine behavior and agent ethology. His concept of "inhumanism" (the human as a process that reasons beyond itself) maps onto posthumanism research.
- **Laura Tripaldi's *Parallel Minds*** — About material intelligence, the agency of matter. Directly relevant to more-than-human design.
- **"Machine Decision Is Not Final"** — The very title suggests AI decision-making as subject of theoretical and artistic inquiry. Must read.
- **Tishan Hsu** — Body wired into networks, digital effects on biological cognition — this is the artistic articulation of what Amber's HoloKit work touched technologically.

7. **Potential: publish with Sequence Press / Urbanomic** — Both publish at the theory-art interface. Amber's speculative design work, agent ethology research, or more-than-human work could find a home in the Urbanomic orbit. Their *Redactions* series documents art-philosophy projects.
