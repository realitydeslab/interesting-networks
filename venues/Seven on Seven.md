# Seven on Seven

Annual conference organized by [[Rhizome]] at the [New Museum](https://www.newmuseum.org/), New York City. Pairs seven contemporary artists with seven technology innovators and challenges them to develop something new together over the course of a single day. Each pair presents publicly the following day. Deliberately unpredictable in outcome — the format is structured but the content is left entirely open.

## Background

Founded in 2010 by [Lauren Cornell](https://laurencornell.net/) (then Rhizome Executive Director), Fred Benenson, John Borthwick, and Peter Rojas. Deliberately modelled on [Experiments in Art and Technology (E.A.T.)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experiments_in_Art_and_Technology) — the project Billy Klüver and Robert Rauschenberg initiated at Bell Labs in 1967, which paired Bell Labs engineers with artists including John Cage, Lucinda Childs, and Yvonne Rainer.

The conference has run annually since 2010, with virtual editions during the pandemic years (2020–2021). It operates under Rhizome's affiliation with the New Museum (235 Bowery, NYC), where it has been presented since inception.

**Organizational note:** Rhizome's current Executive Director is Zachary Kaplan. Key staff include Michael Connor (Editorial Director), Dragan Espenschied (Digital Conservator), Aria Dean. In December 2025, Rhizome announced it would discontinue its Conifer web archiving service in June 2026 — an indication of ongoing organizational restructuring. The relationship between Rhizome and the New Museum has at times been contentious, with members citing workplace issues.

## Topics

The conference does not prescribe thematic focus — pairs work in any direction they choose. Recurring themes that emerge from outcomes:
- Digital culture, internet infrastructure, and its politics
- Data visualization and the politics of information
- Ownership, authenticity, and digital labor
- Search, surveillance, algorithmic power
- Decentralization, blockchain, digital rights
- Internet art and net culture
- Cross-platform creative tools

As AI has become more culturally visible, more recent editions likely engage AI/ML tools. However, Seven on Seven's identity has been more broadly "digital culture" than specifically AI/ML or embodied computing.

## Past Editions & Notable Pairs/Outcomes

### Documented Highlights (curated from most interesting)

**2012: Taryn Simon × Aaron Swartz**
→ *[Image Atlas](https://archive.rhizome.org/artbase/66618/image-atlas/)* — search engine comparing image results across different national internet contexts. Made the cultural specificity (and geopolitics) of Google Search visible. One of the most cited outcomes; Aaron Swartz died by suicide in January 2013.

**2014: Kevin McCoy × Anil Dash**
→ *[Monegraph](https://monegraph.com/)* — one of the earliest blockchain-based digital ownership systems for art, effectively anticipating NFTs by ~7 years. Created on the Bitcoin blockchain; became a startup.

**~2014–2016: Laura Poitras (participation year uncertain)**
→ Created a short documentary film for The New York Times — an example of the format producing real-world journalism/documentary work.

## The Model: Invitation-Only, No Application

Seven on Seven operates entirely by **invitation through Rhizome's curatorial network**. There is no open call, no application, no submission process. Selection reflects:
- Who Rhizome curators know and respect
- Artists with strong net art / digital culture profiles
- Technologists with cultural credibility (not just VC-backed startup people — also critics, tool-builders, journalists)
- Diversity of practices, backgrounds, genders, geographies (Monica Narula's inclusion suggests this)

The commissioning model:
- Pairs are introduced, given one day to work together
- Rhizome may provide modest support/materials
- No formal residency, no output requirements beyond the public presentation
- Works that emerge can be further developed independently (as Monegraph was)

**How to get invited:**
1. **Become known to Rhizome curators** — Michael Connor, Zachary Kaplan, and their extended network
2. **Be published on or commissioned by Rhizome** — they commission essays and artworks
3. **Exhibit or speak in their circle** — New Museum, net art world, digital culture platforms
4. **Be bridging art + technology** in a way that's legible to their sensibility (critical, cultural, NOT corporate)
5. **Invite slot vs. artist slot:** Amber could fit either — the technologist slot (HoloKit, AR tools) or the artist slot (ALife practice, research-art)

## Why Interesting

Seven on Seven institutionalized the idea that artists and technologists should *make things together*, not just comment on each other's work. The Monegraph outcome alone — predating NFTs by 7 years — shows the format can produce genuinely consequential work. The Aaron Swartz / Taryn Simon pairing shows how art can make invisible infrastructure legible.

For Amber: the format resonates with their collaborative, tool-building approach. The E.A.T. lineage (Bell Labs engineers + artists) is a direct ancestor of what Amber does with HoloKit + ALife research. The conference is worth watching even if not a perfect venue — it's a mirror of the art-tech field Amber operates in, and Rhizome's commissions program is an accessible entry point.
