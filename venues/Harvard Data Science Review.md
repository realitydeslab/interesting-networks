# Harvard Data Science Review

Open-access journal published by [MIT Press](https://mitpress.mit.edu/) on behalf of the [Harvard Data Science Initiative](https://datascience.harvard.edu/). Quarterly. Launched 2019. Unusually hybrid structure — part research journal, part magazine, part public forum. Self-described as offering "A Telescopic, Microscopic, and Kaleidoscopic View of Data Science."

Unlike most journals, HDSR has **named sections** with distinct editorial identities, and **named columns** that are essentially editorially commissioned mini-journals within the journal. [[Stephanie Dick]] co-edits the "[Mining the Past](https://hdsr.mitpress.mit.edu/mining-the-past)" column — the key historical/critical voice within the venue.

Fully **open access** — no paywall, no author processing charges. Published on [PubPub](https://pubpub.org/) platform (MIT Press's OA platform). Everything is citable and findable.

## Sections

HDSR organizes content into named sections, not just article types:

- **[Panorama](https://hdsr.mitpress.mit.edu/panorama)** — "Overviews, Visions, and Debates." Broad perspectives from leaders in data science. Includes interviews, opinion pieces, debates, and reflective essays. Highly readable; engages expert and general audiences. *Recent examples: "Amid Advancement, Apprehension, and Ambivalence: AI in the Human Ecosystem" (Francine Berman et al., 2024); "Large Language Models: Trust and Regulation" (2024).*

- **[Cornucopia](https://hdsr.mitpress.mit.edu/cornucopia)** — "Impact, Innovation, and Knowledge Transfer." Applied data science — high-impact applications, social and environmental data science. More technical but emphasizes transferability and social impact.

- **Research articles** — peer-reviewed original research across data science foundations, methods, ethics, education.

## Columns

Columns are recurring themed sections with named editors — a distinctive feature of HDSR that makes it more like an intellectual magazine than a standard journal:

- **[Mining the Past](https://hdsr.mitpress.mit.edu/mining-the-past)** — Co-edited by [[Stephanie Dick]]. Short articles (c. 2,000–4,000 words) exploring historical roots of data science — mathematics, statistics, philosophy, computing history — and asking how those histories should inform contemporary practice. *"The aim is to provide enlightening and useful historical perspectives that contextualize the field, enrich technical discourse, and enhance the public understanding of data science."* This is the critical-historical heartland of HDSR.

- **Conversations** — Interviews with prominent data scientists and researchers.

- **Educator's Corner** — Pedagogy-focused pieces on teaching data science.

## Submission Guidelines Summary

*(Based on HDSR's stated mission and format; verify current guidelines at the submission portal)*

- **Research articles**: Standard peer-reviewed papers. Technical or methodological; should be reproducible where applicable
- **Panorama essays**: Broad perspectives, debates, interviews. ~3,000–6,000 words. Somewhat more editorial-solicited but open to pitches
- **Mining the Past columns**: SHORT historical essays, ~2,000–4,000 words. Targeted to data scientists, not history specialists — must be accessible without sacrificing rigor. Contact column editors (Dick) directly for column submissions
- **Open access**: All content free to read and cite immediately upon publication
- **Format**: Flexible (HDSR is more magazine-like than traditional LaTeX-formatted journals)
- **Submission pathway**: Email or online portal; contact editorial team for column pitches

## Audience

The open-access format and magazine-like presentation makes it one of the most widely-read venues in this space. Not a niche journal — HDSR has significant reach beyond the academy.
