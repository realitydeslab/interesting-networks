- **2026-02-26:** Initial deep research. Full profile created from scratch. 2025 edition (August 18-22, Aarhus) has occurred — proceedings now live in [ACM Digital Library](https://dl.acm.org/doi/proceedings/10.1145/3744169).

## Vibe
Decennial, agenda-setting, intimate. This is the conference that thinks 10 years at a time — each edition tries to define what "critical computing" means for the next decade. Rooted in 1970s Scandinavian labor democracy movements, now the most prestigious venue for critical HCI, participatory design, and computing-as-social-practice research. Not a mega-conference: small, curated, deliberately Scandinavian in sensibility — democratic, egalitarian, politically engaged. The conference ACM doesn't know quite what to do with but can't ignore.

| [2015](https://aarhus2025.org/past-conferences) | **Critical Alternatives** | ACM Digital Library | Utopian principles: things should not only be different but *radically better*. Introduced "critiques" as a new format |
| [2025](https://aarhus2025.org/) | **Computing (X) Crisis** | ACM Digital Library (majority Open Access) | Multiple converging crises: climate, inequality, democracy, more-than-human flourishing |

### Proceedings Evolution
A perfect artifact of 50 years of change in academic publishing:
- **1975**: Self-published reports in Scandinavian languages (group work documentation, early participatory ethos)
- **1985–1995**: Published books (Kluwer/Avebury press), fully international community
- **2005**: Online PDFs
- **2015**: Fully integrated into ACM Digital Library
- **2025**: ACM Digital Library, majority Open Access

## Submission Process

### 2025 (Now Closed — For Future Reference)
The conference uses **[PCS (Precision Conference System)](https://new.precisionconference.com/submissions)** for all submissions. Select "Aarhus" and "Aarhus 2025." Final camera-ready via **ACM TAPS workflow** (same as CHI).

### Track Types
| Track | Format | Review | Archival? |
|-------|--------|--------|-----------|
| **Papers** | Up to conference norms, ACM template, single-column for review | Double-blind | ✅ ACM DL |
| **Critiques** | Flexible format (ACM template optional but must include abstract) | By Critiques PC | ✅ ACM DL |
| **Work-in-Progress** | Max 2500 words, ACM template, single-column | Double-blind by 1 reviewer, curated by chairs | Adjunct proceedings |
| **Demos & Experiences** | ~1500-2000 words, PDF; video strongly recommended | Juried (no feedback given) | Adjunct proceedings (non-archival) |
| **Workshops** | Up to 4 pages | Curated by Workshop Chairs | Adjunct proceedings |
| **Doctoral Consortium** | 4-page PhD abstract + supporting docs | By DC Chairs | ❌ Not in ACM DL |

### "Critiques" Format — Unique to Aarhus
The **Critiques** track is a signature format that distinguishes Aarhus from other conferences. Introduced at Aarhus 2015, it welcomes non-traditional academic formats — essays, visual critiques, artistic pieces, manifestos — as long as they engage critically with computing. This is the entry point for art-science, speculative, and practice-based research that doesn't fit standard paper molds.

## Scandinavian Participatory Design Tradition
The Aarhus Conference is **the** manifestation of a distinctive Nordic computing culture:

**Origins in labor democracy (1960s-70s):** The movement emerged from Scandinavian trade union activism. Researchers at Roskilde University and Aarhus University worked with trade unions to ensure workers — not just management — had real influence over new computer systems. The [UTOPIA Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UTOPIA_project) (1981-84) is the landmark example: gave typographers design authority over new newspaper typesetting systems. The result? Better tools AND better work conditions.

**Key theoretical lineage:**
- **[Kristen Nygaard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kristen_Nygaard)** — SIMULA creator; showed that OOP emerged from thinking about systems AS processes, not just data structures. Used by workers to model their own work.
- **[Pelle Ehn](https://mah.se/person/pelle.ehn)** — *Work-Oriented Design of Computer Artifacts* (1988); theorized design as political practice
- **[Susanne Bødker](https://pure.au.dk/portal/en/persons/bodker%40cs.au.dk/)** — *Through the Interface* (1991); activity theory applied to HCI
- **[Morten Kyng](https://pure.au.dk/portal/en/persons/mkyng%40cs.au.dk/)** — co-edited foundational *Design at Work* (1991); pervasive healthcare IT

**What makes it distinct from US HCI:**
- Workers/users as co-designers, not just "users"
- Political economy of technology, not just usability
- Democracy in design process, not just product
- Utopian imagination as legitimate research mode
- Critical alternatives rather than optimization within existing constraints

**Connection to feminist and post-colonial thought:**
More recent Aarhus conferences have brought in intersectionality, feminist STS (Lucy Suchman is a direct link), and global South perspectives. The critiques track especially enables this.

Aarhus University is the permanent institutional home. Key units:
- **[CAVI (Center for Advanced Visualization and Interaction)](https://cavi.au.dk/)** — within School of Communication and Culture; primary organizing unit for 2025

- **[Department of Digital Design and Information Studies](https://cc.au.dk/en/)** — Parikka, Pold, Andersen home department

## Why Interesting
One of the oldest and most prestigious critical computing conferences in the world. But unlike CHI (which is huge, diffuse, and increasingly industry-captured), Aarhus has maintained its political edge by only meeting once a decade — there's no time for compromise. Each edition is a manifesto. The 2025 Critiques PC reads like a dream list of critical digital aesthetics scholars. The more-than-human framing ("flourishing life for all critters") signals genuine philosophical ambition, not just methodological pluralism. This is where the field goes when it wants to think hard.
