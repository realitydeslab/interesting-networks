## Focus Areas (2026 CFP)
FAccT uses **Focus Areas** to match papers with reviewers, not hard submission tracks:

1. **Evaluations and evaluation practices** — audits, assessments, measurements of fairness/safety/bias in data, algorithms, models, systems. Qualitative or quantitative. Includes cultural, environmental, social, economic impact evaluations.

2. **Experiences and interactions** — HCI work, decision support, human-in-the-loop, visualization, design theories (co-design, **speculative or critical design**), participatory and deliberative methods, organizational practices. **← Amber's strongest zone**

3. **Law and policy** — regulation, governance, legal doctrine, AI Act analysis, effectiveness of governance frameworks.

4. **Normative foundations and implications** — philosophy of science, epistemology, moral/political philosophy, STS, ethics. **← directly applicable to agent autonomy questions**

5. **Power and practice** — sociology, anthropology, cultural studies, history of computing, power relations in tech development. **← Amber's Dissociative Agents angle**

6. **System development and deployment** — design/development/deployment of fairer, safer, more accountable systems. NLP, CV, IR. **← more ML-heavy; less Amber's core**

**Out of scope:** Work without deep engagement with social components; purely hypothetical technical concerns.

### New for 2026: Revision Process
This year FAccT introduced a **three-way decision**: Accept / **Revise** / Reject. Papers getting "Revise" get time to address reviewer feedback and a full re-review. An extra page (up to 15pp) allowed in revision stage. This is new — papers don't just get Accept/Reject.

### Paper Length
Maximum **14 pages** (excluding references). Most papers are 12–14pp. One additional page allowed for ethics/adverse impacts statements.

### Archival vs. Non-archival
- **Archival:** appears in ACM Digital Library proceedings (treated as peer-reviewed publication; standard for CS authors)
- **Non-archival:** abstract only in proceedings; can be presented at conference. Good for law/humanities authors wanting to later publish in discipline-specific journals.

### Open Access (2026 change)
ACM fully transitioning to Open Access in 2026. All FAccT papers will be Open Access. Authors from non-participating institutions pay APC ($250 for ACM members, $350 non-members — subsidized from standard rates).

### Origins
FAccT grew from the **FATE (Fairness, Accountability, Transparency, and Ethics)** research group at Microsoft Research, co-founded by [[Kate Crawford]] and collaborators. The first FAT* workshop was held in 2014 alongside NIPS (now NeurIPS). After rapid growth, it became a standalone ACM conference in 2018 (NYC). The rename from FAT* to FAccT in 2021 dropped the wildcard asterisk (*) that had been placeholder for "and Ethics."

### Scale
- 2022 (FAccT '22) had 401+ papers submitted; acceptance rate typically **~20–25%**
- Community spans CS, law, social science, humanities, policy
- 400–600+ attendees typical; growing each year
- Increasingly global: Rio, Seoul, Athens locations signal deliberate non-Anglophone expansion

## Submission & Review Criteria

What reviewers look for:
- **Interdisciplinary rigor** — does the paper speak to multiple disciplines or just one?
- **Sociotechnical grounding** — is the "social" component deeply engaged, not just mentioned?
- **Novelty** — new frameworks, measurements, critiques, or systems
- **Impact** — policy implications, practitioner uptake, academic contribution
- **Ethics/positionality** — do authors acknowledge their position and potential harms?

What gets accepted:
- Empirical audits of AI systems (bias in hiring tools, facial recognition, LLMs)
- Theoretical frameworks for fairness definitions
- Critical/STS analyses of technology and power
- HCI studies of algorithmic interfaces
- Legal/policy analyses of governance frameworks
- Systems papers with accountability-first design
- Speculative/critical design work (increasingly welcomed)
- Mixed-methods social science

What reviewers dislike:
- Papers with only passing engagement with social dimensions
- Technically sound but socially naive work
- Work that doesn't engage the existing FAccT literature

## Controversies & Critiques

FAccT has attracted internal tensions worth knowing:
- **Big Tech sponsorship critique** — Google, Microsoft, Twitter/Meta have sponsored FAccT while building the systems FAccT critiques. The conference has a no-earmarking policy (sponsors don't influence paper selection), but the structural tension is noted.
- **Theory-practice gap** — criticism that papers are often too abstract for practitioners. Conference has been expanding CRAFT track to address.
- **Field capture concern** — some critics (e.g., Abeba Birhane's work) argue FAccT can become a legitimacy-laundering venue for tech companies rather than genuine critique.
- The **#TiMnit Gebru firing** from Google (2020) was a watershed moment that catalyzed critical discourse within the community about structural power.

These tensions make FAccT a living, contested space — exactly the kind of "edge of chaos" Amber finds intellectually productive.
