A regenerative power station, contemporary art centre, and **Kunststrom (art power) research laboratory** in Luckenwalde, Brandenburg, Germany (~50km south of Berlin). Founded 2017 by artist **Pablo Wendel**; launched publicly 14 September 2019. Co-directed by Pablo Wendel and curator **Helen Turner**. Not-for-profit: all revenue from energy production is reinvested into the contemporary art programme. The first contemporary art centre in the world powered by its own production. Website: [kunststrom.com](https://www.kunststrom.com).

The name *Kunststrom* is a German pun: *Strom* means both *electricity* and *stream/flow*; *Kunst* means *art*. "Art electricity" or "art current." The project frames art-making and energy production as the same act.

## Background & History

**2017** — Artist Pablo Wendel acquires a former brown-coal (*Braunkohle*) power station in Luckenwalde with the vision of reanimating it as a regenerative Kunststrom power station. The conceit is literal: convert the dead coal plant into a living art institution powered by clean energy, feeding power back to the national grid.

**2019** — Public launch. Opening exhibition *Kunststrom* (14 September 2019 – 28 March 2020, curated by Helen Turner): Nicolas Deshayes, Performance Electrics gGmbH, Lucy Joyce, umschichten. ## Mission & Model

E-WERK is the most radical institutional experiment in the German art world:

1. **Energy as art infrastructure**: The building actually generates renewable electricity (solar, likely wind) and sells it back to the grid. Revenue funds the art programme. You can literally *switch your household to Kunststrom* (the website has a switch.kunststrom.com page for switching electricity provider). The art institution and the energy infrastructure are the same object.

2. **Post-industrial site as living institution**: The coal plant is not just an evocative backdrop — it is the subject and the substrate. The machine was dead (decommissioned coal plant); Pablo Wendel reanimated it as a different kind of machine. This is machine death and resurrection as institutional concept.

3. **Quarterly programme, not permanent collection**: Opens Saturdays/Sundays, 12–18:00. Operates as an experimental exhibition/events programme, not as a collection-based museum.

4. **No structural funding**: The institution explicitly operates without institutional or structural public funding. Revenue = electricity sales + donations + project funding. This forces the energy-art integration to be real, not metaphorical.

5. **E-PAVILION**: Yearly commissioning programme — artists, designers, and architects realise permanent functional sculptures powered by alternative Kunststrom on the grounds.

6. **Stadtbad Live**: E-WERK is a founding partner of an urban development project to transform the former **Bauhaus Stadtbad** (historic public swimming pool) Luckenwalde into an internationally relevant venue. Partners: E-WERK, City of Luckenwalde, Luba GmbH. Expansion of the E-WERK ecosystem into the town.

## Institutional Model Analysis

E-WERK solves a problem that almost every alternative art space can't: **autonomous funding**. The coal plant generates electricity; the electricity is sold as "Kunststrom" to households; the revenue funds the programme. This means:

- No dependence on project grants (which force thematic alignment with funders)
- No dependence on state institutional subsidies (which force accountability to cultural policy)
- Revenue from a *physical resource* the institution controls, not from ticket sales or merchandise

The model is currently fragile (only 71 YouTube subscribers; 23 videos; revenue from a small power plant in a small Brandenburg town is limited), but it is conceptually the cleanest solution to the autonomy problem in alternative art institutions. The Stadtbad Live initiative is the next phase — using heritage buildings and public partnerships to build scale without structural dependence.

Compare to: Serpentine (dependent on state + philanthropic support), Ars Electronica (festival model), ZKM (state-funded). E-WERK is the only example of a machine-funded art institution.
